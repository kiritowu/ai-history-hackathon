Mr. Caine.

I shall be grateful if you can tell me whether there have been any developments in the method of dealing with claims against the Straits Settlements' Government in respect of ships requisitioned or chartered by them. There is in this file some correspondence from J. Swire and Sons Limited about two claims that they wish to make: (a) for the hire of two lighters (12) and (5), and (b) for the total loss of these craft. Correspondence has been going on intermittently since the beginning of the year, and we have several times told the firm that the matter is still under consideration. We have been seeking vainly to get details from the Manager of the Harbour Board about the hiring of the lighters. Swire's are now naturally anxious to have something more definite.

I observe from a minute of 24th July (15) on 18330/12E which I attach, that there is a likelihood that the Board of Trade will open a register of claims. I understand from Mr. Forrest that these would primarily be claims for losses of goods but that the register might well include other claims. I have since been told by Mr. Taylor, however, that if the register is opened at all, it will be kept by the Colonial Office as I understand the position is that it has been felt so far best that no claims should be met even when documents in proof of the claim can be supplied. Swire's claim rests entirely upon the statement of the firm that the vessels were hired.

You will see below (26) a minute by Mr. Ross of the Treasury Solicitor's Department in which he says that as far as he knows no decision has yet been taken by the Treasury and Colonial Office on the fundamental question whether the British Government is going to take over the liabilities of Colonial Governments. It appears to me that the only answer that I can give to Swire is that they may file their claims for registration and this must wait until it has been decided who is going to register. According to Number 30 the "HELLAS" is registered at Drammen in the name of Messrs. Bruusgaard Kiosterud and Company and has been in the control of the Norwegian Government, as disponent owners, since the invasion of Norway, so that it appears that the Mission are the persons entitled to receive freights on this vessel.

The cargo was requisitioned by the Straits Settlements Government and a formal protest was made by the Master with the Notary Public, who advised the Government that no freight had been paid and that the Master had a lien on the cargo. No acknowledgment was made by the Government and apparently unsuccessful efforts were made to obtain one.

Lighters A.2 and A.4

We refer to our letters of July 24 and October 1, 1935.

These vessels were requisitioned at Tientsin in October 1941 and left Shanghai, A.2 on October 15 and A.4 on November 19. The latter arrived in Singapore on November 30. A number of our lighters and small craft were on requisition in Singapore to the Ministry of War Transport, whose value for compensation has already been agreed and will, we do not doubt, shortly actually be paid. There is thus a good precedent for treating these similar craft in the same way.

There is no relation between floating property of this kind and rubber factories, tin smelting plants and properties of that kind, the value of which must be very difficult to assess for compensation.

As insurance was for charterers' account, it is not unreasonable to assume that they took out cover and have collected from underwriters.

In view of all the special circumstances we trust that you will see your way to reconsider the case of these two lighters, to agree the value for compensation and to settle our claim.

Dear Morris,

Your Department will have received from the Foreign Office a copy of the telegram from Balfour, Lisbon, No.1540, of the 26th September last, referring to various shipping claims by a Mr. Kmansang against the Hong Kong Government.

So far as we know here the first named ship "HARELDAWINS" is owned by the Tai Yau Steamship Co., 44 Des Voeux Road, West Hong Kong. Dear James,

Will you please refer to our previous correspondence ref: 18335/6/42 about Swire's claim in respect of the lighters alleged to have been requisitioned by the Straits Government towards the end of last year. I see I have a note that I told you over the telephone that we thought it might be advisable to postpone payment till there was some more evidence from Swire's or elsewhere showing what actually took place. Though it may still not be clear whether the Straits Government itself has paid anything for these lighters, and we have no other evidence than that already provided by Swire's of the actual bargain made by the Straits Government, the following comments may be helpful to you in settling the matter.

(1) It seems clear that Rodgers of the Singapore Harbour Board is thinking of some other lighters and not the A2 and A4 referred to in Swire's letter of the 22nd January last. Perhaps you are going to ask Rodgers to think again!

(2) The normal procedure adopted when such craft were requisitioned by our Sea Transport Officers on the China Coast was for hire to begin at the time of delivery to the requisitioning authority before towing. The cost of towage and insurance (if any) during the tow would have been the liability of the requisitioning authority.

(3) The duration of the Straits Government's liability to pay hire would be up to the date from which the lighters may be presumed lost, by capture or otherwise. A2 was towed southwards from Shanghai by the "KANCHOW". We cannot add anything to Swire's statement that "KANCHOW" left Shanghai between 10th and 17th October towing the lighter A2. The last news we have of her is that she arrived at Hong Kong on December 8th, 1941. The "NINGHAI"

Dear Jackson,

On the 19th February Miss James sent to Monson, under cover of a letter numbered S.T.N.10, a copy of correspondence with John Swire and Sons about the hire by the Straits Government of two of their lighters. We think it can be regarded as certain that the Government of the Straits Settlements did enter into an agreement to charter the two lighters, but it is not specifically stated in terms when the charter party commenced. It is clear from (b) and (c) in Butterfield and Swire's letter of the 5th September that the Straits Government had in fact accepted certain liabilities from the time of the departure of the lighters for their destination, but it is less clear that liability to pay hire started.

Started at the same time. This would presumably depend on the terms of the charter party. You may be able to say what would be the normal practice in such cases.

There is a small point connected with condition (c) in the Hong Kong Company's letter, namely, that it refers to departure from Taku Bar only, when the last paragraph mentions departure from Tientsin (Taku Bar) and Shanghai.

We have no information here regarding the movement or fate of the two lighters other than that contained in the correspondence which has been sent to us.

This amounts to:

A2 left Shanghai for Hong Kong between the 10th-17th October towed by "Kanchow." 

A4 left Shanghai direct for Singapore on or just before 21st November towed by "Ninghai."

It seems possible that A2 was caught when waiting at Hong Kong for onward towage and that A4 was nabbed by the Japanese before she could reach Singapore. We wonder whether any light could be thrown on the fate of the lighters by following up the movements of the towing vessels.

Is this a practical possibility? If, as we suggest, the Straits Government are liable to pay hire for the lighters whether they reach Singapore or not, how long does liability to do so continue? The Company have informed us that they also propose to claim compensation for the loss of the lighters.
