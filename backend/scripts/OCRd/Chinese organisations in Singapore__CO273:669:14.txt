On the 2nd January, the Secretary of State asked the Governor for a report on several points including the following:

"Is active co-operation of Chinese communities being organised in any particular forms, either by Government or spontaneously?"

On the following day the Governor replied:

"Yes, for the first time in history the Chinese representatives of all parties, including Straits born, Kuo Min Tang, communists, etc. came to me to say that the defeat of Japan is now their only interest, and placed themselves unreservedly at my disposal. This applies at present only to Singapore. I accepted, and they are organising themselves to undertake (a) supply of labour, (b) watch and ward, (c) propaganda. They can be of immense value, and are being given every help. Post war repercussions do not concern us in this emergency."

On the 8th January, the Governor, referring to air raids and resulting damage, said that the general attitude of the population was satisfactory, and the Indian and Chinese leaders were fully alive to the importance of maintaining this and are doing all they can.

On the 14th January, the Secretary of State telegraphed as follows:

"In present situation particular importance must be attached to fullest use being made of services of Chinese community in Malaya to defeat the Japanese invasion. I emphatically agree with view expressed in your personal telegram 3rd January that post-war repercussions do not concern us in this emergency. The toughest Chinese elements which have given us so much trouble in the past in Malaya are likely to be amongst the most useful for the immediate purposes. I appreciate that this change of attitude may present difficulties for your officers in many directions and particularly to Secretary for Chinese Affairs, whose duties hitherto must necessarily have brought him and his Department into conflict with those elements.

I assume that you have given clear and definite instructions to all concerned in order that the requirements of the new situation may be fully understood and acted upon."

to which the Governor replied as follows:

"Last paragraph correct.

Police report this development under name of Chinese Mobilization Council progressing satisfactorily though there are of course various internal jealousies which have to be tackled. For example, the Communists regard themselves as having been definitely promoted by the release of several members from gaol and the Kuomintang which is Chinese Government party feels in consequence it must assert itself by forming guerrilla bands, which of course we are encouraging. What we have to avoid is definite split."

===== Page 8 =====
Split between factions and this may safely be left to skilled officers with myself as intermediary in the last resort, which at present is not apparent. Meanwhile, police state that the propaganda bureau is excellent; watch and ward developments are favourable.

Labour is in the hands of Simpson, to whom all requisitions are to be sent and who will determine priority. He has Oakeley of the Chinese Protectorate (whom Council asked for) as liaison officer with the Chief Chinese Labour Contractor. Labour presents difficulties owing to the higher risks attached to work in certain areas, competition by contractors, etc., but these are being tackled. Maximum wage rates for daily paid labour have been fixed.

It will be appreciated that the conflict in China between the Kuo Min Tang and the Communists has continuously been reflected amongst the Chinese community in Malaya, and that until the Anglo-Japanese war broke out and we became China's ally in the fight, the Chinese were conducting in Malaya their own measures not only against the local Japanese but also against Chinese individuals and firms whom they suspected of having dealings with the Japanese. These measures frequently brought them into conflict with the Malayan authorities, who were responsible for the maintenance of law and order in Malaya, and who were specially guided by the policy of H.M.G. to see that British relations with Japan were not allowed to be compromised by such measures in British territory.

There is evidence that the jealousy between the Kuo Min Tang and Communist sections of the Chinese in Singapore is not yet laid, in spite of the effort towards a united front. Apart from evidence from most secret sources, the Chinese Ambassador himself has just made representations to the Foreign Office on the subject of the Committee which has been formed by the Chinese community in Malaya to assist the Government in the defence of Singapore. This Committee, he said, appears to consist of twenty members, ten of whom belong to the Kuo Min Tang Party and ten to the Communist Party, and is presided over by Tan Kah Kee, who he described as apparently Communist and a known opponent of the Chungking Government.

Moreover, a sub-committee called the Defence Corps Committee, which he believed to consist entirely of Communist members, was presided over by a man named Tan Kah Kee "who is apparently a communist and a known opponent of the Chungking Government."

Tan Kah Kee is a well-known Chinese industrialist in Singapore and a leading member of the Chinese community. In May 1941, Dr. Cheng K'ang-chai of the Chinese Foreign Office was visiting Singapore, and in the record of an interview which he had with Colonel Grimsdale of the British General Staff contains the following:

"He gave me at length his view of the present unfortunate squabble between Mr. Kao Ling Pei and Mr. Tan Kah Kee. It will suffice here to record that as regards the former, he considers him to be rather tactless and high-handed in his dealings with his opponents. Of the latter, Dr. Cheng is certain that he is not a Communist, though, having personally visited Yenan and other "Communist" areas and seen the efficient administration, etc., which prevails there, and in which ex-pupils of some of his own schools are"

Imperial and Foreign

CHINESE RUSH TO COLOURS

VOLUNTEERS AT SINGAPORE

From Our Special Correspondent
SINGAPORE, FEB. 8

At the headquarters of the newly formed Chinese volunteers, many of whom have already left to take up positions at the front, further details of this remarkable experiment have been disclosed. The officers are mainly British, and are drawn from all professions, but all are men who possess considerable local knowledge of the terrain, of the people, and of their languages.

As for the volunteers themselves, they also come from all professions. Many are labourers, but there are also shopkeepers and student intellectuals among them. Several are veterans of the wars in China who have settled in Singapore, and the Chungking Government have cooperated by sending some leaders well versed in guerrilla warfare to help in the training of them. These units are called the "Dalcoes," or the "Dalforce," after the senior British officer who had much to do with the inception of the enterprise.

In addition to those who have gone to the front, many more are in training. The response to the call for volunteers has been extraordinary. "If only we had arms with which to equip them," one of their officers said, "we could have 10,000 fresh men under arms."

POLITICAL GROUPING SYSTEM

Both the Malayan Communist Party and the Kuomintang, and the various organizations affiliated to them, have been active in setting the movement on foot. Its inspiration is predominantly Left Wing, even where it is not Communist. The men are grouped in military units according to their political sympathies. As they come from many parts of China—from Amoy, Hainan island, Canton, Foochow, and so on—many different dialects are in use. The Malayan Communist Party has always tried to use Mandarin as the common language, but actually Cantonese is the dialect that is most commonly spoken among the volunteers. The language problem, however, is not as serious one. Several Chinese girls have also volunteered. Like the women in Russia, they were eager to stand in the front line with the men, but for the present they have been assigned to first-aid work and to nursing behind the lines.

The local Chinese newspapers reported today that the volunteers had already been in action, and published some vivid accounts of their exploits. It is believed, however, that some of the reminiscences of veterans of the wars in China may have perhaps misled one of the local Chinese reporters. Until the volunteers have actually been in action judgment must be suspended on the success of the scheme.

It is obvious, however, that its potentialities are enormous. A great reserve of manpower is being tapped. These volunteers, unlike British and Indian troops, are accustomed to the climate and the terrain; they look like people of the country because they are people of the country; and they have keen personal interest in the defence of Singapore because they are fighting for their adopted home. Their enthusiasm at this moment is unbounded.

From their first big test they emerged with flying colours. Japanese bombers had set fire to one godown and men were wanted urgently to move valuable supplies. Several lorry-loads of volunteers went down to help in the work. They plunged recklessly into the flames and came out with cases and packages that were sometimes still on fire. One observer told how he saw a Chinese holding a flaming can of linseed oil under a hose to extinguish the flames. While they were at work Japanese bombers came over again and several of the volunteers lost their lives, but the rest went on working with undiminished energy. Later they returned to their billets with their spirit unshaken by the experience they had been through.

TEN RULES FOR SOLDIERS

A series of orders has been issued by the Chinese Anti-Aggression Council in Singapore to Chinese volunteers who are taking part in the defence of the island. These rules, published prominently in the local Chinese press, are as follows:

(1) Pull together; love, help, and respect each other;
(2) Obey all instructions and orders from the authorities;
(3) Do not use rifles and bullets wastefully;
(4) Cooperate with civilians and help them;
(5) Do not disclose military secrets;
(6) Do not grumble, complain, or lose your temper;
(7) Do not surraver; do not desert;
(8) Do not take anything from civilians;
(9) Have no fear of death and no greed for wealth; treat women respectfully;
(10) Do not kill prisoners.

SYDNEY - Everything possible is being done to prevent undue delays in delivery of airmails to Australians who are fighting to hold Singapore, says a joint statement by Air Minister Drakeford and Postmaster-General Ashley. The spread of war in the Pacific to the Dutch Indies has complicated problems, but mails would run the enemy gauntlet and plans are already made to expedite service.

KERR ONE - CALCUTTA: "I do not believe the Japanese will go as far as India," said Sir Archibald Clark Kerr, British Ambassador designate to Russia who is now in Calcutta en route to Moscow from China, interviewed by Reuters. "Nevertheless, if I were Indian, I should make my arrangements on the basis of war coming to India and should lose no time cooperating with authorities in India's defence," he said, adding it has been a stimulating experience to see the spirit with which the people of China have been fighting against Japanese aggression. Kerr declared, "The Chinese know how to fight; they will fight and go on fighting until the end. Of this I have no doubt. The people in Chungking have been constantly heavily bombed for three years, yet have taken it without murmur or complaint and in a way which impels admiration. At Changsha, the Chinese won a handsome victory; they are wise enough to draw upon experience they gained from two earlier battles they fought over the same ground. The Japanese, whose methods are conservative, blundered in old tactics."

KERR TWO - "The Chinese met them with something fresh and gave them a good hammering. The Changsha victory was probably as big as any the Chinese have had in this war," Kerr said. In the unlikely event of the Burma Road being cut, the Chinese would be seriously handicapped, but they will carry on all the same. The Chinese are convinced, and rightly so, that the Allies will win. They would, of course, take their place as equals with other nations fighting for democracy when the time came to talk peace. Comparing present Japanese successes to a sting wasp on the rump of an elephant, Kerr said, "It makes the elephant uncomfortable, but when its tail and trunk get working, not much is left of the wasp." Regarding the possibility of a Japanese drive to India through Singapore, Kerr pointed out that Singapore is well fortified and would hold on. Moreover, it is obvious the American fleet in East Indian waters, commanded by Hart, would not be idle if the Japanese chose the sea route for invasion of India. He also recalled Churchill's Commons statement that British naval reinforcements are on the way.

KERR THREE - Kerr said he must plead complete ignorance of conditions in Russia. "I am, of course, proud to be appointed to this important post to which I am going with full buoyancy and hope. The way in which Soviet troops are rolling the Germans back is one of the most magnificent things in military history," he said. Concluding, Kerr said he has been greatly impressed by the achievements of Indian troops in this war and could not use terms high enough in praise of the way they are fighting.

- ALINEA

Following for Prime Minister. Begins.

My telegram of 2nd February D. No. 54.

We are taking following steps to dispel Chinese pessimism fostered by misapprehensions enumerated in paragraph 1 of my telegram under reference.

As regards (2), Governor of Burma has been asked (a) to arrange that G.O.C. Burma should make a statement to kill story that we scorn Chinese offer of military help; (b) to promote with appropriate publicity association with the Chinese in civil defence; (c) to take steps, subject to military requirements, to ensure that Chinese officials and local leaders are warned if we decide to withdraw from any area and are given the chance to evacuate.

As regards (3), whilst security objections to communicating advance military information to Chiang Kai-shek are considered insuperable, H.M. Ambassador has been authorised to inform him that in order that he should be kept adequately posted, Military Attaché at Chungking will receive situation reports from Commander-in-Chief, South Western Pacific, also from Burma and Malay. We are moreover drawing attention to statements recently made in Parliament by Government spokesman and others applauding China's contribution to the common struggle for world freedom, and emphasising necessity for close co-operation with the Chinese and for providing them with arms and supplies.

As regards (4), more publicity about Chinese co-operation in civil defence is already appearing in the press.

As regards (5), we are pointing out that our scorched earth policy was carried out to the best of our ability having regard to the short time available and dispersal of labour caused by raids.

As regards (6) we are drawing attention to assurance given by Secretary of State for the Colonies in the House of Lords on 28th January on behalf of H.M. Government that "in the Allied councils to-day the very first priority is being given to measures to re-establish our position against Japan".

As regards (7), we are drawing attention to definition of our attitude towards India set out by Mr. Churchill in speech of 9th September and reaffirmed by Secretary of State for India in answer to Parliamentary Question on 9th October; also to references in other speeches by Mr. Amery.

As regards financial assistance to China, see my telegram of 3rd February D. No. 59. Ends.

Since I received your letter of the 5th February (50755/42) about evacuation from Singapore, I have seen a copy of the telegram No. 153 G.H. of 5th February from the Governor stating that the Netherlands Consul-General has apparently disappeared from Singapore without making arrangements to give visas even for the quota of 500 Chinese women and children whose entry into the Netherlands East Indies has been approved.

No. 183 Secret.

THE CHINESE AMBASSADOR told me to-day that he had received one or two telegrams from Chungking on the subject of the committee which had been formed in the Chinese colony in Malaya to assist the Government in the defence of Singapore. This committee appears to be called "The Anti-aggression Defence Committee" and to consist of twenty members, ten of whom belong to the Kuomintang Party and ten to the Communist Party. The committee was presided over by a certain Tan Ka-kee who is apparently a Communist and a known opponent of the Chungking Government. Moreover, a sub-committee called "The Defence Corps Committee" consisted entirely of Communist members presided over by Tan Ka-kee.

Doctor Wellington Koo enquired whether it would be possible for some other chairman to be substituted for Tan Ka-kee. His Government appeared to wish this and he gathered that they had in mind the substitution of some individual, though he was not yet informed as to his identity: he was making enquiries of Chungking on the subject.

I told the Ambassador that without knowing how the committee come to be constituted nor how the chairman had been selected it was very difficult to say whether anything could be done. If the committee were really representative of the Chinese community and if the latter had selected them by any procedure and if the chairman had been elected by the Committee itself, I did not see how the British authorities could intervene in any way or to any useful effect. But I would certainly cause enquiries to be made on these aspects of the problem. If it should turn out that the committee and the chairman were unrepresentative, it might be possible to approach the right elements in the Chinese community with a view to their setting up an alternative body with whom the authorities might have dealings.

Unless we are fully informed of the position here, I suggest that we should ask the Colonial Office whether they would make enquiries by telegraph.

(INTLD.) A.C.

IMPORTANT.

My telegram No. 122.

Chinese are volatile people. Their spirits rise and fall for flimsy reasons. But their anxiety about Singapore is real and it is intense. If we can hold the place, (1) should right itself. If we cannot we must be prepared to stand very severe strain.

In regard to (2) I hope to get General Officer Commanding Burma to make a statement which will kill this story once and for all.

It will be a laborious business to put (3) right. It is prevalent belief among all from Chiang Kai-shek downwards. I would venture to remind you therefore that it cannot be reiterated often enough in Parliament and elsewhere that Chinese are on equal footing with ourselves and the Americans.

As regards (4) co-operation in civil defence is working in Malaya where I hope it will be given more publicity. And I have asked Governor in the event of withdrawal from Singapore to give the Chinese full warning. I am hoping to get similar arrangements on foot in Burma.

I understand that Colonel Knox has himself done something to dispel the impression made by (5) and statements made by Mr. Van Mook after seeing the President of the United States have had reassuring effect. Would it be possible to do something in London also? Chinese criticism about (6) is harsh and persistent. They accuse us of insincerity. No material that I have as yet received from Government of India has been of the nature to enable me to counter it.

IMPORTANT.

My immediately preceding telegram and my telegram No. 709 1941.

Chinese are down in the dumps.

More so indeed than I have ever known them. This is cumulative effect of many things, true and untrue, for instance (1) our continued withdrawals in Malay where they expected us to be reinforced and to hold (2) persistent rumours that we scorn their offer of military help in Burma although our line is very thinly held and situation precarious (3) belief that we are unwilling to take them into our confidence and generally to treat them as equals in the alliance (4) alleged reluctance on the part of Malay and Burma to accept offers of Chinese co-operation in civil defence. Here their old and not entirely unfounded grievances as to a like attitude in Hong Kong are being recalled with bitterness and they are finding it difficult to forgive us for failing, it seems, to warn their consulate when we withdrew so hastily from Penang. They grumble too about supposed inadequacy of our scorched earth action in Malay (5) First Lord of Admiralty's recent suggestion that our policy must be Hitler first which in company with Colonel Knox's somewhat similar statement caused great despondency and suspicion (6) finally, disturbing echoes of Axis propaganda contrasting with President of United States declaration that he would use all his resources to achieve deliverance and independence of Philippines with absence of any kindred declaration by ourselves that might give Indians something to hope for and to fight for.

Our stock is low and is in danger of falling still lower. Chinese censor although he let slip a United Press interview with Dr. Sun Fo (see Minister of State 130) has had to lay heavy hand on local newspapers and to suppress many telegrams which foreign correspondents have wished to send. But he has confessed to me that in no case has the present feeling in Free China been overstated. He is depressed and has asked for my help.
