Stan M. Taylor,

In reply to your letter of the 23rd instant, in your Corr. No. 50767/1942, pleasure at being able to place my services at your disposal in clear questions relating to Malaysian staff in India or elsewhere.

The status of Malaysian staff in India was complex, for, if I remember rightly, some were employees of the Fms. Govt but their salaries were shared by all the Malaysian administrations or by the S.S. RF M.S. Govt or (y) annually from the Indian Immigration Fund of Malaya (= I.I.F).

The remainder were employed by the Indian Immigration Committee, Malaysia who controlled the I.I.F., and their status was not good. My memory has been misplaced by bomb blast in Malaysia, but I seem to submit a scheme in 1940 for regrading all salaries of employees and mounting the first clerk at Negapatam to a scale of Rs.1680-2400. I was away on leave from the midst of August, 1940, to the 18 May 1941, and cannot say how far my scheme went in my absence. I think the first clerk was due to get Rs.2200 under my scheme from the 18th January, 1941, but cannot be sure. If, however, Mr. Stark says that the rise was due, and if the clerk in question is merely a good senator, then I submit that the rise should be conceded. Mr. Stark is absolutely reliable, straight. Explain here that the Indian Immigration Committee (= I.I.C.) upheld 2 defolts in India - affect from Sub-Defolts - for the sufficiency of labour from the Madras Presidency to Malaysia, and the recount of laborers or dependants on the return trip who needed care and shelter. These 2 main defolts are situated at Negapatam and Madras respectively. The Andri defolot was taken over by the military authorities in 1940 or 1941. These institutions were staffed by employees of either the Fms. Govt. or the I.I.C., but the officer in charge at Avadi was usually a member of the Malaysian Civil Service while the officer at Negapatam

An employee of the First Government but not a member of the Malayan civil service. You know in the Indian immigration office. It should be the immigration office.

At Negapatan, normally dealt with minute passers and isolated. Indian public opinion is very assisted migration of her laborers. I think that the question of reduction in India might be referred to Mr. Charles Wilson (31 Ulsterville Avenue), who retires from the post of Controller of Labour in March this year. Mr. C.D. 4/7/42.

My dear Stark,

It is many years since we met, and in the hopes that you have not forgotten me, I am writing to you for advice.

The position is, my wife and I escaped from Singapore on the 11th February, and up to that date she had been with the M.B.C., as Librarian and Ass: Musical Director, while I was running the "Mine Watching Corps" under Admiral Spooner.

The strength of the Corps was 4 Officers and 50 Men of which 17 were paid, miners and planters who had lost everything, and Government agreed that they should be paid at the rate of $10 (ten dollars) per day, while the rest of us, including myself, having still a little money, were honorary.

The paid members were:

G. Dixon. S.A. Mountain. V.R. Conolly. D.H.H. Bird.
N.F.W. Paul. A. Denny. C.H.O. Strettell. R. Duncan.
R. Rowse. H.R. Herttslet. James D. Kemp. J.W. Hosey.
J.E.T. Hasselhuhn. C.R.W. Burnside. R.D. Mitchell. J. Matthiasen.
A.P. Cranna.

And in addition there were 40 Asiatics, Engineers, Sarangs and Lascars etc.

On the evening of the 10th Feby., Commander Edwards one of Admiral Spooner's staff, telephoned me that the Corps was to be disbanded, and I at once got in touch with the members, and had a paragraph inserted in the "Free Press" on the morning of the 11th Feby.

I had a vote of $30000 from the Straits Government, and the Mine Watching Corps account being a little overdrawn at the bank, and wishing to obtain funds in order to pay up everyone for the 10 days of February, I went to the Treasury at 10 a.m. on the 11th, but neither Weisberg, Sleep or the Treasurer were there, and a youngster who appeared to be in charge was unable to give me any information as to their whereabouts.

I asked him if it would be possible to obtain any money, and his reply was that I might obtain a cheque in a week or 10 days.

I therefore decided that the best thing for me to do was to chase round and get my wife and myself out of Singapore, and with the help of Davis (M.B.C.), and Bagot amongst others, and A.P.C., we got away that afternoon on one of their baby tankers which took us to Sumatra, where we bussed for about 1000 miles to Benkoolen, where picked up by the "Pangkor" for Batavia, and from there reached Colombo on the 4th March by the s/s Perak.

We were both pretty well done in, and came up here to recover on the 7th March, and have been here ever since.

We fortunately had a little money in London, which we were able to have transferred, but our money in Singapore is frozen, and our property is also. (Rubber Estate etc.).

VISIT TO EVACUEE CAMPS.

I have the honour to report that by kind consent of the Commandant, Lt. Col. H. Marshall, I was able to visit the Camps set up at Coimbatore and Ootacamund by the Government of India for the accommodation of persons evacuated from enemy-occupied areas in the Balkans, North Africa, Burma, and Malaya.

2. These Camps are meant for Europeans and for persons of mixed European and Asiatic parentage.

3. At Coimbatore, which I visited on the 10th September, there are about 58 evacuees from Malaya; of them 27 are the dependants of officials, quasi-officials, and volunteers and the rest are non-officials. Their number is small compared with the numbers from Burma and the Balkans.

4. The Camp is meant to accommodate 600 adults but there were nearly 800 persons (including children) in it. Each quarter for a family consists of a verandah about 5' x 12', a room about 12' x 12', and a combined dressing room and bath-room about 12' x 6'. The buildings are of brick with tiled roof. The dining hall is a large room which can accommodate about 300 persons at a sitting; meals are therefore served in 3 sittings. Invalids and convalescents, numbering about 100, get their meals first and the others follow at half-hour intervals.

Food is supplied by a contractor who provides service, cutlery, and napery. The charge per head is Rs. 2/- per diem for each adult or child over the age of 12 years and Rs. 1/- per diem each for younger children.

A doctor amongst the evacuees has been appointed medical officer of the Camp and his advice as to food for invalids and convalescents is followed so far as is practicable. There is a small hospital with a nurse in charge but I understood that serious cases are sent to the Government hospital in Coimbatore.

Cooking is done on stoves and open hearths. Fly-proofing of the kitchen appeared to me to be desirable.

Water-borne sanitation has not been provided and I was informed that chlorination of water for domestic purposes has to be carried out by hand.

One of the evacuees, a Dr. Salmon from Singapore, who has been very critical of the Camp and its administration told me that he and several others had suffered from dysentery and other gastric diseases since coming to the Camp but I was assured that on the whole the health of most of the residents had been good.

===== Page 75 =====
6. All Malayan people in the Camp were notified that they could see me if they so desired, and the Commandant kindly gave me the use of his office for this purpose. Several of them came and talked with me. Their main enquiries were as to when they were likely to hear from their people in Malaya and how long it would be before they would be able to return to their homes. A few enquiries were made about allowances I was paying them and whether some reduction was possible in the instalments by which repayment of expenses incurred on them by the Indian authorities at Bombay and elsewhere was being made. I had a very long talk with Dr. Salmon and came to the conclusion that he is mentally affected. He showed me a recommendation for his evacuation from Singapore signed by Dr. R.B. Macgregor, the Director-Adviser, Medical Services, Straits Settlements, Singapore. I think it must have been given because of Dr. Salmon's condition. I suggested that Dr. Salmon should leave the Camp if he were unhappy there. This he declined to do.

7. For recreation, a football and hockey ground and tennis courts have been provided and in the evening, two and three times a week, there is a cinema show at a very small charge.

On the evening following my visit, there was to be a boxing tournament. A special ring for the purpose was under construction in the Dining Hall, its cost being met from funds collected from concerts, etc. held earlier.

A school staffed entirely by well-qualified teachers from Burma has been started and is attended by about 150 children. It was to be inspected by a Government Inspector on 14th September.

8. Col. and Mrs. Marshall were good enough to put me up for the night, there being no suitable hotel in the town. The fact that my host and I discovered that we had known each other and been to school together more than 40 years ago added to the interest of our meeting.

9. I met the Collector and District Magistrate of the District (Mr. J. L. Wood, I.C.S.) at the Camp and discussed with him the policy of the Government of India in regard to evacuees and dependents in India of persons detained in Malaya and also as regards the absorption into the local services of Malayan officials whose services have been terminated. In connection with this, Mr. Wood told me that quite recently as Collector, Nellore District, he had tried without success to employ men with drainage and irrigation experience for work on increasing food production. I therefore made enquiries of some of the Malayan Technical Subordinates of the P.W.D. as to their willingness to be employed by the Government of Madras for this purpose.

10. I left Coimbatore Camp at 6:45 a.m. on Friday the 11th September and arrived at Ootacamund at about 1:30 p.m. the same day. After lunch at a hotel, I called on Miss Looker, who is now Superintendent of the Evacuees'

===== Page 76 =====
Camp under Lt. Col. Marshall. Miss Looker is the lady who brought to India the children of St. Andrew's School, Singapore. All the Malayan evacuees had been notified of my arrival but before I met them I saw at Miss Looker's cottage Mrs. Redman - wife of Mr. B. C. Redman, Colonial Legal Service, mentioned in your telegrams Nos. 74 and 77A, and a Mrs. Osborne whose husband was a merchant in Malaya. Mrs. Redman told me that she found it very difficult to manage on £40 per mensem less a deduction of £10 per mensem for overpayments and Indian Income tax on £40 per mensem which is Rs 37.6.0. She enquired whether it would not be possible for her to receive the whole of her husband's children's allowance seeing that provision, which is more than adequate, is being made by monthly payments from her husband's salary to a bank in England for the child that is at home. I suggested that she should address the Colonial Office or the Crown Agents on the matter by airgraph.

11. I later met almost all the Malayan evacuees who are living in the Camp. One family from Kuala Lumpur was very critical of conditions in Camp, the allowances granted by the Government of India (they are non-officials) and the food, but enquiry showed that even in Kuala Lumpur this particular family had not been in good circumstances. I therefore suggested that some gratitude for all that was being done for them by the Government would perhaps be more appropriate.

In some cases it has been found that the expenditure which the Government of India have incurred in moving some of these families from Bombay to Satara, and then to Coimbatore and Ootacamund, plus the cost of their maintenance at all these places, far exceeds any allowances which they may be eligible for as dependants of volunteers or of quasi-officials. The question of meeting that expenditure therefore presents a problem and by arrangement with the Commandant it was agreed that I should send those evacuees whom I pay the amounts actually due to them, notifying him at the same time, and he will decide with the persons concerned the ways and means of using the money and of keeping the debt from growing. The number of Malayan evacuees here is 51, made up of 20 dependants of officials, quasi-officials and volunteers, - the rest being non-officials. Some of the latter were making arrangements to leave the Camp and take up work which, they hoped, would make them independent of Government assistance. I interviewed about 20 persons.

12. The next morning 6 ladies and a man called on me at the hotel at which I was staying. I had notified them and others in The Nilgiris that, if they so desired, they could see me there from 10 a.m. to 1 p.m. that day. Their main enquiries were concerning Malayan Government and Municipal Loans, deposits in Malayan Post Office Savings Banks and Malayan War Savings Certificates. I was able to give some advice as to Postal Savings Banks Deposits as arrangements to disburse these have been made although the demand by the Indian authorities for an identifier of the depositor and two sureties in every case in which the deposit exceeds Rs 500 makes withdrawal very difficult, especially as the identifier and the sureties must also

===== Page 77 =====
be persons known to the Postal authorities and vouched for by the Revenue authorities. I have taken up this question with the Government of India. It is under consideration by them.

13. I left Ootacamund at 2 p.m. on September 12th and arrived at Bangalore next morning.

Sir,

I have the honour to submit the following report:-

1. TELEGRAMS

Since the submission of my last report No. 200 in E.C.A. 12/42 Pt. 3 dated the 4th May, 1942, the telegrams mentioned in enclosure "A" herewith have been received from the Colonial Office and those in enclosure "B" have been despatched by me.

2. STAFF

On the 6th of June, 1942, I returned to full time military duty at Avadi Camp, but as the work was not heavy I was able to devote much attention to Malayan affairs. To assist me in this work I recalled from leave an acting Extra Assistant Controller of Labour, Mr. Ramaswami Iyer, M.A.B.L., who had been sent to India with a few repatriates from Malaya at the end of January last and I appointed him to be my Personal Assistant on terms set out in my letter to him dated the 16th May, 1942, a copy of which is attached, and marked Enclosure "C".

And as additional clerical assistance was very necessary, I engaged as clerk and stenographer, one Mr. R. Krishnan who had done similar work in Malaya. His appointment had been abolished and services terminated with a month's notice. He is now working at this office on Rs. 50/- per mensem.

Four Malayan Emigration Office clerks continue to work, one being from the Negapatam Office and the other three being from Avadi, the military authorities having arrived at no decision as to their employment.

3. CLAIMS FOR LOSS OF PROPERTY IN MALAYA BY NON-OFFICIALS.

Several references have been made to me concerning the alleged loss in Malaya of private property, such as motor cars etc., said to have been requisitioned or taken for military purposes by the Army or, to have been destroyed when being used for Air Raid Precaution Purposes. In each case I have said that no statement regarding these claims can be made just now but that they will, no doubt, receive due consideration after the successful termination of hostilities. I presume that it may safely be said that tribunals will then be set up which will investigate and adjudicate on all claims arising out of losses caused, or said to have been caused, by enemy action. Among others

===== Page 102 =====
Who have made such references to me is Mr. Tan Cheng Lock, C.B.E., formerly a member of the S.S. Legislative Council. He suggests that a portion of the compensation claimed be paid now to the claimants as they are in need of funds. I cannot, however, recommend such a course, as it would set up a very dangerous precedent, there being no possibility of any proper examination of such claims at present. And as regards requiring funds, such non-official evacuees are able to obtain relief through the local civil authority.

4. TRANSFER OF OFFICE.

I was released from my military duties after handing over the appointment of Commandant of the Transit Camp, Avadi, to my successor on the 29th May, 1942. I left the same night for Bangalore, where my wife had succeeded in getting a house at a very moderate rental, but it had been necessary to pay for the whole of May to do so. The rent is Rs. 65/- per mensem. For an Office, I was not quite so fortunate. The accommodation which I had inspected and which I had been told would be available for me at the Mayo Hall, had, I found, been allotted to another official and I was offered the use of two small rooms a score of yards apart. They were quite inadequate for my purpose. I learned, however, of a portion of Andrews Building being available, and as I had previously met Mrs. Andrews, the owner, in Malaya, where she owns property at Cameron Highlands and elsewhere, I called on her. Mrs. Andrews was good enough to let me inspect the accommodation and to give me the first refusal of it. I saw the premises on the 1st June and finding them suitable and the rent, Rs. 85/- per mensem, very moderate, I at once agreed to move in. But for the next eight days, work had to be carried on under some disabilities in my house because the railway truck containing all the office records and furniture had gone astray. It was for this reason that I had to inform the Ceylon Government, in reply to a telegraphic request to go there for a conference, that I would try to arrange a visit to Colombo after about a fortnight. (That visit has now been found to be unnecessary). The Government of India, too, telegraphed to enquire when I proposed to visit New Delhi. I therefore arranged to do so, via Bombay, leaving Bangalore on the 14th June, 1942.

5. VISIT TO NEW DELHI VIA BOMBAY.

I arrived at Bombay on the night of the 15th June and stayed at the same hotel as Mr. Sanders, thus being able to discuss business with him very fully. He, himself, was awaiting instructions regarding his new appointment and was taking all necessary steps for the handing over of the office to Mr. K.R. Blackwell, M.C.S. I attended the Bombay office on the 16th and met Mr. Blackwell, whom I had known before many years ago in Malaya. I found the work well organised and distributed and the accounts well-kept. Satisfactory arrangements for the regular auditing of the accounts by Messrs. Ferguson & Co. of Bombay have been made. From there I replied to Colonial Office tel. No. 44 regarding Mrs. M.G. Mills. I was interviewed by a few persons, among them Mr. Noble of the Malayan P.W.D., who, hearing that I was going to New Delhi, asked for certain points regarding the employment of Malayan P.W.D. Officers by the Government of India to be clarified and more particularly for the officers concerned to be assured that their "home remittances" would be continued and that the Crown Agents for the Colonies would be notified of deductions made from their salaries as subscriptions to the

===== Page 103 =====
Widows' & Orphans' Pension Fund. I was also asked to take up the question of the possibility of these officers retaining their Malayan incremental dates. Mr. Noble had addressed the Government of India directly on these matters. He provided me with a copy of his letter to simplify reference. In addition, I was asked to remind the Defence Department that Mr. Belton's expenses amounting to Rs.138/- in visiting Calcutta in March last at their request had not yet been met as promised.

On the 17th June, Mrs. Purcell (vide your tel. No.36) saw me at the office and told me that she expected to be employed again by the Air Force, in India. She was grateful for the assistance that the office had been able to give her. I also saw Mr. d'Netto and reported by telegram on his case (vide your tel. No.34). I found that, although he had been in England from the beginning of the War up till the time of his departure for Malaya last November, he had made no effort whatever to join any civil defence organisation or to do any useful communal work there. In India, he had tried to obtain an emergency commission in the Indian Army but had been rightly told that being a barrister-at-law was in itself no qualification for a commission. I understood that a Malay friend now at New Delhi was helping him financially. I urged him to join the ranks of the Indian Army. With his education he should have no difficulty in doing well in the Indian Army Clerical Corps. He has no connections in India but I did not feel justified in recommending that he be assisted to return to England. I left for New Delhi that night (17th). I arrived at New Delhi at about 1:30 a.m. on the 19th June, the train being very late, and was met by Mr. G.S. Rawlings, M.C.S., who was my host during my visit.

In accordance with arrangements previously made, I was at the Secretariat, New Delhi, by 10:30 a.m. on the 19th June. I first met Mr. V. Viswanathan, I.C.S., a Deputy Secretary of the Indians Overseas Department, and after a talk with him about the lines on which I was working, he took me to see Mr. G.S. Bozman, I.C.S., the Secretary, Department of Indians Overseas. One of the matters on which I made enquiries at once was that concerning the allowances that were being paid to non-official European British evacuees from Malaya. I was assured that they were under re-consideration and that proposals to base them on actual earnings in Malaya were being formulated for approval by the Government. I shall be notified if the proposals are accepted. I was told, too, that the information for which I had asked as to the number of such evacuees now in India, their desires as to leaving India for the United Kingdom at the earliest possible opportunity or intentions to obtain work in India and become independent of Government assistance, would take a considerable time to collect. It will be forwarded as soon as available.

The question of the advisability of opening another office in the Punjab and of employing Mr. S.M. Sharma, a lawyer from Malaya, in it, was also mentioned. It had been alleged that such an office might be essential to deal with the claims for relief expected from the dependants of the thousands of North-country men employed as Policemen etc. in Malaya. I gathered that so far District Officers in the Punjab had not reported the receipt by them of many such applications. The number received by me both here and at Bombay has been small. In view of this, and the fact that I would prefer to employ officials from Malaya with some knowledge of Malayan Regulations, more especially Police Regulations, I have decided to defer action in the matter. Mr. Bozman told me that he had let Mr. Sharma know that I would be in Delhi and he hoped that I would consent to see him. I did so the next evening and talked with him for nearly two hours. I let him know that I would not be

===== Page 104 =====
I was not opening another office just now, but I had been told by the Secretary of the Labour Department (Mr. Prior, I.C.S.) that Welfare Officers were required by the Air Force and that I had been asked if I knew anyone who might be suitable. As many young men from the Punjab were joining the Air Force and Mr. Sharma appeared to know something about the duty of welfare officers, I readily gave him a letter to Squadron Leader Geddes at Air Force Headquarters suggesting that he be given a trial. I first met Mr. Sharma in Malaya over 25 years ago and came into contact with him quite a lot in the Magistrates' Courts in the State of Selangor, as his office was at Kuala Lumpur for many years.

Mr. Bozman was kind enough to arrange meetings for me with officers of the Defence Department, Labour Department, and Intelligence Bureau of the Home Department.

At the Defence Department, I first met Lt. Col. Beynon, I.C.S. (Retd.), an Additional Deputy Secretary, and then Mr. Baig, I.C.S., who was going to act for him from the next day as Beynon was going on a month's leave. My business here concerned the transfer of Avadi Emigration Depot to the Defence Department for use as a military camp and the non-payment of certain dues related to this, as well as the unfair treatment of Malayan Service personnel about which I had reason to complain. Also, the question of Mr. Belton's expenses mentioned above. Both officers gave me a very patient hearing, and I hope the matters on which I complained will be resolved in due course. While at this department, Col. Beynon called me to meet Sergeant D.N. Booth of the Malayan Volunteer Air Force, who had been sent to New Delhi from Karachi, only to find that nobody in New Delhi could assist him as the Chiefs of Department were away on duty. I immediately took up his case, and after examining his pay-book, found what appeared to be due to him. I gave him a private cheque to settle the amount and provided the address of our Bombay Office to help him get in touch with Singapore Harbour Board officials, from whom he wished to claim the balance of his civil pay. This was brought to the notice of the Bombay Office on my way back to Bangalore.

On 20th June, I was granted an interview by Mr. Prior, I.C.S., Secretary of the Labour Department of the Government of India, regarding the employment as civilians of Malayan Public Works Service Officers. Mr. Prior assured me that there would be no difficulty arranging for the proper dispatch to England of officers' "home remittances" or subscriptions to the Widows' & Orphans' Pension Fund, but it would not be possible to arrange for officers to retain their incremental dates as fixed in Malaya. "Home remittances" would, however, be limited to the amounts which serving officers of the Indian Public Works Service are permitted to send to the United Kingdom. It was emphasized that all these appointments were temporary and would cease to exist as soon as the work for which they were created was completed. I said I fully understood the position and that it would be made clear to the officers concerned. I was told the only remaining Malayan Public Works Officers in whom the civil departments were interested were Mr. Cambridge and perhaps Mr. Spence.

===== Page 105 =====
At his own request, I saw Mr. F. W. Kidd, Indian Police, one of the Deputy Secretaries of the Intelligence Bureau of the Home Department of Government of India. A list of Malayan Service Indian Policemen on leave in India prepared in our offices was handed to him by me. The possibility of employing some of them is under consideration. (Mr. Kidd is a brother of Mr. George Kidd, M.C.S., who was recently British Adviser, Kelantan, and I was very sorry not to be able to give Mr. Kidd any news of him.)

It was particularly desired by Mr. Bozman that I should meet at Mr. Kidd's Office Rai Bahadur Prithvi Chand of the Straits Settlements Police Service in connection with enquiries as to the possibility of many claims for assistance being received from the dependants of Punjabi policemen detained in Malaya and the necessity of establishing an office to deal with them. I heard, however, from Mr. Kidd that the Rai Bahadur, who is working under him on deputation from Malaya, had only just been granted ten days' leave and that the only address that he had left was C/o The Postmaster, Dehra Dun. He had been telegraphed for but it was not known when he would see the telegram or when he would return to New Delhi.

Mr. Bozman's main fear is that if a special office is not set up somewhere in or near the Punjab, cases may occur of the dependants in India of sepoys detained in Malaya receiving allowances, whilst those of policemen, warders etc., in the same village, do not get anything. But as our Bombay Office has already got into touch with nearly 140 policemen etc., in Northern India, and with some dependants as far north as Peshawar and is making adequate arrangements to help them, and as in that office we have assisting us a Sikh and a Punjabi Muslim, it is certain that their compatriots' interests will be duly cared for.

The heat at Delhi was very severe, being up to 112°F in the shade, and dust reduced visibility to a few hundred yards.

I left Delhi next morning, the 21st June 1942, for Bombay where I arrived at about 10 a.m. the next day. On this occasion I stayed at the same hotel as Mr. Blackwell. It is conveniently situated immediately above the office. I spent the rest of the day and until 11:50 a.m. of the 22nd at the office and then left for Bangalore, stopping at Khandala on the way so as to call at St. Peter's School where I was met by the Lady Principal who showed me all the children from Singapore concerning whom I have already despatched a brief report by airgraph. The Principal gave me a good report on the children, all of whom looked well and happy and pleased at meeting somebody who could speak of Singapore and who understood Malay. A list of them accompanies this report (Enclosure "D").

All of them will probably be known to and remembered by Bishop B. C. Roberts who is now at Canterbury and who took much interest in St. Andrew's School at Singapore. I arrived at Bangalore at 6:30 p.m. on the 23rd June 1942.

6. TANGANYIKA MEDICAL DEPARTMENT - ADVERTISEMENT FOR ASSISTANT SURGEONS.

Whilst at Bombay I saw an advertisement for Assistant Surgeons for the Medical Service of the Tanganyika

===== Page 106 =====
Government in a local newspaper by a Bombay firm. I wrote to enquire whether the advertisement had been published at the request of the Tanganyika Government or on orders from London. The reply had not been received before I left and I was not successful in getting an answer by telephone. As there are some Malayan Medical Department Assistant Surgeons in India who may be suitable and who may be willing to proceed to Tanganyika on secondment from the Malayan Medical Department in preference to having their appointments abolished or to joining the Indian Forces, I considered it desirable to make enquiries. I have now heard that the advertisement was published at the request of the Chief Secretary to the Government of Tanganyika and that only one Assistant Surgeon is required. Should any other Colonial Governments require Indian Assistant Surgeons, it is recommended that consideration be given to the possible appointment of those now in India who have been notified of abolition of their appointments. A list of them is enclosed (Enclosure "E").

7. CARGO EX S.S. "DARDANUS".

On the 1st of June, 1942, at Bangalore I was notified by the Principal Officer, Mercantile Marine Department, Madras, that a railway truck containing 32 packages from the cargo of the S.S. "Dardanus" had been consigned to me at Avadi. I replied that I had no authority to receive the packages and asked for them to be handed to the agents of Messrs. Mackinnon, Mackenzie & Co., whom I knew to be the local representatives of the Crown Agents for the Colonies, the consignors of the goods. At the same time I wrote to Messrs. Binny & Co., Ltd., Madras, as agents of Messrs. Mackinnon, Mackenzie & Co., and asked them to attend to this matter. Messrs. Binny & Co., however, by mistake telegraphed requesting me to ask the Agent for Government consignments then at Calicut, to accept delivery and that officer once more referred me back to the Principal Officer, Mercantile Marine Department, Madras. I therefore telegraphed to the Manager of the Madura Co., Ltd., Cochin, who informed me that his firm represents the Crown Agents in South India and that they would see that Messrs. Binny & Co. would attend to the matter. In the meantime, however, owing to further mistaken orders, the truck was unloaded at Avadi Camp and the goods have been stored with other Malayan property that is not being used by the Military authorities. I have asked Messrs. Binny & Co. to remove these packages and arrange for their storage with the rest of the cargo intended for Government Departments in Malaya and British North Borneo, amounting to over 500 packages, that arrived by S.S. "Dardanus". It was by this ship and as part of the same cargo that the 28 packages said to contain Malayan currency notes, postage stamps, etc., arrived at Madras. The removal of these 28 packages to the Treasury of the Resident in Mysore has been approved by you (vide your telegram No. 43) and I have given the Commissioner of Police, Bangalore Cantonment, authority to remove them from Avadi. No report on the matter has reached me yet.

8. VALUABLES LANDED AT COLOMBO.

On the 25th June, I was notified by the Deputy Financial Secretary, Colombo that 28 packages said to contain valuables consigned to His Majesty's Consul General at Batavia, Java, by the Financial Secretary, Straits Settlement.

===== Page 107 =====
Straits Settlements had been landed at Colombo ex S.S. "City of Canterbury". A list (Copy enclosed) "F" of the packages and their markings was sent to me with a request to assist in tracing the owners, some of whom were believed to have come to India. The addresses of Mrs. Storch and of Mrs. C.K. Loke in India have been traced and passed on to Colombo.

9. HOSPITAL EXPENSES IN CEYLON OF MALAYAN EVACUEE WAR CASUALTIES.

On the 8th June, 1942, the Chief Secretary to the Government of Ceylon informed me that the question had been raised whether in all cases expenses incurred on behalf of Malayan evacuees sent to hospital as war casualties should not be charged to Malayan Government funds. The procedure in Ceylon has been to recover the amount from Government officials from the pension or salary payable to the officer, and in the case of evacuees who are left without funds to debit the expenses to Malayan Government funds. I was asked for my observations in the matter. I recommended reference to the Colonial Office for decision and I added that my own views were that only if the evacuees were members of the Malay Regiment, the Malayan Volunteer Forces, or Local Defence Corps and if there was no doubt that the casualty was due to enemy action, should the expenses be charged to Malayan Government funds. In the case of Government pensioners or officials who were not members of the units mentioned, recovery of hospital expenses from the pension or salary of the officer in accordance with Malayan General Orders would, I considered, be equitable.

10. BOXES WITH THE HONGKONG & SHANGHAI BANK, BOMBAY, SAID TO CONTAIN STRAITS SETTLEMENTS GOVERNMENT RECORDS.

In accordance with your instructions steps are being taken to send to the Crown Agents as much as possible of the contents of the boxes examined by Mr. Blackwell. I have, however, asked that if copies of the Straits Settlements Government Estimates are available at least one copy should be retained for use here. Such documents are most valuable in helping to decide whether or not an officer was on the pensionable establishment and in indicating the provision made for both civil and military appointments.

I explained to the Secretary what my work is and we arranged to exchange information so that double payments to the same individual would be avoided. I was urged to open an office at Bangalore because of the large number of evacuees.

and because the office of the Accountant General, Madras, had also been moved to that town. The month's leave which the military authorities were good enough to give me expired on the 6th May and I have resumed duty as Commandant of this Camp. That necessarily interferes with my Malayan work and causes some delay in settling claims. It also makes it impossible for me to travel and interview applicants personally. In addition to Government servants and their dependants and the dependants of Malayan Volunteers, there are some students from Malaya, whom I particularly wish to meet - a few are at Vellore not far from Avadi but most of them are in the United Provinces. I speak Hindustani and Punjabi so hope to be able to reassure them by personal contact that their affairs will be attended to.

IV. Accounts and Staff.

(a) At Bombay the work of control has been in the hands of Mr. J. O. Sanders ably assisted by Mr. H. Davies. (b) At Avadi on my return on the 26th April I found a very large accumulation of correspondence which, with the willing assistance of three Indian Emigration Office clerks, has been dealt with to a certain extent but further assistance will be necessary very shortly. I have personally been working for over 12 hours each day for the past nine days in an endeavour to wipe off arrears.

(c) Owing to the large number of European officers or dependants of European officers who are for the time being in Bombay and northern India the claims and payments there have been heavy, and Mr. Sanders urged immediate provision of another one lakh of rupees by the end of this month. MALAYAN REPRESENTATIVE IN INDIA.

I have the honour to report that on the 24th March 1942, I received from the Department of Indians Overseas of the Government of India a telegram which stated that the Government of India had agreed to my appointment as Malayan Representative in India with Headquarters in Madras. It was added that I might find it necessary to appoint one or two assistants in other parts of India, that I could communicate directly with the Colonial Office and that the Government of India would supply me with funds on my requisition, these funds being debited to the Colonial Office. On the same day, from Bombay, Sir Alex Small, K.B.E., C.M.G., Colonial Adviser, Eastern Group, Supply Council and formerly Colonial Secretary, S.S., telegraphed recommending me to visit Bombay immediately as there were many Malayan officials there who wished to interview me. Consequently, I obtained leave from the local military authorities and left for Bombay the same night by the mail train.

2. I arrived at Bombay on the morning of 26th March. I did not meet Sir Alex Small as he had left for Australia but I met certain Malayan Service officers, principally Lt. Col. T.G. Husband, the senior-most of 67 P.W.D. officers at Bombay, Major G.W. Somerville of the Malayan Forest Service and Mr. J.O. Sanders, Transportation Manager, F.M.S. Railways. After hearing them I was advised to meet Mr. G.S.I. Collins, C.S.I., I.C.S., one of the Advisers to H.E. the Governor of Bombay as he was the officer who had been dealing with all persons brought to Bombay from Malaya.

3. Mr. Collins was good enough to give me a lengthy interview at which he told me all that the Government of Bombay and the unofficial Reception Committee had done and were still doing on behalf of people from Malaya. I am confident that the Malayan Governments will, in due course, express their gratitude and appreciation to the Government of Bombay and its helpers. Great care was being taken by the local authorities to see that none of these unfortunate persons were either homeless or without sufficient money for their immediate needs.

4. With Mr. Collins I helped to draft a telegram to the Government of India reporting my presence in Bombay. It stated that I meant to transfer temporarily my headquarters there, that I had nominated as my assistant Mr. J.O. Sanders and that if the military authorities at Madras would grant me sufficient leave early in April, I would spend some days in Bombay organising an office. I indicated that in the first instance I would deal mainly with monetary claims on the Malayan Governments by officials, pensioners and their dependants. And the Bombay Government expressed the hope that I would later be able to assist them still more by dealing also with evacuees who are not officials.

5. Later the same day I interviewed other Malayan officials and I agreed to have a private discussion next morning with Lt. Col. Husband on matters relating particularly to officers of the Malayan P.W. Service, whilst Mr. Sanders and others undertook to try to secure a suitable office and office workers for me.

6. On 27th March I had a discussion with Lt. Col. Husband at a room kindly placed at our disposal by the P.W.D. Secretariat of the Government of Bombay. What was particularly desired by the P.W. officers was a clear statement as to their position, if any of them were posted for Government service, either military or civil, in India. Questions

===== Page 154 =====
Relating to their salaries, children's allowances, contributions to W. & O. P. Fund, leave and passages were mentioned. My views were that if any of the officers are posted for either military or civil Government duty in India, and the salaries paid to them are less than those which they had been drawing in Malaya, the difference would be paid to them from Colonial funds, and that allowances such as the children's allowance and any purely personal pensionable allowances would also be paid but allowances that were merely local, such as car allowances or house rent allowances, would not be paid. I also stated that any service in India would be regarded as in continuation of service in Malaya, and that deductions in respect of subscriptions to the W. & O. P. Fund and of home allotments would be made. As regards passage to the U.K. or any other Dominion to which an officer belonged, I said that I would be prepared to guarantee payment of the cost on behalf of the Crown Agents for the Colonies, in accordance with Malayan Government regulations, provided the officer were able to obtain a permit to leave India from the local Government authorities. Some doubt was expressed as to whether Colonial Government officers placed as they were, were legally subject to the Indian Act regarding compulsory military duty by Europeans in India. My attitude to that question was that if any such Malayan Officer objects to an order to report for military duty, his only method of obtaining a ruling on the matter would be by taking it to the Law Courts. I made it clear that all my views on such matters as salary, leave, passage &c., were naturally subject to confirmation by the Secretary of State for the Colonies.

D. 30th April, 1942.
R. 30th April, 1942, 21:45 hrs.

I respectfully suggest that the Secretary of State for the Colonies obtains my immediate release from military duty; Malayan representative work being very heavy entails much essential travelling, interviewing, and also office work.

I have informed the Government of India that I am unable to do this work in addition to my military duties and have therefore asked for a decision as to which I am to do.

My nominal pay as Emigration Commissioner ceases 8th May and pay as Camp Commandant, Avadi, will not be paid when I am relieved. I would be grateful for indication as to remuneration for Malayan representative work. Would the equivalent of dollars 1,400 per mensem with quarters and travelling and hotel expenses according to Federated Malay States General Order be considered excessive?

===== Page 171 =====
Being unable to communicate any longer with the Malayan Governments, I have the honour to state that I consider it my duty to submit a report on action taken by me in connection with certain Malayan Government affairs in India, for your consideration and for such guidance and instructions as may be necessary.

2. My substantive appointment is Assistant Emigration Commissioner for Malaya at Negapatam. I have, however, been acting as Emigration Commissioner for Malaya since the 19th May, 1939, and, since the 1st June, 1941, as Commandant of the Reinforcement Camp, Madras (Avadi), in addition, with the acting rank of Major in the Indian Army, with the concurrence of the Governments of Malaya and India.

3. As Emigration Commissioner for Malaya I operate the Malayan Indian Immigration Fund Branch Account at Madras. Remittances to this account were in normal times made from Penang by the Deputy Controller of Labour, Malaya. And, recently, owing to happenings in Malaya, comparatively large sums have been transferred from Malaya to Madras on my advice. All the usual records of receipts and payments have been maintained by me. Copies of the cash books have also been prepared fortnightly as usual. Normally, these copies with all original vouchers and receipts used to be sent to Penang for scrutiny and audit, and any observations or queries regarding them were returned to me to be answered. Many of these copies of accounts have now been returned to me by the postal authorities because of the suspension of all services to Malaya. I have had all of them carefully preserved in the office here so that they may be audited in due course. Similar action has been taken by the acting Assistant Emigration Commissioner at the Malayan Emigration Office at Negapatam, who works under my control.

4. The outbreak of the war with Japan found a certain number of Malayan Government employees on leave in India unable to get back to duty. In some cases their leave has expired and in nearly every case arrangements for payment of their salaries have been upset. Certain Malayan pensioners, too, who used to draw their pensions through their attorneys in Malaya have been similarly affected and now find themselves without means. Accordingly, for a while I agreed to make advances to such officials from Indian Immigration Fund monies against eventual payment of salaries or pensions. Subsequently, information reached me that all Malayan Government employees were to regard themselves as being on leave without pay and free to obtain suitable work wherever possible. I have therefore felt myself compelled to stop making any more advances against salaries or pensions.

5. On the 9th February, 1942, I received instructions through the Deputy Controller of Labour, Malaya, to discharge all non-pensionable Malayan Labour Department employees in India, and also all employees of the Malayan Indian Immigration Committee who are paid from the Indian Immigration Fund Branch Accounts in India. I, myself,

It was stated that I would no longer be employed as Assistant Emigration Commissioner for Malaya three months after the receipt of this notice by me, but it was added that the Malayan Governments would be grateful if I would consent to carry on as Emigration Commissioner unpaid. If I could not see my way to do this, it was suggested that I might appoint a Mr. T.P. Sundaram, Extra Assistant Controller of Labour of the Malayan Labour Department, to be Depot Superintendent for a period not exceeding one year. It was stated that Mr. Sundaram was on half pay leave in India and that payment of his leave salary should be made from Indian Immigration Fund monies, adjustment against Government balances to be made later. If I found it more economical to appoint someone else, I was at liberty to act accordingly. For the reasons stated in para. 8 below, I have decided to appoint Mr. Sundaram as Depot Superintendent. He will not draw any salary as Depot Superintendent but will receive his leave salary and free accommodation. It was added that non-pensionable Government employees and employees of the Malayan Indian Immigration Committee in India, if of 10 years' service or more, were to be discharged on gratuity; others, with a month's wages, presumably as an ex-gratia grant.

6. Having willingly consented to continue as Emigration Commissioner for Malaya unpaid, I have taken steps to carry out the instructions received from Malaya. All non-pensionable Malayan Labour Department employees have been given notice of the termination of their services. These employees are six Travelling Inspectors, two Travelling Inspectresses, and a few peons. All employees of the Malayan Indian Immigration Committee, being on monthly agreements, have similarly been given a month's notice of discharge. So far as it is possible to do so from the records in India, gratuities where earned will be calculated and paid.

Sundaram), will look after and maintain Malayan property in a sanitary condition. The Resident Medical Officer and five clerks on the pensionable staff draw their salaries through the Negapatam Sub-Treasury; the Federated Malay States Government having an Agency Account with the Government of Madras. I have no information as to how much money there is in that account or for how long payments such as these will be made by the Madras authorities. The Depot Superintendent will, as indicated above, draw his leave salary (Rs 310/- per mensem) only. His duties will be to supervise and pay the staff, also all the local Municipal and Government rates and taxes; he will not be appointed an Assistant Emigration Commissioner under the Indian Emigration Act. All matters concerning emigration to or repatriation from Malaya will be dealt with by me.

In such cases it was formerly the practice of the Malayan Governments to pay the officer concerned the difference between his military and civil pay. I propose, therefore, to pay Mr. Danby the difference from Indian Immigration Fund monies, and I suggest that adjustment be made against Malayan Government balances in London. 10. Guidance is sought in regard to the payment of pensions to Malayan Government pensioners in India who have not been drawing their pensions through any of the Indian Treasuries. Apart from one other such case which has come to my notice, I, myself, am affected. I have been drawing from the Federated Malay States Government a pension of $5184.37 per annum. Monthly payment of this pension, less a contribution of $42/- per mensem to the F.M.S. Widows & Orphans Pension Fund, has been made by the Accountant General, Federated Malay States to a current account which I had with the Penang office of the Chartered Bank of India, Australia and China. I have received information of payments up to the 31st October 1941, inclusive only. If payment of pensions by the Malayan Governments is to continue, I would ask that instructions be issued which will enable Malayan pensioners in India to draw their pensions from the nearest Treasury, if they are not already doing so, statutory declarations as to the date of last payment being taken from them, if necessary. The crediting of subscriptions to the Federated Malay States Widows & Orphans Pension Fund should also be continued through the Treasury paying the pension.

Only one employee is being retained as peon to the Emigration Commissioner for Malaya on a salary of Rs 228/- per annum, which includes payment of an approved "dearness allowance" of Re. 1/- per mensem. The conditions of transfer are briefly that these employees are to continue to receive the same rates of pay as when in the Malayan service and to enjoy the same rights and privileges as before. All will continue to earn gratuities, if they were doing so previously, and the Government of India will be responsible for its share of these gratuities as from the date that it assumed responsibility for the maintenance of these institutions with their various installations (i.e. the electrical and mechanical plant, water supply, sewage system and all buildings). The Malayan Governments have agreed to make no charge for the use of these Depots by the Government of India, if the cost of maintenance is being borne by the Government of the United Kingdom. It is part of my duty to look after Malaya's interests in these Depots and in the employees whose services have been transferred to the Government of India.
