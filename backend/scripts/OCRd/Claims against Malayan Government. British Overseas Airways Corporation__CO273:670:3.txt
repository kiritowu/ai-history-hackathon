Dear Paskin,

Your letter of the 16th July (50759/43) raises a number of important points, and while I have done my best to give you our views, you will no doubt confer with your own legal adviser in cases where your position is in doubt.

2. I gather from Boyle's letter to Morris of the 11th March that the Ministry of War Transport take the view that they can never obtain a good receipt from a company incorporated in Japanese occupied territory unless it has registered under the Defence (Companies) Regulations 1940. We do not share this view. The intention of the Defence (Companies) Regulations was to assist companies incorporated in overrun territories to function. The effect of registration on the "enemy" or "non-enemy" status of the registrant is doubtful, but while it is most improbable that an "enemy" company would ever be registered, I think it possible that a company which has not registered may not be an enemy.

3. It is frequently a matter of difficulty to determine whether a company is an enemy. The relevant provisions of the Trading with the Enemy Act, 1939, by which a company's status is determined, are contained in Section 2. This establishes that a company incorporated in territory which has been overrun is enemy only if it is controlled by persons resident in enemy territory or if it continues to carry on business in such territory. It is this question of control which in practice

===== Page 17 =====
Occasions the real difficulty of determining a company's status as the locus of control depends in each case upon its particular facts.

4. If a company is an enemy under Section 2 then under the Custodian Order, 1939, debts due to it must be paid to the Custodian of Enemy Property and subject to what is said below, compliance with this Order will alone afford a good discharge to the payer. If the payer is in real doubt because he has reasonable cause to believe that the creditor company is an enemy, he can, however, secure a discharge by paying the debt into Court under Section 4(4) of the Act. Where any requirement or direction with respect to any money or property is addressed to any person by a custodian and accompanied by a certificate of the custodian that the money or property is money or property to which the Custodian Order applies, the certificate shall be evidence of the facts stated therein, and if that person complies with the requirement or direction, he shall not be liable to any action or other legal proceeding by reason only of such compliance (Section 7(2) of the Act).

5. Boyle's letter says: "It will seldom, if ever, be safe for the Department to negotiate with, or make payments to, persons who purport to be acting under the authority of Powers of Attorney granted by companies or other persons incorporated or resident in Japanese occupied territory. Authorities to such agents by the Trading with the Enemy Department for the purpose of the Trading with the Enemy Act do not enable the Department to obtain a good discharge, for which purpose the agent must have a valid authority from his principal."

6. I have already indicated that a company incorporated in Japanese occupied territory may not be an enemy under the Trading with the Enemy Act, and if its sole appreciable enemy taint is its incorporation in the occupied territory, we see no reason why it should be considered as an enemy at common law. Consider for example, a simple case where a man and his wife

===== Page 18 =====
Have formed a company of which they are the sole officers and shareholders. The company owns some funds in England and a small plantation in Malaya where it is incorporated, but no money; and before escaping the officials, so far as possible, "scorched" the plantation. The husband and wife reach Australia and, as directors and shareholders, cause the company to continue functioning for the purpose of collecting debts due to it from persons in England etc. We should not regard such a company as enemy and we should not regard as abrogated any authority which the company had conferred upon its agent. We should do, and in fact have, issued authorities under the Act "if and so far as they may be necessary" with a view to encouraging the English debtors to pay the company.

7. Subject to this, I am in accord with Boyle's statement. If a company is an enemy it is legally unsafe to pay its debts to persons purporting to act as its agents; if it is not an enemy payments may safely be made to it or to its duly authorised agents. These remarks apply equally to persons purporting to act on behalf of an individual.

8. In paragraph 8 of your letter (Ref. 50759/43) you propound an argument which I understand to be that because 95% of the shareholders of a company are resident outside enemy occupied territory and 5% within that territory, you should retain 5% of any payment due and pay the 95%. If this is the argument, I think it is open to grave criticism. The assets of a company, including debts due to it, belong to the company as a legal entity and not to its shareholders. They can only reach the shareholders in the form of dividends or on repayment following a reduction of capital or on the company's liquidation; they are available for meeting the claims of creditors, which have priority over those of shareholders. Apart from all this, if the money is legally due to the proposed payee you will have no right to retain a percentage of it. Either the concern is an enemy, in which case the money is due to the Custodian, or it is not, in which event the money is due to the concern itself. Where say

===== Page 19 =====
Only if the shareholders are resident outside occupied territory would it be dangerous to pay that percentage of the company's claim, as you might then be paying enemy monies which should be paid to the Custodian. On the other hand, you have no legal justification for retaining a percentage of the claim of a non-enemy company.

9. In our opinion, it is wrong to countenance applications for payment to individuals claiming in their personal capacity as shareholders or directors of limited companies, however great their interest may be. By their non-enemy status and control of the company, they may bring the company into the non-enemy class so that it will be safe to pay the company. The company's receipt may well be signed by its duly appointed directors who may also be shareholders, but they should sign for and on behalf of the company, and immunity will not be secured by paying a debt due to a company to one of its shareholders.

10. As you point out, the authority given by the Trading with the Enemy Department under proviso 1 to Section 1(2) of the Act permitting the continuation of an enemy company's business outside occupied territory has only a limited effect. It is restricted (1) to ensuring practical immunity from the commission of the criminal offence of trading with the enemy and (2) by the provisions of Article 1(v)(a) of the Custodian Order, 1939, to defeating the claim the Custodian would otherwise have had to receive monies due to the company. If you pay the company's de facto representative who has been authorised by this Department to continue to act for his enemy principal, there will be no question of your infringing the law and you will not be called upon to pay the Custodian, but you will not secure a discharge effective for all purposes; the Company might later call upon you to pay again as it might say the result of its having become an enemy was to take away the agent's authority to receive monies for it, a position which could not be remedied by any licence or authority we may have given under the Act.

11. On the 6th November, 1942, we issued to Mr. I. Pascheove, managing director of Paskoes Ltd., an authority of the type referred

===== Page 20 =====
To in the preceding paragraph. You may think the prospects of a subsequent attack are so remote as to justify your paying this company, but I think the matter is essentially one upon which you should seek your own legal advice. I do not know whether other Departments have paid the company.

12. I see no reason to disagree with your conclusions regarding British Overseas Airways Corporation, but I consider this question also is one upon which you must be guided by your legal advisers.

Dear Carter,

As you may be aware, we in the Colonial Office are engaged in dealing with a number of claims against the various Malayan Governmental Authorities in respect of work done, services rendered, or goods supplied, to those Authorities before the Japanese occupation of the country. Other Departments, in particular the Service Departments, are dealing with similar claims. We have had a good deal of correspondence with the Treasury about this and, of course, have their authority for whatever we have been doing. Some of these claims present no very great difficulty because they arise out of contracts entered into in this country and involve a sterling liability. Other claims which we have received were, however, in respect of contracts entered into in Malaya and where the liability was for payment locally in local currency. For a time we declined to meet any such claims (a) on the grounds that the liability was purely a local liability, and (b) that there were no records in this country in regard to the transactions involved, and that we must, therefore, wait until the re-occupation of Malaya before such claims could be considered.

2. One of the firms in question, however, represented to us very strongly that the work which they had undertaken had been satisfactorily completed before the Japanese occupation of the country (and they produced certificates by Public Works Department engineers to this effect), that payment had most certainly not been made before the fall of Singapore and that it was, therefore, inequitable to postpone the statement of this claim until the re-occupation of

D. CARTER, ESQ.

===== Page 23 =====
of the country, by which time all the relevant local records might well have been destroyed. We took this up with the Treasury and it was ultimately agreed that in cases where we can get sufficient evidence that the work was properly carried out before the Japanese occupation of the country, and can be reasonably satisfied that it has not been paid for, and if the firm in question is prepared to give satisfactory guarantees to refund any payments that may subsequently be found to be not due to them, we should be prepared to make payment here out of Malayan balances.

3. In the course of our discussion with the Treasury, the Treasury Solicitor's Department produced an opinion to the effect that, if the payment had become due before the Japanese occupation, the contracting authority was strictly in default and accordingly could be sued here in damages for the sterling equivalent of the local currency converted at the rate current when the payment became due. We ventured to express doubt as to the possibility of legal proceedings being taken in this country especially where the contracting authority was an authority of the Malay States (which technically are independent sovereign States), and even where the contracting authority was the Government of the Straits Settlements (a British Colony). Nevertheless, in spite of our doubts, we suggested to the Treasury that we should meet these claims (where the agreed conditions were fulfilled) as a matter of policy. To this, the Treasury replied that, in view of what the Treasury Solicitor's Department had said, we must accept in the circumstances that

===== Page 24 =====
That the firm in question had a strong equitable claim for payment, and that we should not be influenced by the fact that, through various legal technicalities of procedure, they might not be able to pursue their claim in the Courts in this country at the present time. The Treasury therefore agreed that such claims should be met. I may mention that in the particular case in which we took the matter to the Treasury, the firm in question was a firm which had been registered in the Straits Settlements but had transferred its registration to the United Kingdom since the occupation of Malaya by the Japanese.

4. We now have a number of claims from companies registered in Malaya, which have not transferred their registration to the United Kingdom or some other part of the Empire. In these cases we are being pressed for payment by members of the Malayan firms (in one case the Managing Director who had a 95% interest in the firm) who escaped from Malaya and are now either in this country or in Australia, and the question now arises as to whether we can meet such claims either wholly or in part. Perhaps we can best put the points at issue to you in relation to a concrete case. The firm in question is Messrs. Paskoe Limited, which was registered in Singapore. The Managing Director of the firm, who has a 95% interest in the firm, is now in Australia, and has been pressing for payment for some considerable time.

5. The facts of the claim in so far as the Colonial Office is concerned are roughly as follows:

The claim is in respect of partially completed contract work to a building in Singapore which was being adapted to the use of the Malayan Broadcasting Corporation and a part of the contract figure had already been paid to Messrs. Paskoe before the occupation of Malaya. As to the balance due in respect of

===== Page 25 =====
Work completed, it has been confirmed by the Chief Executive Officer of the Corporation (now in the United States) that a certificate was passed for payment shortly before the occupation. The actual amount covered by this certificate is not quite certain but is stated to be between £20,000 and £30,000 and, having gone into the matter, we, in the Colonial Office (but for the complication that the firm is a locally registered firm) would now be prepared to authorise a further payment on account of £10,000.

6. When we had reached this point, however, attention was drawn to the fact that on the 18th August, 1942, the Head Office of the Chartered Bank in London wrote to your Department about various claims of Messrs. Paskoe (mainly against the Service Departments, but also mentioning the claim against the Malayan Broadcasting Corporation). From that letter it appears that Messrs. Paskoe's London Solicitors had been informed by the Custodian of Enemy Property that money due from the Air Ministry could only be released on the direction of your Department. We have no information whether, in fact, any of Messrs. Paskoe's claims against the Service Departments have been met and we should therefore, be grateful for any information that you may be able to give us on this point.

7. I was about to write to you about this case when my attention was drawn to a letter from the Ministry of War Transport to the Colonial Office, of which I enclose a copy, from which you will see that that Department is now taking the line that before they can negotiate with or pay money to persons purporting to be acting under the authority of Powers of Attorney granted by Companies incorporated in enemy occupied territory, the Company concerned must be registered in the United Kingdom under Regulation 5 of the Defence (Companies) Regulations.

===== Page 26 =====
Regulations 1940. One of the reasons for this decision is that even if an authority were granted by your Department for a payment to an agent in this country of a firm registered in Malaya, that authority would not enable the Ministry to obtain a good discharge, for which purpose the agent must have a valid authority from his Principal.

8. It seems to us, however, that while a claimant of the kind indicated in paragraphs 4 and 5 above is presumably to be regarded as an agent of a Malayan registered Company, he is in a different position from that of a person or Company in the United Kingdom whose sole interest in the claim arises from the fact that he or it is acting as an agent under a Power of Attorney, since in the case cited as an example the claimant is in fact to all intents the owner of the Company. As a matter of equity, it seems to us that, apart from technicalities, such a person is virtually in the same position as others whose claims we are in fact paying, and we should be disposed to meet his claim in proportion to his interest in the Company. Dear Morris,

Since the outbreak of war with Japan we have at one time and another passed over to you various claims against the Colonial Governments by the representatives of shipping companies whose control and registration are now in enemy-occupied territory. In some of these cases we have told you how, had the claims been against the Ministry, we should have dealt with them.

Recently, however, the House of Lords' decision reversing the Court of Appeal in the WAALHAVEN case (Svfracht v. N.V. Gobr. Van Udens Scheepvaart. T.L.R. 442) has caused us to modify our views somewhat.

The principal question in the above case was whether the Respondent Company, a company incorporated under the laws of Holland which had been occupied by the enemy, was by reason of the mere fact that it continued to carry on business in Holland to be treated as an alien enemy to the extent that it was incapacitated from taking proceedings in the Courts of this country with a view to recovering a business debt. The House of Lords have decided that the Respondent Company is technically an enemy alien and as such not entitled to sue.

There was a subsidiary question as to the validity of the authority of the Respondents' Solicitors to continue to represent them after the Respondents had themselves become technical enemies. The principal question having been decided against the Respondents this subsidiary question did not arise for decision, but both Lord Wright and Lord Porter made some observations on it. Thus Lord Porter said:

"Ordinarily, when the principal becomes an enemy the W.A. Morris, Esq., Colonial Office.

Authority of the agent ceases on the ground that it is not permissible to have intercourse with an enemy alien, and the existence of the relationship of principal and agent necessitates such intercourse.

He went on to cite authorities for the proposition that the relationship of principal and agent is determined when the parties to the agency agreement become enemies one to the other.
