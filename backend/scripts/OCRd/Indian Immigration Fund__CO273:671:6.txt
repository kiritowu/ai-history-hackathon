The proceeds of this enactment formed the Enquiry Fund — forming two parts of General Revenue. This enactment is now incorporated in the Eastern Code (Cap 69 Part 14).

The Committee is a formally constituted independent body with its own revenue and investments.

This letter from the R.G.A., I think, must be viewed against the general background and the feelings of those engaged in the rubber industry and in trade and commerce in Malaya taken into account. They are naturally very anxious that some thought should be given to how their industry and businesses are to be resuscitated after the war. They have the feeling - quite wrongly - that H.M.G. does not much care what happens to them, and is preoccupied with other problems which leave no time for attention to their particular difficulties. Here perhaps I ought to add that many of them are particularly bitter that there has apparently been no defence made by any Minister against the scurrilous attacks upon the planting and commercial communities of Malaya by uninformed journalists and others at the time of the fall of Malaya.

The supply of labour being such an important feature in rubber production, it is natural that the Association should wish that someone should do something in the way of planning for a supply of workers when it is once more possible to produce rubber in Malaya. I agree with Mr. Monson in considering that it would be useless to discuss the question with the India Office now. No one knows what sort of Government India will have after the war nor whether that Government will allow its nationals to emigrate to work as they used to do under the conditions ruling in Malaya and elsewhere. Similarly, we do not know what type of administration Malaya itself will have. Obviously, whatever form of Government or Constitution there may be labour will be necessary to work the rubber plantations. What labour it will be possible to obtain we also do not know.

I feel, however, that all these considerations should be put before the Chairman and one or two members of his Labour Committee at a meeting here on a reasonably high level. They would like to feel assured that the problem had been thought of. It is, of course, only part of the far wider question of post-war reconstruction of Malaya to which consideration has been given for some time, but the R.G.A. do not know this, and I am sure they would appreciate having the whole subject explained to them. I am not in favour of a cross-correspondence: that will only engender misunderstanding and a feeling of grievance. At the present time I do attach enormous importance to creating in this Association and others connected with Malaya a feeling of confidence that H.M.G. is giving thought to the difficulties which are worrying them.

My dear Copeman,

As you know, we have very much in mind (and, to an ever increasing extent, on paper) the vast problems of re-entry and reconstruction in Malaya. I hope you will forgive our delay in responding to your official letter of the 29th October (S/M) about the Indian Immigration Fund. But may I make at any rate a partial response in this semi-official letter.

You will not, I feel sure, think that in the Colonial Office letter of 7th September we were consciously evading or neglecting the question of labour supply which is the essential point in your letter of 29th October. What we were dealing with was the security of the moneys in the Fund (the particular subject of your earlier letter).

On the preparations which we and you can usefully make together now for assuring early and adequate labour forces, we have felt it to be unlikely to lead, in present circumstances, to any substantial assurance if we were to go to the India Office. As you say, immigration from India was in a state of interruption before the outbreak of war in Malaya - at the instance of the Government of India.

We can ask Barron for an appreciation: he is in Fire, but would no doubt respond to an invitation to help us in this way. We ought to be quite clear as to what particular information it is that he can most usefully give us. On the whole, we are inclined to suggest that we should have a talk with you on the best line of action to suit the needs of Malaya and the industry. If you agree would you send me a line to say what date in about a week's time would suit you?

Yours sincerely,

(Sgd.) G.E.J. Gent.

H.F. COPEMAN, ESQ.

29th October, 1942.

Sir,

I have the honour to refer to your letter Ref. 50772/42 of the 7th September, 1942 on the subject of the Indian Immigration Fund, Malaya, and to express the thanks of my Association for the information you have been good enough to furnish.

2. My Association notes that the Secretary of State is advised that it would not be practicable to re-constitute the Indian Immigration Committee at present, and that he doubts whether such a step would in fact be necessary.

3. My Association, while giving due weight to the authoritative opinion expressed by the Secretary of State, respectfully suggests that some form of skeleton organisation should be set up to avoid a complete break in the continuity of the activities previously undertaken and the policy pursued by the Committee.

4. My Association holds the view that on the re-occupation of Malaya there will be in all probability a large and sudden demand for labour and it is understood that at the time of the fall of Malaya there were no complete arrangements for immigration either from India or from the Netherlands East Indies. In the first case the arrangements for immigration from India were in suspense and in the second case no final arrangements had been agreed.

5. My Association understands that Mr. J. M. Barron, who was Chairman of the Immigration Committee, is now in this country, and I would suggest that if he were to submit a short memorandum dealing with the latest position with regard to immigration it would prove useful both to the Colonial Office and to ourselves and other employers of labour.

6. We submit also for the consideration of the Secretary of State the proposal that there should be at the Colonial Office an official whose duty, or part of whose duty it should be, to keep in as close touch as possible with the situation and, if necessary, contact the Governments of India and the Netherlands East Indies.

The Under Secretary of State,
Colonial Office, S.W.1.

Since the occupation of Malaya by the Japanese, my Association has received enquiries as to the status of the "Rubber Fund" and the funds of the Rubber Research Institute of Malaya.

2. As you are doubtless aware, the Malayan Rubber Fund was constituted under the provisions of the Rubber Regulation legislation for the purpose of meeting the expenses of administering the regulation scheme, the payment of contributions to the Rubber Research Institute, the furtherance of rubber research, the improvement of the rubber industry, and the support of propaganda relating to rubber.

The Fund was administered by the Controller of Rubber, Malaya, under the advice of a Committee consisting of representatives from the various States.

Under the Enactment constituting the Rubber Research Institute of Malaya, the funds of the Institute came under the control of the Board upon which representatives of the Industry were appointed by the High Commissioner.

3. Members of this Association owning rubber estates in Malaya are, of course, deeply interested in the conservation of these Funds during the present emergency, bearing in mind how necessary it is for the Industry to have funds available for these particular purposes.
