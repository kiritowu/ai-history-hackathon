Mr. Paskin.

You asked me to look at the draft reply to 59 and also to consider certain general questions. As a reply to 59, I think Mr. Taylor's line is quite correct but should put the matter a little differently as in draft herewith.

I agree with the view that you expressed that it is very desirable to keep closely in touch on all these questions, some of which arise on Departmental files and some on Economic files, and I shall be happy to advise on particular cases whenever you or Mr. Taylor think it desirable to refer them to me. I think, however, that the primary responsibility should, for the present, remain with the Eastern Department.

It is clearly desirable to try and get some definition as clear as possible of our attitude towards different classes of claims. I understand there have been certain inconclusive discussions in the past with the Treasury, but meanwhile it has been necessary to take action in a number of individual cases.

I suggest that we should now write to the Treasury referring to the previous discussions and saying that if they would like to discuss further we are at their disposal but that in the meanwhile we must take action, and have taken action, in a number of individual cases. I should then try and set out the lines we are in effect following and say that we should propose to continue to do so in default of any expression of dissent from the Treasury. I suggest that as a start, although other people more familiar with the details will no doubt be able to add other points, our principles should be:

===== Page 12 =====
(a) We pay pensions, leave salaries, and allowances to dependents on lines already approved.

(b) We pay interest on loans, again on lines already agreed with the Treasury.

(c) We meet claims in respect of applications of a commercial character, e.g., for goods ordered by the Governments concerned, either through the Crown Agents or direct and for reimbursement of firms or Governments who have paid out money as agents for the Eastern Governments, e.g., the case of the Rice Companies and various claims received from Dominion Governments.

(d) We refuse all claims for repayment of deposits with the local Governments, e.g., the Public Trustee deposits or the kind of case dealt with in correspondence entered at No.76 in 18843/31, on the general ground that it is impossible from records here to verify the terms upon which deposits were made and that in any case, repayment is only due in the colony.

(e) We make no payments in respect of any claims for local production of goods by the Governments on the same grounds as in (d).

(f) We make no payments in respect of claims for compensation for any kinds of war damage but agree to record such claims for consideration after the war.

Finally, I doubt whether there is any advantage in making a record at present of claims in Categories (d) and (e) and for the moment should not institute any record. If, however, we later come to the conclusion that such claims ought to be recorded, it will be necessary to establish some systematic register of them. It will not be sufficient to leave them on record in the files.

Mr. Duncan.

I should be grateful for your views on the Opinion of Mr. Speed which forms the enclosure to No. 14.

You will see that in No. 9 we put up to the Treasury the question whether we should pay bills like those submitted by Messrs. Gammon, as a matter of policy, not of law. We had assumed that as a matter of law, there was no means of enforcing in this country payment on contracts entered into in Malaya, which provided for payment locally in local currency. In his Opinion, however, Mr. Speed takes the contrary view. In fact, he says that the "contracting authority" could be sued here for the sterling equivalent of the local currency converted at the rate current when the payment became due.

This Opinion clearly cannot be right in a case where the "contracting authority" is a Malay States authority (as in fact was the case in several of Messrs. Gammon's contracts). The question is whether (especially in present circumstances) Mr. Speed's view is right in a case where the "contracting authority" was a Straits Settlements authority? If he is right, who is the person who could be sued in the Courts in this country?

I am not raising these questions with a view to proposing that we should not authorise payment on such contracts because, as I have said above, we have submitted to the Treasury that they should be paid as a matter of policy, but it would help us to know exactly where we stand as a matter of law.

It is true that the local Government is the Crown, but I do not think that a petition of right would lie against the Crown in this country in respect, e.g., of a claim arising out of a contract entered into by it in, so to speak, its Straits Settlements capacity, having regard in particular to the fact that: (1) provision is made by Section 20 et seq. of the Straits Settlements Crown Suits Ordinance (Cap. 43) for such petitions to be prosecuted in the High Court of the Colony (I presume that there is nothing which affects this in the Straits Settlements (Temporary Provisions) Order in Council, if it has been made); and (2) generally speaking, a petition of right would not lie in this country in respect of a claim for money not chargeable on the public revenues of the U.K.

In correspondence on 50759/4/42 and on 50759/22/42, the question has been raised as to the propriety of making payments now to partners or directors of firms registered in Malaya who have themselves escaped from Malaya, in respect of claims by their firms.

After a good deal of discussion the draft herewith was prepared, but on considering it again I am not quite sure that it brings out sufficiently clearly the points at issue for us, in relation to what seems to me to be a rather different point in the letter from the Ministry of War Transport at No. 26. The question there discussed was whether a payment in respect of a claim by a Malayan registered firm can properly be made to an "agent" of the firm now in this country.

The point that arises on the two files mentioned above, however, seems to me to be rather different; namely whether we can now properly pay to a director or a partner in a Malayan registered firm that proportion of the total claim of the firm which is represented by the interest of the partner or director in the firm. It seems to me that before embarking on this long letter to the T.W.E. Dept., we ought to have legal advice on our own particular problems. In their letter at No. 26, the Ministry of War Transport say that they have been advised that it will seldom, if ever, be safe for that Department to negotiate with, or make payments to, "persons who purport to be acting under the authorities of Powers of Attorney granted by companies or other persons."

Your letter of the 16th July (50759/43) raises a number of important points, and while I have done my best to give you our views, you will no doubt confer with your own legal adviser in cases where your position is in doubt.

2. I gather from Boyle's letter to Morris of the 11th March that the Ministry of War Transport take the view that they can never obtain a good receipt from a company incorporated in Japanese occupied territory unless it has registered under the Defence (Companies) Regulations 1940. We do not share this view. The intention of the Defence (Companies) Regulations was to assist companies incorporated in overrun territories to function. The effect of registration on the "enemy" or "non-enemy" status of the registrant is doubtful, but while it is most improbable that an "enemy" company would ever be registered, I think it possible that a company which has not registered may not be an enemy.

3. It is frequently a matter of difficulty to determine whether a company is an enemy. The relevant provisions of the Trading with the Enemy Act, 1939, by which a company's status is determined, are contained in Section 2. This establishes that a company incorporated in territory which has been overrun is enemy only if it is controlled by persons resident in enemy territory or if it continues to carry on business in such territory. It is this question of control which in practice occasions the real difficulty of determining a company's status as the locus of the control depends in each case upon its particular facts.

4. If a company is an enemy under Section 2 then under the

Where any requirement or direction with respect to any money or property is addressed to any person by a custodian and accompanied by a certificate of the custodian that the money or property is money or property to which the Custodian Order applies, the certificate shall be evidence of the facts stated therein, and if that person complies with the requirement or direction, he shall not be liable to any action or other legal proceeding by reason only of such compliance (Section 7 (2) of the Act).

6. I have already indicated that a company incorporated in Japanese occupied territory may not be an enemy under the Trading with the Enemy Act, and if its sole appreciable enemy taint is its incorporation in the occupied territory we see no reason why it should be considered as an enemy at common law. Consider, for example, a simple case where a man and his wife have formed a company of which they are the sole officers and shareholders. The company owns some funds in England and a small plantation in Malaya where it is incorporated, but no money; and before escaping the officials so far as possible "scorched" the plantation. The husband and wife reach Australia and, as directors and shareholders, cause the company to continue functioning for the purpose of collecting debts due to it from persons in England etc. We should not regard such a company as enemy and we should not regard as abrogated any authority which the company had conferred upon its agent. We should do, and in fact have, issued authorities under the

Act "if and so far as they may be necessary" with a view to encouraging the English debtors to pay the company.

7. Subject to this I am in accord with Boyle's statement. If a company is an enemy it is legally unsafe to pay its debts to persons purporting to act as its agents; if it is not an enemy payments may safely be made to it or to its duly authorised agents. These remarks apply equally to persons purporting to act on behalf of an individual.

8. In paragraph 8 of your letter (Ref. 50759/43) you propound an argument which I understand to be that because say 95% of the shareholders of a company are resident outside enemy occupied territory and 5% within that territory, you should retain 5% of any payment due and pay the 95%. If this is the argument, I think it is open to grave criticism. The assets of a company, including debts due to it, belong to the company as a legal entity and not to its shareholders. They can only reach the shareholders in the form of dividends or on repayment following a reduction of capital or on the company's liquidation; they are available for meeting the claims of creditors, which have priority over those of shareholders. Apart from all this, if the money is legally due to the proposed payee you will have no right to retain a percentage of it. Either the concern is an enemy, in which case the money is due to the Custodian, or it is not, in which event the money is due to the concern itself. Where say 45% only of the shareholders are resident outside occupied territory it would be dangerous if you were to pay that percentage of the company's claim as you might then be paying to an enemy monies which should be paid to the Custodian. On the other hand you have no legal justification for retaining a percentage of the claim of a non-enemy company.

9. In our opinion, it is wrong to countenance applications for payment to individuals claiming in their personal capacity of shareholders or directors of limited companies however great their interest may be. By their non-enemy status and control of the company they may bring the company into the non-enemy class so that it will be safe to pay the company. The company's receipt may well be signed by its duly appointed directors who may also be shareholders, but they should sign for and on behalf of the company and immunity will not be secured by paying a debt due to a company to one of its shareholders.

It is restricted (1) to ensuring practical immunity from the commission of the criminal offence of trading with the enemy and (2) by the provisions of Article 1 (v) (a) of the Custodian Order, 1939, to defeating the claim the Custodian would otherwise have had to receive monies due to the company. It is, I think, clear that, subject to proof that the work had been completed and that the price had become due before the occupation of Malaya by the Japanese, a contractor is entitled to payment. The position in this respect is, I think, the same whether the payment has, by the contract, to be made in sterling in this country, which remains possible, or in Straits Settlements currency in Malaya, which has become impossible, since, if the payment had become due before the Japanese occupation, the contracting authority was strictly in default and accordingly could be sued here in damages for the sterling equivalent of the local currency converted at the rate current when the payment became due. Conversely, if, at the time of the Japanese occupation, payment had not become due, the contractor would legally have no claim.

A position of some doubt might arise if, for instance, the contract called for payment in local currency in Malaya, not against a specific event, e.g., the certificate of the superintending officer, but, say, "within one month of completion of the work". If in such a case the Japanese occupation occurred after the completion of the work and before the month had elapsed, then I think the strict legal position would be that the payment had not become due; that there could accordingly have been no default before the payment in local currency in Malaya became impossible; and that accordingly no action would lie in this country to recover the sterling equivalent as damages. I cannot, however, believe that any Court would hold that the payer was altogether relieved from paying for work that had been completed. The Court would, in my view, adopt one of two lines. One would be to hold that the obligation to pay was suspended until such time as payment to the contractor in local currency in Malaya became possible again; the other would be to hold that the price in local currency was merely a measure of the value of the work, that the stipulation for payment in Malaya was one for the sole benefit of the contractor which could be waived by him, and that he was accordingly entitled, if he wished, to be paid sterling in this country.

I find it impossible to say which of these two lines the Court would adopt. This would no doubt depend on the exact facts of each case; but I think that the Court would lean towards holding that the contractor was entitled to demand payment in sterling.

In stating my views above, I have made the following assumptions.

Although we have thought it desirable now to invite the Treasury to give their blessing to the principles and practice that have now been evolved in these matters, we do not regard it as necessary for the Crown Agents to await this covering authority before proceeding to make any further payments of the kinds mentioned in paragraph 2 above.

Thank you for your letter (50759/42) of the 3rd August regarding Malayan contracts placed direct with firms. In our semi-official correspondence there has been a little over-particularization, to which I am afraid I have contributed. Your letter refers only to the claim of Messrs. Paterson Simons & Co., but we have other claimants and we shall assume that we are to deal with these non-Crown Agents' contracts from whatever source the claim comes.

I have been looking into the question about Malayan contracts generally from the point of view of our authority to deal with them to the extent of making payments without Colonial Office approval. There are four cases: Colony funds or non-Colony funds may be concerned, and a contract may have been placed through the Crown Agents or direct. The main authorities bearing on the subject seem to consist of the draft Order-in-Council for the Straits Settlements and your confidential letter (50763/42) of the 23rd April (replying to our letter of the 16th March) and in particular paragraphs 5 and 6 of that letter. Also, there are James's letter (18855/28/42) of the 24th February, confirming Ransom's record of the discussion on the 17th February, and your confidential letter (same reference and date) authorising cancellation generally.

In the case of:-

(1) Colony orders placed through the Crown Agents: we have understood that authority to cancel includes authority to make any payment which we cannot escape. The Secretary of State is assuming the powers of the Governor; he will have full financial control of the local Government when it is re-constituted; and it seems to be part of our function as Agents that we should do the best we can for our principal in disposing of contracts placed on his behalf.

/Non-Colony

J.J. Paskin, Esq., M.C.,
Colonial Office.

===== Page 128 =====
(2) Non-Colony orders placed through the Crown Agents: apart from the Secretary of State's control, the position is the same as in (1), and no distinction as to cancellation was made in your confidential letter of the 24th February. The last paragraph of the Colonial Office letter of 23rd April may, however, be read to mean that the Secretary of State desires to control these payments to a greater extent than is implied by our reporting quarterly the state of the balances of the various funds, and we shall be glad to have an assurance that we may, without express authority (unless in a particular case we consider it desirable), make any payments involved in cancellation.

(3) Colony orders not placed through the Crown Agents: in view of the great difficulty which we already know we shall have in finding out exactly what happened, any money settlement is likely to be arbitrary, and I think, unless the Secretary of State is willing to give us general authority to use our discretion in these matters, it will be necessary for us to confine ourselves to examination and recommendation to the Colonial Office as to any payment to be made in a particular case;

(4) Non-Colony contracts not placed through the Crown Agents: a fortiori, it would seem that our function should be limited to examination and recommendation as in (3).

I shall be glad to have a ruling on each of these four points.
