(4) s.s. HELLAS - NORWEGIAN SHIPPING & TRADE MISSION.

Referring to Mr. Morris' queries:

(a) The Treasury Solicitor (at 26/18535/6/42) gave his opinion that in English law he did not think there would be a claim for freight against the requisitioning authority, but that ex gratia payments might be made as was done in the United Kingdom in similar circumstances in 1940.

As regards any liability on the part of the Straits Settlements Government to pay the outstanding freight (Bangkok to Hong Kong only - not extra freight due to deviation) I can find no specific reference to such a contingency in the Straits Settlements Defence (Compensation) Regulations 1940, Section 8 of which reads in paraphrase:

1) Compensation in respect of requisitioned goods shall be a sum equal to the price which the owner might reasonably have been expected to obtain upon a sale of the goods, regard being had to the condition of the goods at the time and no account being taken of any appreciation in the value of the goods due to the emergency.

2) Compensation in the case of an owner who had produced the goods and intended a sale shall not exceed the aggregate of the production cost, purchase price and the reasonable profit that might have been expected from a sale immediately before the requisition.

3) Compensation in other cases shall not take account of any profit that might have been expected on a sale.

===== Page 12 =====
4) If the price or maximum price of requisitioned goods had been fixed by law, compensation should not exceed that price or maximum price.

5) The compensation payable shall include a sum equal to the amount of any expenses reasonably incurred (otherwise than on behalf of the Government) for the purpose of compliance with any directions given on behalf of the Government in connection with the requisition.

From the general sense of the 1st paragraph, it seems to me that a reasonable selling price would be expected to be one based on a payment of all charges such as freight etc., in which case the freight charges would have been included in the proceeds obtained by Government sale and held on Hong Kong Government account (at 46). From the fourth paragraph, however, it is very probable that the price of rice - the main cargo requisitioned at Singapore (at 46) - was controlled by Government and consequently any unpaid freight would be no concern of the requisitioning authority.

In a similar case where rice cargoes were diverted from Malaya to Ceylon and requisitioned at Colombo, the Ceylon Government declared (74/18855/Q7/42) that while their Defence Compensation Regulations provided for payment of the value of goods on the basis of the market prices at time and place of requisitioning, their Requisitioning Regulations provided that a Shipping Company's claim for deviation expenses or general average contribution would be for cargo owners' a/c. (In the Hellas case there has been no claim for deviation expenses so far but this may yet come - at 32).

The case of Sovfracht (V/O) v Van Udens Scheepvaart en Agentuur Maatschappij (N.V. Gebr.) (1943) A.C. 203 is summarised as follows:

"Before the outbreak of war between Great Britain and Germany in September 1939, the respondents, a shipowning company incorporated under the law of the Netherlands and having their principal place of business in Rotterdam, chartered one of their vessels to the appellants. Disputes having arisen between them, the respondents sought arbitration in London in accordance with a provision in the charterparty, and in April 1940, each party appointed an arbitrator. In May 1940, the Germans invaded the Netherlands and brought the country entirely under their control. The appellants refused to proceed with the arbitration:

Held, that the respondents, being resident in the Netherlands, were in the position of alien enemies at common law and had ceased to enjoy the right of resort to the King's courts save by permission given by royal licence.

Invasion of territory, resulting in the enemy being in effective control at the material time, and exercising some kind of government or administration over it, as distinct from occupation of a slighter character merely for military purposes, gives the area an enemy character and disqualifies local residents from suing in the King's courts.

Per Lord Porter. The authority of the

s.s. "HELLAS" - Requisitioned cargo

We thank you for your letter of the 15th instant in which you ask us to produce details of the Chartering of this vessel to the Hong Kong Government.

We wish to explain that the "HELLAS" was at that time trading on regular liner service between Bangkok and Hong Kong. As you, however, will see from the copy of the Manifest which we handed to you, a large proportion of the cargo carried was consigned to the Hong Kong Government Rice Monopoly, under whose control we understand also private firms imported rice.

Since the outbreak of war with Japan, we have at one time and another passed over to you various claims against the Colonial Governments by the representatives of shipping companies whose control and registration are now in enemy-occupied territory. In some of these cases, we have told you how, had the claims been against the Ministry, we should have dealt with them.

Recently, however, the House of Lords' decision reversing the Court of Appeal in the WAALHAVEN case [Sovfracht v. N.V. Gobr. Van Udens Scheepvaart. T.L.R. 4427] has caused us to modify our views somewhat.

The principal question in the above case was whether the Respondent Company, a company incorporated under the laws of Holland which had been occupied by the enemy, was by reason of the mere fact that it continued to carry on business in Holland to be treated as an alien enemy to the extent that it was incapacitated from taking proceedings in the Courts of this country with a view to recovering a business debt. The House of Lords have decided that the Respondent Company is technically an enemy alien and as such not entitled to sue.

There was a subsidiary question as to the validity of the authority of the Respondents' Solicitors to continue to represent them after the Respondents had themselves become technical enemies. The principal question having been decided against the Respondents, this subsidiary question did not arise for decision, but both Lord Wright and Lord Porter made some observations on it. Thus Lord Porter said:

"Ordinarily, when the principal becomes an enemy the authority of the agent ceases on the ground that it is /not

W. A. Morris, Esq., Colonial Office.

===== Page 139 =====
Not permissible to have intercourse with an enemy alien, and the existence of the relationship of principal and agent necessitates such intercourse.

That the representatives, legal or non-legal, of a litigant may require to have such intercourse with their principal in the litigation is, I imagine, clear, and I do not think it is an answer to say that in the event it may not be found necessary for the one to communicate with the other: at any moment the necessity may arise; the very relationship requires it even if it is desired only to terminate the mandate itself.

He went on to cite authorities for the proposition that the relationship of principal and agent is determined when the parties to the agency agreement become enemies one to the other.

In the result, so far as these Far Eastern ships are concerned, we are advised that it will seldom, if ever, be safe for the Department to negotiate with, or make payments to, persons who purport to be acting under the authority of Powers of Attorney granted by companies or other persons incorporated or resident in Japanese occupied territory. Authorities granted to such agents by the Trading with the Enemy Department for the purposes of the Trading with the Enemy Act do not enable the Department to obtain a good discharge, for which purpose the agent must have a valid authority from his principal.

We are therefore taking the line that though we are willing to collect and exchange with claimants evidence of what took place in the Far East, before we can negotiate or pay money to them, the companies concerned must be registered in the U.K. under Regulation 5 of the Defence (Companies) Regulations, 1940.

Yours sincerely,

11th March, 1943.

Dear Morris,

When you spoke to Boyle the other day about Swires' claim for hire and compensation for loss of their lighters alleged to have been requisitioned by the Straits Settlements Government, you asked if we could add anything to Lang's letter to you of the 8th January, 1943.

We have discussed the matter with Lang. We understand that Swires (i.e. the China Navigation Co.), having been paid by this Ministry hire and compensation for loss in the case of some of their requisitioned vessels, complain bitterly to you that they are made to wait until after the war in the case of the others merely because the latter happened to have been requisitioned by a Colonial Government instead of by this Ministry.

We cannot but think there is some force in Swires' argument, which I believe may also be used by the Glen Line (see Boyle's letter of 6th October, 1942). As a matter of general policy there appears a good deal to be said for settling claims of this sort here and now, so long as information is provided by either party which can be accepted by us as satisfactory proof of the soundness of the claim and subject of course to any re-adjustment which may be necessary later if material evidence comes to light, rather than postponing settlement until after the war on the assumption, which will not always be realised, that the facts will be a good deal clearer then than they are now. This is the basis on which we have been working and are engaged in settling such claims now.

While it would bring the attitude of the two Ministries towards Far Eastern shipping claims into line if you were to pay now where you feel the evidence would warrant payment, it struck us that in view of your general compensation policy

16 February, 1943.

Dear Lang,

With reference to previous correspondence ending with my letter of January 14th which enclosed a copy of Treasury letter of December 31st last, respecting registration of claims relative to British property lost at sea owing to enemy action, it seems desirable that we should get clearer our understanding as to what shipping claims we should register and what should be left to you or vice versa. The Treasury decision in their letter of December 31st left matters so that where passengers had lost their effects at sea and had neglected to insure them, we were not to trouble to register the loss here. Thus, unless you should consider that in those circumstances there should be some record of these cases with you as necessary to complete the whole tale of shipping losses attributable to enemy action, these losses would go quite unrecorded. These are, however, a very small part of the whole business of shipping losses at sea which are primarily dealt with under your War Risks Insurance Schemes and so would naturally be excluded from our Registers. There are, however, one or two cases which have come to us (see papers enclosed herewith) and in which there does seem to be room for doubt as to the proper procedure in regard to the recording of claims.

In the first place we have the claim from Major W.A. Armitage in respect of what appears to be a sea-going yacht of his lost by being caught up in the German invasion of Holland. Your War Risks Insurance Office has declined any liability because the vessel was not insured with them, nor

Total loss payments for lighters belonging to Messrs. John Swire & Sons.

Your letter to Ward on this subject has been passed to me for attention.

The policy of the Ministry of War Transport with regard to Total Loss payments for ships known to have been requisitioned in the Far East by the Ministry is that if no evidence is received of the existence of the ships after the fall of the port on which they were based, or were stationed at that time, the ship is presumed lost at the time the port in question fell into enemy hands - Shanghai 7th December 1941, Hong Kong 25th December 1941, and Singapore 15th February 1942. In making these payments to owners we have covered ourselves by getting owners to sign a suitable form of indemnity.

Incidentally, we have received confirmation, via Berne and our Foreign Office, sent by the Swiss minister in Tokyo, of the losses of most of the ships for which we have received requests for Total Loss payments.

This policy was arrived at after consultation with the Treasury, but without consultation with the Colonial Office. There was no technical difference between a requisition in that area and a requisition elsewhere, and it seemed to us that the Colonial Office might wish to link its compensation policy for vessels lost while under Colonial requisition to its general compensation policy for requisitioned assets rather than to the policy which this Ministry was bound to adopt.

In view of the lack of documentary evidence of dates of requisition of such small craft, it might be advisable that you should have a list of vessels for which we are in the process of paying hire and Total Loss payments, and I enclose herewith particulars at present available for Messrs.
