I have the honour to refer to the late Lord Lloyd's Federated Malay States Confidential despatch of the 30th September, 1940, and to forward a copy of a letter with enclosures addressed by Mr. Hubback to the Federal Secretary, Federated Malay States, in which he makes a formal request that his complaint be laid before Your Lordship. Mr. Hubback's letter to the Secretary of State is also enclosed.

2. The complaint is based on an official report submitted by Mr. J.F.F. Gregg, at that time District Officer, Kuala Lipis, Pahang, in connection with the constitution of the King George V National Park. This report, a copy of which is attached, was submitted in accordance with the provisions of the enactment governing the constitution of the Park, whereby established rights in the Park could be reserved to the indigenous population. The report is a document for official use only and was never officially referred to Mr. Hubback who saw a copy by chance. Whether or not the document is marked confidential is immaterial in this connection; Mr. Hubback, as a private individual, has no right to demand that it be submitted for his perusal and the publication of such a document rests in the discretion of the Government.

3. Apart from the privileged nature of the document, it will be seen that the contents provide no justification whatsoever for the somewhat extravagant complaint put forward by Mr. Hubback. A perusal of the correspondence will show that his complaint falls into two parts; first that his reputation has been damaged and secondly that the Government has been misled by the fallacious nature of the report. The second ground for his complaint is a matter in which he has no standing; the evidence called for by the Government and the reliance it places on that evidence is no concern of Mr. Hubback nor has he any right to demand that his own evidence in the matter be heard.

4. The first ground for his complaint is a personal one and it is difficult to take it seriously. The District Officer in the course of his enquiry was of the opinion that certain rights which were claimed should be recognised although they had not been exercised immediately prior to the declaration of the Park, the reason being that restraint had been imposed on them by Mr. Hubback. The District Officer was further of the opinion that this restraint had been illegally exercised and he stated so in his report. Whether he was right or wrong in his opinion is immaterial for the purposes of this complaint; he was only performing his duty in recording the reasons for his recommendations. Moreover, there is no suggestion of malice and I am unable to discern the slightest grounds for the personal resentment expressed by Mr. Hubback.

I have the honour to acknowledge the receipt of your letter of the 18th December 1939, (No.26 in Phg.Conf. 52/59A) which, owing to a long illness, I have been unable to answer earlier.

2. I cannot consider the position, so far as I am concerned, regarding the derogatory remarks made by Mr. Gregg about me in the Ulu Tembeling Report, as anything but most unsatisfactory.

In event of action being taken on the recommendations of Mr. Gregg great harm may accrue to the proper establishment of the King George V National Park as a wildlife sanctuary. I therefore take leave to state a case against the present attitude taken up towards me and against the possible acceptance of Mr. Gregg's report which is not only misleading but mischievous.

3. The inquiry was obviously a semi-judicial one to decide on what action, if any, should be taken under Section 16 of the King George V National Park (Pahang) Enactment, 1939.

To make it quite clear I quote sub-section 16 (i).

16.(i) If at any time it is established to the satisfaction of the Trustees that any person, or community or description of persons, living on or near the borders of the State Park, had been, immediately before the coming into force of this Enactment, habitually in the enjoyment of any privilege of way, of fishing, of collecting produce, or otherwise, claimed by such person or persons in or in respect of any part of the State Park, it shall be lawful for the Trustees by notification in the Gazette to authorise the continued enjoyment of the privilege so claimed, for such period of time, and subject to such conditions, as the Trustees shall seem fit.

The significant proviso in lines 3 and 4 above, "living on or near the borders of the State Park" and the binding proviso in lines 4 and 5 above, "immediately before the coming into force of this Enactment," restricted the scope of the inquiry.

To get over this Mr. Gregg has tried to establish a purely fanciful argument by stating that owing to alleged action on my part the exercise of supposed rights of certain inhabitants of Ulu Tembeling were prevented.

4. The inquiry was not conducted in a judicial manner. Ex parte statements were taken from persons who were interested in establishing claims to rights which, had this been a properly conducted inquiry, would have been proved non-existent.

The folly of basing a finding on ex parte statements is well stated by Lord Howard, Lord Chief Justice of England, in his book "The New Despotism" (page 47).

===== Page 19 =====
"Will anybody at this time of day deny that it is essential to the proper administration of justice that the decision should be based on evidence, and that the evidence should be heard in the presence of both parties, who are given the opportunity of cross-examination? Evidence not tested by cross-examination is nearly always misleading and practically valueless. The public official, as has been observed, may, and often does, decide without any evidence at all, and he may act on ex parte statements, made by one party without anything to support them, which are never brought to the knowledge of the other party, so that he has no opportunity to controvert them. Is it too much to say that such proceedings are a more travesty of justice?"

5. Amongst these ex parte statements Mr. Grogg recorded remarks made by some of those who appeared before him complaining of certain alleged actions of mine which Mr. Grogg decided, without ever hearing the other side, were illegal. He went beyond that; he even recommended that certain orders should be made because of these alleged actions of mine.

When were these actions supposed to have taken place? We are not told. Mr. Grogg apart from the acceptance of these statements has entirely ignored in his finding the provisos contained in the first five lines of section 16 of the National Park Enactment. Even had it been proved that certain actions had been taken, proof of their illegality had still to be established. Was this done?

Lord Howard, (page 25) quoting from Professor Dicoy's "Law of the Constitution" seems to cover Mr. Grogg's responsibility in regard to his remarks concerning me.

Lord Howard quotes:

"With us", as Professor Dicoy says, "every official, from the Prime Minister down to a constable or a collector of taxes, is under the same responsibility for every act done without legal justification as any other citizen. The Reports abound with cases in which officials have been brought before the Courts, and made, in their personal capacity, liable to punishment, or to the payment of damages, for acts done in their official character but in excess of their lawful authority. A colonial governor, a secretary of state, a military officer, and all subordinates, though carrying out the commands of their official superiors, are as responsible for any act which the law does not authorise as is any private and unofficial person."

I asked in my letter of the 11th November that His Excellency the High Commissioner would allow me to have a copy of the Ulu Temboling report so that I could make comments on Mr. Grogg's remarks so far as they applied to me and my alleged actions. Report on an enquiry into claims to privileges in the State Park under Section 16(i), Pahang Enactment 2/39.

Briefly the claims made were as follows:

A. Particular claims.

(i) To tap jelutong on the true right bank of the Sat.

(ii) To tap jelutong and cut gambier on the true right bank of the Sepia.

B. General claims.

(i) To take forest produce of all kinds, except, by implication, jelutong and gambier.

(ii) To fish in the Sat and Sepia.

(iii) To travel along these rivers into Kelantan and Trongganu.

A.(i). Made by the third witness on behalf of the people of Kampong Dunai. As this kampong has been deserted and the privileges claimed have not been enjoyed for a long time I think, without prejudice to what I shall have to say later about Mr. Hubback's activities, that this claim should fail.

(ii). Made by the sixth witness, Abdullah bin Haji Suloiman of Kampong Rusal. This man seems to have made a fairly good livelihood out of jelutong and gambier until Mr. Hubback interfered (see also the third witness' evidence of transactions with this claimant). Subject to the general observations which I shall make under B(i) I think this claim should succeed.

B. All general claims were by agreement made by the four ketua Kampong or their representatives (first, second, fourth and fifth witnesses) on behalf of all their anak buah. The jurisdictions of the four ketuas are Kampong Pulau Besar, Kampong Sepia, Kampong Kuala Sat and Kampong Pagi. In view of Mr. Rendle's record of the evidence of the ketua of Kampong Pagi I had left him out of my calculations as far as this enquiry went, but the evidence of the fifth witness makes it clear that the claims of the people of Kampong Pagi are as strong as those of the rest, as far as the area between Kuala Konian and Kuala Sat goes.

B. (i). There is no doubt whatever in my mind that the sale of forest produce used to form and to a much lesser degree still forms the main money crop of the Ulu Tomboling Malays and that a large proportion of their produce was taken from the area now included in the State Park until Mr. Hubback prevented its being taken. The evidence of the Penghulu (seventh witness) is quite clear on this point and carries conviction. Some witnesses (first, second and fifth) professed that they had not taken jungle produce for some years, owing to Mr. Hubback's activities, another (the fourth) said that they had resumed doing so since His Highness the Sultan's visit in October, 1937. It may be contended that the words in Section 16(i) "immediately before the coming into force of this Enactment" are sufficient to invalidate the claims of all but the latter, but it should not be forgotten that the former were prevented, and, as far as I can see, illegally prevented by a servant of the Government...

Government from doing what they otherwise would have continued to do until now. In view of His Excellency's statement in his letter of June 9th to Mr. Gent that no rights would be granted for commercial purposes it is not without some tropidation that I record my firm conviction that forest produce has been taken in the past largely for commercial purposes, and that the claim to continue to do so has been in my respectful submission established.

B. (ii). The fishing in the Sat and Sepia was not done for a commercial purpose, though the catches might be bartered locally for padi. The Sepia, from Kuala Chamir, and the Sat from Kuala Kelapa, as far as their confluences with the Tomboling, are outside the Park. I am not satisfied that anyone was habitually in the enjoyment of the practice of fishing in the remote reaches mentioned by the first witness. He himself says they occasionally went to the Ulu Sat and Ulu Sepia while in Mr. Rendle's enquiry it was stated that expeditions went two or three times a year. I do not think a claim has been established within the wording of the enactment to fish inside the Park.

B. (iii). The right of way into Kelantan and Trongganu by the Sat and Sepia is not, I believe, contested. It is established, I consider, that the right has been enjoyed in the past.

GENERAL.

The identification of any persons to whom might be authorised the continued enjoyment of privileges has caused me a considerable amount of thought. The ideal method would be to give each person a pass, with his photograph gummed to it, specifying the exact privileges to which he was entitled. It would however be quite impossible to get all, or even a reasonable proportion of, the Ulu Tomboling Malays photographed - there is no photographer nearer than Kuala Lipis - and a pass without a photograph would be worse than useless. Furthermore I take the view subject to correction that the words 'community or description of persons' in Section 16(i) introduces (subject to Section 16(ii)) a suggestion of perpetuity and precludes a restriction of the privileges merely to members of the communities born before the date of the enactment. It seemed to me little use therefore even to make a list of present members of the communities and I recommend that if any or all of claims to privileges A (ii) and B ((i) and (iii)) be considered by the Trustees to be established the descriptions of the persons authorised to continue to enjoy them be - A (ii) - 'the indigenous Malays of Kampong Rusal' and - B((i) and (iii)) - 'the indigenous Malays within the jurisdiction of the ketua of Kampongs Pulau Besar, Sepia, Kuala Sat and Pagi.'

I give overleaf a summary of claims and recommendations.

(Signed) J.F.F. Grogg

I have the honour to forward you herewith copies of correspondence that passed between the Government of Pahang and myself, concerning certain statements made by a Mr. J. J. Gregg, officiating as District Officer, Lipis, Pahang, F.M.S.

These statements were made in a report written by him after a visit to Ulu Tembeling, Pahang, in July 1939.

The object of his visit was to inquire into claims made by certain inhabitants of Ulu Tembeling to enable them to establish rights to enter into the reserved area, now known as King George V National Park, for the purposes of collecting jungle produce and other things.

2. In Mr. Gregg's report he made certain allegations against me, basing his remarks on ex parte statements of some of the Malay inhabitants who were anxious to substantiate their claims.

Statements recorded by Mr. Gregg were, in many cases, untrue, but were used by Mr. Gregg as a foundation for an entirely unwarranted attack on me on the grounds of abuse of my authority. At the time referred to in Mr. Gregg's report I was Chief Warden of the Federated Malay States and Straits Settlements, and held statutory duties as Game Warden of Pahang under "The Wild Animals and Birds Protection Enactment, 1925." My appointments were unpaid.

These ex parte statements recorded by Mr. Gregg were never tested by an intelligent cross-examination; in fact I doubt whether they were tested at all.

Mr. Gregg having recorded such statements based recommendations on a purely fantastical argument in which he accused me of having exceeded my powers. He then actually recommended that the provisions of the law, as laid down in Section 16 of the Pahang Enactment for the National Park,

(see Exhibit "F", page 1.) should be ignored so as to give Malay witnesses, or those for whom they had been speaking, privileges to which they were in no way entitled.
