There is now such a miscellany of detail of administrative procedure in this draft that it is difficult to discern much policy in it. In paragraph 1, the classification of appointments is attempted, but all that it succeeds in doing is to state obscurely and in part what is more clearly set out in Colonial Regulations 23 et seq. If the procedure for regulating the affairs of public officers has to be defined in this directive, I think it can only be done by stating quite briefly that Colonial Regulations Part I will apply until such time as the Secretary of State's authority to vary their application to public officers in Malaya has been given. However, it seems unnecessary to make any such statement.

There is also in paragraph 1 a statement of the powers of Governors in relation to public officers. Whether the draft properly expresses the intention or not, I do not know, but it seems quite clear that it is not suitable for inclusion here. Governors must be told their powers quite separately from any policy and directive. They will no doubt have to be given general powers of control over public officers, subject to suitable safeguards, but that is a separate matter.

In paragraph 2, there are various detailed suggestions as to procedure in setting up a public services commission and getting it to function which seem to me to be quite out of place. Surely all that is appropriate or possible at this stage is to say that the Secretary of State attaches importance to the establishment, as soon as conditions permit, of a public service commission, such as has been in operation for some years in Ceylon, and to advise Governors in the exercise of their powers of control over public officers. Even that is not perhaps essential because it is the intention that the White Paper on Colonial Service Organisation which should issue shortly will contain a general recommendation to Colonial Governments to consider the establishment of public service commissions.

For the rest, the only matter of policy is the adoption of the expatriation pay principle. That too will be dealt with as policy in the White Paper on the Colonial Service. When it has been published, it will be for the authorities in Malaya to advise on its application there.

In the circumstances, therefore, I feel that there is hardly need for any directive at all on public services. I am sorry to bring this view forward at this stage when so much consideration has been given to the terms of a directive, but on the whole it seems better not to have a directive, rather than to have, as is now suggested, a collection of miscellaneous detail, which includes very little policy.

This is one of the last of the Departmental directives for the Civil Affairs Staff (Malaya) on long term civil policies for their guidance during the period of military administration.

This directive has been constructed within the framework of our whole political conception of a Malayan Union and a common Malayan citizenship. Its chief features are the institution of a Public Services Commission and the organisation of machinery for the admission of local candidates for the higher grades of the Service.

Judicial appointments which are within the power of the local Government to appoint (other than Supreme Court Judges) are to be made by the Governor on the recommendation of a Judicial Appointments Board.

In paragraph 7 there is a provision that apart from the higher grades of appointment which are subject to the Secretary of State's approval, and save with his consent, no person who is not a British subject or Malayan Union citizen should be appointed to the Public Services in the Malayan Union or Singapore.

In June 13 there is provision for overseas officers in the Malayan Service to be paid eventually on the basis of a substantial salary (suitable to local personnel as well as overseas), plus examination pay.

1. Public Services Commission

There should be a single Public Services Commission for the Malayan Union and Singapore for the purpose of advising the respective Governors in the exercise of their powers relating to appointments, promotions, transfer, dismissal and disciplinary control of Public Officers.

2. The Commission should be official in character and so composed as to command the confidence of the Public Services and the Public. In determining the details of the composition, procedure, functions and duties of the Commission, the Ceylon Public Services Commission should be taken as a guide or model as far as possible.

3. The members of the Commission should be appointed by the Governors jointly, and should include the Chief Secretary for the Malayan Union and the Colonial Secretary for Singapore. When Malayan Union questions are under consideration the Chairman should be the Chief Secretary, and when Singapore questions are under consideration the Colonial Secretary. For questions concerning the Malayan Union and Singapore jointly, these two officers should alternate as Chairman.

4. The duties of and the procedure to be followed by the Commission should be prescribed by regulation made jointly by the Governors and subject to the approval of the Secretary of State.

5. Appointments to the public services

All appointments to the public services of the Malayan Union and Singapore, other than judges of the Supreme Court (Col. Reg. 22), will be made by or on the authority of the Governor concerned.

General control over the selection of persons for appointment to administrative, professional and technical posts will, however, continue to be exercised by the Secretary of State. This control is governed by Colonial Regulations the application of which to the Malayan Union and Singapore may be modified by specific instructions from the Secretary of State. Some modification of the relevant Colonial Regulations may be expected after the publication of a projected White Paper on the Post-War Organisation of the Colonial Service. Such control will extend to posts which are on a salary scale rising to a maximum of more than  a3600 a year. Within this category selection for appointments will normally be made by the Secretary of State, or if made by the Governor on the advice of the Public Services Commission, will be subject to the Secretary of State's approval.

As soon as conditions permit and local educational facilities are sufficient, it will be the policy to establish in Malaya arrangements for the recruitment of local candidates to posts in this category by means of a selection system similar to that of the Colonial Service Appointments Board. Such recruitment will be in accordance with schemes approved by the Secretary of State.

Appointments to posts outside this category will be made by the Governor on the advice of the Public Services Commission, provided that the Governor will determine with the advice of the Public Service

===== Page 29 =====
Service Commission and in the light of directions previously in force in Malaya and Straits Settlements, the extent to which arrangements for selection and powers of appointment in respect of subordinate staff may be delegated to Heads of Departments.

Judicial appointments for which selection is not made by the Secretary of State may be made by the respective Governors on the recommendation of a standing Judicial Appointments Board to be convened by the Chief Justice.

6. For convenience in indicating the application of the further specific directions relating to appointments in paragraphs 7 - the following classification is given:

(a) Appointments for which selection is made by the Secretary of State.

(b) Appointments for which selection by the Governor is subject to the approval of the Secretary of State.

(c) Appointments (not subject to the approval of the Secretary of State) made by the Governor on the advice of the Public Services Commission.

(d) Appointments made by Heads of Departments.

7. Nationality

Save with the consent of the Secretary of State, or, in respect of classes (c) and (d) of paragraph 6, of the Governor, no person who is not a British subject or a Malayan Union citizen should be appointed to the Public Service in the Malayan Union or in Singapore.

8. Liability for service outside the Malayan Union or Singapore

All officers in classes (a), (b), and (c) of paragraph 6 should be liable for service in the Malayan Union, in Singapore, or in Borneo. All officers in classes (a) and (b) of paragraph 6, except non-European citizens of the Malayan Union, will be under obligation to serve in any post in the Colonies to which the Secretary of State may assign them.

9. Qualifications

The qualifications required for appointments in classes (a) and (b) will be specified by, or subject to approval of, the Secretary of State.

10. Probation

All pensionable appointments in classes (a) and (b) will be subject to a three years' period of probation.

11. Conditions of Service

Conditions of service for public officers in the Malayan Union and in Singapore should be prescribed in Public Service Regulations made on the advice of the Public Services Commission with the approval of the Secretary of State. So far as may be appropriate and subject to the general principles contained in the projected White Paper on the Post-War Organisation of the Colonial Service, the provisions in General Orders of the Federated Malay States and of the Straits Settlements and the Malayan Establishment regulations should be used as a guide.

===== Page 30 =====
It should be a normal condition of confirmation in a pensionable post that an officer shall be proficient in the English and Malay languages.

The posting of officers in Malaya will be a matter for local direction subject, in the case of staff appointments, to confirmation by the Secretary of State.

12. Basic salaries

Certain general principles which will be set out in the projected White Paper on the Post-War Organisation of the Colonial Service will be applicable to Malaya.

For all officers, irrespective of race or domicile, there should be common basic scales of salary. In addition to basic salary, officers whose permanent homes are not in Malaya should receive suitable expatriation pay. In general, this addition should take the form of a pensionable allowance, not necessarily at uniform rates for all places outside Malaya but adjusted broadly so as to bring total pay up to a standard adequate to attract officers from such places.

15. Pending review by a Salaries Commission, the basic scales of salary for new entrants should be equivalent to the pre-war scales, with the exception that, in categories of appointments for which candidates are selected by the Secretary of State, the basic scales should, with effect from a date to be decided, be equivalent to the pre-war scales less 30 per cent. As soon as practicable, the C.C.A.O.(M) should make to the Secretary of State for the Colonies a recommendation as to the date on which this provision should become operative.

The Malayan long-term policy directive on The Creation of Malayan Union Citizenship has been revised and has been approved by the Secretary of State for the Colonies for transmission to the C.C.A.O. (M). This copy dated September 12th, 1945 takes the place of all previous copies.

1. There should be created by Order in Council a Malayan Union Citizenship, to which all persons belonging to the country should be admitted.

(Note. It will not be possible to make an Order in Council for this purpose until the Rulers have ceded jurisdiction in their States to His Majesty and the Order creating a Malayan Union has been made).

2. (i) The following persons should automatically acquire Malayan Union Citizenship:

(a) persons born in the Union or the Island of Singapore;

(b) persons who at the date on which the Order in Council becomes operative have ordinarily been resident in the Union or the Island of Singapore for ten years out of the preceding period of 15 years. (In calculating the 15 years period, the period of Japanese occupation of Malaya should be disregarded).

(ii) Machinery should be established (including arrangements for appeals) to enable any person seeking verification of his status as a Malayan Union citizen under para. 2(i) to have the facts of his case examined. In cases so verified certificates of verification should be granted.

(Note. Nothing in the Order in Council will operate to deprive those British subjects who automatically become Malayan Union citizens under this paragraph of their British nationality under the British Nationality and Status of Aliens Act).

3. Provision should also be made to enable a person to acquire Malayan Union citizenship on application after 5 years' ordinary residence in the Union or Singapore, under conditions analogous to those laid down in section 2 of the British Nationality and Status of Aliens Act, 1914. If such applications are accepted, applicants should be granted certificates of naturalization as citizens of the Union of Malaya.

6. Provision should be made on the lines of S.7(1) and (8) of the British Nationality and Status of Aliens Act for the revocation of Malayan Union citizenship when acquired either by certificate of naturalization or by 10 years' residence qualification, but not when acquired by birth.

I think though that the discussion was useful in reviewing some of the problems you raised and that it would be best if in the first place I sent you the enclosed note of the meeting.

The major questions concern the general set-up for salaries and conditions of service in the new Malaya. As to general principles, the Secretary of State has, as the note shows, already explained to the Colonial Governors the broad lines on which these matters in their bearing on salaries and conditions of service of expatriate officers should be tackled. The views he has accepted in this respect are in the following terms:

"The same pay should be given to officers, whether locally domiciled or not, who have the same qualifications and do the same work. This means that basic rates must be fixed by the local Governments (with such expert advice as they may require) in relation to local conditions. It should however be recognised that there are essential differences between the requirements of locally-recruited and externally recruited officers."

MAJOR GENERAL H.R. HONE, C.B.E., M.C.

===== Page 56 =====
The latter have expenses and other difficulties attendant upon the separation from their home country, and their remuneration must clearly be governed by that obtainable in other careers open to them in their own country rather than by current market rates in the Colony in which they serve. These factors are more or less constant irrespective of Colony, and it follows that the basic salaries which are payable to all officers should be supplemented, if necessary, in the case of externally recruited officers, by expatriation pay broadly adjusted to bring remuneration in the Colonial Service up to a common standard. A similar principle should govern leave and passage regulations and other conditions of service. For instance, the expatriate officer may reasonably expect to be found quarters (though there is no reason why they should be "free").

These are general principles which we should hope to see accepted by Colonial Governments in general and having regard to the views in your letter we should not expect any difficulty in their acceptance for Malaya (vide paragraph 11 of the draft Public Service Directive). They are, however, only general principles and the important question is how soon you can apply them. I hope you will not think that the discussion we had is unduly dampening. It is an endeavour to recognise the practical difficulties. As we see it there is unfortunately no clean slate for us to start on in Malaya and even if there were, the stage at which we could start writing clearly on that slate has not yet been reached.

Note of a meeting on 2nd February 1945 to discuss points raised in General Hone's letter of 12th January 1945 about a public service directive.

Sir Charles Jeffries, Mr. Gent, Colonel Newboult, Colonel Turnbull, Mr. Seel, Mr. Whittle, and Mr. Wodeman were present.

Sir Charles Jeffries said that the Secretary of State had given his approval to certain general principles regarding the fixing of basic salary rates applicable to all officers in a Colony irrespective of race or domicile and of the addition of expatriation pay to overseas officers. These general principles have been set out in a letter to Sir Donald Mackenzie-Kennedy of 8th December 1944. The propriety of adopting these principles in Malaya was not questioned, but there was much discussion as to how and at what stage they could be applied. It was quite clear so far as concerns officers who were already in the Malayan Service before the war, or who were serving in other Colonies and have since been transferred to the Malayan Establishment, that the salary scales and conditions of service in force in Malaya before the occupation would have to continue to be applied, since even if it were practicable to devise new basic salaries at this date, serving pensionable staff would have to be given the option of retaining their old conditions. If new conditions were to be more favourable this would not create a difficulty, but as far as could be seen the likelihood was that present salary would be divided into basic salary and expatriation allowance. In that event, even though, as is the intention, the expatriation allowance were pensionable, the change would not necessarily be more favourable. Thus, we should in any event have to contemplate having two sets of conditions side by side.

The next point was whether new basic salaries which were related to local conditions could be devised. No one knew whether eventually, when conditions became stabilised in Malaya, remuneration generally would be at a lower rate than before the occupation or not. It was understood that some of the commercial concerns, such as the rubber companies, believed and hoped that salary scales would be lower in the new Malaya, but they could go no further than that they would like to be free to consider the position in due course. Thus any attempt to fix basic salaries now would have the disadvantage of being entirely arbitrary and of failing to comply with the essential condition that such salaries should be related to local conditions. It was agreed that the attempt must be deferred.

The next point was whether, in relation to new appointments, some suitable warning could be given that although basic rates cannot be devised at this stage, the salaries now paid do include an element for expatriation and that it is the intention that eventually salary should be separated and shown as basic salary and expatriation pay. Most of these new appointments would be people who would be offered contracts under the A.C.I. for periods up to ten years, and the A.C.I. made it clear that officers appointed on contract would receive salaries within the range of the principal scales in force in Malaya and Hong Kong in 1941. It was therefore likely to give rise to some dissatisfaction and perhaps to recruiting difficulties if on appointment on contract some statement were... [POSSIBLY MISSING]

===== Page 59 =====
To be made to the effect that although the pre-war salary was being offered, it was contemplated that some division of the remuneration into basic salary and expatriation allowance would be made later. It would, however, be possible, when these people on contract were later offered pensionable appointments, to make it clear that the basic salary system would be introduced. It was, therefore, agreed that it would be advisable, when the form of contract for these appointments was drafted, that some words should be inserted so as to make it clear that eventual appointment to a pensionable post would not necessarily imply the retention of the salary scales which were in force prior to 1941.

A few appointments of Asiatics were being made by C.A.M.P.U., and further appointments would be needed, both of officers who had previous service in Malaya and of new people. It was agreed that, since it was quite impossible to draw up now a revised scheme of basic salaries, the Asiatic officers who had previously served in Malaya should continue to do so, on the old salaries and terms of service, and that as regards new people, they should all be appointed on short agreements for, say, three years, by which time local conditions should have become sufficiently stabilized to allow a suitable basic salary to be devised. Officers who were promoted to the Malayan Civil Service would have the old arrangements applied to them.

In general, it seemed that the principle of establishing basic salary rates, applicable to all races, must be regarded as a definite future aspiration, but that it must be recognized that it would only add to difficulties if any attempt were made to apply it as from "D" Day.

As regards General Hone's comments on ministerial organisation and devolution of responsibility, Sir Charles Jeffries explained that it was likely that a memorandum would be prepared and sent out to Colonial Governments calling attention to the various views that had been expressed and considered, such as those of Captain Peter MacDonald, M.P. and that Colonial Governments would be invited to consider the extent to which the principles included in such a memorandum could be applied in their territories. These questions, in any case, affected the constitutional position rather than Colonial Service organisation, and it seemed unlikely that any scope for getting to grips with constitutional problems would arise for three or four years at least in Malaya.

Colonel Newboult mentioned a point of difficulty, which C.A.M.P.U., had come up against, over the pensionability of officers for whom a post on the Malayan Establishment could not at present be given, because it was not known actually what would be the order of responsibilities in comparison with the responsibilities of the somewhat similar posts in the old Malayan Establishment. It was agreed that in such cases where the Malayan post could not yet be designated, the officer should be told at the time of transfer that this was the case, but that if, before a post on the Malayan Establishment could be designated, he should either die or retire, he would be deemed to have occupied such and such a post on the old Malayan Establishment.

Representations from some technical officers in the Malayan Public Works Service which were forwarded with C.A.M.P.U., letter of the 3rd of January, were discussed. It was thought that the representations were deserving of sympathy, in that the Malayan administrative time scale maximum was £1,000 a month, and that though, in actual fact, this did not apply to technical officers, the general intention and effect of the differentiation

===== Page 60 =====
The situation was somewhat similar to that in African territories, where technical officers had a promotion bar below the maximum, whereas for Administrative officers it was only an efficiency bar. In other words, posts between $800 and $1,000 are not really super-scale posts but upper time-scale posts. On these grounds, it seemed that the device of dividing the time scale by a promotion bar for technical officers and an efficiency bar for administrative officers had, since no promotions had been made in Malay since the occupation, resulted in technical officers being somewhat worse off than others, due to the loss of promotion prospects. It seemed that this difficulty might be overcome by allowing the time scale limit of $1,000 to apply to technical officers, but it would, at the same time, bring other problems, including the question of treatment, in promotion matters, of interned officers, and officers who had been released for service with the Forces. There was also the question of technical officers who had been promoted before the fall to posts carrying fixed salaries of more than $800, e.g., $850 in the Public Works Service, and also the position of Administrative Officers who might be held at the maximum of $1,000.

It was agreed that further consideration should be given to these matters, and that in this connection enquiries should be made as to the present position as regards the promotion of members of the Home Civil Service who are internees or Prisoners of War. In the meantime, the M.P.U. would examine these problems further.

PUBLIC SERVICE DIRECTIVE

With further reference to Watherston's letter reference ADM/232 of 8th January, 1945, I should like to make a few observations regarding the long-term policy of the Colonial Civil Service in Malaya.

2. When I suggested that a Public Service Directive should be issued to us I hoped that it would cover the whole field of the future of the Public Services in Malaya in accordance with the present views of the Secretary of State in so far as they may be settled, for I assume that it is intended after the war to introduce some entirely new ideas throughout all the Colonial dependencies. We know from past experience that reforms are extremely difficult to introduce in established territories but it seems to me that, in the case of Malaya, we are about to start with a clean slate. This, then, is a golden opportunity and, whatever the views of the Colonial Office may be with regard to the future, it seems that in Malaya we have a chance to experiment and to try out the new ideas untrammelled by local legislatures and vested interests of serving Colonial Officers.

3. I was talking generally on this subject with Sir Charles Jeffries a few days ago and was asking him what the latest ideas are in the Colonial Office with regard to the organisation of the post-war Colonial Civil Service. You will remember that, prior to the war, the tendency was to set up unified Colonial Services in the various technical spheres which were generally restricted to European officers appointed by the Secretary of State. In various dependencies, however, in course of time a number of local inhabitants had acquired sufficient education and experience to be suitable for appointment to the unified services, e.g., puisne judges and solicitors-general in West Africa and in the West Indies. Simultaneously with the unification of the European staff of the Colonies there was growing up in almost every dependency a local Civil Service. In the case of Malaya, the Government had already established a Malayan Administrative Service and, in the Straits Settlements, I understand there were the beginnings of a Malayan Legal Service; both of these were restricted to local Malays and Chinese. Rapidly, therefore, we were heading for the situation where the Public Service in Malaya was being sharply divided into the sheep and the goats. This, no doubt, was happening in other Colonial dependencies and it seems to me a situation which is bound to lead to trouble. As Sir Charles Jeffries remarked to me, the wartime crop of new constitutions in the various parts of the Empire is leading the Colonial Office radically to change its views about the constitution of the Public Service in Colonial dependencies. It seems now that instead of heading for unified Colonial Services in the various branches of Government activities, each Colonial dependency or group of dependencies must be encouraged to have its own Civil Service on a single basis with equal opportunities for the local people, if they have sufficient education and experience, to graduate through the various grades of the local Civil Service even to the highest posts. This postulates a local uniformity of salary for all races and the application of an expatriation allowance to those who are serving outside their country of domicile.

4. In the case of Malaya we shall, at the conclusion of the Military Period, be importing into the Service a large number of new entrants and I think it is essential that we should examine now, with the greatest possible care, the fundamental organisation of the Public Services in Malaya so that, when the time comes, we shall be able to fit the new entrants into the service according to a post-war scheme which has the approval of the Secretary of State. If we fail to do this, it means that we shall be offering the pre-war arrangements with a warning that, in the course of a year or two, the whole thing will be put into the melting-pot again. To the individual officers concerned this will be most unsatisfactory and the practical difficulties of a complete re-organisation at a later date will be greatly enhanced. Let us, therefore, act boldly in this matter and

G.S. Wodeman Esqre., C.M.G.

===== Page 64 =====
Draw up our scheme according to the best opinion and advice available in the Colonial Office and launch the new regime and see how it works.

5. To conclude, it was because I had ideas on these lines that I asked for a Public Service Directive and I am disappointed to see that the present draft does not really face up to the crucial problems which I had in mind. I do not know to what extent you have consulted Sir Charles Jeffries on this matter and I suggest that it may be useful if you were to have a talk with him on the subject. The matter is of some urgency because I feel we ought to get this thing thrashed out before I and my staff leave England. It is clearly a matter which will take a long time to ventilate and therefore I think we ought to start now.

6. There is also another question which I should like ventilated at this stage with regard to the organisation of the Public Service of the future. There has been some correspondence in "The Times" recently regarding the setting up in Colonial dependencies of a number of senior officers who might be regarded at a later stage of political development as Ministers - the theory being that each Colonial dependency should have three or four officers of the rank of Chief Secretary or thereabouts, each of whom would be an administrative officer in charge of certain spheres of activity. The correspondence in "The Times" started, I believe, with a letter from Captain Peter Macdonald and his suggestion was supported by a letter at a later date by Sir Bernard Bourdillon. I, too, am attracted by this idea. In the Sudan, for example, for a number of years they have had a Finance Secretary, a Civil Secretary and a Legal Secretary - all of equal status and addressing the Governor direct. The senior of the three at any one time is the one who acts as O.A.G. in the absence of the Governor-General. This is, in fact, Cabinet Government in embryo. I feel that in the circumstances of Malaya there is room for a re-organisation on this basis but it would, of course, require the approval of the Secretary of State and should be in accordance with general Colonial policy. I had hoped that the Public Service Directive might contain guidance on this important aspect of the future of the Public Service in Malaya and I should like to press for the inclusion of something on these lines. You are also very familiar with this type of organisation in the case of Ceylon.

7. The above matters are connected also with the question I have been discussing at great length within the Unit, namely, how existing Government officials are to be fitted into the Malayan Civil Establishment even whilst they are acting as Military Officers of the Military Administration. This is a very thorny problem and I attach a copy of a Minute which I addressed to my officers on this subject. I understand that the lines of this Minute appeal to most of my officers and that they are trying to work out something in accordance with my wishes. I do not want you particularly to examine my ideas and I only send you a copy of the Minute to illustrate how it links up with the broader questions to which I have referred above.

Yours sincerely,

Major-General.

G.S. Wodeman Esqre., O.M.G.,
The Colonial Office,
Whitehall,
LONDON, S.W.1.

===== Page 65 =====
I think this matter is one which we must settle among ourselves as quickly as possible. The root of the problem is really to provide civil emoluments for the members of the previous Malayan Administration and other Colonial Servants who are going to be used in Malaya in the future for the very considerable additional responsibilities that they are shouldering, first on the planning staff and ultimately on the ground. The acceptance of the principle of my dual responsibility (although not reflected downwards in the organisational sense) will, nevertheless, place a great extra burden of responsibility on the Heads and Deputy Heads of all the departments of the Military Administration. They will, unlike officers of other Military Administrations, be doing from the very first work for Malaya which is of vital importance to the Colonial Office and which, if well done, will give the Colonial Office such a flying start when it resumes control that its task and that of its future Governors will be easier. There is, therefore, the greatest weight of argument in favour of adequately remunerating the officers of the Military Government who are also members of the Colonial Civil Service; they are all taking up positions in the Military Administration of far greater responsibility than they have hitherto held in the Colonial Service or for which they are being paid as Colonial Civil Servants. Since our previous arrangement was framed with the Treasury there is no doubt that we have a much stronger case than we had at that time.

2. These remarks lead me to the conclusion that the problem must be looked at through the medium of the projected Military Administration Establishment and the comments of Lt. Col. Turnbull, in the final paragraph of his memorandum at 30, show that, in fact, the greatest chaos will arise when the Colonial Office resumes control unless the first Civil Establishment of the new Malayan Government is based on the Military Establishment for the Military Administration.

3. As I have indicated above, the real problem is to fix adequate civil salaries for the Heads and Deputy Heads of the various departments, for they are the people who will carry the great load of responsibility from the Colonial Office point of view. The more junior officers of the Military Administration who are Colonial Servants are, presumably, already on time scales which have been securing for them steadily increasing emoluments during the war period and, in the Military Administration, they should in my opinion remain on their time-scales because, generally, I do not think their responsibilities on the ground will be much in excess of their responsibilities in normal times. For officers who are now on a time-scale and are going to become Heads or Deputy Heads of departments, a fixed civil salary for their posts in the Military Government Administration should be arrived at, it being understood that the War Establishment will become the Civil Establishment overnight when the Colonial Office resumes control; subsequently, the Civil Government then constituted will amend and expand that Establishment in any way it wishes.

4. I am driven, therefore, to believe that the Colonial Office must frame a new Civil Establishment for Malaya which should be designated perhaps "Reconstruction Civil Establishment, Malaya". It will contain in civilian terms all the Heads of departments and the posts of the junior officers of the Military Administration plus estimated provision, as well as we can give it at the moment, of the additional Civilian Staff which will be infiltrated during the Military Period and for, say, at least six months after the resumption of Colonial Office control. Even during the Military Period the accretions of civil staff on this provisional Reconstruction Establishment can be varied by correspondence with the Colonial Office in the light of the circumstances arising during the period of the Military Administration and the prospects of recruitment, etc. For instance, we may provide six European schoolmistresses to arrive at D+4 months and, soon after we get to Malaya, notify the Colonial Office that we shall be ready for ten on that date. At any rate, the accretion of civilian staff can be dealt with progressively as the situation clarifies and the first draft of the Reconstruction Civil Establishment for Malaya need only for our immediate purposes cover the projected posts in the Military Administration. If those posts are filled by Colonial Service officers, they will get the civil salary attached to them from the date of embarkation or some other date to be fixed. If the posts are to be filled by military officers recruited under the A.G.I., we shall know what salary they will get the day after the Colonial Office resumes control.

===== Page 66 =====
5. This scheme has not yet considered the position of officers on the existing Malayan Establishment who are in internment or otherwise not immediately available for service with the Military Administration. I have come to the conclusion that Lt. Col. Turnbull was right. The old Malayan Establishment can never apply on the ground to Malaya again. It must be kept alive in order to hold the interned officers, etc. and under my scheme, therefore, there will be two Establishments, one gradually dying and the other being built up. The safeguard for the officers on the old Establishment who may eventually come to Malaya would be this: Every officer appointed to a post on the Reconstruction Establishment would be notified that the post given to him would be pensionable under the salary shown against the post if he retires or dies whilst holding that post. The officers so appointed must all be informed that, when interned officers are released and are passed fit for service in Malaya, they may, on their return to Malaya, supersede the officers in appointments held on the Reconstruction Establishment.

Take a concrete example. Colonel X is appointed Director of Medical Services (Peninsula) on the Reconstruction Establishment on 1st March, 1945; he will immediately draw the civil salary of that post, the difference between his Army pay and his Colonial Office pay being made up by the Colonial Office. Colonel X will, however, have been told that his appointment to the Reconstruction Establishment is subject to his being downgraded if the Colonial Office decides, when it resumes control, to appoint another officer to the post or to abolish the post and create a new post of Director of Medical Services (Peninsula & Singapore). Colonel X will be told, further, that if he is degraded when the officers concerned are released, he will be posted to other employment, either in Malaya or elsewhere, but that, whatever happens to him, he will certainly not get less than the Colonial Office salary to which he is entitled immediately prior to his posting to the Reconstruction Establishment. What would probably happen is that Colonel X would find that Dr. Y, now interned, recovers his health, comes out to Malaya and is appointed D.M.S. (Peninsula) and Colonel X, who is much junior in the Service to him in fact, will hand over and probably become his deputy on the salary fixed for the Deputy. I do not think that Colonel X could have any complaint with regard to this and, if he had, he could retire on pension with his salary calculated at the salary figure fixed for the D.M.S. (Peninsula). Certainly officers treated in this manner could have no complaint if it is fully explained to them before they are posted to the Reconstruction Establishment. If they do not like it, they will, of course, continue to draw the Colonial Office salary to which they are entitled on the existing pre-war Malayan Establishment so they can have it which way they like.

6. I shall be glad if you will discuss these proposals among yourselves and see if we cannot arrange a conference very soon.

(SGD) H.R. Hone.
Major-General, C.P.

1. There should be a single Public Services Commission for the Malayan Union and Singapore to be appointed by the Secretary of State. Its functions will be to advise the respective Governors in the exercise of their powers relating to appointments, promotions, transfer, dismissal and disciplinary control of Public Officers.

2. Save with the consent of the Governor, no person who is not a British subject or a Malayan Union citizen should be appointed to the Public Service in the Malayan Union or in Singapore.

3. All officers of the Public Services should be liable for service in the Malayan Union, in Singapore, or in Borneo.

4. Appointments (with the exception of judicial appointments) to the Public Services in the Malayan Union and in Singapore may be made locally by the respective Governors on the recommendation of the Public Services Commission after selection on lines similar to the selection system of the Colonial Service Appointments Board, subject to the right of the Secretary of State to select on the advice of the C.S.A.B. such candidates as he may at any time consider it advisable to appoint from outside Malaya. Such selection by the Secretary of State will be open to non-European candidates who are citizens of the Malayan Union provided that they are not resident in Malaya at the time of selection. Officers so selected by the Secretary of State will be liable for transfer anywhere in the Colonies at the Secretary of State's discretion.

Judicial appointments may be made by the respective Governors on the recommendation of a Standing Judicial Appointments Board to be convened by the Chief Justice.

===== Page 68 =====
5. Qualifications of candidates for appointment will be prescribed and published as approved by the Secretary of State.

6. All appointments will be subject to a three years' period of probation.

7. It should be a condition for confirmation in the higher grades of appointment that an officer shall be proficient in the English and Malay languages.

8. Courses of instruction or study, on selection or at any time during an officer's service, may be prescribed by the Governor on the advice of the Public Services Commission with the approval of the Secretary of State.

9. The posting of officers in Malaya will be a matter for local direction, and, in the case of senior appointments, will be subject to confirmation by the Secretary of State.

10. Leave, allowances and other conditions of service in the Malayan Union and in Singapore should be prescribed in Public Service Regulations made on the recommendation of the Public Services Commission with the approval of the Secretary of State.

11. For all officers wherever selected there should be common basic scales of salary, but officers whose permanent homes are not in Malaya will receive in addition to the basic Malayan scale, a pensionable expatriation allowance at rates approved by the Secretary of State.

12. Pending review by a Salaries Commission the basic scales of salary for new entrants should be equivalent to the pre-war scales, with the exception that in categories of appointments for which candidates were selected by the Secretary of State, the basic scales should be equivalent to the pre-war scales less 30 per cent.
