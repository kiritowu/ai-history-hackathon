The proposal to raise a loan for the purpose of a war contribution to His Majesty's Government has been canvassed recently in Malaya, as an alternative to income tax, particularly by the Straits Times, which has bitterly opposed Sir Shenton Thomas's advocacy of the latter. The idea of a loan has been supported by at least two leading unofficials (European) in the Federated Malay States - Mr. Ward-Jackson and Mr. Shearn (see press extracts in 21 on 50640/40).

Against the suggestion of a loan Sir Shenton argued in his broadcast about income tax (page 4 of enclosure in 8 on 50640/40) (1) that it involved no sacrifice and he could see no reason why the Government should pay 3% - or indeed anything - for money which they ought to get for nothing (to which it may surely be answered that the sacrifice will be made by the taxpayer who provides for the service of the loan) and (2) that it may be expected that there will again be a post-war slump and the Governments guaranteeing the loan would have to find large sums for interest and sinking fund at a time when they were already short of money for essential social services.

There is obvious force in Sir Shenton's second point (particularly as regards the Federated Malay States, which are more vulnerable to effects of the vicissitudes of rubber and tin than are the Straits Settlements); but the financial resources of the Federated Malay States are substantial and I think a liability of the order of $45,000,000 could be accepted with equanimity. Estimated revenue in the current year (apart from the proceeds of special war taxation) is $65,000,000 and the year was expected to begin with a Reserve Fund of $41,000,000 and a General Revenue Balance of $29,000,000. Public Debt amounts to $65,000,000 (round figure), none of the loans being repayable before 1956 at the earliest. Charges on account of this debt (including sinking fund) will amount to $3,617,574 in the current year.

I think it is most important in the case of a free contribution of this kind to let the local community of taxpayers have as much discretion as possible in deciding the method of raising the money. It is, however, essential to ensure that there is no diversion of resources from the provision of social services and this is particularly necessary in connection with a scheme involving continued payments for several years after the war. It can perhaps best be done by specifically earmarking for the service of the loan the proceeds of special taxation imposed for the purpose, it being understood that if in any future year the yield of this special taxation is insufficient, the deficiency will not be made good at the expense of other services but, if required, additional special taxation will be raised.

===== Page 4 =====
It would be also desirable to ensure that the amortisation of the loan was spread over a fairly short period. It is, no doubt true, as Mr. Shearn says, that this war is being waged not only for the existing generation but also for posterity; but posterity will have its own burdens and, in any case, there is clearly no reluctance on the part of the present generation of taxpayers in Malaya to accept a substantial liability. If there is provision for amortisation in, say, 15 years, there need not be criticism on this score.

In view of Sir Shenton Thomas's strenuous advocacy of income tax and the violent and often offensive manner in which the Straits Times has argued against that and in favour of a loan, the adoption of the latter income tax being dropped, would perhaps be something of a personal rebuff for the Governor; but I feel sure that he would not wish this to weigh with the Secretary of State.

Reply that the Secretary of State would be prepared to accept the suggestion of a loan if it had substantial unofficial support, provided that arrangements were made (as above) to provide for the service of the loan from the proceeds of special taxation and that its amortisation was secured within a reasonably short period (say, not exceeding 15 years).

1. I agree with "A" of Mr. Martin's minute, as the governing factor here. We wish to avoid acute controversy in war time; we wish to get substantial aid from a country which can very well afford to give it; and, if Malaya is prepared to give, readily and willingly, a loan of £5 million or so, I should be inclined to let most other considerations go, and take it.

2. As between effective yields, of a 45 million dollar loan, and income tax, there do not seem to be accurate data available in the papers here. It obviously depends on the length of the war. But such data as I can find give an average yield, from income tax in the 1917-1922 period, of say 4½ million dollars a year. That would now, undoubtedly, be largely exceeded. Sir Shenton talks of a minimum yield, now, from the Colony alone, of 10 million dollars, in the first year.

It rather looks as if, on a war-duration of three years, there isn't perhaps going to be much difference in terms of money between a 45 million dollar loan, and income tax? I cannot say, on the information available; but that seems a reasonable probability.

3. I think we all agree that, in this matter, the Govr. is undoubtedly right. But that does not get us much further, if paragraph 1 above be accepted.

4. I do not like the ear-marking of specific revenues for specific purposes. That involves all sorts of difficulties; it has usually proved inadvisable in practice; and it is heretical from the point of view of sound normal finance. But the circumstances here seem to me abnormal; and I would, in the reply, put forward the suggestion Mr. Martin has made. I would do so with some saving clause—"in the exceptional circumstances of this case", or something of the kind, to save the point taken above.

5. I should also like it made clear that, if it be finally decided in Malaya to make their contribution on a loan basis, this must not be taken as excluding, definitely, the future imposition of an income tax. We must, I think, save that point, in the most clear manner. It is an absurd anomaly that there is no income tax in Malaya;—by far the richest, and the most advanced, of the territories under the C.O. As the Govr. says, all the countries round Malaya have it; many Colonies, far less wealthy and much less advanced, and provided with less ample and efficient services, have it. Broadly, it works, reasonably well. Malaya must have it, some time or other; and we should be careful to make it quite clear that there is no undertaking that income tax will not be introduced in Malaya. If this loan arrangement has, for political and "war" reasons, to be accepted now as a definite alternative to the introduction of income tax, for the war period, well and good; but there must be an absolutely free hand as regards the after-war period, unhampered by any suggestion of quasi-promise now.

An Enactment to provide for the raising of a loan the proceeds of which are to be presented to the Government of the United Kingdom as a contribution from the Federated Malay States towards the prosecution of the present war.

S. W. JONES,
[26th August, 1940.]

President of the Federal Council.

It is hereby enacted by the Rulers of the Federated Malay States by and with the advice and consent of the Federal Council as follows:

1. This Enactment may be cited as the War Loan Enactment 1940.

2. The High Commissioner may raise a sum not exceeding twenty million dollars by means of a loan issued to the public.

3. (i) The loan shall be known as "The Federated Malay States War Loan (1940)" and is hereinafter in this Enactment referred to as "the War Loan".

(ii) The War Loan shall be issued at par and shall bear interest at three per centum per annum payable twice in each year on such dates (the second of which shall be a date six calendar months after the first) as the High Commissioner may by notification in the Gazette direct.

(iii) The War Loan shall be issued in the form of bearer bonds and in such units or denominations as the High Commissioner may from time to time by notification in the Gazette determine.

(iv) The issue and management of the War Loan may be entrusted on such terms as the High Commissioner may direct to such Bank or Banks as the High Commissioner may appoint.

4. The whole of the sum raised by means of the War Loan shall be presented to the Government of the United Kingdom as a contribution from the Federated Malay States towards the prosecution of the present war.

5. The principal monies borrowed by means of the War Loan all interest thereon and all such sums as are necessary to provide for the expenses of the flotation and of the management of the War Loan are hereby charged upon and shall be payable out of the revenue and assets of the Federated Malay States.

===== Page 20 =====
6. In each year after 1940, the High Commissioner shall appropriate out of the revenue and assets of the Federated Malay States such sum as he may consider necessary, not being less than two per centum of the total amount of the War Loan issued (including any which may have been redeemed). Such sum shall be paid into a sinking fund: Provided that if at any time the High Commissioner is satisfied that the value of the sinking fund, with any interest accruing thereto but without further appropriations, will be sufficient to meet all payments payable out of such sinking fund, he may, with the approval of the Federal Council, suspend further appropriations towards the sinking fund until such time as, with like approval, he may direct that such appropriations shall again commence.

7. (i) The sinking fund shall be applied in the first instance to the payment of all expenses of or incidental to the redemption of the War Loan and then in repayment of the principal monies represented by the War Loan.

(ii) If the sinking fund is insufficient for the payment of the principal monies at the time such payment is due, the High Commissioner shall make good the deficiency out of the revenue and assets of the Federated Malay States.

8. Monies appropriated for the sinking fund, if not required for the purposes of the preceding section, shall be invested in a manner approved by the High Commissioner, and the dividends, interest, or produce of such investment shall be similarly invested.

9. The War Loan shall be redeemable at a date not later than September 30, 1959, and not earlier than October 1, 1952, as the Federal Council shall at any time decide by Resolution (which Resolution shall be published in the Gazette).

10. All interest paid or payable in respect of the War Loan shall be exempt from all charges, duties, or taxes (except duties imposed under the Estate Duty Enactment or any substitute Enactment) imposed by any written law in force on the date this Enactment comes into force or thereafter.

11. The High Commissioner may, by Regulations not inconsistent with this Enactment, provide for any matter necessary to make effective the purpose of this Enactment.

Passed this 20th day of August, 1940.

M. C. ff SHEPPARD,
Clerk of Council.

An Ordinance to authorize the Governor to borrow money for the purpose of making a gift thereof to His Majesty’s Government in the United Kingdom for the prosecution of the war; and to provide for the issue of War Savings Certificates for this purpose and for other matters incidental thereto.

[30th August, 1940].

It is hereby enacted by the Governor of the Straits Settlements with the advice and consent of the Legislative Council thereof as follows:

1. This Ordinance may be cited as the War Savings Certificates Ordinance, 1940.

2. In this Ordinance, unless the context otherwise requires, the following expressions have the meanings assigned to them respectively, that is to say:

“Auditor” means the Auditor of the Straits Settlements and Federated Malay States;

“certificate” means a certificate issued under the name of a war savings certificate by the Government through the Post Office, for the purpose of raising money authorized to be raised by this Ordinance;

“committee” in relation to a person of unsound mind, includes a person authorized under section 9 of the Mental Disorders Ordinance to whom the charge of the estate is committed;

===== Page 25 =====
"Director-General" means the Director-General of Posts and Telegraphs, Straits Settlements and Federated Malay States;

"Financial Secretary" means the Financial Secretary of the Straits Settlements;

"guardian" means in the absence of a person declared or appointed to be a guardian by or under the Guardianship of Infants Ordinance, or any other law, or appointed by deed any adult person with whom the minor is residing and by whom he is being maintained;

"minor" means a person who has not attained the age of eighteen years;

"multiple certificate" means a certificate representing any number of unit certificates as provided in subsection (4) of section 14, and entitling the holder to receive the aggregate amount of the sums repayable in respect of that number of unit certificates;

"society" means a registered or exempted society as defined by section 2 of the Societies Ordinance and a Co-operative Society registered in the Colony or any Malay State;

"unit certificate" means a certificate of unit value as provided in subsection (1) of section 14.

3. The Governor may, at any time after the 31st day of August, 1940, borrow in accordance with the provisions of this Ordinance such sums not exceeding in all ten million dollars as he thinks proper to raise for the purpose of making a gift of the said sums to His Majesty’s Government in the United Kingdom for the prosecution of the war in which His Majesty is at present engaged.

4. Any sums which may be raised under this Ordinance shall be raised by the issue through the Post Office of war savings certificates, and any certificate so issued shall bear such rate of interest and shall be subject to such conditions as to repayment or otherwise as are contained in this Ordinance and in the regulations issued thereunder.

5.—(1) The principal of and interest on any war savings certificate issued under this Ordinance are hereby charged upon and shall be payable out of the general revenues and assets of the Colony, and, in order

===== Page 26 =====
To facilitate the repayment of such principal and the payment of such interest, a sinking fund shall be formed in the manner set out in the following subsection.

(2) So long as any war savings certificate remains to be repaid, the Governor shall, not later than the first day of September, 1941, and not later than the same day in each succeeding year, appropriate out of the general revenues and assets of the Colony a sum equal to fifty cents for each unit certificate issued and not repaid, and in addition a sum equal to one-tenth of the total purchase price of all certificates issued and not repaid on that date, and shall place these sums to the credit of a War Savings Certificates Sinking Fund.

6. The War Savings Certificates Sinking Fund shall be applied in the first place in payment of all expenses of, or incidental to, the issue and repayment of the war savings certificates, and in the next place, and subject to the aforesaid payments, in repayment of the principal and payment of the interest due on the certificates.

7. FINANCIAL SECRETARY'S OFFICE, SINGAPORE, 6th June, 1940.

We were appointed a Committee on the 29th of May by His Excellency the Officer Administering the Government with the following terms of reference:

(1) To recommend whether the Straits Settlements Government should raise a loan the proceeds of which would be presented to the Imperial Treasury as a contribution towards the prosecution of the war; and if so, to suggest—
A.—(i) The amount.
(ii) Price of issue and redemption.
(iii) Rate of Interest.
(iv) Period.
(v) Denominations of bonds or certificates.
B.—What measures should be taken to guarantee the means of servicing the loan.

(2) To make any general recommendations or observations which the Committee think desirable in connection with the foregoing items.

The following are our recommendations and observations on the terms of reference:

RECOMMENDATIONS

(1) We recommend that for the purpose of providing our contribution to the war effort the Colony should float a loan as soon as possible, either separately, or preferably in conjunction with the other Malayan Governments. This should be the first of a series of loans to be floated for the same purpose.

A.—(i) The amount of the first Colony loan should be $25,000,000. If a Joint loan is floated, we estimate that subscriptions from the Colony would amount to at least $20,000,000.

(ii) Price of issue and redemption—Par.

(iii) Interest 3 per cent.

(iv) Period 19 years or less at the option of Government. The date of redemption should be 1959 or such earlier date not before 1952 as Government may decide, subject to six months' notice.

(v) The loan should consist partly of inscribed stock and partly of bearer bonds or certificates at the subscribers' option of the following denominations: $10,000, $5,000, $1,000, $500 and $100.

We suggest that 10 per cent be paid on application and the remaining 90 per cent within one month.

===== Page 47 =====
Banks might be asked whether they would be prepared to advance money at 3 per cent interest to approved applicants.

Interest to be payable half-yearly.

B.—For the purpose of servicing the loan and for other purposes directly connected with our contributions to the war effort we recommend a graduated tax on incomes. The details of a scheme for income tax can be left to a Committee, who would no doubt consider such matters as the taxable minimum, concessions in respect of wives, children and dependents, and avoidance of double taxation, and particularly the position of sterling companies which are liable for income tax in the United Kingdom.

We recommend that if income tax is introduced certain of the new war taxes recently imposed or under consideration should be withdrawn, and that other taxes should also be reviewed.

(2) We recommend that other Malayan Governments should be invited to participate in a united effort to provide our maximum contribution by means of joint loans to be given to the Imperial Government and such periodical gifts as we can afford, to be financed by uniform methods of taxation.

OBSERVATIONS

2. In considering the terms of reference we agreed that the issues now before us concern the whole of Malaya rather than the Colony alone. For, while the Colony can finance loans for the purpose of making contributions to the Imperial Government, there is not the slightest doubt that only co-operation between all administrations in Malaya will produce sums at all commensurate with the paying capacity of the country. We consider it desirable that loans for this purpose should be Malayan, otherwise the whole cost of servicing is thrown on the administration which floats the loan. If separate administrations were to float simultaneous loans the bulk of the subscriptions would go to that administration which affords the best security and which would therefore have to bear a disproportionate share of the burden. Further, the floating of separate loans financed by different methods of taxation would not only give rise to difficulties which should be avoided, but would also leave the complex problem of apportioning the local expenditure on war measures, for which no suitable basis has yet been discovered, still unsolved.

3. It appears to us desirable that the sums which are collected specifically for and spent solely on war purposes should, if possible, be kept separate and distinct from the normal revenue and expenditure of the administrations concerned.

4. We propose therefore first to discuss measures which appear desirable if the various governments agree to united action. Next we shall consider measures which can be adopted if such co-operation is not forthcoming.

5. The ideal method.—The ideal method in our opinion would be to create a pool specifically designed for war purposes, i.e. Malaya's joint

===== Page 48 =====
Contribution to the war effort, to be financed from the proceeds of a graduated income tax uniformly introduced throughout Malaya. All expenses of collecting the tax, and all expenditure on civil establishments directly attributable to war measures, e.g., censorship, control of shipping, imports and exports, and foreign exchange, food supplies, etc., would be a first charge on the pool. The remainder would be used to service Malayan war loans and secondly to be remitted as further war contributions.

6. Such a pool can be closed whenever it is decided that the specific purpose for which it is created has been fulfilled.

The advantages of this method are—

(a) There would be no controversy as to how the servicing of a joint loan is to be borne;

(b) Each administration would be giving its utmost to the war effort according to its capacity;

(c) The smaller administrations who can afford to contribute little towards servicing a joint loan would at any rate be giving their utmost and would jointly share in the credit;

(d) Those who can afford to pay will contribute according to their means wherever they may reside;

(e) No discussion would arise as to how the local war expenditure chargeable to Malaya is to be borne.

7. This scheme would of course not preclude any Government from making such separate gifts from its own funds as its circumstances may permit.

8. In putting forward this proposal for united action in this matter we have fully appreciated the desire of the various administrations in Malaya to be regarded as separate entities. We are however confident that they will not fail to respond to the clear call which the present emergency makes for prompt and united effort from all parts of the Empire.

9. The component parts of the Malaya Peninsula will realize that in this war the problem of defending one is the problem of defending all, and that they are one and all vitally concerned in the issues now at stake. All the Malayan Governments have therefore a common interest in making the maximum contributions consistent with their circumstances, and there should therefore, if possible, be uniformity in the methods by which these contributions are acquired. It is recognised that some forms of taxation may not be suitable for every administration, but where it is possible to apply the same form of taxation throughout for the same purpose, it is desirable that it should be adopted. In particular, this would apply to an income tax for war purposes, the burden of which would be borne in equal measure by people of equal means and equal commitments in whatever part of Malaya they may reside.

10. Loans.—We have recommended the raising of a loan or a series of loans because they will enable us to acquire and remit, in advance of taxation, a great portion of the funds which are awaiting investment and would not otherwise become available for use by the Imperial Government. The strength of the loan market during recent months suggests that considerable sums are available for gilt-edged securities, a position which is no doubt partly due to the restrictions on new capital issues under the Defence Finance Regulations. Loans would open new avenues for investment and in some measure tend to check extravagant expenditure from balances at present lying idle. We believe that the patriotic appeal will result in large subscriptions from people who in the ordinary way would

===== Page 49 =====
not be attracted by a 3 per cent investment. Loans have the further advantage of ensuring that the incidence of our war effort is spread over a number of years.

11. We do not consider it essential that the fullest possible amount of a loan should be raised at once, but it is essential that the first flotation should be a success. Hence we recommend proceeding by means of a series of loans to be floated at different periods as circumstances permit. We estimate that, if a joint loan were floated as the first of a series, subscriptions from the Colony would be sufficient to take up at least $20,000,000. The amount of such joint loan would depend on the estimate of subscriptions from other administrations. If a separate Colony loan is floated we would suggest an amount of $25,000,000. A period of 19 years is recommended because this appears to follow home practice and the period suggested gives a round figure of 7 per cent for interest and redemption. But we consider that it should be left open to Government to redeem the loan at an earlier date, not before 1952, at its discretion, subject to six months' notice. We suggest this in order that the date of redemption in the case of a joint loan may not clash with other loans, and that it can be redeemed at an earlier date if the fund formed for the purpose is found to be sufficient.

12. It is suggested that one of the loans to be floated might be a short-term loan of $10,000,000 redeemable in, say, five years, with interest at 2 per cent.

13. It is desirable that the loans should be made attractive to small as well as large investors, and for this reason we have suggested making the basis of appeal as wide as possible. We consider that a system of War Savings Certificates or of Loan Certificates bought through the Post Office Savings Bank should form a part of the general loan scheme. We attach at Appendix A a scheme which has been submitted by the Chief Accountant of the Posts and Telegraphs Department on the subject of Loan Certificates.

Appendix B gives the scheme for War Saving Certificates.

14. Servicing of Loans.—Although we recommend a loan as the best means of securing a large sum quickly, we must emphasize that it should not be used merely to absorb the war taxation already agreed upon, or to cloak the fact that there are vast sources of wealth in this country which are at present immune from taxation. Nor can a loan be serviced from reserves or surpluses which are required to protect our revenue and as capital for reconstruction after the war. To raid our reserves for servicing loans would be unsound finance and a deterrent to prospective investors, since it would diminish the security upon which loans are raised. Further, as our object is to raise the maximum contribution and a series of loans is suggested, it is clearly better to deal with this question as a whole than to leave each separate loan to be serviced by separate forms of taxation introduced and sufficient solely for that specific purpose.

15. War Taxation.—We have therefore given the very closest consideration to the general question of war taxation and to the particular problem of taxation for the servicing of loans. Experience has already proved that some of the taxes which have been introduced or are under consideration for war purposes are either very disappointing in yield or almost impossible to collect. Scarcely one has escaped criticism or failed to produce difficulties and complaints. Even as temporary expedients pending investigation of more suitable forms of taxation they are unsatisfactory. As a means of financing our maximum contribution to the war they are wholly inadequate, for it is important to remember that

===== Page 50 =====
None of the indirect taxes hitherto introduced impose even a moderate burden on the wealthy. Indirect taxes are regressive in operation, the normal incidence being proportionately heavier on the poor than the rich. We know of no system of graduating taxes on commodities, whether they are necessities or luxuries, which can adjust the burden to capacity to pay.

16. For the purpose of war taxation and especially for the purpose of creating a war pool such as that suggested in paragraph 5 above, a single tax of adequate yield is clearly preferable to a multiplicity of taxes of small or moderate yield which cause much difficulty and irritation. We have already experienced this to our cost. It is our belief that if we are to make our maximum contribution it can only be satisfactorily obtained by methods of taxation which are uniform throughout the country, and while it is undoubtedly the case that considerable sums can be raised by various indirect means, only one channel will produce the maximum contribution of which we are capable, namely a tax upon incomes. We are well aware of, and agree with some of the views expressed regarding the objections to such a tax in normal times, e.g., the uneven incidence as between different races, evasion, opportunities for corruption, etc., but these must in our view give way to the surpassing need for the country to produce, in the very interests of self-preservation, the largest sums possible.

17. We consider it necessary to emphasize our opinion that to introduce Income Tax in one part of Malaya and not in others would seriously accentuate the undesirable features of the tax.

18. Our recommendations provide for an income tax which is to be introduced for the specific purpose of securing our maximum contribution to the war, and which can be discontinued when that purpose is achieved. The machinery should function with firmness and thoroughness. So much is due to that section of the community which cannot avoid payment even if it would.

It is essential to realize that the stake of all residents in Malaya in the outcome of the war is a common one; it is essential also to recognize the necessities of a war-time economy. For these reasons it is hoped that prompt co-operation will be forthcoming. The events of the last few weeks have made it clear that every possible resource of the Empire will be necessary to effect the defeat of Germany. We are confident that in this hour of need Malaya will respond to the call for a national unity of purpose and will not allow local differences or prejudices to prevent her from worthily playing her part.

ALTERNATIVE TAXATION

19. We propose now to discuss certain forms of taxation which could be introduced if co-operation from the other Governments in regard to income tax is not forthcoming, and it is not considered desirable to introduce income tax in the Straits Settlements alone. It is realized that income tax in the Colony alone would give rise to many administrative and other difficulties. Even if all the taxes which we mention below were imposed they would still leave sources of considerable wealth untouched, and the incidence of the burden cannot be made equitable.

Petrol.—It is estimated that if the duty were increased by five cents from 35 to 40 cents per gallon the additional tax would yield approximately $500,000. If the tax were increased by as much as 15 cents it would probably have a considerable effect on consumption and the additional yield would only be about $1,000,000. Possibly an additional 10 cents per gallon would yield nearly the same amount.

===== Page 51 =====
Kerosene. — An additional one cent per gallon would yield about $25,000.

Liquors.
Tobacco. Import and Excise.

In our opinion the taxes could be still further increased. THE ACTING COLONIAL SECRETARY: Sir, I move that the Council do now adjourn until April 22nd.

THE FINANCIAL SECRETARY: Sir, it has been repeatedly suggested in the Press that a local loan should be raised as a gift to the Imperial Government. The service of such a loan would be undertaken by the Governments by means of taxation other than an income tax. In other words, the loan is offered as an alternative to a war tax on incomes.

===== Page 86 =====
The most recent suggestion is for a Malayan loan of one hundred million dollars to be repaid in 12 years so that it will not bear too hardly on posterity. The loan would be borne by the administrations in what are known as the "Blackett Ratios", that is to say, the Federated Malay States and the Colony would each be responsible for 37 per cent, and the Unfederated Malay States for 26 per cent.

In order to service its share of such a loan with interest at three per cent, the Colony would have to find about $3,700,000 a year.

There has also been a suggestion for a Federated Malay States loan of one hundred million dollars as a substitute for war tax on incomes. The argument used, as I have understood it, is that one hundred million dollars at three per cent could easily be raised in the Federated Malay States and such a contribution to the war would be of far greater value than the yield from a Federated Malay States income tax which would probably not exceed $64,000,000 over a period of five years.

Now in the first place I have made enquiries on the subject and as a result I doubt very much whether a local loan would attract anything like one hundred million dollars. In the second place the suggestion that the Federated Malay States alone can raise one hundred million dollars appears to me to ignore the realities of the situation. Does anyone really imagine that the money could and would be found within the boundaries of the Federated Malay States? If, on the other hand, the sponsor of this scheme relies upon support from other parts of the country, is it a fair argument to balance the proceeds of such a loan against the yield from a purely Federated Malay States income tax? Surely it would be more logical to balance the loan against the prospective yield from a Malayan Income Tax, and that would be a far different figure from $64,000,000. Furthermore, the Federated Malay States might not be alone in the field. Supposing each one of the Malayan Administration agreed to raise or participate in a loan, is it conceivable that the share of the Federated Malay States alone would amount to as much as one hundred million dollars?

Now, Sir, assuming that a Malayan Loan is immediately resorted to. As I have already said I doubt very much whether the anticipations of its advocates would be realised, but I would not say for one moment that the suggestion of a loan should be rejected solely on that account. I would, however, most emphatically advise against any loan which was to be regarded as a substitute for a war tax on incomes.

I have already mentioned that the service of the Colony's share of a Malayan loan of one hundred million dollars bearing interest at 3 per cent, repayable in 12 years would cost the Colony $3,700,000 a year. That is an unduly high figure because it assumes that so large a sum as one hundred million dollars could be raised, and it allows a very short currency for the loan, but let it stand. Now if any person who has the interest of the Empire at heart considers that a payment of $3,700,000 a year is a fair contribution by this Colony towards the cost of the war he must possess a distorted notion of the resources of the people of this country and a very poor understanding of what we have at stake in this war. (Applause).

A loan has indeed the merit of yielding a comparatively large sum of money in a short space of time and for this reason I would not oppose a loan provided that it was not intended to take the place of any war taxation which this country was able to bear.

===== Page 87 =====
People here have pointed to the 300,000,000 loan which has just been floated in Great Britain and have asked, if a loan is good enough for the home country why is it not good enough for Malaya? The answer in my opinion is simple. The loan in question is complementary to the revenue derived from the already heavy taxation in Great Britain, and if the people of this country want a loan in addition to war taxation, or in other words, if a loan is demanded as an immediate contribution to the Imperial Government while ways and means of raising war taxes apart from those required to service the loan are being considered, then I can see no objection to such a loan.

I would like, Sir, to add one or two general remarks on the relative merits of loans and taxes in war time. An administration such as this Colony has its system of peace-time taxation which is so graded and distributed as to prove satisfactory for ordinary purposes. We have a shrewd idea from year to year of what we are likely to derive in revenue and we devise our expenditure accordingly. The result has been that with a comparatively light system of taxation which does not include an income tax, a feature of the fiscal system of almost every civilised country in the world, the Colony has been able to build up in years of peace a sound financial position.

But the Empire is now at war, a war which entails expenditure on an astronomical scale, a war in which this country is vitally concerned. The problem which at once faces the people and the Government of the Colony is: can money be raised locally to assist the home country in the prosecution of this war, and if so, to what amount and in what manner? From all that has been said in the Press and elsewhere there can be no doubt that large sums of money are available here for the purpose, and it is to the credit of all the communities which make up the population of this country that they proclaim in one voice their eagerness to contribute.

The question which therefore remains is: how should the burden of this giving be distributed? And in seeking the answer it should be remembered that there is a distinction to be drawn between an ordinary tax system and a war tax system.

In order to make the implications of this distinction clear I can do no better than quote the words of Professor Pigou, one of the leading economists of our time:

"War charges ought to be thrown on the rich in greater proportion than the principles appropriate to peace taxation would warrant. In a pre-eminent national emergency the call from each should be for his utmost rather than for his share. Men are required to give of their physical strength not in equal proportions but from each his all. There is no question of proportionate sacrifice between men of fuller and emptier lives. Indeed, the strong are taken and the weak rejected. It is difficult to see what ground of equity there could be for any different distribution in the summons to financial strength; but this is certainly not the distribution arrived at in the ordinary tax system and, therefore, some departure from the principles that underlie that system seems to be called for. This suggests that from the standpoint of a sound distribution a great war ought not to be financed predominantly by loans interest on which will afterwards be provided out of ordinary taxation. Rather a large proportion of the costs should be met by taxation levied at the time on principles calculated to throw a much greater portion of the burden on the rich than they are accustomed to sustain under the ordinary forms of taxation."

===== Page 88 =====
This statement appears to me, Sir, to be particularly applicable to this country. So long as there is a reserve of taxable capacity, and there can be no doubt that there is such a reserve in this country, some of it indeed still in a virgin state, there is in my opinion no justification whatsoever for a loan to take the place of taxation which in the existing circumstances ought to be so distributed as to fall with abnormal weight on the wealthy. Such a loan simply means this: those who can afford subscribe partly, shall we say, to secure a safe investment for their surplus funds and partly from patriotic motives. The whole country is then called upon to provide the interest and the repayment of the loan. In other words, the rich man has found a safe and not unremunerative repository for his savings and at the same time has avoided his real obligations to the state.

It has been said that taxation is that part of a man’s income which he gives up in order to ensure the remainder, and I can think of no time in our history when this definition was more apt than it is to-day. (Applause).

The value of Your Excellency’s efforts to further the interests of this Colony and the success with which they have met are well understood and fully appreciated.
