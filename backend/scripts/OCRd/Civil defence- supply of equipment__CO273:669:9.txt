I was rung up yesterday by Mr. Whitley of the Ministry of Home Security regarding the Straits Settlements demand for babies' helmets and children's size gas masks in No. 10 on this file. They are asked for 10,500 babies' helmets, but Mr. Whitley tells me that the essential reserves in this country are very low, and the most which they could spare would be somewhere in the neighbourhood of 100, which their "experts" stated they would only release for the use of the European or Eurasian population, as they apparently regarded the working of these helmets as too complicated for any other mind. I told Mr. Whitley that we could not accept such a diagnosis, but since the number was so small, and they were reluctant to release any at all, it did not seem worthwhile taking so few. As regards the children's size of masks for which the Straits asked for 44,000, the Ministry of Home Security have only 1,000 to spare. Mr. Crook of the Crown Agents tells me that most of the small, medium and large masks on order are ready for shipment, but that Crown Agents have not yet been notified of a sailing by the Ministry of War Transport. We might telegraph to the Governor as in draft herewith.

The position regarding the Singapore gas masks is as follows:

7,400 large, 258,000 medium and 120,000 small, with 229,500 cardboard containers are already loaded on two transports at Birkenhead. 50,000 small, 150,000 childrens and 130,000 cartons are in process of being loaded onto another transport at Barry (South Wales). It is understood that most of the contents of these transports are destined for Singapore and that it is intended that they should sail in spite of the present position. It would be impossible at this late stage to have the respirators from the Birkenhead transports removed, but it might be possible for those on the Barry transport to be taken off.

It seemed at first desirable that we should consider having these diverted to Ceylon, but I understand that there is practically no chance of the transports calling at Colombo. They will be sent to General Wavell's area, and if Singapore has fallen it will be for him to decide where the goods should be sent. The matter was considered at yesterday's meeting of the Civil Defence Committee on the point whether it was worthwhile having the respirators unloaded from the Barry transport and loaded onto a Ceylon boat. It was felt, however, that as there would probably be some delay in transferring them to a Ceylon boat, and as there would be plenty of use for the masks in, say, the Dutch East Indies or Australia, it would be best to let them all proceed.

C.A. have since come up to imagine that, after the breakdown of 30,000 small respirators which are ready for loading at Birkenhead, should be put on board. After speaking to Mr. Hile (Home Security) I said "Yes." They will go out labelled "Singapore" and will then get out into General Wavell's area by the earliest opportunity. I am sure that is the right course.

a) 365,000 respirators, worth 760,000-765,000, were ordered by the Government of S.S. but diverted to India. Their cost continued to be borne by the Ministry of Home Security, which is paying the necessary storage fees.

b) A small portion of the above were disposed of in Aden, Union of South Arabia, to the RAF in Iraq, Aden. The Government or Service concerned is being charged by the Ministry of Home Security with cost plus freight from India to the destination.

c) 80,000 of the above were loaned to the Government of India, and their cost is being borne by the Ministry of Home Security.

The question of freight charges from Britain to India now arises, though the actual amount has not yet been fixed. As the shipment would not have taken place if S.S. had not ordered the masks, it appears proper to regard the freight from M.K. to India for the whole consignment as a charge on Malayan funds, with the exception of (b) and (c), which ought properly to be recovered from the Government or Service concerned.

In the case of (b), however, the amount is reported to be negligible and not worth recovering. It might, therefore, be paid from Malayan funds.

In the case of (c), the C.A. should approach the D.S. for a refund of freight charges if the Government of India wishes to keep the respirators. Meanwhile, the charges should be paid from Malayan funds.

There was an unexplained loss of some masks or accessories after landing in India, possibly due to theft, which were not covered by the insurance policy. Alternative suggestions by the Ministry of Home Security for meeting freight charges are:

1) Accept as a charge on Malayan funds, or
2) Ask Treasury that the loss should be borne by the Exchequer. This would be done by the Ministry of Home Security if any loss were to occur in connection with (a).

As the amount involved would appear to be small (vide 2° end. to 22), I suggest alternative 1).

I have the honour to refer to telegram No. 98, Government House, dated 25th January 1942, addressed by the Governor of the Straits Settlements to the Secretary of State for the Colonies, a copy of which was passed to us under Colonial Office reference 50746/1/42, and to state that in accordance with telephonic instructions received from the Colonial Office on 16th January 1942, the 500,000 respirators detailed therein were ordered by demand on the Ministry of Home Security.

2. At the time of the fall of Singapore, some of the respirators had already been shipped and others were in the course of shipment. In view of the possibility of the respirators being required elsewhere, it was arranged by telephone that these consignments totalling 365,000 respirators, to the value of between £60,000 and £65,000, should be allowed to go forward. Eventually, in consequence of the Far East situation, the boats in which shipment was effected were diverted to India where the respirators were later taken into Store. Since their arrival, the Ministry of Home Security has disposed of a small number of the respirators to the Governments of Aden and the Union of South Africa and to the R.A.F. for use at their establishments in Iraq, Aden, Kenya and Sudan. Also, 80,000 of the civilian type were issued to the Indian Government on loan. Up to the present, the only payments made by us have been in respect of insurance and incidental shipping charges amounting to the total sum of approximately £5,400. Freight charges are as yet unknown but we understand they are now under discussion by a committee appointed by the War Office for the purpose and it is anticipated that these charges will be available shortly.

The respirators were actually ordered by us and the Ministry of Home Security proposes to accept liability in respect of the cost of the respirators which actually reached India, but suggests that the freightage on the whole consignment from Britain to India should remain the liability of the Government of the Straits Settlements. Dear Crook,

With reference to our discussion regarding the various questions of cost outstanding in connection with the consignment of respirators which was prepared for shipment to Singapore and was diverted to India, I am writing to let you know what we propose, and should be glad to learn whether your Department concurs.

The cost of the respirators which reached India continues to be borne by the Ministry of Home Security and the storage of these respirators which has been arranged by the Government of India, is charged to H.M.G. through the Defence Expenditure Plan.

The exception to the above paragraph is a quantity of respirators which have been sold to Aden, the Union of South Africa and the R.A.F. for use at their establishments in Iraq, Aden, Kenya and Sudan. The Government or Service concerned is being charged with the cost of these respirators and their freight from India to the country of destination.

It remains to be decided who shall pay the freight of the whole consignment of respirators from Britain to India. You told me that a War Office Committee are examining the manifests of the vessels which were diverted in this way and will eventually arrive at a figure to be charged to the various Government Departments (other than the War Office) on whose behalf goods were carried on these transports. The War Office will, therefore, eventually present an account to you as representatives of the Straits Government and I would suggest that the charge should remain a liability of the Straits Government. As you know it was not possible, between the time of the fall of Singapore and the sailing of the transports, to unload the respirators. We should not have sent the respirators to India unless they had already been loaded for Singapore. I would, however, suggest that the Straits Government might recover from the Government of India a proportion of this freight charge, in respect of 80,000 civilian respirators taken from the consignment by the Government of India for its own use pending the arrival of civilian duty respirators. Assuming that the Government of India is not able to collect these civilian respirators and return them to store when the civilian duty respirators are available for issue, it seems right that the cost of the freight of the 80,000 civilian respirators should be borne accordingly.

Dear Thomas,

With reference to previous correspondence regarding the supply of respirators for Singapore, we are now making arrangements for the dispatch of 180,000 small size, 258,100 medium, and 7,400 large size. Owing to our stock distribution, we cannot supply more than 180,000 small size in place of the 223,500 asked for. We have accordingly added the difference - 43,500 - to the requisition for medium size.

The 10,000 baby helmets can be released, but I must make it perfectly clear that it is essential that these helmets should be sent out in proper packings, and I could not regard it as consistent with our instructions from the Cabinet that they should be despatched without proper packing. The Crown Agents have pointed out that they have no Depot or timber from which to arrange crating, but we certainly have not the labour or facilities at our Regional Stores for this purpose. An alternative suggestion, made by the Crown Agents, was that crating should be carried out at the Ordnance Depot at Didcot, but I am informed that this also has fallen through. I believe the Crown Agents are now trying shipping agents. If an allocation of timber is necessary for this purpose I think it falls upon your Department to obtain it from Timber Control.

44,000 children's respirators were asked for but our stock position will not permit the release of such a quantity, nor does it appear that this is related to the total population of Singapore. On the usual distribution, 15,000 children's respirators would be the appropriate proportion for a population of 500,000 and we are willing to make this quantity available as soon as possible. Only 7,000 children's respirators are at present in store fitted with Mark III A containers suitable for tropical climates. The remaining 8,000 will be specially made up as soon as possible.

You will remember I told you that this was a matter which had been directly before the Cabinet and, in this connection,

PROVISION OF GAS-MASKS FOR SINGAPORE.

Before the outbreak of the Far Eastern War, the Straits Settlements had been supplied from this country with 5,732 service respirators and 8,526 civilian duty respirators for the use of those engaged in essential services. The question of providing the civilian population as a whole with civilian respirators was considered last summer by the local civil and service authorities and technical experts who agreed unanimously (and Commander-in-Chief Far East concurred) as follows:

1. Chances of gas attack very small in Singapore climate.

2. Only aerial mustard bombs expected, interspersed with high explosive and incendiaries.

3. Civilian population could disperse from main target areas.

4. For these reasons, a general issue of gas-masks was unnecessary.

At the end of December we advised the Singapore authorities of the greater likelihood of the Japanese using gas and asked them to overhaul existing plans. The War Council then reviewed the position and recommended that the whole population of Singapore be equipped. 200 respirators a day can be manufactured locally, 10,000 can be supplied from Australia and 500,000 have been requested from this country. The last are ready for shipment from here with the exception of babies' helmets and children's type respirators of which the Ministry of Home Security state there are very few to spare for export.

Of these 500,000, the greater part are ready for shipment.

Dear Hill,

about [POSSIBLY MISSING].

About half a million general civilian-type respirators are requested for issue to the general public.

I am afraid that this is rather a large request, but it is unnecessary for me to emphasise the high priority which Singapore can claim in these matters at the present time.

We should be very grateful for your views on the prospects of the supplying authorities in this country being able to meet this request.

As it will in any case take some considerable time to get the respirators out from the U.K. to Singapore, it has occurred to us that some at least of their requirements might be met by asking other Colonies, such as Aden, in the Indian Ocean area, whether they could immediately release any stocks of gas masks which they may have, to be replenished later from the U.K. We are making enquiries to see what numbers actually were sent to our places in that area, but I gathered in talking to you over the telephone that you did not think that this possibility offered any chance of meeting Singapore's requirements to an appreciable extent.

Yours sincerely,

Your telegram No.2026 Secret.

Following receipt of your telegram No.609 Secret and report mentioned therein, conference was held with the C.-in-C. China, G.O.C., representative of A.O.C. and technical experts. C.-in-C. Far East was not represented at own request but agreed with unanimous conclusions as follows:

1. Chances of gas attack as against high explosive and incendiaries very small in this climate.

2. Only serial mustard bombs expected interspersed for nuisance value with high explosive and incendiaries.

3. Nature of targets suggest danger confined to Singapore.

4. In Singapore you equip with civilian duty respirators disciplined armed and passive defence and essential services personnel who would be unable to avoid in the course of duty contaminated areas rather than the general public, largely uneducated and able in any event to disperse from such areas.

5. Emphatic that general public issue unnecessary.

2. War Council has now reviewed the position and in view of the heavier scale of attack now possible recommend that the whole population of Singapore be equipped. Australia can supply 10,000 General Service Respirators which will be used for personnel mentioned in sub-paragraph 4 above. Local manufacture of civilian duty type about 200 per diem. Can arrangements be made to supply and ship from the United Kingdom about half a million general civilian type respirators for issue to the public.

Inter-Services Committee of Chemical Warfare consider that as Japan has not signed Geneva Convention and doubts exist whether she has used gas in China war, precautionary defence measures should be overhauled. They advise checking and examination of personal equipment (respirators, eye-shields, ointments etc.); also that gas exercises should be carried out and suitable medical arrangements made.

3. Recent information indicates that Japanese have used mustard gas and Lewisite in China.

TO STRAITS SETTLEMENTS

TO: Governor (Sir S. Thomas)
FROM: Secretary of State for the Colonies.

Sent 13th May, 1941. 15:30 hrs.

No. 609 Secret.

Your telegram No. 196, Government House. Gas mask policy has been reviewed by Overseas Defence Committee in light of report by Chemical Board of Ministry of Supply, copy of which was sent to you by air mail on 22nd April. View of Committee was that respirators should be issued to inhabitants of main centres of population, say towns of over 20,000 inhabitants, and also to those of any selected areas of military importance decided upon by the Governor and Commanders in Chiefs in consultation. Stock to be held locally and issued free when judged necessary.

Please telegraph information as to estimated numbers, sizes and fittings of respirators which would be required, and as to any other points regarding decontamination stores. Please also report to what extent supply can be arranged locally or through Eastern Group Supply Council.

Cost of respirators excluding freight approximately two shillings and sixpence each if supplied from U.K. Financial responsibility for issue of respirators would under normal practice lie with Colonial Government. Please confirm that cost of any measures necessary to carry out policy now recommended will be borne by Colonial funds.
