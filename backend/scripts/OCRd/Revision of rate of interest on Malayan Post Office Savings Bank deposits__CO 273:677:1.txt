Mr. Paskin
Mr. Caine.

The question at issue is the rate of interest which should be offered on deposits in the Malayan Savings Bank on reoccupation. I trouble you because I think that the rate is a matter of some importance even though (as I understand is the case) it may not be possible to trace any direct mathematical relationship between the rate and the volume of deposits.

The rate paid before the war by the Savings Banks in Malaya (with the exception of those in Johore and Kedah) was 3% and when this question came to me last it came with a suggestion that the rate should be maintained at 3% and I expressed doubt whether the Banks could maintain that rate on likely yields from investments.

The proposal has now swung to the other extreme and it is suggested that the rates should be reduced on re-occupation to 2%.

A table of current rates of interest paid by Post Office Savings Banks in other Colonies is at 2 on this file. It will be seen that by far the most common rate is in force in this country, namely 2½%, and I myself would suggest that we should recommend to the Malayan Planning Unit that we should compromise on this figure - i.e. that the rate should be reduced on re-occupation from 3% to 2½%.

As in the case of most compromises it would be difficult to justify this on logical grounds, but it seems to me to strike a balance between a rate which might require substantial element of subsidy and a rate which would err on the side of over-caution. Whether or not changes in the Savings Bank rate of interest do reflect this closely in the volume of deposits, it is I think a good thing to build up the impression among the community both that Government do attach importance to investment in the P.O.S.B. and that when such investment is made by the public, the latter are not being exploited but are being offered by the Government a good return on their money. Both these psychological factors point strongly towards a rate which is as high as can be managed without an undue element of subsidy and it looks to me from the figures quoted at the end of Mr. Smith's letter at No.5 that 2½% would about fulfil this condition - i.e. it may involve some element of subsidy, but not very much.

? reply to No.5 suggesting 2½% for the reasons broadly indicated in this minute.

SAVINGS BANK

I believe that there was at one time a Post Office Savings Bank in Hong Kong but certainly for the past 30 years and more this work has been carried out as a sub-section of the Hong Kong and Shanghai Bank, under the name of the "Hong Kong Savings Bank". They had a separate counter for this in their main building (also later at their Kowloon branch); they and the depositors got free postage facilities; and I think there was some undertaking as to earmarking of a special cash balance. I see from the 1938 Blue Book that at the close of that year there were 19,770 depositors in Hong Kong and 3,017 in Kowloon, and that on that date they had at their credit $13 million odd (Hong Kong) and $1 million odd (Kowloon). The rate of interest in both cases was 2½% per annum.

2. I cannot find in the recent bound Ordinances any legislation to control Savings Banks generally. This was certainly in draft and I thought (wrongly apparently) that it had gone through. The point of this was that certain native banks had been using the sacred word "Savings Bank" and its Chinese equivalent rather too freely and the Chinese Members of Council wanted the public protected by restrictions similar to those imposed on Life Insurance Companies.

Dear Emanuel,

Some time ago I promised to let you have a further note on Post Office Savings Banks in explanation of my letter of the 17th February to Wodeman. I am sorry that it has been so long delayed.

In the figures that follow I have for purposes of illustration referred to the combined accounts of the F.M.S. and S.S. Savings Banks. The position in respect of the U.M.S. Banks is similar though their operating costs are slightly higher. This latter point however has little or no effect on the general position.

In seeking to give you this information two difficulties arise: the first difficulty is that the exact Savings Banks deposits are unknown. We know that a last minute run on the Banks took place towards the end of 1941, but the extent of this run cannot be ascertained. All that we can do therefore is to take the last known figures of deposits and bring them up to date by adding the accrued interest on depositors' accounts (which we have accepted as a liability). This figure will of course tend to be an unfavourable one.

The second difficulty arises from the fact that though the Banks in 1939 had total assets of approximately $27 millions (of which $2 millions were surplus) no less than $4 millions were held at call either as cash in Post Offices or in the form of credit with the Currency Commissioners. To whatever extent this $4 millions were used in making payments during the run on the Banks, to that extent will the Banks' liabilities be reduced,

===== Page 64 =====
But large sums are still unaccounted for, and it remains to be seen how much of this $4 million will be recoverable.

The calculated position at 31st December 1943 was:

Assets (securities): $25,700,000
Deposits: $26,750,000

i.e., $1,000,000 deficit. We have so far worked on the assumption that the Savings Banks would be in a position, when we re-occupy Malaya, to meet all their old liabilities to their depositors from their own resources, but it would be unwise, we feel, to hope that when the position is straightened out any appreciable surplus will be revealed.

Turning now to our earnings on our sterling and rupee investments, we are now getting 3.7%, but we shall have to liquidate some of these investments when the Banks begin to operate again. If we ultimately liquidate as much as $4 million, our investments will be reduced by that amount and their earnings will fall to 3.15%. For the future, the Crown Agents advise on new investments we can expect no more than 3%. Moreover, since the present higher yield is due to holdings of old investments, the Banks must expect some depreciation in the market value as those holdings approach maturity—which means that the overall position of the Banks will worsen.

The latest appreciation of the position shows the Banks' operating costs to be 0.75% in the F.M.S. and S.S., and slightly higher in the U.M.S. On the reoccupation of Malaya, these costs may be higher for a brief period, but we see no reason why a return to the previous rate should not be achieved within a few years. The position might then be:

Net earnings, say, 3% to 3.2%;
Operating costs, 0.75%;
Proposed interest rate, 2%.

FIN/305

1. In Turnbull's letter to you of the 20th October, 1944, he enclosed a note as to the policy to be pursued, on our return to Malaya, in the recognition of Post Office Savings Bank deposits. That policy was accepted by the Colonial Office in your letter No.55203/44 of the 4th December.

2. You will see from the note that, as far as can be ascertained at present, the resources of the Banks are such as to enable us to re-open them at a reasonably early date after re-occupation, and we have accordingly made preparations with this purpose in view.

3. In doing so we have considered the question of the rate of interest payable on deposits and, having regard to past experience and the present earning power of the Bank's investments, have concluded that, from the point of view of the Bank alone, the generally current rate of 3% (the only exceptions being Johore and Kedah) cannot be maintained and that early steps should be taken to reduce it to 2% if the Bank is to remain solvent after we return.

4. There remains, of course, the question as to whether such a reduction is, in fact, politically expedient. This aspect of the matter has been examined by the Chief Planner in Advisory Council when it was considered that, inasmuch as a reduction is inevitable sooner or later, any undesirable effect it might have

===== Page 70 =====
Shaking confidence in the Administration would not be mitigated by a brief postponement. It was therefore decided that the reduction should be effected at the earliest possible moment.

5. Two points arise from this decision. Firstly, the laws governing the Post Office Savings Banks throughout the S.S., the F.M.S., and the U.M.S. all require the giving of not less than three months' notice of any change in interest rates. It is proposed, therefore, that such notice should be given soon after re-occupation in order that the new rate could be brought into effect by the time the Savings Banks were ready to reopen.

6. Secondly, though the laws of the U.M.S. provide for a change of rate by a decision either of the Ruler or of the State Council, those of the S.S. and F.M.S. require the prior approval of the Secretary of State (vide Section 9(i) of the Post Office Savings Bank Ordinance, No.35 of 1936 in the case of the S.S. and see 9(i) of the corresponding Enactment, No.43 of 1936 in the case of the F.M.S.).

7. I shall be glad to know whether the Colonial Office has any comments or suggestions to offer on the subject and whether the necessary approval can be given to the proposal. It is perhaps unnecessary to record that the calculation of interest due on outstanding deposits for the period of occupation must be at the old rate.

Yours sincerely,

for Colonel (Finance).
