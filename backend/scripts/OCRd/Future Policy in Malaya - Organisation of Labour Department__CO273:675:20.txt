Mr. Paskin.

I have minuted to you today on one of the sub-files to this file, about the progress of the Dutch and Indian negotiations, and about the necessity of keeping Sir George Gater, Sir Gerard Clauson and the Rubber Growers' Association informed of these negotiations as they proceed. If possible (see the second paragraph of my minute of 3/12 on 55104/34/44 beneath), I should like to see all questions relating to the Dutch and Indian negotiations pursued on the appropriate sub-files, so that the main file can be kept for the question of the future organisation of the Malayan Labour Department.

As to this, no progress has been made for a long time. Most of the correspondence up to and including No. 38 on 55104/34/44 beneath, as well as the minutes on that file, were devoted to the question, but consideration of this problem seems to have petered out in the summer of last year. By that time, the Malayan Long Term Policy Directive on Labour and Immigration had been approved in a form which left the main organisational questions open, the intention being to pursue these questions separately. A "Working Party" had been in existence to study them, and had included a member of the Ministry of Labour (Mr. Veysey). On July the 19th 1944 (see No. 38 on 55104/34/44) Mr. Wodeman wrote to Mr. Veysey, suggesting that the working party should be re-established (it had lapsed during Major Orde Browne's absence from this country.) The suggestion, however, was not pursued, largely because Major Orde Browne again went abroad. In my minute of 3/12/44 referred to above, I suggested that the question might be re-opened, but since that date all the emphasis has been on the negotiations with the Indian and Dutch Governments.

I do not think there will be much point in resuscitating the working party now, since the Malayan Planning Unit have packed up and gone abroad. I suggest, however, that we should consider communicating with General Hone, referring to the interrupted work of the Working Party and asking him to consider the matter afresh and let us have firm recommendations. In doing so, besides incorporating the views of his own staff,

I think the file might be brought up in, say, 3 months, and in the meanwhile Major Orde Browne may like to see the papers again.

Your telegram No. 1371 dated 3rd October, 1946.

Conciliation machinery Malaya.

1. Your paragraph 2. Present organisation of the Labour Departments Malayan Union and Singapore consists of officers who are trained in languages and psychology of local races, to perform duties of conciliation officers in industrial disputes and are skilled in local industrial conciliation in addition to inspection duties. They receive help and advice from Trade Union Adviser and two assistant Trade Union Advisers, who are experienced in United Kingdom industrial conciliation machinery. Policy is to encourage collective bargaining between employers and employed, and this is being fostered as Trade Unions are registered. Industrial Courts Laws 1940 provide machinery for conciliation, and no additional machinery at present considered necessary. Assistance in industrial conciliation at present is also given by Trade Union Advisers in performance of their duties under the directive. For this please see paragraph 3.

2. Further instrument in conciliation, pending establishment of Whitley Councils for Government services, is a Joint Interim Council of official and staff sides, which is in process of formation.

===== Page 31 =====
3. Your paragraph 2. Continued functions performed by Trade Union Advisers.

Brazier has now accepted appointment, subject to your approval, as Trade Union Adviser to the Governments of Malayan Union and Singapore, effective from 1st October. Please see my telegram No. 961 dated 4th October, 1946, with pensionable status ante-dated to first appointment, 2nd September, 1945. He has two assistant Trade Union Advisers, one in Penang and one in Singapore.

A directive has been issued to him, agreed to by the Governments of Malayan Union and Singapore, a copy of which follows by air mail. Under this directive, the functions and duties of the Trade Union Adviser include advice and guidance to organisers of Trade Unions with regard to objects, methods, organisation procedure, voting and preparation of applications; encouraging formation of Trade Unions where unions do not exist and where workers would benefit from such organisation in achieving unity and thus finding means for joint settlement of differences or grievances; encouraging members of Trade Unions to take active part in the affairs of the Union; assisting the emergence of sound leadership; inculcating the spirit of discipline; teaching principles of a democratic Government within the Union; the necessity of complying with majority decisions; readiness to accept agreements made by elected representatives with employers or other unions; educating workers in the principles and practice of collective bargaining, methods of presenting demands for redress of grievances; emphasising the disadvantage of strike action before presentation of demands and the need to regard strike as a weapon for resort only when all means of settlement have failed; encouragement of workmen to undertake responsibility of representing members in trade disputes and fostering growth of Trade Unions on sound lines.

The Adviser's duties also include assisting Trade Unions, both of employers and workers, to formulate arrangements for the settlement by conciliation or arbitration of disputes, and finally to bring to the attention of officers of the Labour Department any grievances of workers or any matters affecting conditions of employment which might impair labour relations, in order that officers of the Labour Department may take suitable action under the Labour Code or Industrial Courts Ordinance, by which the Commissioner of Labour is required to take steps to promote a settlement of any trade dispute existing or apprehended.

MR. J. BRAZIER

During my recent visit to Singapore I saw a good deal of Mr. Brazier in connection with the labour unrest which came to a head there during the Christmas period.

The position was that Mr. Brazier had been sent out as a trade union advisor to the F.M.S. Railways but, before he had taken up these duties, the labour situation in Singapore deteriorated so seriously and so rapidly that he was asked to devote his energies to the efforts that were being made to deal with that situation. I was present at two long meetings which were attended by Mr. Brazier and I was very much impressed indeed with the astonishing grasp of the local political and trade union situation which he had acquired in such a short space of time. He had established close relations, on an informal basis, with the leaders of many of the trade unions in Singapore, who were already going to him for advice as to how the affairs of a union should be conducted. He appeared also to have won their confidence by his assistance to them in getting the actions of the unions on the right lines and in helping the leaders of these unions to maintain a grip of the situation in spite of the efforts of political extremists (e.g. Indonesian sympathizers) to exploit the strikes and the general labour unrest for their own political purposes.

I was also very much impressed by the sound and balanced views which Mr. Brazier contributed to the deliberations of the Committees which were attempting to find some means of bringing this labour unrest to an end.

Many of the senior officers of BMA(M) volunteered to me expressions of their highest admiration for and appreciation of the wholehearted and devoted manner in which Mr. Brazier had flung himself into this wholly unfamiliar atmosphere. The relations which Mr. Brazier had established with trade union leaders of all races (including some Chinese Communists) seemed to me to conclusively rebut the views which had previously been expressed in this Office that a man of some age, with nothing but a United Kingdom trade union background, would find it almost impossible to get to grips with their wholly alien notions of Chinese trade unionist and political leaders.

I am also glad to be able to record that Mr. Brazier volunteered to me his appreciation of the sympathetic and enlightened manner in which the senior officials of the BMA(M) were endeavoring to find a solution for these labour troubles.

Just before I left, Mr. Brazier told me that, although he had been sent out for a specifically railway job, he now felt that he was acquiring such a knowledge of, and influence in, the wider trade union affairs of Singapore that it would be a pity if this experience were wasted by transferring him to the narrower railway field. He also told me that he found the Singapore work so very congenial that he himself would regret having to make a change.

I fully share Mr. Brazier's view; that it would be a pity for him now to be transferred to the narrower field. This aspect of the matter should therefore receive careful consideration in connection with the appointments of the further two trade unionists, which were under discussion at the time when I left for Singapore at the beginning of December.

Note of a meeting with Major-General H.R. Hone held in Mr. Gent's room, Colonial Office on Wednesday, December 5th, 1945.

PRESENT:
Mr. G.E.J. Gent (In the Chair)
Major-General H.R. Hone and his Personal Assistant, Jnr. Commander Williams.
Mr. J.J. Paskin - Colonial Office
Mr. B.D. Edmonds - Colonial Office
Mr. W.A. Morgan - Colonial Office
Mr. G.S. Wodeman - Colonial Office
Major Orde-Browne (For items 1 and 2) C.O.
Mr. N.J.B. Sabine (For item 3)

1. Labour

Mr. Ahearne's letter was mentioned and it was considered advisable that he should visit Malaya as soon as possible, if not during Mr. Paskin's visit, at any rate, early in January.

General Hone said that it is important to introduce wage fixing machinery as early as possible, but the problem is most complicated at the present time owing to currency complications, shortage of supplies and shortage of transport, resulting in increased cost of living and industrial unrest. The wider question of long-term policy for Indian immigration was mentioned. It was considered that the Malayan Union Citizenship directive should afford a complete answer to any points which may be raised by India.

Major Orde-Browne thought that there should be some written agreement between the Indian and Malayan authorities in this connection. It was important that Ahearne should discuss this question and the cost of living question with Duckworth as early as possible.

2. Labour Department

Major Orde-Browne thought there should be a unified Labour Department dealing with all labour, including Chinese, probably with headquarters at Kuala Lumpur and a Deputy at Singapore. It was considered advisable that the Head of the Department should be called Commissioner for Labour, and should be a Malayan Civil Servant, and that he should have an Industrial Relations Adviser procured through the Ministry of Labour. A vacancy form should be prepared for this appointment. Upper age limit 40; suggested salary $1,000; secondment for three years. Action to be taken to approach the Ministry of Labour through Mr. Grossmith.

The question of securing at a later date one or more Trade Unionists as Assistant Labour Advisers would be for discussion with the Industrial Relations Adviser. Major Orde-Browne thought that Oswell (M.C.S.) would be a suitable Head of Department if he was available. He did not know Duckworth. General Hone said that Duckworth was doing excellent work.

===== Page 73 =====
3. Information and Publicity

General Hone said that the Publicity Department was doing very useful work in maintaining relations with the press. Brigadiers McKerron and Willan held weekly press conferences. The Department was conducting publicity campaigns in connection with price control, methyl alcohol poisoning, waste of water, etc., and hand-outs of news were prepared by the department. They had set up Information Offices and found the people were avid for news, having been starved of all news of the outside world for some years. An Information Bureau was preparing pamphlets and Government news-sheets. In fact, a big organisation was required to put information across. As regards the future of the Department, Mr. Sabine thought that a Publicity Department would be needed in peacetime, (a) to explain local policy to Malaya, (b) to explain Malaya to the outside world, (c) to explain the British policy to Malaya, and (d) act as a clearing house for propaganda in any international campaigns in connection with health, etc. He thought that there should be a unified department and that the Director should be a Malayan Civil Servant. He thought it important that the interests of Singapore and Malaya should be kept in view if and when the general P.I.D. Scheme for Far Eastern publicity comes up for discussion. He stated a large number of P.W. Officers are now going into Singapore under the aegis of S.A.C.S.E.A., and the Far Eastern Bureau is moving in. The numbers were estimated at about 600.

4. Head of the Department

It was considered that Mr. Rawlings, M.C.S., should be sent out as early as possible as Head of the Department. He should first be asked to come up to the Colonial Office to discuss matters. There would be a Director of Broadcasting under him and this post should be filled by Mr. Dumeresque.

5. Visit of Capt. Gammans to Malaya

Mr. Morgan mentioned the question of the visit of Capt. Gammans. It was considered that it would not be politic on his part to ask that his wife should accompany him, in view of the fact that no women had been allowed into Malaya except Nurses, Doctors, etc., sponsored by the Military. It would be necessary to refer the question of his visit to S.A.C.S.E.A.

6. Mr. Tooke

Colonel Cole's minute on the subject of Mr. Tooke was discussed. General Hone said that there was still a considerable amount of work for Tooke to do, and that if he were required to come back to this country he should be relieved by somebody else.

7. Municipal Officers

Mr. Edmonds brought up the question of Municipal Officers. General Hone said this should be discussed with Colonel Sanders.

8. Unanswered Telegrams

The question of telegrams to B.F.A.(M) still awaiting answer was mentioned, and General Hone said that he would bring this to the notice of his Secretariat.

9. Compulsory retirement at 55

Mr. Edmonds said it had been decided that any officers above 54 should not go back to Malaya unless specially selected. Those

===== Page 74 =====
Between 50 and 54 could have the option to retire if they wished. Any who expressed the desire to return must undertake to do at least an 18 months' tour of duty.

10. Rations and Accommodation

General Hone said that it was the policy to provide rations and accommodation to all Government officers and sponsored civilians entering Malaya at the present time while Military Administration continued. At the end of this period, considerable difficulties were likely to arise owing to the high cost of living, and it might be necessary to consider a cost of living allowance.

11. Passages

Mr. Edmonds mentioned the matter of passages and said that he proposed to give a rough estimate of the number required month by month in the next few months to the Communications Department.

A Note of an Informal Meeting held at the Colonial Office on December 6th, 1944 to discuss Labour Matters

Present: Mr. G.E.J. Gent (in the chair)

Sir C. Figg
Mr. J.J. Paskin
Mr. N.L. Mayle
Mr. J.M. Barron
Miss Clauson

Captain M.C. Hay Malayan Planning Unit

Mr. H.B. Egmont Hake
Mr. T.B. Barlow
Mr. T.J. Cumming
Mr. A.P. Hadow
Mr. J.W.M. Kennedy

1. Mr. Gent stated that the meeting had been called to discuss informally the question of the amount of labour likely to be required in Malaya after its liberation, and the countries to which we might find it possible to have recourse if immigrant workers were needed to meet a shortage.

2. Mr. Hake was of the opinion that a shortage of labour would be experienced from the beginning, and it was therefore advisable to take immediate steps to reach a working agreement with the Governments of India and the Netherlands Indies respectively, so that as soon as Malaya was liberated, arrangements could be made to obtain any labour that might be required. It was impossible to formulate any estimate of the number of labourers likely to be required, and the extent to which it could be made up from those workers in the country who did not want to leave for their homes abroad, but Mr. Hake believed that considerable numbers would be required owing to such factors as deterioration in health among Indians during the Japanese occupation, and a big flow-back to India of labourers whose return to Malaya after their visit to India might be impeded by the policy of the Government of India.

3. Mr. Barron described the arrangements prevailing before the war for obtaining labour from Java. Provided the Malayan Labour Department certified that the conditions on an estate were satisfactory, the owner or his agent was at liberty to obtain a license from the Netherlands Indies Government - through their Consul General, Singapore - to recruit labour in Java. This license was then handed to the appropriate officially authorised Commercial Agency - whose headquarters were in Batavia - which engaged the recruits and forwarded them to Singapore. It was a great defect that the whole operation was very slow, and sometimes as many as 8 months passed before the arrival of the recruits.

4. Mr. Hake stated that he had had, about a year ago, an informal talk with Dr. Van Mook of the Dutch Government, and the impression he gained as a result was that the Dutch would favour the migration of labour to Malaya from the overpopulated island of Java. They would have to be satisfied however, that the conditions of employment and recruitment were suitable and they would require a gentleman's understanding that this labour would not be sent back in bulk if there was a rubber slump. To meet

===== Page 80 =====
This reasonable requirement. Mr. Hake suggested that any Javanese labour imported into Malaya should be encouraged (e.g., by the grant of gardens and good general conditions) to settle down permanently in Malaya. Mr. Gent said that the Colonial Office had gathered the same impression of Dr. Van Mook's attitude.

5. It was generally agreed that the Government of India were likely to connect the question of a resumption of immigration from India with the particular matters which were under discussion with them before the war, especially as regards the political status of Indians in Malaya.

6. Captain Hay, when asked if the depots in India and Malaya could be reopened soon after the liberation of Malaya, thought that they probably could, with the exception of the one at Avadi, Madras, which had been in the hands of the Military authorities from 1939 onwards.

7. It was agreed that, during the pre-occupation planning stage, it was inadvisable to provoke any exchange of views with the Chinese Government as to the emigration of labour from China, as the problem after the liberation of Malaya would probably be one of keeping the quota of fresh "unskilled" immigrants from that country as low as possible. Mr. Barron mentioned that before the war the Chinese living in the villages near estates were a growing and important source of useful labour.

8. It was agreed that the first step now should be an approach to the Government of India and the Netherlands Indies with a view to ascertaining their policy as to the emigration to Malaya of unskilled labour from India and the Netherlands East Indies; and Mr. Gent stated he would consult the India Office as to the best method of approach to the Government of India.

The Colonial Office undertook to keep in touch with the R.G.A. on these matters.
