I have etc. to acknowledge the receipt of Sir Shenton Thomas' confidential despatch of the 29th July 1939 forwarding a memorial from nineteen senior members of the Malayan Customs Service regarding the application of the revised salary scale for officers in the Customs Department.

2. The decision to improve the initial salaries of Customs probationers, with effect from the 1st of January 1937, was based upon the recommendation in the MacGregor Commission Report, and, in considering the application of that recommendation, it clearly became necessary in practice, to effect an adjustment of the salaries of certain Customs probationers, who had been appointed in the recent years preceding 1937, in order that their position might not be prejudiced in relation to new entrants.

I am, however, satisfied that it was never intended to involve a general enhancement of remuneration of officers on the time scale, and I have no reason to believe such a course to be justified.

Report of the Committee appointed by His Excellency the Governor and High Commissioner to consider certain suggestions put forward in paragraphs 29 and 30 of the report of the MacGregor Committee on Temporary Allowances, 1937.

===== Page 13 =====
We were appointed as a Committee to consider certain suggestions in paragraphs 29 and 30 of the report by the Committee presided over by Sir A. D. A. MacGregor and to report how these suggestions could best be implemented.

While it is true that the MacGregor Committee confined their remarks to the minimum salaries paid to certain classes of the more lowly-paid European officers, it is not in our opinion satisfactory merely to increase the minimum salary payable under a scheme without taking into consideration the consequent effect that such an increase may have upon the scheme as a whole. We have therefore not hesitated to suggest the recasting of certain schemes, even at the risk of being held to have gone outside our terms of reference.

The question of the inadequacy of the basic salaries drawn by certain classes of European officers, such as Prison Warders and Police Inspectors, has been raised from time to time throughout the last ten years and in 1933, although the financial situation did not render the time opportune for a revision of basic salaries, a so-called "Palliative Allowance" was granted to married officers drawing salaries below certain levels. This was admittedly a purely temporary measure and there was at least an implied promise that basic salaries would be revised when the financial situation improved. We venture to suggest that that time has now come. If the various revisions which we recommend are accepted the "Palliative Allowance" will of course disappear.

Our task in drawing up revised salary scales has been immensely facilitated by the acceptance of the MacGregor report, as it is no longer necessary for us to face the dilemma that a salary scale adequate but no more than adequate for a married man without children may be inadequate for one with several children, whereas a scale based on the latter possibility will in many cases be unduly generous and therefore unwarrantably expensive. This problem, which was perhaps the most difficult of all the problems that faced the "Palliative Allowance" Committee, is no longer a serious one and we have based our scales on the salary we consider necessary to enable a married man without children to live in a way consonant with his position in life and have allowed for a moderate increase as his service lengthens. We accept as approximately correct the findings of the "Palliative Allowance" Committee as to the minimum salary necessary for the various classes of officers with which we are concerned.

In view of the fact that the recommendations of the MacGregor Committee have had effect from the 1st January 1937 and for precisely similar reasons to those adduced by that Committee, we recommend that the salary scales proposed by us, if agreed to, should be brought into force from the same date, namely the 1st January, 1937. This would mean that the Palliative Allowance would disappear as from that date and be replaced by the "Children's Allowance" recommended by the MacGregor Committee.

In the unlikely but just conceivable case of this involving any officer in a refund, we recommend that such refund should be waived. If such a case does in fact exist, which is doubtful, the amount involved cannot at most exceed a few dollars.

===== Page 14 =====
Our specific recommendations in respect of the various classes of officers involved are as follows.

(a) Police and Customs Probationers—

In paragraph 29 of their report the MacGregor Committee suggest that the salary of a Probationer should be raised from $250 to $300 and that of a Passed Probationer to $325. This was considered by a Committee consisting of the Commissioner of Police, F.M.S., the Inspector-General of Police, S.S., and the Commissioner of Customs and Excise, S.S. and F.M.S., a copy of whose report we attach. As will be seen from this report these officers do not consider the suggestion made by the MacGregor Committee goes far enough or allows sufficiently for the change in recruiting conditions that has taken place in the last few years. They recommend that the salary of a Probationer should be $300, that of Passed Probationer $350 and that an Assistant Commissioner or Assistant Superintendent should draw $400.

We have looked into this matter in some detail and we are satisfied that the claim that the age of recruitment has altered and is now some two years greater than it was formerly is well founded; for instance the four Police Probationers recruited in 1931 were all born in 1912, while of the four Police Probationers recruited in 1934 three were born in 1912 and one in 1911. In the case of Customs Probationers the increase in age is, if anything, slightly greater and the change started somewhat earlier than in the case of the Police. Of the last 16 Customs Probationers 10 were over 22 years of age when they arrived in the country. Educational standards have risen too and probationers with University degrees are now quite common. In view of these facts we support the recommendation. It is perhaps of interest to point out that if allowance be made for the greater age of recruitment, what we are now recommending is precisely what was recommended by the Bucknill Commission in 1919 (paragraph 598) as will be seen from the following table:

This argument does not apply to officers who were recruited at a time when it was the normal practice to recruit candidates direct from school and we do not propose that any increase of salary should be given to Police Probationers recruited before 1934 or to Customs Probationers recruited prior to the latter half of 1931. In the case of Customs Probationers we have had to make a few adjustments in their incremental dates so as to ensure that no officer should draw a greater salary than one senior to him in service. In the case of the Police, owing to the gap due to the cessation of recruitment during the years 1932 and 1933, no such adjustments are necessary. ===== Page 15 =====
We also venture to suggest that the time scale for Customs officers should be brought into line with that of the Police. The two scales were the same $250-350-A25-575/Bar/A25-750—until the 1st of January, 1929, when the scale $250-350-A25-575/Bar/A25-750-3 years-A25-$800 was introduced for Police officers. This latter scale was again revised in 1936 when it was decided that the rest of three years at $750 a month should be abolished and that increments should be continuous throughout the scale. The differentiation has not so far adversely affected Customs officers because officers in the F.M.S. service have normally been promoted to the grade of Senior Superintendent of Customs on the scale $625-A25-$800 before they reached the maximum of the time scale and no officer in the S.S. service has yet reached the maximum of the time scale. Except that certain specific requirements as to height and chest measurements are laid down in the case of Police appointments, the conditions of eligibility for appointment to the Police and Customs services are identical and we consider that the work and responsibilities of the officers in the two services are not now sufficiently dissimilar to warrant any differentiation in regard to the maximum salary attainable by officers on the time scale. If our recommendation is accepted, we suggest that no further appointments should be made to the F.M.S. grade of Senior Superintendent of Customs. Two of the six appointments in this grade are at present vacant and two more will fall vacant in the near future on the retirement of the present holders. We have ascertained that these proposals have the support of the Commissioner of Customs and Excise, S.S. and F.M.S.

(b) Prison Warders—

These officers are at present on a salary scale of $200 p.m. rising by annual increments of $10 p.m. to $350 p.m., while married officers drawing less than $320 p.m. receive a "Palliative Allowance" in addition.

We accept the opinion of the MacGregor Committee that $250 per mensem is the lowest salary that should be paid to any European Officer. This is in close agreement with the views of the Palliative Allowance Committee who put the figure at $260. We recommend that the initial salary should be raised to $250 and that as this would make the time scale too short the maximum should be raised from $350 to $380. The average Warder is recruited at about 25 years of age and is liable to be retired at 45 years of age. So unless he can obtain promotion to the rank of Gaoler, and comparatively few can hope to do so, he may be retired on a pension based on this salary after some 20 years of service. The increase of the maximum salary to $380 will just enable him to draw a pension of £200 per annum after paying Widows and Orphans' contributions.

This increase of the maximum necessitates reconsideration of the salary of Gaolers, Grade III, who at present draw $370-A15-400. This scale is in our opinion too short as many Gaolers Grade III never rise beyond that grade and remain in it for a number of years. We suggest that Gaolers Grade III should draw $390-15A-450 and Gaolers Grades I and II should draw $500 and $475 respectively. This amounts to giving these officers an increase of $20 and $30 respectively over their present maxima.

These salaries are only very slightly less than the salary scale recommended by the Inspector of Prisons in C.S.O. 9065/34, and will, we understand, satisfy him.

(d) European Attendants, General and Mental Hospitals, Singapore, and European Male Nurses, Central Mental Hospital, Tanjong Rambutan—

The Straits Settlements officers are on a scale of $240–10A–400 while the F.M.S. officers are on a scale $200–10A–350.

There does not appear to be any justification for this differentiation; the qualifications demanded are the same and the duties performed are almost identical. The Adviser, Medical Services, has recommended that the pay also should be identical. It has been represented to us that these officers have to obtain qualifications before appointment and therefore should be placed on a considerably higher salary scale than Prison Warders, of whom no specific qualifications are demanded. There possibly is something in this argument, but these officers are well treated in the matter of allowances and we are not satisfied that there is any need or justification for giving them a much higher salary than a Prison Warder, though a slightly higher salary is perhaps warranted.

We recommend that these officers should draw $270–10A–290 on probation, and when confirmed $300–10A–400 with an efficiency bar at $350.

My dear Gent,

I have received your letter No. 50007/172/39 dated the 15th of December, 1939, in which you propose that the application of the revised salary scale be modified to the advantage of the nineteen Senior Officers of the Malayan Customs Service whose petition was forwarded with my Malayan Establishment confidential despatch dated the 29th of July, 1939. Before giving a considered reply I have thought it necessary to consult the Governments of the Straits Settlements and Federated Malay States and I will reply fully when their views have been obtained.

Yours sincerely,

G.E.J. Gent, Esq., O.B.E., D.S.O., M.C.,
Colonial Office,
Downing Street,
LONDON, S.W. 1.
