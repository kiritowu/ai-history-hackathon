When the payment of interest on Malayan Municipal dollar loans of the Chastend by Bank of India was discussed by the informal committee on the 21st day, the Bank's representation stated that they were not prepared to be associated with any agreement as to return money or pay them, therefore must declare to pay the interest. It was recommended that the Crown Agents should pay. To effect this a transfer of funds would be required to which the Bank had no obligation provided they were authorized by the S. 95.

The Crown Agents suggest that the general funds of one of the Municipalities in the custody of the Bank should be transferred. Each marked fund such as Condiment Funds should remain with the Bank. A list of the funds belonging to the Municipalities in the hands of the Bank is being prepared by the Bank and will be sent.

As the Bank won't pay, the Crown Agents should now be instructed. They have all the requisite machinery to impose a bond once the funds are in their hands.

They will be able to look after their employment.

As this may take a few days to settle, perhaps in the meantime the C.A. shall be authorized to advance the money from S.S. funds.

All is now in order to pay interest on the Government loans to the necessary forms and bring addressed it within a period. Not able to pay on Municipal loans because of a technicality. The public, many of whom have investments in both Government and Municipal funds, will not understand it.

Mr. Gent,

The Bank has twice gone back on us when we thought that we had brought them up to the point of making payments from Municipal funds. (The other case was that which involved the fees of the Consulting Engineers, which has not yet been cleared up). This being so, I do not think that we can adopt Mr. Taylor's suggestion of authorising the Crown Agents to make advances from Straits Settlements Government funds in respect of interest on the Municipal loans, until the Municipal funds have actually been transferred to the Crown Agents or until we have a written understanding with the Bank that they will be transferred.

You will see that the suggestion is that all the Municipality's general funds should be transferred to the Crown Agents, leaving only with the Bank "earmarked" funds such as Provident Funds and, I suppose, Sinking Funds.

From our past experience with the Bank, it is quite certain that the Bank will not hand these funds over to the Crown Agents (and I think quite rightly) unless they have a very clear and definite indemnity from the Secretary of State.

As to the extent to which the Crown Agents should take over these funds, and as to the form of indemnity

A preliminary discussion (Mr. Gent, Sir S. Abrahams, Mr. Caine, Mr. Foster Hall and myself) was held on Monday when it was agreed that it would probably be convenient to all concerned if we were to adopt the suggestion of transferring the Municipal Funds of the Municipalities from the custody of the Chartered Bank to the custody of the Crown Agents. It was also felt (subject to anything that the Bank or the Crown Agents might have to say) it might also be convenient to transfer the Provident Fund to the management of the Crown Agents, thereby obviating the necessity for the very cumbrous procedure which now has to be gone through before the Bank is prepared to make any payments from this Fund.

It was also agreed that (if the Crown Agents were willing) it might be convenient for the Crown Agents to be appointed as agents of the Municipalities with functions vis-a-vis the Municipalities generally similar to their functions vis-a-vis Colonial Governments. If this suggestion were adopted it would be necessary to retain the services of Messrs. Peirce and Williams in an advisory capacity, at a suitable fee. The present is a convenient opportunity to make this change because we have just had a letter from Peirce and Williams indicating that their agreement with the Municipalities expires on the 21st December and proposing that it should be continued but with a slightly reduced fee. The meeting was that if the Crown Agents were appointed agents of the Municipalities, they should be asked to go into the whole matter with Peirce and Williams and then submit recommendations as to the functions of that firm and the fee which they should be paid for their work.

It was agreed that these suggestions should be discussed at a further meeting on Thursday which Mr. Cockburn and Messrs. Peirce and Williams should be invited to attend.

It was further agreed that, pending the transfer of funds from the Bank, the Crown Agents should be authorised to proceed with the payment of interest on the Municipal Dollar Loans, advancing the necessary funds from Straits Government funds pending the transfer of Municipal Funds from the Bank. It was agreed that, in the documents to be used in connection with the Municipal Loans, the Crown Agents should be described as acting "on behalf of the Municipality of Singapore, etc."

After the meeting I discussed this matter with Mr. Downie who, rather to my surprise, told me that, on reconsideration, he was now disposed to suggest that all the general funds of the Municipalities should not be transferred to the Crown Agents but only sufficient to meet the payment of interest on the loans. When, however, I told him of the conclusions reached at our meeting, and our general view that it would probably be very convenient if the Crown Agents could be appointed as agents of the Municipalities, and take over the management of all the Municipal Funds, he did not wish to demur but said that he would wish to consult Sir C. Bottomley and Mr. Goldburg before finally agreeing. He thought that if the arrangement would not involve any increase in staff in the Crown Agents' office, they would be prepared to take it on. As to this, after some discussion, we came to the conclusion that it would no doubt be possible for them to make some arrangement with Peirce and Williams for the issue of individual remittances, etc., so that no increase of staff in the Crown Agents' office would be required.

We have on our hands the affairs and certain assets of the Municipalities of Singapore and Penang, and certain obligations of those Municipalities which have to be met even in present circumstances. Both Municipalities employed as Agents for limited purposes the firm of Peirce and Williams, Consulting Engineers, in London. The Municipality of Singapore has issued dollar loans in Singapore, some of the holders of which are in this country, and under the recent decision of H.M.G. have to receive interest on the loans in sterling. Other obligations of the Municipalities which have to be met are certain commercial debts and also payments to dependants of their interned employees.

The Bank which normally keeps the funds in London of both Municipalities is the Chartered Bank of India, Australia, and China. The Bank has seen difficulty in making payments on the instructions of Messrs. Peirce and Williams in view of the very limited nature of the latter's agency position, and as a result of discussions which we have had here with the Manager of the Bank and the partners of Peirce and Williams, and the Crown Agents, in which Mr. Caine and Sir Sydney Abrahams have advised us, we agreed that the only satisfactory course would be for the Crown Agents to assume the charge of the funds of both Municipalities and the power to operate on them with the approval of the Secretary of State.

The Bank is prepared to transfer these funds to the Crown Agents and to allow the Crown Agents to operate on them. The Agents are also content with that position, although we shall probably agree to an exception in the case of certain relatively small obligations, the settlement of which can best be left with Peirce and Williams themselves. But the Bank ask for a form of indemnity to cover them against any possible

===== Page 11 =====
Possible claims which might result from their transferring to the Crown Agents the charge of the Municipalities' monies. It was agreed that they should submit to us the sort of form of indemnity which would meet their requirements, and they have now done so in 3.

This has been examined by Sir Sydney Abrahams and the Eastern Department with the result that we prefer to revise it as in pencil. We think the draft form should be referred to the Treasury for their concurrence since in the last resort they must stand behind the solvency of the Straits Settlements Government, and to that extent it implies an ultimate obligation on Treasury funds here.

If the draft as now revised is approved by the Secretary of State we will refer it to the Treasury and, subject to their concurrence, we will inform the Chartered Bank that the Secretary of State is prepared to give them an indemnity in those terms.

The Secretary of State has asked me to note that he considers that the responsibility for indemnifying the Chartered Bank of India, Australia and China against any claims which might be made against them by the authorities of the Municipalities mentioned should rest fairly and squarely on the shoulders of H.M.G.

He would, therefore, suggest that an official approach should be made to the Treasury with a view to obtaining their approval for the issue of an indemnification in the form proposed in the enclosure to (3) as amended in pencil.

Mr. Gent has agreed.

As the Crown Agents are anxious to get on with this transfer of funds I recovered this file from the Private Secretary and Sir George Gater has not yet seen the Secretary of State's direction.

I have explained the position fully to Mr. Chance of the Treasury and now submit letter to Mr. Wilcox.

After I dictated the above, I spoke to Mr. Downie and have now had the attached letter from him which explains the position in more detail. ===== Page 74 =====
The Joint Colonial Fund contains securities such as British Government or Corporation stocks, the dividends on which are paid to us after deduction of United Kingdom Income Tax. We are able to reclaim the tax from the Inland Revenue Department by making a declaration that "the stocks held by the Joint Colonial Fund are the property of various Colonial and Protectorate Governments and are not held in connection with a trade."

It is therefore necessary for all the deposits in the Joint Colonial Fund to be regarded as the property of Colonial or Protectorate Governments.

The Singapore and Penang Municipalities have not had to pay Income Tax as their money was invested in stocks the dividends on which are not subject to deduction of tax at the source if held by persons resident abroad. The stocks so held are as follows:
