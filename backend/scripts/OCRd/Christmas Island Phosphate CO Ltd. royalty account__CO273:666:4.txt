The Christmas Island Phosphate Company are asking that Royalty for 1942 on the phosphate shipped in 1942 be waived.

After some correspondence it is found that the amount shipped was 8,912 tons of rock phosphate to Australia, sold for 3,155 and paid for.

It is usual to have the yearly accounts audited and this has been done up to 31st December, 1941. As the above shipment was the only one made in 1942, I do not think that the expense is warranted if we are going to forego Royalty. The Crown Agents estimate that Royalty would amount on the above consignment to approximately 350 to 380, and the point is are we to demand Royalty or not?

The

===== Page 8 =====
The Company asks for this to be waived owing to the fact that they have lost practically all their assets in the Far East and that taxation has made heavy inroads into their cash resources. By taxation, I imagine British Income Tax is meant, so it appears that the Inland Revenue insists on their pound of flesh anyhow. If the Company are meeting large expenses for relief of their employees or their dependants, and as in any event they will have heavy expenses to meet on reoccupation, I think we might help by waiving the Royalty as their cash resources must be limited.

There is also another sum of £875 being freight on 1,750 tons of ground phosphate in stock on 31st December, 1941, in Singapore.

In the Royalty account for 1941 this £875 was allowed for, and it was decided that when the Royalty account for 1942 was examined, enquiry should be made as to whether any of these stocks had been sold and what were the proceeds, such proceeds being assessable without deduction of freight. The Company have no information but say it is unlikely in the then existing conditions in Malaya that any of it was sold. In these circumstances Royalty cannot of course be collected.

Apart from the decision not to collect Straits income tax with effect from the 1st of January, 1942, this is, I think, the first case in which the remission of Straits taxation has been proposed; and in this particular case it is tax to which the Company become liable before the fall of Singapore.

I am disposed to agree with Mr. Taylor that we should not now attempt to collect this tax, especially

===== Page 9 =====
Especially as it would involve, as a first step, the auditing of the accounts for 1942. On the other hand, I do not think we should remit this tax completely.

In due course, no doubt the Company will submit a claim for war damage and war losses and I suggest that the most convenient course to all concerned would be for the question of this tax to be left over for settlement at the time when the Company's claims for war damage etc. are being considered.

19th April, 1943.

Sir,

I thank you for your letter of April 16th and beg to inform you that:

(1) The 9,000 tons mentioned was shipped in 1942. One shipment was made on 9th January, 1942 (out-turn weight 5,232 tons) and later in the month 3,680 tons had been loaded into another vessel when she was beached after suffering damage from enemy action. Information regarding these two shipments was received by cable from our Manager at Christmas Island.

(2) The 1,750 tons in stock at Singapore on 31st December, 1941 did not form part of the 9,000 tons. This 1,750 tons was ground phosphate in bags for sale in Malaya whereas the 9,000 tons was rock phosphate shipped in bulk to Australia.

(3) The whole of the two shipments referred to in (1) amounting to 8,912 tons were sold and paid for. Proceeds amount to 317,155. No information is available regarding the 1,750 tons of ground phosphate in stock at Singapore but it is very unlikely, under the then existing conditions in Malaya, that any of it was sold.

This Company continued to make shipments of phosphate to Australia as long as possible and it was only after the vessel, referred to above, had been torpedoed and beached that shipments ceased. Even after that I understand that production was continued until, in February, extensive demolition of plant [POSSIBLY MISSING].

It was in view of the above and the fact that taxation has made heavy inroads into the company's cash resources that I am to request that favourable consideration be given to the question of Royalty.

Owing to the non-arrival of December accounts from our Singapore Agents, you will notice that figures under the following headings are shown up to November 30th, 1941 only:

- Freight on ground phosphate
- Storage, etc., Singapore
- Insurance on phosphate cargoes
- Wharfage, etc., "Islander"

At the bottom of the statement, we have deducted the cost of freight, insurance, and wharfage on the 1,960 tons sold in December, the cost of these being based on the average cost per ton during the period January 1st to November 30th, 1941. With regard to storage costs during December, we have deducted one-eleventh of the total storage cost during the first eleven months of the year. As no definite figures are, or are likely to be, available, we think this is the fairest method of dealing with the December Singapore costs. We have also deducted the freight on the 1,750 tons of ground phosphate being the unsold Singapore stock at the end of the year. It is extremely unlikely that any sales were made from January 1st, 1942 to February 15th, 1942, when Singapore fell, and this stock must be considered as lost.
