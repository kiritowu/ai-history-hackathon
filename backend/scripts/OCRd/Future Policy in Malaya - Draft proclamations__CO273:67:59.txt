Proclamation No. 1.

At first, I thought that the omission from Proclamation 1 of the words set out in 7(1) of Mr. O'Connor's memorandum, though the omission of the words themselves was of no importance since the omission could not alter the legal position, implied that Malaya or a part thereof was foreign territory. I do not think that this view is correct. I have had a discussion with Mr. O'Connor and I understand his point. The Straits Settlements is British territory, and I think there can be no question as to the kind of martial law which is proclaimed therein. In the F.M.S. there is enacted the Court Laws Enactment No. 3 of 1937 which makes the Common Law the law of the country provided it is not inappropriate. I think it would apply but I gather that Mr. O'Connor is not so sure. But in the U.M.S. there is no such law, and it may be that it will be necessary to justify acts done under some other legal cover. In all these cases indemnity legislation will be necessary and I do not think that the omission of these words need concern us.

Proclamation No. 2.

Paragraph 3 - end. I should have thought that some limit was desirable both as to length of sentence and amount of fine.

Paragraph 8(1). I think this is a very doubtful provision especially with regard to Assessors. The decision as to sentence after trial with assessors in the ordinary law rests with the Judge - though there is consultation in a military court, i.e., Court Martials, but in that case all the personnel of the Court are on an equality so far as legal training is concerned. It is not clear who is to decide if the members of the Court disagree. Can the President be overruled as to sentence by the Probate Officers or by the Assessor? The latter cannot overrule him on the question of conviction.

I think the responsibility of the sentence should be placed on the President who has legal training.

Paragraph 8(2). I should not have thought that confirmation of conviction was necessary. It seems to me that it must result in duplication of work and therefore in delay in all cases where there is an application for review. I would suggest that there should be provision that the Resident should wait until it is seen if the convicted person makes an application for review under paragraph 13, and forward the view for compensation only if there is no such application. The same in 11(2).

Proclamation No. 5.

4. I must say that I think that this provision goes too far - but I admit it is difficult to suggest a satisfactory alternative - perhaps "provided it has been published at the Court houses of the Division." I think Mr. O'Connor would agree to this.

13.6.45.

I have no legal observations on this proclamation. Its object is to make V.D. an "infectious" disease under these enactments and therefore a 'notifiable' disease. Most of the provisions of these enactments, which are in all essentials the same, do not seem to me to be appropriate to V.D. They are designed to deal with what is ordinarily described as 'infectious' diseases. The main result will be that it will become (1) an offence for any person not to disclose that his or her friend is suffering from V.D. and (11) incumbent on a doctor.

===== Page 7 =====
Doctor to disclose that his patient is suffering from it. There is, or perhaps I should say was, a great deal of V.D. in Malaya and in my opinion this proposal is deplorable. It will lead, I suspect, to a good deal of blackmail. It violates what has become a cardinal principle of the policy of dealing with this evil, namely that any person suffering from it can obtain medical advice without his or her condition becoming known. I take it that the object of the policy is to protect the health of troops. I venture to doubt whether, assuming the policy to be successful, any considerable benefit would be achieved during the period of military occupation. Lastly, if this policy is carried out something more will be required than the words of this Proclamation to bring home to the minds of the ordinary inhabitants of Malaya that it is now their legal duty to give information that their friends are suffering from this disease.

Mr. Y. H. Hastings

Mr. Bourdillon
(Through Mr. W. W. Clark)

I note that the Malayan Food Control Proclamation is based on the Palestine Food Control Ordinance, 1942, as amended by Defence Regulations, 1944. I don't know whether Palestine is the best model to take for Malaya and, so far as I have been able to ascertain, there is no one in Economic Division who can advise on this point. There is no time to make extensive enquiries and I think, therefore, that we must assume that it is the best model for the purpose. I believe that the Food Controller designate is an officer from Palestine, and that is, of course, an important factor in favour of adopting the Palestine rules.

I pass this through Mr. Clark of the Middle East Department who will, perhaps, say whether he is aware of any developments in connection with the Palestine Food Control arrangements which have not yet been reflected in the legislation, but which might be taken into account in connection with this Proclamation, or whether he has any general comments on the matter.

The only comment I have to offer on a very brief examination is that there seems to be no provision enabling the Controller to obtain periodic returns of stocks of controlled articles. I should have thought that this was very desirable, if not essential.

Mr. Bourdillon.

Sir Frederick Bovenschen telephoned me today regarding the matter of "martial law" in Malaya. He said that, as regards our "severe letter" to the War Office, he had consulted the Attorney General whose opinion was that:
(a) it was undesirable to use different terms in Malaya compared with Burma.
(b) on the whole he would have preferred "martial law" for both, on the principle of calling a spade a spade.
(c) that legally "military administration" was just as effective as "martial law" and both would depend on a subsequent indemnity measure.
(d) as we have avoided "martial law" in Burma, on the whole we should avoid it in Malaya.

Sir F. Bovenschen asked me whether we should maintain any serious objection now to the War Office adopting the revised proclamation for "military administration." I said that in view of his reference to the Attorney General and the latter's recorded opinion (of which I asked him to send us a copy) I felt sure that we should now accept the decision in that sense.

BRITISH MILITARY ADMINISTRATION, MALAYA

PROCLAMATION

DELEGATION OF POWERS.

A Proclamation to provide for the military administration of the civil population of Malaya.

I, General Officer Commanding Military Forces, Malaya, in exercise of the powers conferred on me by the Supreme Allied Commander, South East Asia Command, DO HEREBY PROCLAIM AND ORDER:

SHORT TITLE.

1. This Proclamation may be cited as the Military Administration (Delegation of Powers) Proclamation.

DELEGATION.

2. Subject always to any orders and directions which I may issue from time to time, I hereby delegate to the Chief Civil Affairs Officer, Malaya, full authority, power and jurisdiction to conduct on my behalf the military administration of the civil population of Malaya, with power further to delegate the exercise of any such authority, power or jurisdiction to such persons and within such areas as he may think fit.

DIVISION OF MALAYA INTO DIVISIONS, REGIONS, DISTRICTS AND SUB-DISTRICTS FOR MILITARY ADMINISTRATION.

3.—(1) For purposes of the military administration of the civil population, Malaya shall be deemed to be divided into two Divisions to be known respectively as—

(a) The Malay Peninsula Division, comprising the Settlement of Penang and the Settlement of Malacca, the Malay States of Perak, Selangor, Negri Sembilan, Pahang, Johore, Kedah, Kelantan, Trengganu and Perlis, all islands forming part of such States and the territorial waters thereof;

(b) The Singapore Division, comprising the Settlement of Singapore.

(2) Each such Division shall be administered by a Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer.

POWERS OF DEPUTY CHIEF CIVIL AFFAIRS OFFICERS.

4.—(1) The Chief Civil Affairs Officer may, by warrant, delegate to each Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer sufficient authority to conduct the military administration of the civil population in the Division assigned to him, subject always to the provisions of any Proclamation and of any written law, to the orders and directions of the Chief Civil Affairs Officer and to the requirements of any local Naval, Military or Air Force Commander stationed in the Division in relation to all matters concerning the safety and well-being of the forces under his command and the maintenance of security.

(2) For the purposes of the administration of his Division the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer may by public notice constitute Administrative Regions, Districts and Sub-districts within the Division as he may think fit. Subject to the provisions of any Proclamation or of any written law the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer may in each Region, District or Sub-district invest the Civil Affairs Officers under his command with such authority as he may think fit.

M. C. DEMPSEY,
Lt.-Gen.,
General Officer Commanding
Military Forces, Malaya.

Dated this 1st day of October, 1945

Dear O'Connor,

Would you please refer to your letter to Paskin of the 13th of September about the draft of the Malayan Venereal Disease Proclamation.

As you are aware, the feeling in the Colonial Office on this Proclamation was that the powers taken were too extensive to be appropriate to Venereal Disease. It was felt that it would have been sufficient for the purposes of the Proclamation to confine the powers taken thereunder to Sections 8 and 10 of the F.M.S. Prevention of Disease Enactment.

We have noted, however, from reports received from B.M.A. (M) that Venereal Diseases of exceptional severity were found to be rampant in Singapore and that steps were being taken to declare Venereal Disease an infectious disease so as to attempt the segregation, isolation, and treatment of the worst carriers of these infections. We have also assumed from B.M.A. telegram No. 197 of the 27th of September that arrangements have been made to print the Proclamation locally. We assume therefore that it has already been put into effect and in these circumstances would not be disposed to raise objections at this stage to its terms. Dear Bacon,

By way of confirming our interview this morning, I wish to make it clear:

(1) That there is no insuperable legal objection to the omission of the words "martial law" from the proclamation. A proclamation in a form similar to that used in regard to Burma implies the use of "martial law" whenever the latter is necessary for the protection of the troops under the C in C's command or the carrying out of his operations.

(2) The form suggested in the redraft at ZA of Discussion BM to CAMPU/BM/248 has in my view the same effect. It merely proceeds direct to the action required to carry out a proclamation of martial law under modern conditions. There is therefore no objection to it.

(3) I cannot myself see any political objection to the use of the term "martial law" which is only giving the true description to what is intended to be done. It has the advantage of relating any action which the C in C may take strictly to the military necessities which form its justification. I should have thought that this would have been easy to explain if any question arose.

(4) The above views apply to the course to be taken either in British territory or in the territory of British protected States.

(5) The matter referred to my predecessor was the interaction of a proclamation of martial law on the treaty position with the various Sultans. On the assumption that the various treaties either impose a duty on or give a right to H.M.G. to protect, I am of opinion that the C in C of any force used for such protection in the territory of the various states would have the duty of imposing martial law when military necessity required it. This is in no way inconsistent with my predecessor's view.

A Proclamation to establish a Military Administration.

WHEREAS by reason of military necessity and for the prevention and suppression of disorder and the maintenance of public safety it is necessary to place the territories of the Settlements of Singapore, Penang and Malacca, all islands and places forming part thereof and all British waters adjacent thereto, and the Malay States of Perak, Selangor, Negri Sembilan, Pahang, Johore, Kedah, Kelantan, Trengganu and Perlis, all islands forming part of such States and the territorial waters thereof (hereinafter called Malaya) under military administration:

NOW, THEREFORE, I Admiral Lord Louis Mountbatten Knight Grand Cross of the Royal Victorian Order, Commander of the Most Honourable Order of the Bath, Companion of the Distinguished Service Order, Aide de Camp to His Majesty the King, Supreme Allied Commander South East Asia Command hereby PROCLAIM as follows:

Establishment of a Military Administration.

1. A military administration to be called the British Military Administration is hereby established throughout such areas of Malaya as are at any given time under the control of the Forces under my command, and shall continue only so long as I consider it to be required by military necessity.

Assumption of powers and jurisdiction.

2. I hereby assume for myself and my successors full judicial, legislative, executive and administrative powers and responsibilities and conclusive jurisdiction over all persons and property throughout such areas of Malaya as are at any given time under the control of the Forces under my command.

Delegation.

3. Subject always to any orders and directions which I may issue from time to time, I delegate to the General Officer Commanding Military Forces, Malaya, all the powers, responsibilities and jurisdiction as he may deem necessary to any officer under his command and to empower such officer further to delegate any of such powers, responsibilities and jurisdiction.

Orders to be obeyed.

4. All persons will obey promptly all orders given by me or under my authority and must refrain from all acts which impede the Forces under my command or are helpful to the enemy, from all acts of violence, and from any act calculated to disturb public order in any way.

Existing laws etc. to be respected.

5. (1) Subject to the provisions of any Proclamation of the British Military Administration and in so far as military exigencies permit -

(a) all laws and customs existing immediately prior to the Japanese occupation will be respected;

Provided that such of the existing laws as the Chief Civil Affairs Officer considers it is practicable from time to time to administer during the period of military administration will be administered;

===== Page 69 =====
(b) All rights and properties will be respected:

Provided that rights and properties acquired during the Japanese occupation may be subject to investigation and to such action as justice requires.

(2) With regard to paragraph (a) of sub-section (1), the inhabitants of the said territories are advised to consult the nearest Civil Affairs Officer if in doubt as to whether any existing law is being administered.

Suspension of Courts.

6. All Courts and tribunals, other than military courts established under my authority, are hereby suspended and deprived of all authority and jurisdiction until authorized by me to re-open.

Revocation of Japanese Military Administration Proclamations.

7. It is hereby declared that all Proclamations and legislative enactments of whatever kind issued by or under the authority of the Japanese Military Administration shall cease to have any effect.

Short title.

8. This Proclamation may be cited as the Military Administration Proclamation.

Signed at this day of 194[UNCLEAR].

Supreme Allied Commander

Malaya Proclamation No. 1. Issue regarding "Martial Law"

1. I discussed this matter with Mr. Gent, Mr. Bourdillon, and Mr. Hastings at the Colonial Office this morning.

2. I stressed that the War Office desired the omission of "Martial Law" as a matter of policy and not as a matter of law. I also stressed the urgency of the time factor.

3. Mr. Gent stated that the term "Martial Law" had been deliberately included in the Proclamation for Borneo and deliberately excluded in the case of the Cocos Islands. In the case of Malaya the Colonial Office deliberately wished its inclusion. The Colonial Office was not concerned with what we had done in Burma.

4. After considerable discussion it was agreed:

(a) that if there was no insuperable legal objection (i.e., if the Act of Indemnity to be passed subsequently would legalize acts under the Proclamation irrespective of whether "Martial Law" appeared therein or not), the Colonial Office was prepared to accept the amended draft omitting "Martial Law".

(b) The Colonial Office would examine from the legal point of view this afternoon and inform me.

A Proclamation to make provision for the Control and Rationing of Food

I, General Officer Commanding Military Forces, Malaya, in exercise of the authority conferred upon me by the Supreme Allied Commander, South East Asia Command, DO HEREBY PROCLAIM AND ORDER

Short title.

1. This Proclamation may be cited as the Food Control Proclamation.

Interpretation.

2. In this Proclamation, unless the context otherwise requires -

"animal" includes birds, reptiles, fish and every kind of vertebrate animal and the young thereof, and the eggs of birds, reptiles and fish;

"controlled article" means any foodstuff which has been declared by the Controller to be a controlled article;

"Controller" means the officer appointed by the Chief Civil Affairs Officer to be Food Controller and includes a Deputy Food Controller;

"foodstuff" means any animal whether alive or dead, and any substance or commodity, which is used as food by man or which is used for feeding any animal which serves some purpose for the use of man, whether as food or otherwise, or which ordinarily enters into the composition or preparation of human food or of the food of any such animal, and includes rice bran containing more than ten per centum of whole or broken grain rice;

"offence against this Proclamation" includes any contravention of or failure to comply with any order, rule or regulation made under this Proclamation and

===== Page 96 =====
Any contravention of or failure to comply with the conditions of any licence or permit issued or authority granted in pursuance of the provisions of this Proclamation, and any failure to comply with any request or direction lawfully made or given thereunder;

"Rationed foodstuff" means any foodstuff which may from time to time be declared by the Controller to be rationed;

"Retail dealing" includes every sale of any article other than a wholesale dealing;

"Wholesale dealing" means any sale of any article, commodity, or thing in whatever quantity for resale in the same form or state or as part of a manufactured product.

3. The Chief Civil Affairs Officer may appoint a Food Controller whose duty it shall be to regulate and control the food supply of Malaya. The Chief Civil Affairs Officer may also appoint a Deputy Food Controller and Assistant Food Controllers for such areas as he may think fit.

4. (1) The Controller shall have the following powers:

(i) to declare any foodstuff to be a controlled article or to be a rationed foodstuff or both, either generally or with reference to some specified part of Malaya;

(ii) to fix maximum prices for the sale of any foodstuff, whether wholesale or retail;

(iii) to prohibit the purchase, sale or barter of any controlled article for the purpose of resale in or export from any area specified without the written permission of the Controller;

===== Page 97 =====
(iv) to prohibit or control the import or export of any controlled article to or from any specified area;

(v) to prohibit or control the movement of controlled articles between any specified areas;

(vi) to limit the wholesale or retail dealing in any controlled article to such persons as shall hold licences or permits therefor under this Proclamation or any rules or orders thereunder;

(vii) to restrict the sale of any controlled article either by any individual or generally by all persons dealing in such article in any manner which he shall deem fit;

(viii) to prohibit, restrict or otherwise regulate or control the manufacture or production of any controlled article either generally or as regards form, shape, quantity, quality, constituents or otherwise;

(ix) to regulate and control the rationing of rationed foodstuffs; to provide for the registration of all or any persons for the purposes of such rationing, and for the issue of registration cards, ration cards and other rationing documents; to appoint enumerators to enumerate the public for the purposes of registration and rationing; and to regulate the distribution of controlled articles;

(x) to prescribe such forms as he may think necessary in connection with the matters referred to in paragraphs (i) to (ix).

===== Page 98 =====
(2) The powers of the Controller referred to in sub-section (1) of this section may be exercised either by orders in writing under his hand or by rules or regulations which may apply to the whole or any part of Malaya, and such orders, rules, or regulations may be altered, varied, or revoked by the Controller from time to time. Any such orders, rules, or regulations shall be published either by posting the same in prominent places accessible to the public, by publication in the Gazette, or by such other means as may be from time to time directed by the Chief Civil Affairs Officer.

Hoarding of Controlled Articles Prohibited.

5. No person shall hoard, conceal, destroy, or withhold from the market any controlled article. Any person acting in contravention of this Section shall be guilty of an offence against this Proclamation.

Power of Controller to Deal with Hoarding.

6. (1) If the Controller has reason to believe that any foodstuff is being hoarded, concealed, or withheld from the market contrary to the provisions of Section 5, he may:
   (i) take possession of any supply of that foodstuff in such manner as he shall deem fit; or
   (ii) give such orders and directions and take such measures in relation to supplies of that foodstuff as may appear to him necessary or expedient in order that any such foodstuff so hoarded, concealed or withheld may be placed on the market.
   (2) Where, under the provision of this Section, possession has been taken of any supply of any foodstuff by, or on behalf of the Controller, then the Controller:
   (i) if in his opinion the owner of such foodstuff has hoarded, concealed or withheld any such foodstuff and criminal proceedings have been

===== Page 99 =====

(ii) In any other case, may restore possession to the owner, or

(a) Where a maximum price has been fixed in respect of such foodstuff, may pay to the owner of such foodstuff the price so fixed less an amount not exceeding twenty-five per centum;

(b) Where no maximum price has been fixed in respect of such foodstuff, may forthwith fix such maximum price in accordance with the provisions of Section 4 and pay to the owner of such foodstuff the maximum price so fixed, less an amount not exceeding twenty-five per centum.

9. Power of the Food Controller to sell animals or perishable foodstuffs.

If possession has been taken of any foodstuff in circumstances in which there is ground to believe that an offence against this Proclamation has been committed and criminal proceedings have been instituted against any person in respect of such offence, then, if such foodstuff is a live animal or if the Food Controller is satisfied that such foodstuff, by reason of its perishable nature or otherwise, is liable to deteriorate, he may order that the foodstuff shall be sold in such manner and at such price as may be specified by him. Use of Information in Evidence.

Any information given by any person in compliance or purported compliance with any request made or in response to any question put to him under this Proclamation may be given in evidence in any proceedings to which he is a party, notwithstanding that it may tend to incriminate him, provided that it is otherwise admissible in evidence.

Burden of Proof.

14. In any prosecution under this Proclamation upon a charge of doing any act which is unlawful unless the person doing such act is entitled to do so by reason of his being the holder of a licence, permit or written permission, consent, authorisation or exemption entitling him to do such act, it shall be sufficient for the prosecution to allege and prove his doing of such act and the onus shall then be upon the accused to show that he was entitled to do such act.

Unlawful Possession of Controlled Articles.

15. Any person in possession or control of any controlled article in such circumstances as to raise a reasonable suspicion that an offence against this Proclamation has been or is intended to be committed by him in relation to such controlled article, shall be guilty of an offence against this Proclamation and shall be liable to trial and punishment in the manner provided in section 17, unless he satisfies the Court that such offence has not been and was not intended to be committed by him.

Power of Court to Confiscate.

16. (1) Where any person is convicted of an offence against this Proclamation, the Court may order the confiscation in whole or in part of -

===== Page 104 =====
(i) any article in respect of which the offence has been committed which has been seized by, or has otherwise come into the possession of the Controller or of any person acting under his authority, or of any vehicle or other article by means of which such offence has been committed or which is, in the opinion of the Court, intended to be used for the commission of an offence against this Proclamation, or

(ii) the proceeds of sale of any such article if it has been sold under section 9 of this Proclamation.

(2) Where confiscation is ordered under sub-section (1) hereof, the Court shall deliver such article or the proceeds thereof (if not already in the possession of the Controller) to the Controller or to any person designated by him, and shall give directions as to their disposal. Confiscated articles shall ordinarily be sold and the proceeds paid into the Treasury of the British Military Administration.

Penalty

17. (1) Any person who commits an offence against this Proclamation shall be liable on conviction, in addition to any confiscation which may be ordered under Section 16, to the following penalties:

(a) for every first offence to a fine not exceeding five thousand dollars or to imprisonment for a term which may extend to one year or to both such fine and imprisonment;

(b) for every subsequent offence, to a fine not exceeding twenty thousand dollars or imprisonment for a term which may extend to three years or to both such fine and imprisonment.

In addition to any such penalty as aforesaid, the Court may order cancellation of any licence or permit issued under this Proclamation.

A Proclamation to proclaim Martial Law and to establish Military Administration.

WHEREAS by reason of military necessity and for the prevention and suppression of disorder and the maintenance of public safety it is necessary to place the territories of the Settlements of Singapore, Penang and Malacca, all islands and places forming part thereof and all British waters adjacent thereto, and the Malay States of Perak, Selangor, Negri Sembilan, Pahang, Johore, Kedah, Kelantan, Trengganu and Perlis, all islands forming part of such States and the territorial waters thereof (hereinafter called Malaya) under martial law, military administration:

NOW, THEREFORE, I Admiral Lord Louis Mountbatten Knight Grand Cross of the Royal Victorian Order, Commander of the Most Honourable Order of the Bath, Companion of the Distinguished Service Order, Aide de Camp to His Majesty the King, Supreme Allied Commander South East Asia Command hereby PROCLAIM as follows:

1. Martial law is hereby established throughout such areas of Malaya as are at any given time under the control of the Forces under my command, and shall be enforced only so long as I consider it to be necessary required by military necessity.

Assumption of powers and jurisdiction.

Orders to be obeyed.

4. All persons will obey promptly all orders given by me or under my authority and must refrain from all acts which impede the Forces under my command or are helpful to the enemy, from all acts of violence, and from any act calculated to disturb public order in any way.

In that instance, martial law had to be proclaimed following civil disturbances within His Majesty's dominions. The textbooks draw a distinction between that species of martial law and the martial law, subject to the principles of International law, which is imposed by the will of a military commander upon territory occupied in war. The latter kind of martial law would not, I think, be aptly described by the words quoted. (b) the written law of the Federated Malay States as regards the four Federated Malay States and the written law of any one of such States as regards that State;

(c) the written law of the State of Johore, Kedah, Kelantan, Trengganu or Perlis respectively as regards each such State.

13. 15. (3) In cases coming to them respectively for review under this section, the General Officer Commanding Military Forces, Malaya, and the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer may exercise any of the powers conferred by section 12 of this Proclamation upon a confirming authority, notwithstanding any action which may have been taken previously upon the same case by a confirming authority.

15. RULES OF PROCEDURE FOR MILITARY COURTS (MALAY PENINSULA)

In exercise of the powers conferred upon me by section 22 of the Military Courts (Malay Peninsula) Proclamation, I, Herbert Ralph Hone, C.B.E., M.C., K.C., Chief Civil Affairs Officer, hereby make the following Rules.

1. These Rules may be cited as the Military Courts Procedure (Malay Peninsula) Rules and shall have effect in all areas of the Malay Peninsula in which Courts are established under the provisions of the Military Courts (Malay Peninsula) Proclamation as from the respective dates of the establishment of such Courts.

2. These Rules shall be read with and subject to the Proclamations and Orders of the British Military Administration.

3. In these Rules unless the context otherwise requires:

"Assessor Court", "British Officer Court", "Confirming Authority", "Court", "President", and "Presiding Officer" have the respective meanings assigned to them in the Military Courts (Malay Peninsula) Proclamation;

"Forces" means the British and Allied Naval, Land and Air Forces;

"Police" means the Police Force of the Civil Affairs Service; and

"Reviewing Authority" means the officer or officers empowered by the Military Courts (Malay Peninsula) Proclamation to review a judgment, sentence or order of a Court.

COURTS, PROSECUTORS AND COUNSEL, AND OFFICIALS.

4. (1) Before proceeding to hear any case, a Court shall satisfy itself that it is properly constituted and that no member of the Court has a personal interest in, or prejudice with regard to, the case or will be needed as a witness therein, and that it has jurisdiction over the person to be tried and the offence, and in the case of a British Officer Court, the President shall administer to each member of the Court an affirmation in the form set out in the First Schedule hereto.

Record

(2) The President or Presiding Officer shall be responsible for making and signing the record.

Change in membership.

(3) No addition to or substitution in the membership of the Court shall be made in the course of a trial. No member who has been absent at any time shall take any further part in the trial.

===== Page 194 =====
Prosecutor and Counsel.

5. (1) Any person appointed by the Legal Officer or Deputy Legal Officer, any Police Officer of or over the rank of Inspector, any officer of the Forces or any other person acceptable to the Court may act as prosecutor.

(2) Any Advocate and Solicitor permitted by the Legal Officer, Deputy Legal Officer or by the Court, or any other person with the consent of the Court may conduct the defence of the accused. The Court may appoint an officer of the Forces or, with the consent of the accused, designate an Advocate and Solicitor to represent the accused or assist in his defence. Before the Superior Court where a sentence of death may be imposed and the accused is not represented, the Court shall appoint an officer of the Forces or other fit person to represent him.

Officials.

6. A Senior Civil Affairs Officer may appoint interpreters, reporters, and other officials who need not be members of the Forces in and about the business of the Courts generally. A Court may appoint interpreters and reporters for the purpose of particular proceedings or parts thereof or for any term or session of the Court. Any interpreter or reporter shall, before assuming his duties, make affirmation in the appropriate form set out in the First Schedule hereto.

PRELIMINARY PROCEEDINGS

Commencement of proceedings.

7. (1) All proceedings in the Court shall be commenced by summons to appear, warrant of arrest or arrest without warrant.

(2) A warrant of arrest or summons to an accused person may be issued by any Court, and may be executed or served by any member of the Police or of the Forces or any person acting under the authority thereof.

(3) A summons to attend as a witness may be issued by a Court or any Police Officer of or over the rank of Inspector and may be served by any member of the Police or of the Forces or any person acting under the authority thereof.

Search.

8. (1) When a Court considers that the purposes of justice or of any inquiry, trial or other proceeding will be served by a search it may issue a search warrant and the person to whom such warrant is directed may search or inspect in accordance therewith.

(2) Any person acting under the authority of the Military Administration may without a warrant from a Court:

(a) arrest and search any person suspected of having committed or being about to commit an offence against any Proclamation or rule, regulation or order made thereunder or against the laws and usages of war;

(b) enter and search any place and seize and take away any property for the purpose of investigating any such offence or arresting the offender or preventing any such offence where there are grounds for believing that it is intended to be committed.

===== Page 195 =====
(3) No woman shall be searched except by another woman.

(4) Any person authorised to carry out a search under these Rules or under the provisions of any written law or Proclamation or rule, regulation or order made thereunder relating to search may lawfully use all reasonable force necessary to break or enter a place to be searched and may lawfully detain without warrant during the period of the search any person found in such place.

Charges.

9. (1) A copy of the charges shall be delivered to an accused person or his representative as soon as practicable after arrest and in any event before trial. Where proceedings are commenced by service of a summons adequately stating the charges, no separate charge sheet need be used. A copy of any amendment to a charge shall also be given to the accused unless waived in open Court by him or his representative.

(2) Each charge shall disclose one offence only and shall be particularised sufficiently to identify the place, the time and the subject matter of the alleged offence, and shall specify the provision under which the offence is charged.

Joinder of Charges.

(3) Any number of charges may be contained in the same charge sheet and alternative charges may be based on the same facts.

Conviction of minor offence.

(4) When a person is charged with an offence consisting of several particulars, a combination of some only of which constitutes a complete minor offence, and such combination is proved but the remaining particulars are not proved, he may be convicted of the minor offence though he is not charged with it.

(5) When a person is charged with an offence and facts are proved which reduce it to a minor offence, he may be convicted of the minor offence although he is not charged with it.

Joinder of persons.

(6) Two or more persons may be charged and tried jointly for the same offence where the charges arise out of the same set of circumstances.

Arraignment and Plea.

10. (1) Every person arrested for an offence, with or without a warrant, shall be brought, as soon as practicable, before the lowest Court with jurisdiction to try the offence or offences charged, except that the Chief Legal Officer, the Legal Officer or any officer authorised by either of them may order that any particular case or class of cases may be brought before the Superior Court for trial.

Hearing may be deferred.

(2) A Court, on the accused appearing before it, may defer the hearing if the accused has had insufficient time to prepare his defence or for other sufficient reason and, in that event, shall order the accused to be detained in custody or released on or without bail on such terms as the Court thinks fit.

Arraignment.

(3) In the event of a decision to proceed, the Court shall read to the accused the charges brought against him and ask the accused, after the reading of each charge, whether he pleads guilty or not guilty to it. If necessary the Court shall explain these terms.

===== Page 196 =====
To the accused. The Court shall enter in the record of the case the plea made to each charge.

Pleas.

(4) The Court may accept a plea of guilty to an offence other than that charged and a plea of not guilty to the offence charged.

(5) If the answer of the accused to any charge is such that it appears he may not be guilty of the offence charged, then, whatever his plea may be, the Court shall enter a plea of not guilty.

Failure to plead.

(6) If the accused refuses to or does not plead, the Court shall enter a plea of not guilty.

Plea to capital charge.

(7) If the accused tenders a plea of guilty of an offence for which the Court may impose a sentence of death, such plea shall be entered as one of not guilty.

PROCEDURE ON PLEA OF GUILTY.

Procedure on plea of guilty.

11. Upon a plea of guilty to all offences charged, the Court shall convict and shall hear such statements for the prosecution and the defence and such evidence as it requires to enable it to determine the sentence or sentences to be imposed.

TRIAL PROCEDURE.

Outline of trial procedure.

12. (1) Upon a plea of not guilty, Courts shall be guided by the following outline of procedure:

Opening.

(a) A statement may be made by the prosecutor outlining the facts to be proved by the prosecution. The witnesses for the prosecution shall then be called.

(b) After each witness has given his evidence in chief, the accused or his representative shall be given an opportunity to cross-examine him.

Re-examination.

(c) The prosecutor may re-examine any prosecution witness upon any matter arising out of the cross-examination or, with the Court's consent, upon any other matter.

Close of Prosecution.

(d) After the close of the case for the prosecution, the Court may discharge or acquit the accused on any charge if it decides that there is not sufficient evidence to support the charge and that the accused should not be required to answer it. Any such discharge or acquittal shall be entered in the record of the case. The Court may, on the application of the prosecution, also direct that any further or amended charge or charges be preferred against the accused and may grant any necessary adjournment for that purpose or for the purpose of enabling the accused to prepare his defence to any such further or amended charge.

===== Page 197 =====
Defence called upon.

(e) If the Court considers that there is evidence which, if unrebutted, would justify a conviction, it shall call on the accused to enter on his defence. The Court shall then inform the accused that he is not compelled to give evidence or to make a statement, but that he may elect to give evidence on affirmation, in which case he may be cross-examined, or he may elect to make an unaffirmed statement, in which case he will not be liable to be cross-examined.

Opening of defence.

(f) The accused or his representative may then state the facts and law on which he intends to rely and make such comments as he thinks necessary on the evidence of the prosecution, followed by the calling, examination, cross-examination and re-examination of the witnesses, if any, for the defence.

Witnesses for defence.

(g) If the accused elects to be called as a witness, his evidence shall be taken before that of other witnesses for the defence and he may be cross-examined by the prosecution and by or on behalf of any other accused person.

Witnesses in rebuttal.

(h) When all the witnesses for the defence have been called the prosecution may, with leave of the Court, call or recall any witness for the purpose of contradicting any new matter raised by the defence which the prosecution could not have reasonably foreseen.

Court may question or recall witness.

(i) The Court may at any stage of the examination question any witness and may call or recall any witness at any time before the finding if it considers it necessary in the interests of justice so to do.

Order of final reply.

(j) If any witness, other than the accused or a witness as to character only, is called by the defence the prosecutor shall have the right of final reply on the whole case, but if no such witness is called, the defence shall have the right of final reply on the whole case.

Findings.

(k) The findings shall then be considered and shall be announced in open Court, subject to any adjournment which the Court deems necessary.

Acquittal.

(l) In the event of acquittal on all charges the accused shall be acquitted and discharged.

Character.

(m) In the event of conviction, there shall be heard statements, and evidence, if tendered, for the prosecution and defence, including evidence of any prior conviction, bearing upon the sentence to be imposed.

Sentence.

(n) A President shall then consult with the members of the Court, or with the assessors as the case may be, and pass any sentence authorised by law and announce the same in [POSSIBLY MISSING].

===== Page 198 =====
Open Court. A Presiding Officer shall pass sentence and announce the same in open Court.

Remission to Superior Court.

(c) If at any stage of the proceedings it appears to a District Court that there is a prima facie case of an offence beyond its jurisdiction having been committed, the Court may stay the proceedings and remit the case for trial before the Superior Court.

(p) A Court may grant any necessary adjournment at any stage of a proceeding and may issue remand warrants in the form contained in the First Schedule.

Affirmation.

(2) Each witness shall make affirmation in the form contained in the First Schedule hereto before giving evidence, except that a child under fourteen years of age who, in the judgment of the Court, does not understand the nature of an affirmation but nevertheless understands the duty of speaking the truth may give evidence without being affirmed. The affirmation may be administered either in English or in any other language and will bear all the duties and liabilities of an oath taken in solemn form.

Trial in camera.

(3) The Court shall have power to order a trial or any part of a trial to be held in camera if it is necessary to prevent any prejudice to the security of the Forces or for some other exceptional reason.

EVIDENCE. AMENDMENTS.

Evidence.

13. (1) A Court shall in general admit oral, written and physical evidence having a bearing on the issues before it and may exclude any evidence which in its opinion is of no value as proof.

(2) The Court shall in general require the production of the best evidence available.

(3) Evidence of bad character of an accused person shall be admissible before finding only when the accused person has introduced evidence as to his own good character or as to the bad character of any witness for the prosecution.

(4) In all other respects relating to the rules of evidence Courts shall be guided, in so far as may be practicable, by the law of evidence in force at the place of trial immediately prior to the 8th December, 1941.

Amendment of Charges and Pleas.

14. (1) A Court may amend a charge at any time before finding provided that an adjournment be granted if necessary and that no injustice be thereby done to the accused.

(2) An accused person may, at any time before the finding, with the leave of the Court, alter a plea of not guilty to one of guilty.

(3) The Court may, of its own motion, or at the request of the accused, at any time before sentence, alter a plea of guilty to one of not guilty.

===== Page 199 =====
FINDING. SENTENCES.

15. (1) A Court shall announce and record its finding on each charge before it and shall pronounce separate sentences in respect of all the charges upon which the accused is found guilty. Such punishments when consisting of imprisonment shall be consecutive or concurrent as the Court directs; but it shall not be necessary for a District Court by reason only of the aggregate punishment for the several offences being in excess of the punishment which it is competent to inflict on conviction of one single offence to send the offender for trial before the Superior Court.

Date at which imprisonment is to commence.

(2) Every sentence of imprisonment shall state the date of commencement thereof, which, if the accused was previously in custody, shall ordinarily make allowance for the period of custody.

Imprisonment in default of payment of fine.

(3) A Court when imposing any fine shall impose a sentence of imprisonment to be served in default of payment of such fine and shall direct the period within which the fine shall be paid, and may allow payment by instalments. The Court may also issue a warrant for the levy of the amount by distress and sale of any property belonging to the offender.

Levy of fine by distress and sale.

(4) A warrant for the levy of a fine by distress and sale may be executed at any place in the Malay Peninsula. If it is required to be executed in the Settlement of Singapore it shall be endorsed by a President or Presiding Officer having jurisdiction within the district where it is to be so executed.

Limits of imprisonment in default of payment of fine.

(5) The term for which the Court directs the offender to be imprisoned in default of payment of a fine shall be as follows:

(a) If the offence is punishable with imprisonment for any term exceeding six months, it shall not exceed one-fourth of the term of imprisonment which is the maximum fixed for the offence.

(b) If the offence is punishable with imprisonment for any term not exceeding six months, it shall not exceed one half the term of imprisonment which is the maximum fixed for the offence.

(c) If the offence is not punishable with imprisonment, it shall not exceed:

(i) When the fine does not exceed twenty-five dollars, one month;

(ii) When the fine exceeds twenty-five dollars but does not exceed fifty dollars, two months;

(iii) In any other case, three months.

Imprisonment in default to cease payment.

(6) Any imprisonment which is being served in default of payment of a fine shall terminate whenever that fine is either paid or levied by process of law.

===== Page 200 =====
(7) If, before the expiration of the time of imprisonment fixed in default of payment, such a proportion of the fine is paid or levied that the time of imprisonment suffered in default of payment is not less than proportional to the part of the fine still unpaid, the imprisonment shall terminate.

Suspension of sentence.

16. A Court in imposing any sentence may, in exceptional circumstances, suspend the execution thereof in whole or in part on such terms as it thinks fit and may apply the provisions of the law in force at the place of trial immediately prior to the 8th December, 1941, relating to release of offenders on probation.

Sentence of death not to be passed against person under sixteen years.

17. Sentence of death shall not be pronounced on or recorded against a person who in the opinion of the Court was under the age of sixteen years at the time of the offence; but in lieu thereof the Court shall sentence such person to be detained until further order and thereupon he shall be liable to be detained in such place and under such conditions as the Chief Civil Affairs Officer directs, and, whilst so detained, shall be deemed to be in legal custody.

Sentence of death not to be passed on pregnant women.

18. (1) Where a woman convicted of an offence punishable with death alleges that she is pregnant, or where the Court before whom a woman is so convicted thinks fit so to order, the question whether or not the woman is pregnant shall be determined by the Court which returned the finding of guilty, or if such Court has ceased to sit, by the Superior Court.

(2) If such woman is found to be pregnant, a sentence of imprisonment shall be passed on her and such finding and sentence shall be subject to confirmation.

(3) If such woman is found not to be pregnant, she may file a petition for review of that finding, and the Reviewing Authority, if satisfied that for any reason the finding should be set aside, shall, if she has been sentenced to death, commute the sentence to one of imprisonment.

RESTITUTION AND CLOSING ORDERS AND RESTRICTIONS ON RESIDENCE.

Restitution Orders.

19. A Court may:

(a) make such order as it thinks fit relating to movable property seized by a police officer or any officer of the Forces under the suspicion of the commission of an offence or in the process of a search, respecting the delivery of such property to the person entitled to the possession thereof, or if such person cannot be ascertained, respecting the custody and production of such property;

(b) order the restitution to the lawful owner, or the forfeiture to, or temporary custody by, the Forces or a local public authority, of any property including any proceeds thereof, whether in their original form or converted

===== Page 201 =====
Into some other form of property, where the accused is found guilty of an offence of which the illegal possession, use, purchase or sale of such property is an essential element.

20. In addition to or in lieu of any sentence a Court may:

(a) order the closing of any business premises or residence, the suspension of any business, or the withdrawal or suspension of any licence for the operation of the same, or any similar business, where the accused is found guilty of the illegal operation of a business, and, in any such case, may order the forfeiture to, or temporary custody by, the Forces of any stock in trade to which such illegal operation relates;

(b) order the accused to establish his residence within or without a designated area or not to leave or enter a designated area without permission.

INQUIRIES INTO DEATHS.

Inquiries to be held.

21. (1) In all cases of Judicial execution or of the death of a prisoner in a prison or lock-up or of an inmate of a lunatic asylum or where the dead body of a person has been found or exhumed and there is reason to suspect that such person came by his death in a sudden or unnatural manner or by violence, or if the manner in which such person came by his death is unknown, a District Civil Affairs Officer or the Presiding Officer of the District Court within whose jurisdiction the death occurred or such body was found shall inquire into the circumstances of such death and, in so doing, may summon witnesses and record evidence and employ all lawful means to discover the identity of the deceased and how and after what manner the deceased came by his death and whether any person is criminally responsible thereof.

(2) A District Civil Affairs Officer or Presiding Officer shall keep or cause to be kept a record of all inquiries into deaths and shall forward his findings thereon as soon as practicable to the Legal Officer.

Warrant of arrest.

(3) If the officer holding the inquiry is of opinion, during the course or at the close of such proceedings, that sufficient grounds are disclosed for charging any person with having caused or assisted in causing the death of the deceased person, he may issue his warrant for the apprehension and committal of such person to prison to be brought before a Court to be prosecuted according to law and he may bind over any witness who has been examined before him in a recognition with or without surety to appear and give evidence before such Court.

Copy of depositions.

(4) Every person who has been committed to prison as aforesaid may require, and shall be entitled to, a copy of the evidence recorded and of his own statement, if any, and that of any other accused person.

===== Page 202 =====
Bail.

(5) Where a person has been committed to prison by an officer holding an inquiry, such officer may accept bail if he thinks fit, in cash or with good and sufficient sureties for the appearance of the person so committed, and thereupon such person, if in custody, shall be discharged therefrom.

When guilty person unknown.

(6) If the officer holding an inquiry is of opinion, at the close of such proceedings, that there is ground for suspecting that some person is guilty of an offence in respect of the matter inquired into, but he cannot ascertain who such person is, he shall transmit a copy of the record made by him on such inquiry to the Senior Officer of Police stationed in the District, together with a certificate to that effect.

GENERAL PROVISIONS.

Attendance of Witnesses.

22. (1) A Court shall have power to summon as a witness any person except a child under fourteen years of age, in which case it may summon the parent or guardian to bring the child to attend as a witness, and except a member of the Forces, in which case it may request the Commanding Officer of such member to order his attendance.

(2) Any person whom the Court may summon as a witness may be ordered to bring with him any document or article in his possession or under his control which has a bearing on the issue in the case.

Warrant to secure attendance of Witness.

(3) In any case in which it is made to appear to a Court that any person whose evidence is required is unlikely to obey a summons to attend or is evading service of a summons to attend or that any person, having received a summons, has failed, without reasonable excuse, to attend, the Court may issue a warrant for his arrest and may take bail or security or make such other order as may be necessary to secure his attendance when called upon.

Summonses and warrants may be served or executed anywhere in Malay Peninsula.

23. (1) Any summons or warrant issued in the Malay Peninsula may be served or executed in any part thereof.

Singapore Summonses and Warrants.

(2) Any summons or warrant issued in the Settlement of Singapore may be served or executed in any part of the Malay Peninsula if it is indorsed by a President of the Superior Court of the Malay Peninsula or by a Presiding Officer of a Court having jurisdiction within the District where the summons or warrant is to be served or executed.

Compellability of Witnesses.

24. Any person other than the accused may be required to testify before a Court except a person of unsound mind; provided that no witness shall be required to incriminate himself; and provided also that a Court shall not compel:

(1) a husband or wife, or a parent or child, to give evidence against the other; or

===== Page 203 =====
(2) A qualified legal adviser is not required to disclose any communication between himself and a client made in the course of a professional relationship except when the communication was part of or in furtherance of a fraud or crime;

(3) A priest is not required to disclose any communication made in the course of a confession.

Impounding

25. A Court may, in its discretion, impound, by an order directing any person to be charged with the care thereof, any document or article relating to proceedings before it, whether or not it has been received in evidence.

Insanity.

26. Whenever a Court is satisfied that the accused is unable, by reason of insanity, to understand the nature of the charge against him or the proceedings of the Court, or that the accused committed the offence for which he is being tried but was insane when he committed it, the Court shall record a finding accordingly and may make an order providing for temporary custody pending direction by the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer for permanent custody or other disposition, and, in the case of any such order, shall forward the record forthwith to the Legal Officer for the direction of the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer to be obtained.

Youthful Offenders.

27. As far as is practicable, youthful offenders shall be dealt with in accordance with the laws relating to youthful offenders in force at the place of trial prior to the 8th December, 1941.

Records.

28. (1) Every Court shall in every case make a record containing the date and place of its proceedings, the names of the members of the Court or assessors, if any, the name of the accused, of the prosecutor and of the defence counsel, the original charge-sheet or summons and pleas, any amendments to the charge or plea made during the course of the trial and the findings and the sentences or orders of the Court. In addition, in every case in which there is a plea of not guilty, the record shall contain minutes or a summary of the essential evidence.

(2) In every case in which any finding and sentence are subject to confirmation, or where a petition for review has been addressed to the trial Court, such Court shall, as soon as practicable after sentence or after receipt of the petition for review, forward a true copy of the record and of the petition, if any, for confirmation by the Confirming Authority or review by the Reviewing Authority as the case may be.

(3) Every Court shall keep a permanent register of its proceedings in the Form in the First Schedule.

Effect of Irregularities.

29. No proceedings shall be invalidated nor any sentence set aside for any error or omission technical or otherwise occurring in the proceedings unless, in the opinion of the Confirming or Reviewing Authority after an examination of the entire record, it shall appear that the error or omission has resulted in injustice to the accused.

===== Page 204 =====
30. (1) In any matter in which no procedure is directed by these Rules or by any Proclamation or rule, regulation or order made thereunder, every Court shall be guided by the law relating to criminal procedure in force at the place of trial immediately prior to the eighth day of December, 1941, in so far as such law is ascertainable and capable of being applied. If such law is unascertainable or inapplicable, the Court may adopt such procedure as it thinks best provided that no injustice is done to the accused and, in any such case, a note of the procedure adopted shall be made in the record of the case and a report shall be made to the appropriate Reviewing Authority.

(2) Notwithstanding anything contained in sub-Rule (1) of this Rule, no Court shall, during the period of Military Administration, give effect to those provisions of the laws relating to criminal procedure which are set out in the Second Schedule hereto or to any further or other provision relating to criminal procedure which the Chief Civil Affairs Officer may from time to time direct to be included in the said Schedule.

31. Forms used by Courts shall conform substantially with those set out in the First Schedule hereto with such modification as may be necessary in particular cases.

INSTRUCTIONS.

1. Every Military Court shall maintain this register as a permanent record of its proceedings. It should form an accurate and complete summary of each proceeding. Every case shall be entered and numbered in its chronological order. If an officer who has been presiding over a Court is moved to another locality, he should hand over the Register to his successor.

2. Each place in which a Court holds sessions shall have a separate Register.

3. Each Register shall be kept in duplicate. One copy shall be retained by the Court and the other copy shall be forwarded at the end of each month, in the case of the Superior Court to the Legal Officer and in the case of a District Court to the Deputy Legal Officer of the Region.

4. Entries with respect to sentence and orders of the Court in the Register and in the Case Record must be complete (including date of commencement of sentence of imprisonment, terms of suspension of sentence, any further order in the case, etc.) so that further action can be taken by the Court or by the Confirming or Reviewing Authority from either the Register or the Case Record as the case may be.

5. Dates relating to all action taken in confirmation and review must be carefully recorded. The date of filing a petition for review shall be taken to be the date upon which the accused filed the petition in Court or handed the same to the gaoler of the prison in which he was confined; and the date on which confirmation is communicated to the accused shall be the date upon which the accused received such confirmation.

My dear Gent,

Your 55104/25/44

There has been a certain amount of discussion between C.A.4 and ourselves regarding the Martial Law and Civil Affairs Service (Administrative Divisions) Proclamations.

2. I enclose amended copies of these two Proclamations as agreed between C.A.4 and ourselves.

3. Martial Law Proclamation

The General Staff will not agree to a direct delegation from the S.A.C. to the C.C.A.O. They insist, and Major-General Hone has agreed, that the C.C.A.O. should derive his authority from the Military Commander in Malaya directing operations. Hence the redraft of Clause 3.

The only other amendment is Clause 4(1)(b). As you are aware the intention is to carry out investigations regarding land transactions, civil judgements etc. made during the Japanese occupation and these investigations will be carried out under Proclamations still to be drafted. When these Proclamations are drafted the details covering those investigations will have to be carefully considered. Hence Clause 4(1)(b), which is merely a notification to the public of Malaya that any rights and properties acquired during the Japanese occupation may be subject to investigation, has been redrafted in wider terms so that our hands may not be fettered in any way when we come to draft the Proclamations on these matters.

Proclamation No. 1 of 194.

A Proclamation to proclaim Martial Law and to establish Military Administration.

WHEREAS by reason of military necessity and for the prevention and suppression of disorder and the maintenance of public safety it is necessary to place the territories of the Settlements of Singapore, Penang and Malacca, all islands and places forming part thereof and all British waters adjacent thereto, and the Malay States of Perak, Selangor, Negri Sembilan, Pahang, Johore, Kedah, Kelantan, Trengganu and Perlis, all islands forming part of such States and the territorial waters thereof (hereinafter called "Malaya") under martial law as martial law is understood and administered in territories under His Majesty's jurisdiction and protection:

NOW, THEREFORE, I

hereby proclaim as follows:-

1. Martial law is hereby proclaimed throughout such areas of Malaya as are at any given time occupied by the Forces under my command, and shall be enforced only so long as I consider it to be necessary.

2. I hereby assume for myself and my successors full judicial, legislative, executive and administrative powers and responsibilities and conclusive jurisdiction over all persons and property throughout such areas of Malaya as are at any given time occupied by the Forces under my command.

3. Subject always to any orders and directions which I may issue from time to time, I delegate to the General Officer Commanding the British and Allied Forces in Malaya

===== Page 236 =====
All the powers, responsibilities and jurisdiction assumed by me, and such General Officer Commanding is allowed to delegate such powers, responsibilities and jurisdiction as he may deem necessary to any officer under his command.

4. (1) Subject to the provisions of any Proclamation of the Military Administration and insofar as military exigencies permit -

(a) all laws and customs existing immediately prior to the Japanese occupation will be respected:

Provided that such of the existing laws as the Chief Civil Affairs Officer considers it is practicable from time to time to administer during the period of military administration will be administered;

(b) all rights and properties will be respected:

Provided that rights and properties acquired during the Japanese occupation may be subject to investigation and to such action as justice requires.

(2) With regard to paragraph (a) of subsection (1), the inhabitants of the said territories are advised to consult the nearest Civil Affairs Officer if in doubt as to whether any existing law is being administered.

5. It is hereby declared that all Proclamations and legislative enactments of whatever kind issued by or under the authority of the Japanese Military Administration shall cease to have any effect.

6. This Proclamation may be cited as the Martial Law Proclamation, 194[UNCLEAR].

Signed at this day of 194[UNCLEAR].
