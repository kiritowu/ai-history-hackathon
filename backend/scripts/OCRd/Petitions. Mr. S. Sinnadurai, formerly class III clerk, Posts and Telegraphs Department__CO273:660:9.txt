The petition concerned Class III claims in the Posts and Telegraphs Department, dismissed as those services terminated after 1918. This request is for the payment of a pension from the date on which these services were terminated.

The High Commissioner's report shows that his petition was inaccurate in important respects. In particular, it was not true that he had a clean record until the case under the petition at Siembien. On the contrary, it showed 14 fines and 20 cautions prior to July 1918, which from the petition would seem to be the date on which he became Mr. S. Thumburch's subordinate officer. It is also not true to say that he was not given an opportunity of clearing himself of the charges brought against him. The decision to discharge his services only followed after his reply to the charges had been considered in accordance with General Order No. 24.

The Ash Governor Cayton was to inform the petitioner that the Secretary of State has consigned his petition as not to be pursued to interment and send a copy of the draft to the High Commissioner (F77.S of 6).

This is difficult. Mr. Sinnadurai was dismissed; the Governor decided that this was too severe, and presumably cancelled the dismissal. It was open to the Governor to substitute another recognised penalty. We can find no reference in the General Orders to retirement for inefficiency, but assuming that there was such a thing, it is, in circumstances like this, the only alternative of which I am aware to reinstatement in the service. I have never heard of compulsory retirement constituting dismissal, except on grounds of age or inefficiency. It seems therefore that the Governor's order was self-contradictory, i.e., the direction that the petitioner's services were to be dispensed with amounted to dismissal, which he had said was too harsh.

In any case, his services were terminated in circumstances in which presumably the pension law at the time gave him no right to pension; none could now be awarded without a special vote.

===== Page 5 =====
The case was badly handled at the time, and if it were not 22 years old I think we should have to do something about it. Having regard, however, to the delay, the reasonable course, I think, is to decline to interfere; but I suggest that we should explain the difficulty to the Governor.

J. Robert Wray
13/1/41.

My Lord,

In reply to Your Lordship's Straits Settlements despatch No. 254 of the 26th September, 1940, transmitting, in original, a memorial from S. Sinnadurai, I have the honour to submit below a resumé of the case.

2. The memorialist joined the service of the Posts and Telegraphs Department on the 1st of March, 1907, as a Class III Clerk and held the pensionable appointment of a Class II Clerk when his services were terminated on the 13th November, 1918.

3. On the 1st of October, 1918, the memorialist was reported by the Director, Posts and Telegraphs, to be unreliable and untrustworthy, and a list of irregularities of which the memorialist had been guilty in July and September, 1918, was forwarded with a recommendation that his services should be terminated. The memorialist was accordingly charged under General Order 24 with these irregularities and after considering his replies the Chief Secretary to Government, Federated Malay States, with whom the power lay, directed that he should be dismissed from the service.

4. On the 29th November, 1918, the memorialist addressed a petition to the High Commissioner who decided that dismissal was too harsh a punishment and directed that although the memorialist's services should be terminated he should be given four months' salary as a form of gratuity. The stigma of dismissal was thereby withdrawn.

Two subsequent petitions to the High Commissioner for re-employment or a pension in February 1919 and 1920 were rejected, and when the memorialist again addressed a petition to the High Commissioner on the 13th January, 1936, requesting the grant of a pension, it was decided that although the records did not show that the memorialist was retired for inefficiency nor was he dismissed, his record which prior to July, 1918, showed 14 fines and 20 cautions was sufficiently unsatisfactory to justify his retirement without pension, and his request was therefore refused. Under the circumstances, I recommend that Your Lordship reply that Your Lordship is not prepared to grant the memorialist's request.

5. The memorial is returned herewith as requested.

I have the honour to be,
My Lord,
Your Lordship's most obedient, humble servant,

The Right Honourable
The Lord Lloyd of Dolobran,
P.C., G.C.S.I.,
etc., etc., etc.,
HIGH COMMISSIONER.

To the Right Hon'ble
The Secretary of State for the Colonies,
Downing Street, London.

The humble Memorial of Sethunather Sinnathural,
residing at Vaddukoddai West, Vaddukoddai Jaffna,
Ceylon.

Most respectfully sheweth:

On the 1st day of March 1907 the Memorialist joined the service of the Government of the Federated Malay States at Kuala Lumpur as a III grade Clerk in the Posts and Telegraphs Department on a salary of 420 dollars per annum, and was in the service for 12 years.

2. The Memorialist was given an annual increment of 5 dollars and in 1912 he was drawing the maximum salary of 720 dollars per annum relating to the above grade. He was thereafter promoted to Grade II on a salary of 840-960 dollars per annum, as he was found to be competent for such promotion. As a result of both increment and promotion the Memorialist was in 1918 drawing a salary of 880 dollars per annum.

The Memorialist had a clean record without any charges or complaints and had received regular promotions on account of faithful and efficient service for 12 years.

3. On the 12th November 1918 when the Memorialist was Assistant Post Master at Seremban where he was serving under one Mr. Sinnavar Thambiah, Post Master in charge, the Memorialist was, to his great surprise and regret, informed that he was dismissed from service for insubordination to the Post Master who was his immediate superior without any charges being framed against him, without any inquiry and without giving the Memorialist a chance to be heard in defence.

4. The Memorialist submitted his representation against the sudden and unjust dismissal to Sir Arthur Young, K.C.M.G., who was then the High Commissioner for the Federated Malay States at Singapore and he was pleased to order that the dismissal of the Memorialist was unfair and unreasonable and the treatment meted out to him was very harsh. Vide Chief Secretary's Office Papers No. 4520/18 dated 20th November 1918. The dismissal was consequently withdrawn. But the only relief given was the payment of four months' salary. The Memorialist was not reinstated in his post nor was he given a pension as he was entitled to under the Pension Rules after 12 years of efficient and faithful service.

5. The Memorialist begs to submit this Memorial to the Right Honourable the Secretary of State for the Colonies on the following grounds:

(a) In the absence of either a properly recorded Departmental Enquiry or a Judicial Trial in the presence of the Memorialist the dismissal was highly unwarranted and unjustifiable and is against the principles of natural Justice and is something unheard of in British Administration either at home or abroad.

(b) The Memorialist was not served with any order of charge or accusation to the effect that he had committed any offence or that he was found to be (i) insubordinate so as to warrant a dismissal or (ii) was inefficient in the discharge of the duties entrusted to him.

===== Page 10 =====
(c) The Memorialist was neither afforded the inherent right of defending himself or proving his innocence nor was he given an opportunity either to explain any charge that might have been brought against him or to prove that he had not committed any offence.

(d) It is not likely that the Memorialist, who was in independent charge of many Post and Telegraph Offices in various places with many clerks working under him, would be insubordinate at Seremban to his immediate superior, the said Post Master, while he had not worked there under him for even 3 months.

(e) It is absolutely incredible that a person of the experience and knowledge of the Memorialist would be insubordinate, as alleged, so as to cast to the winds the benefit of his 12 years' service and stand the risk of losing his appointment.

(f) If a charge had been framed and the Memorialist was given a chance to prove his innocence, it would have been held that the dismissal of the Memorialist was the result of certain domestic differences between him and his superior Officer, the Post Master, who was a close relative of his and who tried in vain on various occasions to get the Memorialist's brother-in-law married to his niece against the wish and consent of the Memorialist.

(g) It is inequitable that the Federated Malay States Government should have treated the Memorialist in this merciless and harsh manner and subjected him, his wife, and 3 children and dependants to absolute poverty notwithstanding the fact that he had rendered 12 years efficient and meritorious service to the said Government.

(h) On the finding of Sir Arthur Young K.C.M.G., the High Commissioner for the territory, that the order of dismissal of the Memorialist was unfair and unreasonable as stated in paragraph 4 above, the Memorialist should have been reinstated or a pension given him from the date of the order of Sir Arthur Young on the 20th of November 1918. Even Sir Arthur Young's order that the Memorialist should be given one year's full salary was not carried out, but 4 months' salary was paid and the order of dismissal was withdrawn, though the words conveyed to the Memorialist was "the stigma of dismissal is withdrawn and four months' salary given." Vide Chief Secretary's Office Papers No. 4520/18 dated 20-11-1918.

(6) The Memorialist submits that as the order of dismissal and its "Stigma" were withdrawn on the finding that the dismissal was unfair and unreasonable by His Majesty's High Commissioner, the payment of 4 months' salary was not a proper or a just adjustment of the case, and that the Memorialist should have been paid a monthly pension dating from the date of expiration of the period of 4 months for which the Memorialist was paid his salary as the Memorialist was entitled in law to such pension after 12 years' faithful and efficient service under the Government of the Federated Malay States.

Wherefore the Memorialist prays that the Right Honourable the Secretary of State for the Colonies, after considering the facts of this case, may be pleased to order that the Memorialist be paid a monthly pension in terms of the pension rules from the date on which the payment of his salary was terminated till his death so that he may support himself, his wife and 3 children who for so many years have endured hardships on account of the unjust and unfair treatment to which he was subjected for no fault of his own.

The Memorialist has suffered mental and physical agonies for nearly 22 years as a result of the sudden termination of his services and the deprivation of his livelihood.

He has made representations to the Government of the Federated Malay States whenever his deep grief and mental condition permitted. He submits this Memorial to the Right Honourable the Secretary of State for the Colonies with the hope that though Justice has been delayed, Justice will not be denied.

For this act of Goodness the Memorialist will ever be thankful.
