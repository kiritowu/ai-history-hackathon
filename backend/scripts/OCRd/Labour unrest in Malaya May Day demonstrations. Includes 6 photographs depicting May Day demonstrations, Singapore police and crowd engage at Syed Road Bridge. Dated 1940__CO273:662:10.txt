The consideration of 9 has been interrupted by the recent Parliamentary question and telegrams about the labour disputes in Singapore.

Two satisfactory features may be noted -
(1) the fact that it was possible to retain a considerable proportion of the labour force in Malaya during the recent depression, so that there was no embarrassing shortage of labour on the return of new prosperity, and
(2) the fact that steps were taken by the authorities and by the larger organisations of employers to meet reasonable claims of labour, in view of rise in cost.

===== Page 10 =====
Cost of living and greater prosperity of the principal industries, in advance of actual demands for their satisfaction. As regards the retention of the labour force in Malaya, this is not only a more humane method of dealing with the problem of unemployment than that of repatriating surplus labour as soon as trade begins to slacken off, but also avoids the delay and expense of recruitment when the demands of industry once more expand. There is, of course, on the other hand, a darker side of the picture (which we have seen described recently in the report of the Agent for the Government of India in Malaya) in the lower wages which have had to be accepted and in the disappearance of such advantages as the small additions to the family income supplied by wives and other dependants, who secure casual employment on estates when the demand for labour is high. As regards steps taken to deal with the situation at an early stage, the Governor's minute of 3rd November (enclosure 1) seems a wise and timely pronouncement. His remark that "social questions" must take the second place evidently only refers to officers of the Labour Department and of the Chinese Secretariat.

(14) explains that the recent labour dispute in Singapore rose from the increased cost of living since the outbreak of war and also, in the case of some strikes in commercial concerns, from the "demand of employees for a share in the alleged increased prosperity". As regards the two main industries of Malaya, i.e. rubber plantations and tin mines, there is no question that there has been a substantial increase in prosperity, and the leading rubber estates and mine owners have been able to grant an increase in wages. As regards

===== Page 11 =====
Cost of living: The figures quoted in paragraph 4 of the despatch show that there has been no very serious rise yet, though in fact we know from the subsequent telegram (10) that the cost of living allowance, based on an index of cost of living, has twice had to be revised in an upward direction since its introduction in November. I do not think we need to be afraid of a "vicious snarl" in Malaya. The increase in wages will only have a minor effect on internal cost of living and will, to a great extent, be paid for by foreign consumers of the principal exports. At the same time, the effect of increased labour costs on the market prices of the latter will be negligible compared with the effect of other controlling factors.

The interesting essay on the development of Trade Unions in Malaya in the Monthly Review of Chinese Affairs, prepared in the Chinese Secretariat for June 1939 (in 12 on 50055/39 Pt.2), recalls that in 1937 the Communist Party of China, in agreeing to co-operate with the National Government, undertook to quell all trouble and that the Malayan Communist party had declared that its policy should now be devoid of strikes and the cultivation of harmonious relations between employers and employees, so that all energies might be devoted to the cause of National Salvation. "National Salvation" is an effort embracing the collection of relief funds for transmission to the Chinese Government and the enforcement of the boycott of Japanese goods. It led to the formation of Relief Fund Committees for particular trades and industries, and this association of persons (usually employees) in single industries for the collection of subscriptions encouraged a spirit of solidarity among the labourers and the formation of Trade Unions. It is unfortunate that this

===== Page 12 =====
This national patriotic movement, which was from the first likely to have far-reaching effects on the social activities of the Chinese community in Malaya, should not have been so much mixed up with militant Communism. It is unnecessary to point out the dangers which would result if the Communist Party is able to fix itself firmly on the organisation of relief assistance and work them up into an anti-British campaign. The change in the relations between Great Britain and the U.S.S.R. has no doubt intensified Communist efforts in that direction. Fortunately, the Governor has taken firm action and has also eliminated one of the principal leaders of the movement, Mr. Hau Say Hoan, whose activities in sending local students to a Communist University had been causing anxiety even to the authorities in Chung-Kiang (1 on 50658/39).

In Nos. 37 and 39, the contents of which have already been useful in connection with Mr. Creech Jones' question in the House on the 24th April, the Governor gives us full reports on the situation in respect of labour unrest, particularly in Singapore, which was occurring before the decision to pounce upon certain Chinese leaders in Malaya who were conducting subversive activities under cover of the various Chinese patriotic and charitable local organisations. He also brings the story up to date since the expulsion from Malaya of the leading offenders - Hau Say Hoan and others. The effect on the China Relief Fund of the genuine patriotic arrangements for lawfully contributing to Chinese war chest against Japanese aggression is described as being thoroughly salutary and very welcome to the Chinese community as a whole. The Chinese organisation known as the Anti-Enemy-Backing-Up-Society, which was instituted some three years ago amongst the overseas Chinese for collecting funds for Chiang Kai Shek, but which in Malaya at least had deteriorated into a tool of the subversive political "anti-Imperialist" agitators, has been largely destroyed in the Peninsular, and the Governor says that the war with the communist elements has now entered upon a new phase and the enemy has lost the disguise of Chinese patriotism. Its leaders are difficult to identify, although its activities are obvious, and it has become well established in Singapore.

In No. 39, the manifestations of the "red" hand in industrial disturbances are more particularly reviewed. Since the outbreak of war, over 130 strikes have occurred in Singapore. In the early stages these reflected a genuine discontent with the failure of wages to follow the cost of living in spite of increased

===== Page 20 =====
Profits which industry was earning. We know from other papers that the Government took active and successful steps to bring the leading employers, including the Defence Departments and such semi-public bodies as the Harbour Board, into line in a generous policy of allowances to wage earners to compensate for the rise in cost of living, so that such allowances be constantly activated to the prime concern.

The later strikes in many cases have, we are told, been of a political nature and instigated by the Communist Party as part of a campaign conducted by the Malayan Communist Party against the Government and the capitalist system. Until strong measures were taken by the Government it was possible for the communist activities to be exercised under the guise and through the agency of various Chinese patriotic societies. The Governor comments on the traditional susceptibility of the Chinese to intimidation, a failing with which we have been continually faced and which was singularly exhibited in the disastrous general strike at Hong Kong some fifteen years ago. It is a failing which is always likely to obstruct the growth of genuine trades unionism amongst Chinese labour.

The recent strikes, the Governor says, can be distinguished from those in the past by a much plainer evidence of a single organising force in the background and by the development of a greater efficiency both in their strategy and tactics. He records the growth of resistance that labourers themselves should decide whether any of their members should be dismissed at the instance of the employer, and even in extreme cases a claim to a right to dismiss fellow labourers. He records also the standard demands, typed in English, presented by labourers having no knowledge of English. At first there were no representatives of the strikers to conduct negotiations. Later, well-coached representatives were put in for the job, although no settlement could be relied upon to be kept.

Politically, the most significant of the strikes was that amongst the employees of the Singapore Harbour Board - a strike which had been carefully led up to by an organisation described as the Singapore All-Trades (Red) General Labour Union. One of the objects of this strike, which extended to the principal engineering shops both in the Colony and the Federated Malay States, was to break the established and reputable organisation of skilled workers known as the Singapore Engineering Mechanics Association. It was aimed to lead up to a strike of artisans at the naval base and finally to a political demonstration against the Government itself. The Government decided to accept the political challenge of the "Red" Labour Union, and work in the dockyard was declared under the Defence Regulations to be an essential service. To bring extra pressure, the General Labour Union called a strike at the General Hospital, and the Harbour Board's electric power station was stopped. With the services of non-Chinese volunteers, however, the General Hospital strike collapsed and the United Engineers skilled workmen returned to work with an agreed increase of wages. The Harbour Board stood firm and their employees returned to work on promise of arbitration. The Governor pays a tribute to the restraint, patience and impartiality of the Chinese Protectorate Officers and of the Police in this period.

===== Page 21 =====
Of strain in Singapore. It is probable, he thinks, that the position and prestige of the General Labour Union has been badly shaken by this failure, and that a period of industrial peace may now be expected during which the political aims of the Communist Party must be thwarted.

New Trade Union legislation has been enacted in the Straits Settlements, and is on the stocks in the F.M.S., and the Governor is rightly insistent on the importance of preventing the new Unions which will be formed under these measures from being collared by the Communist Party. In paragraph 14 to the end of No. 39, the Governor proceeds to give his ideas on the extent and character of communism in Malaya in case the Secretary of State may wish to discuss it with him when he is in England. In paragraph 20 of the despatch he suggests that it may be useful for him to be authorised to discuss the subject also with Mr. Creech Jones, M.P., by whom questions have been addressed to the Secretary of State in Parliament in a sense critical of the Malayan Governments' attitude toward labour. It has been arranged with Sir Shenton Thomas that this subject shall be one for discussion in the list which he has set out in a letter to Sir George Gater. The Governor says in this part of his despatch that the ultimate aim of the Communist Party in Malaya is open revolt, and the overthrow of British rule in the Peninsular. He says there are believed to be in the country some 6,000 members of the women's branch alone, and it is the experience of the Police that the women readily take a singularly militant hand in any disturbance. The party leaders are rapidly improving in technique and more subtle on their methods within the law. Responsible officials of the party are exceedingly mobile, and it is difficult for the Police to keep touch with their movements between the various territories in the Peninsular. One of the most disquieting features of their success is in the Chinese vernacular schools and the effective propaganda to which Chinese children in those schools are being subjected. The Secretary for Chinese Affairs advocates the provision of English education for Chinese children either compulsorily or on very cheap terms for all who desire it. It is believed that the opening of a sufficient number of English elementary schools for this purpose would soon empty the Chinese vernacular schools, although politically the promotion of English education on these lines would have the advantage of drawing the Chinese children away from their Chinese national connection and give them a Malayan and pro-British training. It has, however, always been recognised that the cost of such an educational policy would be altogether excessive even if English teachers could be made available. However, in paragraph 18 he records that a start has been made in the improvement of facilities for English education for Chinese, and the experience of these measures may help in the formation of a long-range programme.

The Governor refers to the correspondence three years ago which he had with the Colonial Office in the matter of the power to exclude from importation into Malaya all communist publications emanating from the Left Book Club, and he asks that the decision of the then Secretary of State against the generic prohibition.

===== Page 22 =====
Prohibition of publications emanating from the concerns who make a habit of circulating "seditious" literature should be refused. His impression of that effect was that there was a policy of hesitation here and an overcareful desire not to upset the feelings of Mr. Pritt and his friends. But the Governor urges that the situation is too serious for hesitation, and the most vigorous and whole-hearted policy is essential.

In the penultimate paragraph of 39, the Governor refers to the new immigration bill which has been on the stocks for some two years now in Malaya. The first draft of that bill in 1938 was generally agreed to be wholly unsatisfactory and was severely criticised by the Departments here concerned. The present stage is that the Governor has recently reported that the bills are being recast to meet many of the chief points of criticism, and has asked whether he need comply with the request that the recast measures should be again submitted to the Colonial Office for examination. It was thought here that unless there was any reason why copies of the new draft bills should not be sent home by air mail before introduction, it would be more satisfactory for that course to be followed as it was apparent that all the criticisms had been met.

As this problem is to be discussed with Sir Shenton Thomas, I do not think it necessary at this stage to make any other suggestions for action on these two despatches. The vital importance both of the security of law and order and of our defence positions in Malaya will be accepted no doubt, and with it the importance of the Governor having all the powers he needs to suppress any other politically subversive "anti-Imperialist" activities. The history of China itself and the story of the Chinese in the various Far-Eastern countries in which they have settled (Siam, Netherlands Indies etc.) provides the same picture and varying shades of an imperium in imperio seeking to establish itself, and in this generation that imperium has been invariably the imperium of the Kuo Min Tang. Whether that organisation must invariably be communist in the European sense or whether its extreme nationalist ideals necessarily give it an "anti-Imperialist" and anti-capitalist form in such territories as Malaya may be a matter of doubt, but its capacity for gangster methods, dangerous insinuation into the vitals of an organised community, have been shown over and over again in British and other territories as well as in China itself.

There have been a number of strikes engineered by the Communist party in Malaya and other anti-British agitation since the time of the Russian-Finnish war. The agitation was first conducted through local Chinese societies formed originally for the organization of relief for China in her war with Japan but subsequently took the form of maritime Trade Union activity. The position seems generally the same in all its forms, viz., the militant and active minority with apparently considerable financial resources imposing their will on the majority of workers who would be content to work under the present conditions, particularly since they are generally receiving cost of living bonuses on a large scale. The employers show little readiness to take united action on their side and consequently lay themselves open to intimidation.

The government has intervened effectively on occasions in the past; for instance, the influence of the Communist organizations in the various China relief Societies was crushed by the timely banishment of certain leading figures in the movement. This step has apparently been followed again in the present strikes. On other occasions, such as the strike in the Singapore Dockyard, the government arrested the ring-leaders under the Defence Regulations.

It will be seen that the O.A.G. considers that matters have gone beyond the remedy of negotiations and that the spread of strikes beyond Singapore can only be prevented by suppressing Communist activities there. 86. MALAYA - COMMUNISM.

(a) Review of Local Situation.

The danger of organised communism in Malaya may now be considered to be abating. At the end of 1939, the organisation and control attained by the Malayan Communist Party over patriotic sentiment in Malaya, the tremendous increase in membership of the Party arising therefrom, and the subversion of the whole organisation into a Bolshevik anti-British movement had led to a situation which could not be considered as other than grave. Its first manifestation was the flood of anti-war and anti-British propaganda of an inflammatory and seditious nature. This was shortly followed by a wave of strikes in Singapore directed in each case as "political struggles" by the (Red) General Labour Union.

Three things have had far-reaching effects in undermining and weakening the movement and to a large extent liquidating its danger:

i. The expulsion of Hau Say Hoan at the end of December 1939 gave an opportunity for the responsible leaders of the Chinese community to stop their support and encouragement of the communist-controlled patriotic societies.

ii. The numerous arrests of important directive members of the Communist Party and its patriotic societies throughout Malaya made it quite plain to the whole Chinese community that communism, even though cloaked in the disguise of patriotism, would not be tolerated by the Government in Malaya.

iii. The third and possibly most far-reaching effect is the Kuomintang circular letter mentioned below.

===== Page 85 =====
Below, which has to some considerable extent torn from the Communist Party the cloak of patriotism and which may be expected to result in the more conservative and responsible members of the Chinese community ceasing to support the Party.

It is almost certain that the Malayan Communist Party will in future be forced to restrict its more intensive activities to the labour side of its organisation and will be forced to drop its pseudo-patriotic organisations, such as the Anti-Enemy Backing-up Society, for lack of sympathy and support.

(b) Kuomintang.

The most outstanding and important event during the month of March was the issue of a circular letter by the Overseas Chinese Department of the Central Executive Committee of the Kuomintang from Chungking containing a denunciation by the Government of the Malayan Communist Party, the Malayan Anti-Enemy Backing-up Society and the Chinese National Emancipation Vanguard Corps. The letter was published in the newspaper Sin Chew Jit Poh on 18th March, but it did not appear in the Nanyang Siang Pau newspaper, which is the newspaper of the Tan Kah Kee clique and of the China Relief Fund Organisation.

Labour Unrest in Malaya.

There was a certain amount of surplus labour in Malaya in the years immediately prior to the outbreak of war owing to reduction in the tin and rubber quota releases, but private action by the rubber estates and relief works started by the Government helped to absorb much of it, and there was no major labour disturbance. The outbreak of war, with the consequent expansion once more of the tin and rubber industries put an end to the existence of this surplus labour. It was, however, accompanied by a definite increase in the price of living and there were some signs of unrest on this account. In order to meet this the Government introduced a cost of living allowance for its poorly paid employees. The Singapore Chamber of Commerce and the U.P.A.M, as well as important mining interests, followed suit or went even further so that in the end the majority of employers had increased wages in order to compensate workers for the rise in the cost of living and to give them a share of the increased profits arising out of the war.

There was, however, considerable danger from subversive activity particularly inspired by the Malayan Communist Party. This organisation in the first place took advantage of the patriotic societies among the Chinese population that had originally been formed to support China against Japan. The rapprochement between the Chinese Government and the Chinese Communist Party in the Japanese war made this easier and under the influence of Hau Say Hoan a United Action Committee was set up to establish liaison between the Anti-Enemy Backing Up Society, which was controlled by the Malayan Communist Party, and the Chinese National Emancipation Vanguard Corps which was controlled by the Singapore-China Relief Fund. There appeared to be considerable danger of this anti-Japanese agitation taking an anti-British turn. This danger appeared, however, to be met by an expulsion order being issued against Hau Say Hoan on the 1st January, a step which was followed by a clean-up undertaken voluntarily by the China Relief Fund Organisation. The Anti-Enemy Backing Up Society in its turn broke up.

The Communist Party, however, continued to exercise a dangerous influence through the organisation of the Singapore All-Trades (Red) General Labour Union which interfered in a strike of mechanics in the United Engineers works when a settlement was on the point of being reached with the Engineers Mechanics Association. Prologue.

After two years of resistance to aggression led by our Generalissimo Chiang Kai Shek, we are now on our road to victory. Such an achievement attained only after the greatest sacrifice on the part of all, cannot be rightly attributed to any particular army or clique. Those who have taken advantage of the crisis to further their own ends by profiteering, strengthening their own position, undermining the administration, and dissipating the national energy, will therefore, stand condemned in the eyes of the entire nation and all heading for certain destruction.

There have been widespread rumours that in their circulars of December 1939 and January 1940, General Chu-Teh of the 18th Army Corps and his subordinate officers have accused the Kuomintang of creating all the friction between the Kuomintang and them. Is this the truth or merely the usual propaganda? Apparently, the cat is now out of the bag, and our countrymen will have to judge for themselves as to the significance of such accusations. However, a word might not be amiss here to trace the history of the union of the Kuomintang and the C.C.P. and to point out the stumbling blocks which stand in its way.

It was after the Si-an coup that the C.C.P. put forth the following four points in its "Rally to National Crisis" manifesto:

(1) Strive for the complete realization of the Three People's Principles which the party regards as a necessity for present-day China.

(2) Abolish the anti-Kuomintang policy of violence and sovietization and stop the practice of forcible confiscation of all capitalist lands and estates.

(3) Abolish the present (Chinese) Soviet Government and realize the democratic form of Government in order to unify political administration throughout the country.

(4) Abolish the Red Army and reorganize it as a National Revolutionary Army under the control of the Central Military Affairs Commission prior to entering upon active service against the Japanese invaders.

After the issue of this manifesto, General Chiang expressed the hope that since the C.C.P. was prepared to drop all differences, it would remain sincere and fulfill its promise, by helping the National Government to complete the National Revolution under unified command. General Chu-Teh was then appointed Commander-in-Chief of the 18th Army Corps with General Pan-Teh-Huai as his deputy. All past grievances were put aside and a chance was given to all who wished to contribute to our war of resistance.

Although fear was entertained that the C.C.P. might abuse its recently acquired legal status to develop its army and party organization and resume its work of seditious propaganda, yet it was thought that the presence of a common enemy and the consideration of national interests should call for mutual trust and co-operation.

However, the C.C.P. soon broke its facile promises, and did not scruple to spend on communistic propaganda the subsidy given by the Central Government for the upkeep of its army. It began to blame the Central Government for the unfair treatment meted out to its army, and its financial position was further aggravated by its unlimited expansion. Taxes were levied, followed by the issue of currency notes. The existence of complex elements within its own party, coupled with lack of discipline, caused frequent disturbances which it did not hesitate to attribute to the innocent populace or the Kuomintang. The Governor:—I should like to say a few words in continuation of the comments on the Trade Unions and Industrial Courts legislation which I made at the last meeting of this Council when the Bills were read a first time.

Those Bills have now been passed and, as I said previously, I am firmly convinced that they provide a wise and proper means for the settlement of industrial disputes if only employers and employed will cooperate to make use of them. I repeat that to make this legislation a success there must be goodwill on both sides.

I wish to emphasise that there is nothing new in the recognition of trade unions in Malaya. They already exist, but for the most part they are neither very clearly defined nor effectively conducted. One of the chief difficulties with which the Labour Department or the Chinese Protectorate is confronted when requested to assist in the settlement of a dispute is to find out who can speak for the men. It is obvious that negotiations cannot be conducted with several hundred people. Our object therefore should be to assist these incipient unions rather than to suppress them, so that those who are appointed to be the leaders shall possess in a real degree the confidence of the rank and file and shall be able clearly and precisely to represent their views. I am confident that this will come in time.

We have all listened with sympathy and appreciation to the very constructive speech made by the Hon. Mr. CHARLWOOD and I am grateful to him for his suggestions. When I last spoke on this subject, I said that a strike is the worst possible way of settling a dispute, and in time of war it becomes a very serious matter indeed. It is unnecessary and unjustifiable for labourers to strike before ever they have made any demands or before they have given reasonable time for the consideration of such demands as they may have made. We have witnessed a number of such strikes within the past few months and I regret to have to say that in respect of several of them we have evidence to show that they have been forced upon the labourers by irresponsible youths whose aim is not the improvement of working conditions so much as the upsetting of law and order. We have evidence that the trash which is being circulated among the labouring classes is often violently seditious. We have evidence that many of these strikes have been planned, not by the labourers themselves, but by subversive organisations.

There can be only one answer to a campaign of this sort. It is the function of the Government to govern and we do not intend to surrender that function to anyone else. Every power that we possess shall be brought against these enemies of the State. They are the disciples of those against whom the British Empire is now fighting and whom the whole of the civilised world regards with loathing. In their way they represent the rule of force as opposed to the rule of law and justice. I warn these people that the offence of sedition in time of war is one of the most serious that it is possible to commit. I warn them too that although they may think themselves clever enough to escape the consequences of their acts, yet their time will inevitably come and they will suffer as they should suffer.

But if the Government is to govern as it should, it is neither just nor seemly that it should carry the whole burden itself. It requires, and it has the right to require, the cooperation of the public. The people of this Colony cannot stand aside and say that this is no affair of theirs. It is very much their affair, and it is their bounden duty as loyal British subjects to bring the whole force of public opinion to bear against these evil doers. Why is it that in Great Britain there is law, and order, and freedom, and justice? It is because public opinion demands them and will never let them go. And so here, if the people stand up for the rights and privileges which become theirs by virtue of their residence in a British Colony, if they refuse in a body to listen to the voices of this small—this very small—minority who wish to lead them to violence and unrest, if they will say, once and for all, that they will have none of it, then this period of difficulty will be over. And the end of the sedition-monger, which in any case is certain, will come more quickly.

I do not want to exaggerate. I would not have it supposed that Singapore is a hot-bed of sedition. Any suggestion of the sort would be a gross insult to the thousands of honest law-abiding citizens who live here. It is because of what may happen if this insidious, malignant disease is not cut out now, that I have spoken as I have. (Applause).

Sir,

I have the honour to report on the strikes which have taken place in Singapore between the outbreak of war and this date, which sees the Settlement free from labour disputes of any importance.

2. During the period under review there have been, in addition to much labour unrest, over 130 strikes. This figure has been reached by counting as one strike any which have occurred in different factories in the same industry within a brief space of time where the demands of the employees or the matter in dispute were the same.

3. The origins of the earliest strikes are to be found in discontent with the failure of wages to follow rising costs of living, a determination to have reflected in wages some part of the increased profits which undoubtedly were accruing to many industries after the outbreak of war, and the opportunity for Communist activities afforded by these facts and by the reluctance of certain employers to concede the demands for better terms.

4. Many of the later strikes were of a political nature and instigated by the Communist Party, and they became part of a campaign conducted by the local Communists against the local Government, the Empire and the capitalistic system in Malaya. Although the strikes were at their worst during the Russian attack upon Finland, their political connections are to be found not in the Comintern movement but in the alliance with the Chinese Communist Party into which Chiang Kai Shek's Government was driven by the Japanese invasion of China. The universal support given to that Government by the Chinese of Malaya, expressed overwhelmingly in financial aid, carried an implication of agreement with Chiang Kai Shek's recognition of the China Communist Party and this was seized upon by the local Communist Party and exploited with great skill in organization and propaganda, its activities being given the colour of patriotism and national sacrifice. Working largely through the Anti-Enemy Backing-Up Society (AEBUS) the Party penetrated schools, clubs, associations and other forms of corporate life in the Chinese community and there preached its gospel which, upon the outbreak of the war between Russia and Finland, became one of rabid hostility towards Great Britain and the Empire and of denunciation of its motives in going to war against Germany.

5. AEBUS was the form of this organization but its spirit was the Singapore All Trades (Red) General Labour Union. AEBUS has been destroyed by the Government but the Labour Union still lives prosecuting this policy:

First Stage .. To incite the workers to demand better living conditions;

Second ..

===== Page 120 =====
Second Stage: To unite and consolidate the workers and organize Labour Unions;

Third Stage: To spread Communist propaganda with the ultimate aim of open revolution and the overthrow of British Rule in Malaya.

I will disclose the Government's attitude towards these aims later in this despatch. Their relevance to the strikes has been shown in the instigation and actual conduct of the strikes by the Communist Party. Such labour organizations as exist in Singapore take the shape of Mutual Help Associations and the Party has secured a footing therein by obtaining the appointment of their better-educated members to the secretarial posts in those Associations. Through this agency the Party has planned and directed many of the strikes itself, working up the case of the employees' grievances and providing them with leadership and organization, supplying propaganda in pamphlet form and the machinery to distribute them, and offering its assistance in many other ways.

6. There is another general observation to be made. Strikes are often started and prolonged through a profound lack of public spirit on the part of the Chinese, traceable to the history of their national development, and a timidity in the face of intimidation. Time and time again a handful of determined men is able to coerce hundreds of their fellow labourers to strike against their will and to prevent them, by threat of violence, from returning to work when they are weary of inaction. There would be plenty of protestation that they would return to work if properly protected against their leaders, but no response at all to Government's request for identification of those offering intimidation or for a lesser form of assistance in singling them out. It is true that fear of the attentions of secret societies rules the spirit of most humble Chinese but it is their indifference to their duties as citizens that prevents them from co-operating with the Government in dealing with the forces of disorder of which they go in terror. Those experienced in labour disputes have learnt to be a little sceptical of professions of reluctance to strike and a readiness to return under police protection; they believe that at first any reluctance there may be is modified a good deal by a hopeful expectancy of profits to come. It is only when the fight seems likely to be lost and with it their employment that these protestations of antagonism to the strike are made. But the general result is the same, an unjustified strike in many cases and a foolish continuance.

7. The strikes as a whole can be distinguished from those in the past by the much plainer evidence of a single organising force in the background and by a greater efficiency both in the strategy and tactics of the strikes after the early days. The source of the difference was the Labour Union. The old features were present; the strike without adequate or any notice, perhaps provoked at first by the refusal of bad employers to listen to demands but later a standard move irrespective of the attitude of the other side; the demand for an undertaking that there would be no dismissals of labourers without good reason, in itself not improper but soon to become an insistence that the labourers themselves should decide whether any of their number should be dismissed at the instance of the employer and even, in extreme cases, a claim to a right to dismiss fellow-labourers. It is easy to imagine the detrimental effects upon the labourers' forces in Singapore when some employers, intent

===== Page 121 =====
Upon seizing the present golden opportunity for large profits, the parties yielded even to the extreme of these demands.

8. It became noticeable that as time went on, the technique of managing the strikes showed increasing efficiency. Instead of a letter written in Chinese and signed by labourers who were ignorant of its contents, the demands became couched in a standard form presented, type-written, in English. At first, there were no representatives to negotiate and the strikers hung sullenly about, refusing to move until their demands had been granted in full. Later, representatives who were well-coached in the part they were to play were forthcoming, though it is doubtful whether that assisted the settlement substantially, as those they were supposed to represent did not hesitate to repudiate terms they did not like and there was always interference by the Communist organizers.

9. Although the Communist Party was clever enough to try to keep on the right side of the law and frowned upon assaults and acts of violence and disorder (which were, in fact, remarkably few), there were some cases in which the detention of the employer on his business premises was attempted and more where the removal of finished articles from places of employment or the introduction of materials into places of employment was prevented, sometimes through the labourers lying on the ground in the track of the lorries, sometimes by the opposition of females and once or twice by the employment of force.

10. Another hindrance to settlement was the naive theory of the strikers that a strike could not possibly end without some sort of concession to them and the success of the great majority of the strikes contributed a good deal to this belief. In a few instances the employers were successful, and the most prominent victory was won by the Singapore Harbour Board. As this strike was politically the most significant of all, I will deal with it at some length.

11. The Singapore All Trades (Red) General Labour Union detailed a man specially to organise strikes in the engineering shops after it had invited the Singapore Engineering Mechanics' Association to join it and this Association had refused. This Association was registered under the Societies Ordinance in 1929 and has since been operated successfully on the lines of a trade union; for some years it has been the body recognised by the Singapore Harbour Board and the United Engineers, the largest European engineering firm in Singapore, to conduct all negotiations on behalf of its engineering artisans; it is a lawful body and acts lawfully and has therefore gained the enmity of the General Labour Union, which has declared it to be a "yellow" union. Strikes were declared first in the Chinese engineering shops and there, of course, they succeeded. Next a strike was declared in the shops of the United Engineers, the demand being for a flat rate increase of four cents an hour; this the firm resisted, and the workmen in its shops in Kuala Lumpur and Ipoh were then brought out; next a strike was declared at the Singapore Harbour Board dockyard, less than 24 hours' notice being given. The objects of this strike were four-fold; one was to bring pressure on the United Engineers to concede the demand made; another was to break the Singapore Engineering Mechanics' Association; a third was to complete its grip on labour, it being essential to dominate the engineering mechanics, the best educated and most intelligent labour in Singapore, if the General Labour Union was to go on to its next step of declaring a strike of artisans at the Naval Base; and a fourth was to stage in the most impressive way a political demon-

===== Page 122 =====
The Singapore Harbour Board is a public corporation which is semi-Government in form; its artisans are amongst the best paid manual labour in the Colony (their wages before the strike were better than those of the United Engineers' artisans after their strike); some big employer had sooner or later to fight the "lightning" strike, and no trial of strength could be more spectacular than one with a Port Trust of which the artisans were so highly remunerated and which was at the same time engaged on important war work for the Admiralty.

The time had come for the Government to accept the political challenge which the growing activities of the General Labour Union offered. Work in the dockyard was declared by Gazette Notification to be an "essential service" under the Defence Regulations. When the General Labour Union realised that the Board was not going to capitulate, bad temper was shown for a few days, a strike being called at the General Hospital and the Board's electric power station being stopped. There was a fair amount of intimidation in the first few days, too. But, with the collapse of the General Hospital strike, due mainly to the services rendered by non-Chinese volunteers, and the return of the United Engineers' artisans to work on an increase of 3 cents an hour, the bad temper disappeared and the General Labour Union instructed its adherents that they must take the maximum care not to have any clashes with the law (its speakers at strikers' meetings have denied throughout the strike the validity of the application of the Defence Regulations to this dockyard work); and, on the whole, the conduct of the strikers was surprisingly good.

The Board has been hampered in dealing with the strikers by its anxiety not to be harsh to the members of the Singapore Engineering Mechanics' Association amongst its employees, by the fact that there is no alternative labour available so that it must in the end take the bulk of the strikers back, by the fears of the members of the Singapore Engineering Mechanics' Association to resume work (some years ago a strike was broken by a section of the men returning to work and the first man that crossed the line of the dockyard gate on their return was murdered six months later), and by the intricacies of the legal position, as the operation of the Rent Restriction Ordinance had made it peculiarly difficult to dismiss the strikers or to eject them from their quarters.

The strike was prolonged by the ability of the strikers to find work, though at considerably lower rates of pay, in Chinese engineering shops and by a doling out to those upon the General Labour Union members in other industries. Owing to the indication given by the action of the Board in serving notices of ejection from quarters that its patience was becoming exhausted and by the discovery that the loyal work of the European staff, aided by Malays, Eurasians and Indians, was keeping the Board going without anxiety the vitality of the strike began to ebb after a few weeks, until the return to work soon became only a matter of face-saving.

The leaders were very reluctant to admit the fact that this was a strike in which no concession would be made before work was resumed and slow to realise that the Board's offer to open negotiations as soon as work was resumed and to accept the decision of an arbitrator could best be dealt with by concentrating on the choice of an arbitrator instead of talking at large in mass meetings. In the end the maintenance of the Board's firm attitude and the imminence of ejection from quarters prevailed and the strikers accepted defeat and returned to work upon an agreement that their grievances would be examined and if necessary carried to binding arbitration but without any immediate concessions.

I have been informed that upon return the strikers were much chastened by the evidence of the capacity

===== Page 123 =====
of the Europeans and non-strikers to carry on and by the Board's decision to reward the loyalty of the Malays, Eurasians, and Indians by opening in their favour an engineering shop which had been closed for some years.

12. It remains to be said that, although the strikes were started and gained momentum from the obduracy of some of the employers who were by no means in the right, this attitude was not general. The good employers, who on the whole are in a majority, did their best to deal equitably with the claims of labour and are not open to criticism in any way. On the other hand, many of the strikes were unreasonable and their conduct objectionable, and this aroused a good deal of public resentment which relieved itself in criticism of the Government on account of what was considered to be its excessively charitable treatment of the strikers. Very few employers have much knowledge of the law and this lack tempts them to demand the rigorous enforcement of some law which has been evolved from their own sentiments of indignation and, their imaginations thus aflame, to scold the Government for its apparent inactivity. In this way, a severe strain has been placed upon the Chinese Protectorate and the Police, and great praise is due to them for their strict impartiality and consistency of treatment as well as for their restraint and patience.

13. The prestige of the General Labour Union has been badly shaken and its position substantially undermined by the failure of the Singapore Harbour Board strike, and their reported threats to call a general strike if the returned Harbour Board labour does not win some concessions seem to me largely the bluster of the bad loser. It is probable that there will be industrial peace for some time which will enable the Government to turn its attention to the professed aims of the Communist Party set out in paragraph 5 above. The first two stages in their campaign will require attention apropos of our trade union legislation. It will be disastrous if the union which we are endeavouring to organize should be captured by the Communist Party and these strikes have been valuable in showing how close that danger is. It is clear that simultaneously with our efforts to find and train worthy labour leaders, steps must be taken to stop the penetration of the new unions by persons with aims expressed in the plans for the third stage of the Communist programme.

14. This third stage is so important that at the risk of making this despatch unduly long, I have decided to deal with it in case you may wish to discuss it with me when I am in England. Communism exists in Malaya and it is too much to hope that it will ever be completely eradicated. I have mentioned that it is being taken up by Chinese women and the Police believe that there are some 6,000 members of the Women's Branch of the Communist Party in this country: they add that "young women are particularly skilled and powerful disciples of communism once they are started." It might also be added that the Chinese women, when excited, are quite ready to be ferocious and empty bottles are a favourite weapon. The dilemma of a police constable who is clasped in the arms of several stalwart females while others beat him over the head with anything that comes to hand can be better imagined than described. I do not exaggerate: such assaults have actually happened and the fact that they have not ended in tragedy hitherto is merely good fortune.

I have said also that the leaders of the movement are rapidly improving in technique and are becoming more clever in keeping within the law and more subtle in their methods. They are known to have considered, in the event of further arrests of strikers, ordering strikers to return to work...

===== Page 124 =====
And then to wreck all the machinery. One such case is reported to have occurred in Singapore in a small shoe shop during February last. Cyclostyle outfits for the printing of seditious pamphlets are installed first in one room, then in another. Responsible Party officials may appear today in one Settlement or State, and tomorrow in another, thus giving more work to the Police though admittedly at some loss of continuity of effort.

15. One of the most disquieting features of the campaign is the headway which the teaching of communism is making in the Chinese vernacular schools. It seems to offer a life of excitement and adventure, it suggests the uplifting of the Chinese people, it fosters the impatience of youth and the instinctive desire to run counter to the lawful authority of older and wiser men. Early in February there was a demonstration in Penang, ostensibly arranged for the purpose of welcoming Chinese singers who were touring Malaya in aid of the China War Relief Fund. A large crowd gathered at the pier, the majority being school children. Very soon, AEBUS pamphlets began to be distributed and these youths were arrested. Later in the afternoon a crowd of some 2,000 assembled at the Police Station and hurled abuse at the Police. Girls and small boys were made to stand in front. After some 2½ hours of this the crowd started to assault the police and had to be driven off by a baton charge. This is merely one instance of the way in which children are used by the Communist Party.

16. At the end of 1938 the Government of the Colony considered once again the difficulty which has been apparent to the Malayan Governments ever since the rise of Chinese nationalism, namely how to bind to Great Britain those Chinese who are Malaya-born and how to induce British sympathies and loyalties in those others who are domiciled here and have thrown in their lot with Malaya. Both Mr. A. M. Goodman, the late Secretary for Chinese Affairs, and Mr. A.B. Jordan, his successor, are convinced that there can be no real success in this until English education is provided for Chinese children, either compulsorily, or as a free and very cheap measure for all who wish to avail themselves of it. It is believed that if the Government were to open English elementary schools for all who care to send their children to them the Chinese schools maintained by the Chinese community would soon be practically emptied and the desire to learn Chinese literature would become very much reduced, so that the majority of the children would in time acquire a British outlook and few of them would be trained to Chinese politics. Mr. Goodman advocates a return to the policy laid down in 1918 by Sir Arthur Young: "It is the aim of the Government to afford facilities for the free education of all children in English up to the IVth Standard".

17. The Chinese vernacular schools are satisfactory from the point of view of the Chinese educationalist: the textbooks are excellent and have been compiled by experts, in numbers they are well staffed, their finances are sound and now many of them could keep going even without grants from Government. But, they are Chinese schools not only because they teach in Chinese but because their whole spirit is a Chinese national spirit and gives the pupil an outlook which is in no sense Malayan. This spirit is not necessarily anti-British, but it is no less dangerous. It is indifferent to things that matter to the other races in Malaya, and it has no support on which it can lean in time of stress.

===== Page 125 =====
18. The cost of the policy laid down by Sir Arthur Young has always been the obstacle: it would clearly be difficult to provide free English education to the Chinese and to deny it to other nationalities. Approximate calculations show that in the Colony some 150,000 pupils would be affected and the cost would be about $16 million. In any case, teachers are not available. A start has, however, been made by providing (page 178, item 18 of the Estimates for 1940) for training classes for teachers of English in Chinese schools and the classes have already been begun in all three Settlements of Singapore, Penang and Malacca. Furthermore, as an experiment a special Chinese class (i.e. a class of selected boys from the Chinese vernacular schools who will be given an intensive course through the medium of English in an English school for a period of two years and will thereafter join the general stream of English school pupils) is to be formed in each of the Settlements in 1941. The pupils will be selected by examination and interview, and the Chinese schools were notified of the scheme at the end of last year. When these various classes are in full swing it should be possible to draw up a long range programme in the light of the experience gained.

19. But, if we must protect our children from the enemy within our gates it is no less our duty to protect them from the evils which come to them from outside. For years past Malaya has been troubled by the importation of seditious literature, and its seizure is one of the normal duties of the Police. I addressed your predecessor at some length on this subject in my confidential Straits Settlements despatch, dated the 24th of April, 1937, following on protests raised by Mr. D.K. Pritt at my action in Executive Council prohibiting the importation of all communist publications emanating from the Left Book Club. In this I did no more than follow the action taken by the Government of India, but the power of generic prohibition which that Government possesses and which the Colony of the Straits Settlements had used reasonably and profitably since 1915 even though such use might not have been successfully upheld in Court has been denied to us. As we are frequently not aware of the issue of a book or pamphlet until it arrives in Malaya and as it cannot be prohibited until it has been read, it is not to be supposed that all copies of a seditious publication imported here and then proscribed will be seized. The generic prohibition of publications emanating from concerns who make a habit of circulating seditious literature is therefore necessary, and I ask that Lord Harlech's decision may be revised.

20. In his Straits Settlements confidential despatch (4), dated the 23rd of June, 1937, Lord Harlech desired that the powers vested in me in regard to the importation of seditious literature "should be no greater than are essential for the purpose and that they should be exercised with the utmost discretion". This, read with his personal letters, dated the 5th of March and the 18th of June, was the reverse of encouraging. The correspondence suggested to me a policy of hesitation, in that I must not act when action is merely desirable but wait until it becomes essential, that I must ignore the smoke and wait for the flame, and that I must be careful not to upset Mr. Pritt. I am not going to maintain that the advance of communism in Malaya is solely due to this policy, but it is my duty to warn you that if it is to be prevented from spreading further it will not be possible to deal with it save in the most vigorous and wholehearted manner. I cannot understand how any responsible person can maintain that what is suitable in England is suitable here; and when the people who hold these views have never visited a British Colony and can have no con-

===== Page 126 =====
Ception of the conditions there, they ought to be told of the difficulties which they create by misguided interference. They would be horrified if I offered pornographic literature to their own children: why cannot they allow to us the rights which they demand for themselves?

I had just written this when I received your despatch, dated the 29th of March, enclosing the opinions of Mr. Creech Jones on communism in Malaya. I do not know if it would be helpful if I were to see him, and those who think as he does, when I am in England, but I would gladly do so if you think it desirable. I am bringing home documents which will show that communism is far from being a bogey and I hope that I could persuade him that a Colonial Governor is not a servant of imperialism in the sense in which that word is commonly used by those who attack the Government. With some knowledge of the Colonial Empire I have found imperialism to mean the uplift of races more or less primitive to lead happier, healthier, and more useful lives, to teach them how to help their fellow men and to have due regard for justice, truth, and honest dealing. We have not done all that we should or could, but the wonderful and spontaneous rally of the Empire to the Mother Country on the outbreak of war shows that we have not worked altogether in vain. Let those who doubt or disbelieve go and see for themselves.

21. I may remind you that the Bill to provide better control of immigration was submitted as long ago as the 3rd of May, 1938 - see my despatch No: 136 Straits Settlements - and has not yet been settled. I venture to suggest that the matter is of sufficient importance to warrant a quick decision. I sincerely trust that the extensions of my present powers which are provided in the Bill will not be denied.

22. Communism in England may be almost a joke or, as Lord Harlech said, of the palest pink colour, but in an oriental country it is an insidious and dreadful menace with the knife and the gun as its accompaniments; and unless it is tackled firmly in the earlier stages the ultimate use of force becomes inevitable. We have seen what has happened in Palestine, Malta, Cyprus, and Trinidad through lack of resolution: I do not want similar incidents in Malaya. I am responsible for the good government of this country, and I submit that I ought not to be fettered by restrictions which may seem perfectly reasonable at home but which out here are positively dangerous.

EXTRACT FROM REPORT ON FIRST THREE MONTHS OF THE WAR IN MALAYA.

The Marine Department played a leading part in dealing with trouble which developed in October with the crews of local ships. The large increases in pay given by Shipping Companies to crews of vessels plying in the Atlantic and the "War Area" generally naturally had local repercussions, and the position was not improved by the sinking of the S.S. "Hoegh Transporter," which lent colour to claims for extravagant increases in pay by local seamen. The Marine Department, cooperating with the Chinese Protectorate, handled this situation with success, and the difficulty in shipping seamen in Singapore was short-lived. The most effective measures, apart from the normal procedure of sympathetic negotiation under the aegis of the Protectorate, were the finding of enough Malays to man the engine-rooms of two ships, which the recusant Chinese seamen treated as a test case, and the hint of repatriation of those who were unwilling to work for a fair wage.

LABOUR TROUBLES IN MALAYA

Government Hastens Forward Trade Union and Social Legislation

From our Singapore Correspondent

The statement on labour policy in the colonies made by Mr. Malcolm MacDonald, the Secretary of State for the Colonies, at the Colonial Labour Officers' Conference in October has been studied nowhere more closely than in British Malaya. Mr. MacDonald's remarks have a special application to conditions in Malaya, where industrial unrest has caused the Governments of the Straits Settlements and Malay States some concern during the past few months.

The tin and rubber industries of Malaya, which are making a vital contribution to the Empire's war effort both in providing essential raw materials to the Allies and by securing foreign exchange for war purchases by selling the commodities to the United States and other neutrals, are particularly susceptible to labour disturbances. Rubber production is dependent, to some extent at least, on the cycle of nature, and time lost through strikes of rubber tappers cannot usually be made up by working overtime when the men return to work. Tin-mining is in a different category, but the present world demand requires almost maximum production from Malayan mines, so that a strike can have a serious effect on output figures.

WAGES IN TIN AND RUBBER

There have been many threatened strikes among rubber tappers and a few have taken place, but, as with the tin industry, the workers have been kept happy by following a policy of anticipating as far as possible their legitimate claims in respect of higher wages. The mistake made in 1937 when wages did not rise with the higher world prices for rubber and tin and large-scale strikes were staged, has not been repeated. When rubber and tin prices increased at the outbreak of war wages were increased correspondingly, and they have since been kept at levels which allow for the higher cost of living due to the war.

It can be said with confidence that this satisfactory state of affairs has been partly the result of the close attention which the colonial Governments are paying to labour problems, particularly since the Colonial Office gave the lead at the time of the widespread disturbances, in the West Indies. The policy followed throughout has been to increase workers' wages where increases are reasonable and to announce the increase before the men themselves have formulated any demand. This has resulted in greatly improved relations between employers and employed and in its present application is something new in colonial labour policy.

The same tendency may be noted in other industries, but unfortunately, owing to the less rigid organisation of employers, the result has been less satisfactory. As from November 1 the Malay Governments announced the payment of war-time cost-of-living allowances to labourers and artisans engaged on public works, and this lead was followed by the municipalities, the harbour boards, the Admiralty, War Office, and Air Ministry (which are big employers of labour in Singapore), and other employers. These allowances were increased from January 1 to meet further rises in the cost of living as revealed in typical workers' budgets. Yet there has been a serious epidemic of strikes in Singapore. Why?

STRIKES AT SINGAPORE

Mr. MacDonald, in his speech, remarked that "Labour movements in the colonies are very often led by men who, however well-meaning, are completely inexperienced in industrial and political practice. Some of these leaders are inclined to jump to the conclusion that the weapon they should use in order to obtain justice is the weapon of the strike and riots." That is precisely the situation in Singapore today, with the additional danger that political agitators, variously described as Communists, "Chinese patriots," and "Japanese saboteurs" have been endeavouring to advise any group of labourers who felt they had grievances, real or imaginary, against their employers.

Thus there is abundant evidence to show that grievances among workers in Singapore engineering factories, metal works, tobacco factories, and even the harbour board, which could easily have been settled around the conference table if the opportunity had been given, were made the excuse for strikes that developed more on political than on industrial lines.

The subject is not discussed freely in the press, but Sir Shenton Thomas, the Governor of the Straits Settlements, referred in a recent speech to those "who are trying to stir up trouble and who act as if the Nazis were their friends." Furthermore the recent expulsion from Malaya of a prominent Chinese on the grounds that he had encouraged illegal societies that were associated with the Communist party of Malaya is a pointer to one cause of trouble.

The Communist party in Malaya has been particularly active since the Soviet attack on Finland. Communist activity has been largely directed at influencing the labour organisations—one can scarcely call them trade unions—which exist among Singapore workers. It is held to be responsible for the strike of 1,500 workers at the Singapore Harbour Board and for the strike of several hundred domestic servants at the general hospital. Several suspected ringleaders have been arrested and in the case of the harbour board dockyard strike eleven men have been charged under the Defence Regulations for stopping work on an essential war-time service.

ENCOURAGING TRADE UNIONS

Although the authorities are making every effort to deal with the "agitators" by police methods, the most constructive approach to the problem is a scheme for trade unions and industrial courts of arbitration embodied in two bills now before the Straits Settlements Legislative Council.

Trade unions have not hitherto been illegal, but they have had to be registered under the Societies Ordinance and, in fact, few have been formed. Employers have not regarded them with favour. The new law seeks to encourage the formation of unions and the coming forward of a genuine industrial leadership among the workers. Sir Shenton Thomas said of the law: "The formation of trade unions is desired by the colonial Government so that the views of labourers may be definitely ascertained." Incidentally, the definition of trade union given in the law puts organisations of employers and employees on exactly the same footing.

Those familiar with labour conditions in Great Britain may feel that there is a good deal of ground to cover before trade unionism in a colony such as Malaya becomes a really potent factor in relations between employers and employed, but at least good progress is being made. It is particularly satisfactory to know that the trade union law and other social legislation is not being postponed because of the war and that on the contrary, these problems are being tackled with fresh energy and a very live sense of responsibility for the future of labour movements in the colonies.

MALAYAN DISPATCHES ARE STILL BEING CONSIDERED IN DELHI.

New Delhi, Dec. 8.

It is understood that the Governments of Malaya will not receive for a long time to come the Government of India's reply to the Singapore dispatch of last May regarding Indo-Malayan questions, with particular reference to Indian immigration.

The primary reason for the delay is that certain important economic factors have been introduced into the conditions of the Malayan rubber industry and the Indian estate labourers in Malaya by the present war.

The Government of India convened a session of the Standing Committee on Emigration on Aug. 28, but the outbreak of war a week later made it impossible for the Government to draft and send out its counter-proposals to Malaya, as originally scheduled. If necessary, the Government of India might convene another meeting of the Standing Committee before it dispatches its reply to Malaya, meanwhile closely studying the trends of change now observable in Malayan industry and cost of living.

Firm Attitude.

That the Malayan Governments have not conceded the points raised during the Delhi talks is now clear in informed circles, and that the Government of India is prepared to contest every point raised in the Malayan dispatch of May is not concealed either.

The main contentions of the Government of India, who had the consistent support of the Congress Government of Madras until its resignation last month, are that the Indian question in Malaya is a composite one and that the family, and not the individual labourer, should be taken as a unit in the calculation of wages which, while having some relation to the position of the industry concerned from time to time, should be capable of conforming to basic minimum human standards of living and comfort.

There is no question of the Government of India today departing from this position, but it is also clear that New Delhi would do anything in its power to bring about an honourable understanding between the two governments concerned. Meanwhile, the ban on assisted emigration enforced in June 1938 continues.

Two New Factors.

There are two new factors noted by the Government of India since the outbreak of the war: the rise in the cost of living of Indian labourers as a result of the war and the restoration of their wages to the 1931 level by the planters, even though it lacks statutory confirmation.

===== Page 200 =====
It is believed here that during the early stages of the war, the cost of primary commodities in Malaya had gone up. It was estimated that during the first month of the war, the cost of living of the Indian labourer in Malaya had gone up by one-eighth, while the restored wages registered only an increase of one-ninth.

Later estimates show that the increased cost of living of Indian labourers has maintained its level at 7 percent, while the position of the restored wages in relation to the standard rates of wages in force before the Delhi negotiations (which constituted one of the points of discussion between the two governments) remained the same.

"Belated Gesture"

The Government of India, while appreciating this belated gesture on the part of Malayan employers, feels that the future of Indian wages in Malaya must have some definite relation to the condition of the rubber industry, yet determined by certain minimum considerations of human comfort, and with the added security that at no time will they be permitted to go below the basic level.

MINUTE BY H.E. THE GOVERNOR

Labour and Increasing Prices

Already there are clear signs of possible labour trouble. The cost of living is going up and, even though the rise may not be appreciable at present, the unsatisfactory feature about it is that it has occurred in spite of the efforts of the Government to keep it down. The price of raw products has risen, and may rise still further. I imagine that the price of many locally manufactured articles has also risen as the result. We are faced therefore with the fact that it is costing labourers more to live, while the value to employers of those engaged on productive work is far greater than it was a few months ago.

2. The refusal of a few employers to raise wages in sufficient proportion to the increase in the selling price of raw products was in itself enough to cause the 1937 strikes in which, through the efforts of agitators, at one time some 20,000 men were involved; but if this failure to recognise that higher produce prices justify higher wages is aggravated by an increase in the cost of living, then we may have a much worse situation to face than we had two years ago. The labourer knows perfectly well whether his particular industry is prospering or not, and it is in times of prosperity, not in a slump, that strikes are most likely. The agitator then comes into his own, and the strike for better conditions is converted into resistance of lawful authority.

3. Insistence...

===== Page 206 =====
3. Insistence on fair and proper conditions of labour is always one of the most important duties of Government, but today in Malaya it is possibly the most important of all. Apart altogether from the need to keep the country peaceful, an adequate and regular supply of raw products and the quick completion of naval and air defences is essential for the prosecution of the war and the safety of the Far East. I know that this is generally realised, but unfortunately we have some black sheep among our employers and it is these people—this small minority—who can do so much harm.

4. Our aim should be to ensure that it shall be impossible for the Malayan Governments suddenly to be confronted with a strike caused by admittedly inadequate pay or admittedly unsatisfactory general conditions of employment. If ever remedies are necessary, they should be foreseen and introduced before disaffection induces a strike. There is every virtue in this, but none in concessions given under compulsion. The average labourer responds to fair and sympathetic treatment just as anyone else.

The U.P.A.M. in advising an increase of wage in the rubber industry so soon after the price of the raw material rose and before ever they were approached by the Government, and all that, I wish to remind them now is that very possibly the existing wage will have to be increased still further.

6. In the previous paragraph I have indicated that all employers of labour should be on the alert so that hardships may be met even before complaints are formulated. It is just as important, perhaps even more important, that the Labour Department and the Chinese Protectorate should be on the alert, and that the officers should know what is happening and have a good idea of what is likely to happen. The inspection of labour should be even more constant and systematic than heretofore, and should be regarded as the first duty of every Labour and Protectorate officer. Social questions must take second place for the time being. Any abuses that are brought to light should be reported to the Government at once and brought to the notice of the employer with the demand that he shall take immediate steps to rectify them. Inspections of bad or indifferent employers should be more frequent than those of good; it is the likely sources of trouble that must be most carefully watched. It should be ascertained for certain that labour conditions in important concerns such as the Malayan Collieries are satisfactory. Contact between the Labour Department, the Chinese Protectorate, and the Police must be close and continuous. Office work must be reduced to the absolute minimum and routine duties strictly curtailed. Correspondence must be very concise; it is only the bare facts and suggested recommendations that are required.

===== Page 208 =====
The prime need is for unremitting vigilance and prompt remedial action, and only daily work on inspection can ensure these.

7. It goes without saying that in every State the B.R. or B.A. will enjoin on his administrative officers the need for similar vigilance and will ensure that he is completely informed at all times of the attitude of labour in his State. It is essential that he shall be in a position to forecast future developments as far ahead as possible. So, too, the Colonial Secretary and the Federal Secretary will be in touch with Heads of labour-employing Departments, Municipalities, and the Harbour Board. I ask, also, that the Permanent Labour Committee (consisting of the S.C.A., the D.P.W., and representatives of the three fighting Services and the Municipality and Harbour Board) which considers wage rates and other labour matters in respect of Singapore, will be equally watchful.

8. There is no need to notify me of minor incidents which have no ulterior significance, but I wish to be kept fully informed of the general trend of events and of possible developments. Any incident which has justified or is thought likely to justify the drafting of more police to the spot must of course be reported to me at once.
