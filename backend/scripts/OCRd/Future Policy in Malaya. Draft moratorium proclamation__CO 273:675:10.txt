I am sorry to have held this up for a few days. The matter, however, is rather delicate—besides being urgent—and I am not quite sure what line we ought to take.

The extreme urgency of having the Malayan Draft Proclamations finalized, printed, and despatched to S.E.A.C. is a matter of which we have not been suffered to remain in doubt. The telegrams from S.E.A.C. have been increasingly pressing, not to say minatory in tone, and have been the cause of much anxiety to the Rear Party of M.P.U. (Col. O'Connor and Mr. Spencer Wilkinson) who have done everything in their power to speed matters along. This has not been easy in view of the tendency of Departments to raise a stream of new points, and hardly to wait for these to be settled before they raise others. This has happened on other Proclamations besides the Moratorium Proclamation, and the Colonial Office has in each case tried to help the M.P.U. by cutting through the tangle of comment and counter-comment, and by pointing out that it is much more important to have the Proclamations issued in time than to have them perfect in every respect. In the case of most of the Proclamations, results have at last been achieved, and the printed copies are in the course of being shipped out to India. The Moratorium Proclamation, however, in spite of the efforts of Mr. Emanuel and myself as embodied in No. 4, is still with us. The latest development is the letter from Mr. Harris at No. 5.

I agree with Mr. Emanuel's comments on this letter. In the first place, it is somewhat depressing that new versions of the draft are still being prepared and circulated to Departments at this stage in history. In the second place, it is quite wrong that the War Office should be commenting on a draft by M.P.U. as though M.P.U. were a separate Department. Mr. Harris is new to his present work and probably does not realize this. When the Main Body of the Unit left this country, it was impressed upon us that the Rear Party would technically have no separate existence apart from the branch of the War Office with whom the different sections of the Rear Party would be concerned. As Mr. Emanuel says, the latest draft ought to have been prepared by M.P.U. and the War Office in consultation. That is to say, if the War Office did not like the M.P.U.'s draft, they should have settled their difficulties with M.P.U. before circulating it. As it is, we are not only starting again but are starting again on a basis of disagreement. It will be seen that the War Office, rightly or wrongly, prefer the original draft in some respects. If these second thoughts continue, it is difficult to see how the matter will ever be brought to a conclusion.

On the other hand, it is difficult for the Colonial Office to find fault with War Office procedure or to suggest a meeting. This matter is in the hands of the War Office. They know the urgency of the situation as well as we do, and it is for them to call inter-departmental meetings if and when they consider them necessary.

Dear Harris,

I write with reference to your letter of 14th July to H.H. Thomas about the draft moratorium proclamation for Malaya. You sent a copy of this letter and enclosure to Fisher inviting comments.

I do not feel qualified to approach the matter from a legal standpoint and will therefore confine my remarks to the particular points on which we here may be entitled to have definite views. The following are the comments I wish to offer:

On the whole we prefer McFadzean's definition of banks but slightly modified as follows:

"'Financial institution' means any person or body of persons whether incorporated or not carrying on the business of banking including the receiving of money on current account or on deposit, or of granting in any form, or of dealing in foreign exchange, maintaining safe deposit facilities."

With regard to McFadzean's suggested deletion, Part II paragraph 4, we feel that 4(d) and 4(e) should be retained as otherwise the banks will have no authority to operate at all within the limits of the moratorium. Dear Harris,

Reference our conversation concerning the Malaya Moratorium Proclamation, I enclose a redraft for your consideration.

2. The original draft appears to have been based largely on the Italian model, but the circumstances likely to be prevailing in Malaya on our arrival will be radically different from those we found in Europe. In Italy, for example, we recognised the Italian dire, whereas our intention in Malaya is to demonise the Japanese currency — likely to be the only one in effective circulation — by squeezing it out. As regards the banks, we permitted all the Italian banks to function at a fairly early date, whereas in Malaya the Japanese banks will be closed and liquidated.

3. Turning to the proposed amendments:

a) The definitions of "banks", "banking business" and "financial institution" in the original were unduly cumbersome and too embracing. The premises we want to close and the business we want to suspend are those of the banks in the narrower sense of the term and the definitions have, therefore, been simplified and brought under one head.

b) Part (II) paragraph 4 has been deleted in full and as a consequence Part (II) paragraph 3(1) has been amended by the deletion of the first line from "Except" to "Mentioned". The reason for this is that the Japanese banks will be closed and liquidated.

===== Page 22 =====
Liquidated and no transactions, including the collection of money due to them, can be permitted until they are under proper control. As regards non-Japanese banks which have been allowed to function during the occupation, we know insufficient about their business to justify the automatic operation of the provisions of the original paragraph 4. Some of the non-Japanese banks may have hidden reserves of Malay dollars, but the bulk, if not all, of their till money is almost certain to be in Japanese currency which we will not accept. Moreover, if depositors' accounts are simply expressed in dollars without specifying Malay or Japanese - what is the obligation of the banks to their customers bearing in mind the provisions of our Currency Proclamation? Again, it is very possible that the Japanese may have forced these banks to invest part of their assets in Japanese securities and this, coupled with the fact that their till money will be valueless, may result in these institutions being insolvent on our arrival. In the circumstances, it would be clearly insoluble to grant any creditor a preferred position merely, for example, because he has a debt due to the Government. It seems to me that we know so little about the banks in Malaya that we should wait until we find out the facts and not prejudice an equitable solution by prejudging the position.

c) Part (III). Paragraph 7 of the original draft has been deleted because it commits us to, or, at the very least implies, a policy which we may not be able to carry out, and in any case, it is not necessary in a Proclamation declaring a moratorium to state what the status of debts will be when the moratorium is lifted. It is almost certain that in order to achieve justice as between the local inhabitants some form of debt revaluation will be necessary, and the details of this can only be worked out when the facts are known.

===== Page 23 =====
d) Part (III) paragraph 8 has been amended to bring it into line with the policy laid down in the previous sub-paragraph.

4. The above also, I think, answers Young's query about the possibility of there being no moratorium. The demonetisation of the Japanese currency must inevitably upset the debtor/creditor relationships in the territory, and there is, therefore, a strong prima facie case, which did not exist in Europe, for the establishment of a moratorium.

5. I have spoken to Turnbull on the above lines and he agrees to the principles, although he has not been consulted on the actual detailed wording.

6. I am sending sufficient copies of this letter and the enclosure for onward transmission by you to Gent and Young.

Yours sincerely,

(sgd) F. S. Mac Haddon

A Proclamation to close financial institutions, to proclaim a moratorium, and to prohibit certain dealings in land.

I,

General Officer Commanding Military Forces, Malaya, in exercise of the authority conferred upon me by the Supreme Allied Commander, South East Asia Command, DO HEREBY PROCLAIM AND ORDER:

PART I - PRELIMINARY.

Short title.

1. This Proclamation may be cited as the Moratorium Proclamation.

Interpretation.

2. In this Proclamation unless the context otherwise requires:

"Controller" means the officer for the time being performing the duties of Controller of Finance and Accounts under the British Military Administration;

"dealing" in relation to land means any transaction between parties, of whatever nature by which land is affected and includes a caveat;

"debt" includes any form of claim or obligation for the payment of money;

"financial institution" means any person or body of persons whether incorporated or not carrying on the business of receiving money on current account or on deposit or of dealing in foreign exchange or of maintaining safe deposit facilities;

"Government" means the Government of the Straits Settlements or the Government of the Federated Malay States or the Government of any Malay State;

===== Page 25 =====
"land" includes land of whatever tenure; tenements and hereditaments corporeal or incorporeal; rights over, and interests in land; houses and other buildings and fixtures attached to the earth; and an undivided share in land;

"Local Authority" includes the Municipal Commissioners of the Town of Singapore, the Municipal Commissioners of the Town and Port of Malacca, the Municipal Commissioners of George Town, any Rural Board, Sanitary Board or Town Board, the Singapore Improvement Trust, the Singapore Harbour Board and the Penang Harbour Board.

Suspension of business and closing of premises.

Re-opening of premises and resumption of business.

PART II - CLOSING OF FINANCIAL INSTITUTIONS.

5. (1) Every financial institution shall forthwith suspend business and close its premises to the public.

(2) The re-opening of any premises so closed and the resumption of any business so suspended shall be subject to such terms, conditions and restrictions as the Controller, or any officer authorised by him in that behalf, may prescribe, by notice or by general or specific order or by written communication addressed to the financial institution concerned.

PART III - MORATORIUM.

4. Except as hereinafter provided, a moratorium is hereby proclaimed in respect of all debts.

5. (1) During the period of the moratorium, all debts affected thereby are suspended and extended for the said period. In computing

===== Page 26 =====
Any period of limitation, the period of the moratorium shall be excluded.

(2) No interest shall run during the period of the moratorium in respect of any debt affected thereby.

(3) No rights, remedies, or powers in respect of any such debt shall, at any time, be exercised or enforced by process of law solely by reason of non-payment as a result of the provisions of this proclamation; but nothing herein shall prevent the voluntary payment of any debt.

6. The moratorium hereby imposed shall not apply to any debt -
(a) incurred after the date when this Proclamation takes effect within the area in which such debt is incurred;
(b) due in respect of:
   (i) wages or salary;
   (ii) professional services;
   (iii) work and labour;

7. The Chief Civil Affairs Officer, or any Officer authorised by him in that behalf, may, in any part of Malaya, by notice or by general or specific order grant exemption in respect of any debt or class of debts.

PART IV - DEALINGS IN LAND.

8. During the period of the moratorium no person shall transact, or attempt to transact, any dealing in land except -
(a) any lease or tenancy for a term not exceeding one year; or
(b) with the written approval of the Controller or any officer authorised by him in that behalf any lien or equitable mortgage by deposit of documents of title with a financial institution.

===== Page 27 =====
Provided that the Chief Civil Affairs Officer as regards any part of Malaysia, the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer, Malay Peninsula, as regards any part of the Malay Peninsula, or the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer, Singapore, as regards any part of the Settlement of Singapore, or any officer empowered by any of them in that behalf, may by general or specific order, and upon such conditions, if any, as he may think fit, modify or terminate the restriction contained in this section or grant exemption in respect of any dealing or class of dealings in land.

9. Except in respect of dealings permitted under the provisions of section 8, no dealing in land shall be registered at any Registry of Titles or Land Office, and no instrument effecting any dealing in land shall be registered at any Registry of Deeds.

10. Any instrument purporting to effect any dealing in land contrary to the provisions of this Part shall be void.

PART V - PENALTIES.

11. Any person contravening the provisions of this Proclamation shall, on conviction by a Military court, be punishable with imprisonment of either description not exceeding five years, or with fine up to any amount, or with both such fine and imprisonment.

I, General Officer Commanding Military Force, Malaya, in exercise of the authority conferred upon me by the Supreme Allied Commander, South East Asia Command, DO HEREBY PROCLAIM AND ORDER:-

PART I - PRELIMINARY.

1. This Proclamation may be cited as the Moratorium Proclamation.

2. In this Proclamation unless the context otherwise requires:-

PART III - MORATORIUM.

5. Except as hereinafter provided, a moratorium is hereby proclaimed in respect of all debts.

===== Page 38 =====
6. (1) During the period of the moratorium, all debts affected thereby are suspended and extended for the said period. In computing any period of limitation the period of the moratorium shall be excluded.

(2) No interest shall run during the period of the moratorium in respect of any debt affected thereby.

(3) No rights, remedies or powers in respect of any such debt shall, at any time, be exercised or enforced by process of law solely by reason of non-payment as a result of the provisions of this Proclamation; but nothing herein shall prevent the voluntary payment of any debt.

7. Upon the termination of the moratorium all debts affected thereby shall again be of full force and effect.

8. The moratorium hereby imposed shall not apply to any debt -

(ii) professional services;

(iii) work and labour;

(iv) rent not exceeding one year's rent; or

(d) due to any Government or Local Authority.

PART IV - DEALINGS IN LAND.

10. During the period of the moratorium no person shall transact, or attempt to transact, any dealing in land except -
(a) any lease or tenancy for a term not exceeding one year; or

Provided that the Chief Civil Affairs Officer as regards any part of Malaya, the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer, Malay Peninsula, as regards any part of the Malay Peninsula, or the Deputy Chief Civil Affairs Officer, Singapore, as regards any part of the Settlement of Singapore, or any officer empowered by any of them in that behalf, may by general or specific order, and upon such conditions, if any, as he may think fit, modify or terminate the restriction contained in this section or grant exemption in respect of any dealing or class of dealings in land.
