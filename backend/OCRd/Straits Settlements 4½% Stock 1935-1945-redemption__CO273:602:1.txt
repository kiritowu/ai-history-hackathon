The two loans - B and C in Mr. Costar's list with which we are now concerned, were both raised by the Straits Government for reloan in their entirety to the F.M.S. The Straits Settlements Government itself will not be particularly concerned to represent the loans and borrow at a cheaper rate of interest since the entire expense is recouped from the F.M.S. Government, but it is clearly in the F.M.S. interest that these two loans should be paid off and advantage taken of the lower rates of interest now ruling. We shall not know until the Governor replies to our air mail despatch of the 28th September whether the F.M.S. and Straits Governments would prefer the method of the new issue by the F.M.S. Government on the market, or whether they will come to an arrangement between themselves for a refunding operation for which there are likely to be sufficient available monies in the funds of the Straits Government next year. There is, I suppose, a further possibility that a local dollar loan might be raised in Malaya itself under present conditions.

It is now agreed that on, or shortly before, the 15th December notice should be given of the intention to repay the 4½ Loan on the 15th June 1935.

The Crown Agents recommended that the Sinking Fund of this 4½ Loan should be used towards repayment. The fund will amount to approximately £600,000 in June 1935. The F.M.S. Government would, however, prefer to retain this Sinking Fund to help in the redemption of another loan in December 1936, and to obtain funds to redeem the 4½ Loan by means of a new issue of £4,000,000, and to provide the balance of £200,000, plus expenses of the new issue, by drawing upon current account and if necessary to a small extent on the 4½ Sinking Fund.

If the F.M.S. Government prefer that course I do not think that there is any reason in Mr. Ezechiel's letter (No.2 in this file) sufficiently strong to justify our refusing to accept their view. In that case the 4½ Sinking Fund - or the balance of it - will remain to be added to the 6% Sinking Fund and the Supplementary Sinking Fund (respectively amounting in December 1936 to £830,000 and £2,017,000) to assist in the redemption of the 6% Stock.

The High Commissioner anticipates that the new issue of approximately £4,000,000 could be floated on more favourable terms locally in Malaya (presumably a dollar loan) than is contemplated by the Crown Agents for an issue in London. They were aware of the Government's preference for a local issue and in paragraph 9 of their letter they proceeded to show reasons for concluding that a sterling issue in London would be appreciably cheaper. The High Commissioner asks to be informed of the estimated expenses of an issue in London and we should ask the Crown Agents for their observations on that point, and also on the period of currency of the contemplated new loan. On the latter point the High Commissioner suggests a minimum period of 20 years, while the Crown Agents have so far only told us that a period of 45 years would be too long.

STRAITS SETTLEMENTS LOANS (REPAYMENT) BILL.

The Clerk of Council places on the table a certificate of urgency, signed by the President, in connection with the introduction of this Bill.

READING.

The Acting Financial Adviser: Sir, I rise to move the reading of the Straits Settlements Loans (Repayment) Bill.

This Bill provides the Government with legislative power to enable them to raise loans in London at any time to effect the repayment of the outstanding Straits Settlements loans.

Honourable members are aware that the major part of the public debt of the Federated Malay States comprises two sterling loans. The first of these is the 6 per cent. loan of 1921 for 3,155,000 which matures in 1936; and the second is the 49 per cent. loan of 1922 for 3,200,000 which matures in 1935. These loans were raised in London in the name of the Colony and the proceeds were lent to the Federated Malay States. Repayment is consequently due to the Colony on the dates I have just mentioned.

The loans were raised under the authority of a single enactment, the Straits Settlements Loans Ordinance No. 182 in the Colony and the Straits Settlements Loans Enactment, 1921 (No. 2), in the Federated Malay States. It is therefore considered desirable to take power to deal with both of them in the Bill now before you.

The Bill gives power to borrow money for the specific purposes I mentioned at the beginning under the authority of the General Loan and Inscribed Stock Enactment, 1921. That Enactment is the general authority for raising loans in London; and honourable members will remember that, by His Majesty's Colonial Stock Acts Extension (Federated Malay States) Order, 1932, which was published in the Gazette about two months ago, and by the Federated Malay States Government Trustee Securities Enactment, 1934—that is, Enactment No. 21 of 1934—loans raised by the Federated Malay States in London rank as trustee securities in the United Kingdom, so that we can now go to the London market and, if need be, borrow money in our own name and no longer indirectly as before.

The reason for introducing this legislation now is this. The Government desires, now that the financial position has improved, to be free to take any steps that may be found to be necessary or advisable to complete the repayment of the sterling loans. I am authorised to say that the Government has been advised by the Right Hon. the Secretary of State for the Colonies that it would be imprudent, in the condition of the money market in London, to defer the taking of legislative powers to deal with these sterling loans, because it is obviously desirable to be in a position to take advantage of market conditions at the first favourable opportunity.

Sir, I move that the Bill be read.

The Acting Legal Adviser: Sir, I second the motion.

Mr. Shearn: Sir, there are three matters arising on this Bill which appear to me to be of some importance.

In the first place, the Bill makes no provision for the repeal of the 1921 Enactment. That is the Enactment which authorises the Federated Malay States Government to borrow up to 30 millions sterling from the Straits Settlements Government. The result is this, that after we have passed this Bill we shall have power to borrow in England up to 39,355,000, and we shall still retain the power to

===== Page 49 =====
Borrow £10 million from the Straits Settlements Government without reference to the Federal Council. It appears to me that this Enactment, the existing Enactment, giving authority to the Federated Malay States Government to borrow money from the Straits Settlements Government, should be brought to an end as soon as we borrow the new money in London. That is to say, the Enactment should be repealed as from some future date.

The next matter which strikes me on this Bill is clause 4. Clause 4 reads:

"The monies to be borrowed under the authority of this Enactment shall be appropriated and applied to the purposes specified herein."

On perusing the Bill I can see no specification of the purposes to which the money is to be applied. The purposes can be inferred from clause 3; they are not specified. Therefore it appears at present that, although power is given to the Government to borrow the money for the purpose of repaying the Straits Settlements Government, they are under no obligation to do so; and I suggest that clause 4 requires amendment to make it quite clear that the Federated Malay States Government will be obliged to devote the money to the purpose of repaying the Straits Settlements Government.

Now the third point—it is one, I think, of greater importance than the last two—is this. Section 7 of the Enactment, under which the existing loan is authorised to be obtained, requires that the principal monies shall be repaid in Singapore. This loan, which we are now contemplating, will be obtained in England, and, quite apart from the statement we heard when this Bill was put before Council, that must be so, because we are obtaining the loan under the General Loan and Inscribed Stock Enactment which applies only to loans obtained in England. The result of that is prima facie that the money obtained in England must be remitted to Singapore to repay the loan in Singapore. Now the amount involved is £9,355,000. I ask is the whole of this sum really to be remitted from London to Singapore? The cost of such a transfer would undoubtedly be very considerable. I would like to know, and I think the public would like to know, whether in fact all the money raised in England will have to be remitted to Singapore in accordance with section 7 of that 1921 Enactment? Where are the stockholders? If they are in England, is it necessary to remit the money from England to Singapore? Cannot arrangements be made so that the money should remain in England? Alternatively, if in fact the whole of this sum has to be repaid in Singapore, can arrangements be made so that as much as possible of the loan is raised locally? (Applause.)

The President puts the question that the Bill be read.

The motion is agreed to.

The debt of the Straits Settlements consists of:

(3) The debt contracted by the Straits Settlements for money raised by loan in Malaya during the war and handed over to His Majesty's Treasury, which in return pays the interest on, and will repay the capital of the loans less a comparatively small percentage of interest, which represents a gift from the Colony to the Mother Country, and a debt which amounted to a little over twenty million dollars (£2,333,333) which has been raised locally chiefly to enable the Federated Malay States to repay the War Loan referred to later. The Federated Malay States will pay off their portion of this loan from assets already held and earmarked for the purpose. It will therefore be seen that the general revenues of the Straits Settlements are lightly cumbered by debt charges which are not recoverable from His Majesty's Treasury and other sources.

An Enactment to enable Federated Malay States Government Loans to be treated in the United Kingdom as Trustee Securities.

A. CALDECOTT,
President of the Federal Council.

WHEREAS by an Act of the Imperial Parliament known as the Colonial Development Act, 1929, it is amongst other things provided that His Majesty may, if a representation is made to Him by the Treasury and the Secretary of State that it is desirable so to do, make an Order in Council directing that the Colonial Stock Acts, 1877 to 1900, shall be extended, subject to such modifications and to such conditions as may appear expedient to His Majesty, so as to apply to stock issued after the passing of that Act and forming part of the public debt of any territory specified in the Order which is under His Majesty's protection;

AND WHEREAS His Majesty has, in pursuance of the powers vested in Him by section 3 of the said Colonial Development Act, 1929, and of all other powers enabling Him in that behalf, by Order in Council dated October 1st, 1932, ordered that the Colonial Stock Acts, 1877 to 1900, should, subject to the modifications set out therein, be extended so as to apply to stock issued after the passing of the said Colonial Development Act, 1929, and forming part of the public debt of the Federated Malay States as if the Federated Malay States were a Colony;

AND WHEREAS the said Order in Council prescribes that a condition that the Government of the Federated Malay States shall provide by legislation for the payment, out of the revenues of the Federated Malay States of any sums which may become payable to stockholders under any judgment, decree, rule or order of a Court in the United Kingdom shall have effect in relation to stock to which the Colonial Stock Acts, 1877 to 1900, apply by virtue of the said Order in Council as if it had been prescribed by the Treasury under section 2 of the Colonial Stock Act, 1900:

===== Page 109 =====
AND WHEREAS it is expedient that provision should be made to enable advantage to be taken of the said Acts in respect of investment of trust funds in Government securities, now therefore:

It is hereby enacted by the Rulers of the Federated Malay States by and with the advice and consent of the Federal Council as follows:

1. This Enactment may be cited as the Government Trustee Securities Enactment, 1934.

2. In this Enactment the expression "Colonial Stock Acts" means the Acts of the Imperial Parliament known as the Colonial Stock Acts, 1877 to 1900, and any amendments thereof.

3. This Enactment shall apply to all securities heretofore or hereafter created or issued on behalf of the Government of the Federated Malay States, to which for the time being the Colonial Stock Acts apply, and which are for the time being registered in the United Kingdom in accordance with the provisions of these Acts, each and all of which securities are hereinafter referred to as "Government securities".

4. (i) Whenever by the final judgment, decree, rule or order of any Court of competent jurisdiction in the United Kingdom, any sum of money is adjudged or declared to be payable by the Government of the Federated Malay States in respect of any Government securities, the Government shall forthwith pay that sum out of the funds in the hands of the Crown Agents belonging to the Government, without further appropriation than this Enactment.

(ii) For the purposes of this section "final judgment, decree, rule, or order," means in the case of appeal the final judgment, decree, rule or order of the ultimate Court hearing the appeal.

5. In order to enable every such payment to be duly made, a certificate under the hands of the Crown Agents, specifying the sum so paid under order of any such Court, shall be sufficient authority to the Auditor-General or other officer having the auditing of their accounts for passing such sum, without further appropriation.

===== Page 150 =====
At a very early date, with a request that the Government's approval, if it is to be given, should be telegraphed not later than the end of October so as to leave us a full month in which to make all arrangements.

It may occur to someone at the Colonial Office to ask why we have not followed the precedent of the Southern Nigeria 3½% loan and suggested converting the whole of the Straits 4½% loan and retaining its sinking fund towards the redemption of the 6% loan in 1936. There is, however, an important difference. In the Nigeria case, as the result of converting the whole of the 3½% loan, no further borrowing will be necessary in 1936; but if the whole of the Straits 4½% loan were converted and its sinking fund retained, it would not obviate the necessity for a large new loan in 1936. We consider it desirable to use a sinking fund for its original purpose unless there are very special advantages (as in the Nigeria case) to be gained by diverting it.

As the repayment of the 4½% loan will be an optional one, the raising of a new loan for the purpose comes within the so-called "embargo" and the consent of the Chancellor of the Exchequer will therefore be necessary. 7. We have shown the sinking fund contribution for the new loan at the minimum of 1% per annum (as in the case of the existing 4½% loan).
