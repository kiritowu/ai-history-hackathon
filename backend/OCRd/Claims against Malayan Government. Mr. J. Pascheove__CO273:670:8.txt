1. Mr. Foster Hall.
2. M. Parkin.

As I do not know what the terms and conditions of the lease were, it is difficult for me to advise. Generally speaking, however, I should say that there would be liability to pay rent for the whole period of the lease notwithstanding the Japanese occupation. As Asquith J recently said in Leighton's Investment Trust and the Cricklewood Property and Investment Trust Ltd (1942 2 All E.R. 580): "A contract may be frustrated, but a demise is more than a contract. It creates an estate in land. It transfers proprietary as well as personal rights. This seems to me just as true of a building lease as of any other kind of lease."

I have not seen a report of the whole judgment, but he apparently held that a building lease, under which, since the war, it had become legally impossible to build, was a lease and unaffected by the doctrine of frustration.

5/5/43.

P.S. Subject to the terms and conditions of the lease, and assuming that it is valid and subsisting and governed by Straits Settlements law, and that there is nothing in that law which makes the position different from that under English law, I would cite the following additional authority for the view that there would be liability to pay rent for the whole period of the lease notwithstanding the Japanese occupation.

In Paradine v Jane the defendant pleaded that Prince Rupert with other enemy aliens "by force entered upon the defendant's possession and him expelled...whereby he could not take the profits"; but the plea was held to be insufficient for an action in debt for the rent.

Further

Further, writers of international law seem to be unanimous in holding that enemy occupation does not affect the title to immovable property in the territory occupied.

Lt. Col. McKerron:

We should be grateful for the advice of the Malayan planning unit on the issue raised in this file, and I imagine that you will probably wish to refer it to Major-General Hone on his return from leave.

The background is that, as I think you are aware, we have been doing our best to settle all claims against the Malayan Governments, when we could be satisfied as to their validity, both on the grounds of equity (from the point of view of the claimant) and also in order to reduce the number of such matters, as far as possible, which will have to be dealt with locally on the reoccupation of Malaya. We have had a variety of such claims, relating mostly to works of a "P.W.D." character, completed before the Japanese occupation. This is, I think, the first case that we have had where the application is for rent due on premises rented by one of the Malayan Governments. We were at first disposed to think that it would be impossible to deal with the claim, until we get back to Malaya, on the grounds that we could obtain no evidence as

To the transaction involved. Recently, however, it has come to notice that one of the sealed packets sent from Singapore to Canberra for safe keeping, (of which we have lists in the Office) is said to contain a copy of the lease in question. We could therefore arrange for this package to be opened and thereby obtain the necessary evidence on which to effect a settlement of the claim.

As you will see from previous minutes on the file, we have been advised that the Straits Government will be liable to pay rent for these premises for the whole period of the lease, notwithstanding the Japanese occupation, and it will be seen from 55152/43 that the Government of Burma has received similar advice.

It seems certain that when the Military Administration begins to function in Malaya, they will receive many claims of this character and we ought not to do anything now which will in any way prejudice any action which that Administration will wish to take in respect of other similar claims. You will see that in the letter of the 27th February from Messrs. Arthur Robinson and Co., the suggestion is made that there should be a compromise arrangement which would provide for the surrender of the lease. This suggestion was made before it was known (it is still presumably unknown to Mr. Pascheove) that there is probably a copy of the lease in the sealed packet mentioned above. That package ought presumably to be handed over to Mr. Pascheove (who is the virtual owner of Messrs. Paskoe) and he will then be in a position to support his claim. Whether, with that evidence, he would still wish to compromise, one cannot say, but it would seem to be probable (as he has several times intimated in another of his claims that he is short of money). One of the difficulties would be to negotiate a compromise at such a great distance, and the question might arise whether we (on knowing the terms of the lease) should make him an offer or whether we should leave it to him to make a suggestion.

Mr. Paskin.

I consider that the C.O. should continue to settle, as far as it can, claims against the Malayan Government, and that before my party leaves this country we should be given a note of the claims which have been met and the principles which have been applied.

Where the law is clear, as in this case, I see no reason for hesitating to apply it, but where cases are referred to the Colonial Office and the law is in doubt, we should resist the claim and leave the claimant to file an action in the Malayan Courts in due course.

I see in File 55152 that there has been some suggestion that ad hoc legislation should be passed for the purpose of relieving Governments from the law which attaches to them under the existing law. I think that this is a question of important policy which should be considered further now. At the same time, I think we should be open to very considerable criticism if we attempt to amend the settled law on this subject merely in order to save money.

What I have stated above in regard to the settlement of claims as they arise is, of course, subject to a decision to leave the law as it stands.

18th August, 1943.

Mr. Gent,

You should, I think, see these papers at this stage.

The question as to the revision of the law which is raised on 55152/43 relates to leases which are for an indefinite period, and liabilities arising from the requisitioning of property. As I understand it there is no suggestion that there should be any attempt to relieve lessees (including the Government) of their responsibilities where, as in the case of Mr. Pascheove, the lease is for a definite period. As General Hone says, the Government would be open to very considerable criticism if it amended the law so as to relieve itself of such liabilities merely with the object of saving money. As regards private leases, I can conceive that, on the reoccupation of Malaya, there may have to be a moratorium, the effect of which, however, will be more to postpone liability for payment.

In the circumstances I think that we shall have to settle Mr. Pascheove's claim, and as the lease is now found to exist I do not see that there can be any further question of a compromise. As regards this particular claim, therefore, I think we should now telegraph to Australia as in draft herewith.

As regards General Hone's views to the question of the revision of the law on the lines suggested in 55152/43, I have always assumed that this sort of problem would have to be tackled by the legal member of the Malayan Planning Unit when he becomes available. There must be many such conundrums to be solved, and they will clearly require intensive study. I do not see how the Department can be expected to visualise the various problems of this kind which will arise, and I do

My collection of the ruling in the Sammon case is that it will not affect the present.

===== Page 13 =====
The Treasury Solicitor's ruling in question was that, subject to proof that the work had been completed and that the price had become due before the occupation of Malaya by the Japanese, a contractor is entitled to payment. The question of currency is immaterial. Conversely, if, at the time of the Japanese occupation, payment had not become due, the contractor would legally have no claim.

In the matter of this rental, the S.S. Government had paid for the first year of the lease (1941) and the claim is for a continuing payment for which the S.S. Government is legally liable, since the lease was for a definite period, i.e., up to 31st December 1943.

The Official Secretary, Office of the High Commissioner for the United Kingdom, CANBERRA, A.C.T.

Dear Sir,

Your ref. 10/61.

Re Mr. I. Pascheove.

We thank you for your letter of the 23rd instant.

While there is no doubt that the Government is entitled to postpone decisions on claims submitted to it until after Malaya has been re-occupied, we wish to respectfully express our doubt as to the wisdom of their policy.

We find ourselves quite unable to believe that on the re-occupation of Singapore the local records will be found neatly filed in their proper places, all ready to provide the necessary verification. It seems to us much more likely that all of them will have been pulped and re-manufactured into paper. If this is not their fate, the enemy may set fire to everything inflammable before leaving, or they may be destroyed by our own operations against the Japanese. It would seem to us little less than a miracle if the records were ever recovered.

We quite realise that if the claimant is unknown or of doubtful character and no verification of his claim can be obtained from independent sources, the claim must inevitably wait and may never be established. But at the present time when the claimants are refugees in various parts of the world with little or no money, most of them would be not only willing but delighted to make substantial concessions in settlement of claims not capable of strict legal proof, but of which the Government has been able to obtain some general verification. If the Government is able by enquiry to satisfy itself that there was some such contract, agreement or arrangement as that on which the claim is based, we suggest that the better policy would be one of compromise.

We have no doubt that the Government could now compromise many claims with substantial saving to itself, whereas once Malaya is re-occupied and the currency is again established all claimants will press strongly for payment in full.

===== Page 19 =====
It would not be unfair to claimants to offer them now less than they are probably entitled to, because they will have the option of waiting, but it may be unfair to claimants to simply record their claims and postpone the examination of them until after the war.

If there is anything at all in our expectation that documentary proof will in most cases be no greater after the war than now, it is also probable that less verbal testimony will be then available, because the memories of persons who know something of the various matters will not become clearer as time passes.

There are, we believe, a number of Malayan Government officials and leading citizens in India, and if one of them were appointed to do what he could to check claims, much might be done to secure some degree of confirmation.

For example, some responsible person from Singapore might be found who could say: "I know that Mr. Middlebrook, the Immigration and Passport Officer, was living in a house in Clarke Road, Singapore, rented by the Government from Mr. Pascheove. I have often visited him there. I do not know what the rent was, but I imagine it would be between 175 and 225 dollars a month."

We realize that you will have no authority to deal with these suggestions, but if you think it worth while we should be glad if you would pass them on to the proper authorities.

Yours faithfully,

(Signed) ARTHUR ROBINSON & CO.

CANBERRA A.C.T.

Dear Sir,

re Property, Clarke Road Singapore
occupied by Mr. Middlebrook

We are acting for Mr. I. Pascheove, Managing Director of Paskoe's Limited, Singapore. Mr. Pascheove is a British subject and is at present living in Melbourne.

Mr. Pascheove leased a house in Clarke Road Singapore to the Malayan Government for three years from 1st January 1941 at $200 per month and the rent was paid up to and including January 1942. The house was occupied by Mr. Middlebrook, Immigration and Passport Officer, and the lease was signed by Mr. Jones the Colonial Secretary.

Although the Government has no doubt vacated the premises owing to the advent of the Japanese, the rent is still payable and we should be glad if arrangements could be made for payment.

On the 6th August last we supplied the above information to the Malayan Government Representative at 61 Hunter Street, Sydney and he has been endeavouring to ascertain for us the proper procedure for us to follow in making the claim.

We have to-day received a letter from him in which he states that the claim should be addressed in writing to the Under Secretary of State, Colonial Office London. He adds however that it would be in order for us to send the claim to you and that you will arrange for it to be forwarded to London.

We should be pleased if you could have the matter dealt with by the proper authorities and if the claim could be considered expeditiously we should be obliged. As you will see there has already been a fair amount of delay.

Yours faithfully,

(Sgd) ARTHUR ROBINSON & CO.
