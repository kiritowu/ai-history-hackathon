In the course of discussion yesterday with Mr. Caine it was decided that the main file should be split up and that the correspondence should be registered on sub-files each of which would relate to a separate transaction. This is partly to enable correspondence relating to any particular transaction to proceed even when the main file is in circulation, and thereby to prevent the accumulation of unanswered correspondence as has occurred in this case.

Mr. Taylor and I discussed with Mr. Caine the question whether the considerations adduced in Messrs. Gammon's letter of the 7th of October are sufficient to warrant the reconsideration of the decision notified to them in our letter of the 24th of September that the settlement of this claim will have to await the reoccupation of Malaya.

The general line on which we are proceeding (as explained in the draft letter to the Treasury on the main file) is that, unless in any particular case special circumstances are disclosed which would be held to justify payment, claims should be refused in the following cases:

(b) in respect of the supply locally of goods or services to the Governments (except payments due under contracts which provide for payment in the U.K. or other non-enemy occupied countries), on the ground that repayment is only due in the territory concerned and in local currency and that, in any case it is impossible from records in this country to verify whether the services have in fact been rendered or payments in respect of them already made locally.

In the present case Messrs. Gammon claim that certain of these services were rendered to the satisfaction of the local authorities, and they assert that no payment has been received for these services and they represent therefore that it is equitable that payment should now be made.

Mr. Caine on the other hand still thinks that we should refuse to pay and to take our stand on the line that these claims must stand over until the local records can be examined when Malaya is reoccupied. His reasons for this view are that neither we nor Messrs. Gammon are in a position to say whether payment was or was not made for these services before the fall of Singapore; and that although the services specified in their letter of the 7th of October may have been satisfactorily performed, there is no evidence on such matters as (e.g.) the question whether they (Messrs. Gammon) have discharged their liabilities to sub-contractors etc. etc. Mr. Caine therefore thinks that we should indicate to Messrs. Gammon that the Secretary of State is not prepared to reconsider the decision conveyed to them in the letter of the 24th of September.

We discussed whether we should attempt to send a reasoned reply explaining why we are not able to reconsider the decision, but Mr. Caine thought that this would only lead to further fruitless arguments and that the letter should be quite short and in the terms suggested above.

There is no doubt that this decision will be regarded by Messrs. Gammon as arbitrary and unfair;

===== Page 5 =====
And of course it might be urged on their behalf that the Secretary of State might authorise a payment on account (say 50%) which would leave an adequate balance in hand to cover such contingencies as the (unlikely) possibility that payment for some of the items might have been made locally, and possible undischarged liabilities by Messrs. Gammon themselves.

We also discussed whether we should be influenced in any way by the consideration whether the claimants are responsible authorities whose word can be thoroughly relied upon. Mr. Caine felt, however, that if we attempt to introduce any such discrimination we shall get ourselves into very deep water indeed and that we should maintain a firm front on this matter against any claims for payments for services rendered locally.

Mr. Caine suggested that the qualification quoted at 'X' above (as regards justification for payment in particular cases where special circumstances may be disclosed) should be used extremely sparingly. The kind of case we had in mind inserting this qualification was a case where a Planters' Provident Fund had invested its assets in local Government securities, and where the withholding of interest might result in great hardship to the members of the Fund or their dependants who have escaped from Malaya.

The issues raised in Messrs. Gammon's letters are important, and I accordingly submit for a ruling by higher authority.

Mr. Caine.

At a meeting held in Mr. Caine's office on 1st December, Mr. Gammon took his initial stand on Colonial Office letter of 24th September (No.14) and contended that it was quite unnecessary to refer to Malaya as most of the work had been certified by Public Works Department engineers who had since escaped. It was then explained to him that a further bar to payment was that the debts were of a local nature and that a settlement in sterling would infringe the principle adopted by the Colonial Office after consultation with the Treasury.

Mr. Gammon's contention that there were funds in this country was not denied, but the establishment of a precedent in his case might give rise to a flood of claims so far not presented (e.g. compensation for requisitioned cargoes, payment for work done, services rendered etc.). The Secretary of State was in the position of Trustee for the Malayan Governments and had to be correspondingly scrupulous in making payments on their account. In addition the Treasury were concerned since, if Malayan funds should be exhausted before the re-establishment of British administration in Malaya, the British taxpayer would find himself responsible for the maintenance of essential payments now being made by the Crown Agents.

Mr. Gammon claimed that his was probably the only engineering firm qualified to do the work with which he had been entrusted in the Straits Settlements and Federated Malay States and that he was scrupulous in making full and immediate payments to his sub-contractors and staff. Regarding the work in question, all his debts and salary payments had been liquidated with possibly a mere $5,000 outstanding. In most of the items, payment due to him had been subjected to departmental delays and he claimed that in common fairness they should now be made good.

He stated further that the Admiralty was settling local dollar accounts in sterling, and regretted a diversity of practice in Government departments. The War Office was also considering a similar action.

Mr. Caine said that the difficulty remained that of departing from the principle, and it was finally decided to study the matter in the light of the reported attitude of the Service Departments and perhaps approach the Treasury to see whether any action could be taken to satisfy Mr. Gammon. A decision was unlikely for some weeks. It was quite out of the question to assist Messrs. Wm. Jacks and Company in their claim against the Straits Settlements Government for refund of their deposit.

It was suggested during a discussion after the meeting that items 7, 8 and 10 might possibly have been for account of War Office, and that they might perhaps be induced to pay. I note, however, from letter No.7 that these were Kedah and Pahang Government contracts.

If enemy occupation should have taken place during this period of 6 months, then the monies should be refunded.

3.4(a)

Certain advice was recently given to the Admiralty by the Treasury Solicitor on the question of retention monies held on contracts executed by Gammon (Malaya) Ltd. in Singapore; a copy of the advice is temporarily enclosed - 44A. In view of the ruling that there was no legal liability for repayment of retention monies on frustrated contracts, we are doubtful as to the propriety of paying the whole of claims numbered 2 to 5 of enclosure 42B.

2. A set of contract documents for contract No. W/408 (Item 5 of the claim) is temporarily attached and attention is invited to Clause 18 of the contract under which certain sums are withheld from payments on account of work done and are payable to the contractor in the following events:

(a) As to 2/3rds, on the handing over of the works and

(b) As to the remaining 1/3rd, on the expiration of a further six months or until the final claim is agreed upon, if this is not settled within that period.

These provisions would be included in the other contracts referred to in Claims no. 2 - 4.

3. The circumstances attending the agreement of the final bills differed for each of the contracts forming the subject matter of heads of claim 2 to 5.

Head 2. Road surfacing at Seletar.

The completion date for this contract was fixed at 30th September 1941 (i.e. some 4½ months before the fall of Singapore) and presumably the works were taken over at this date. It is thought that in the circumstances 2/3rds of any retention held should be released but we are doubtful about the remaining 1/3rd which was not due for payment until 6 months from the date of handing over of the buildings.

Head 3. Petrol Expense Stores.

Work on this contract had been completed at the time of evacuation of the Seletar Base, but had not been measured or billed. The final figure for the contract was subsequently agreed at ¥70,000 leaving a balance payable of ¥42,039.

In view of this lump sum agreement it is thought that the question of "retention" does not arise.

Head 4. Temporary Timber Buildings.

This contract was placed in November 1941 and the final bill agreed in January 1942. We can presume that the huts were taken over at or before this latter date when 2/3rds of the retention money would fall due. The 1/3rd balance of the retention money was, however, under the contract, not due for payment until a date after the enemy occupation, and we should like to know whether in these circumstances the remaining 1/3rd can legally be claimed by the contractors. It is of interest that payment on two contemporary contracts was made in full.

Head 5. Station at Lubak Kiab. Redah.

This Station was evacuated by order one day prior to enemy occupation - the contract work being 75% completed. A final bill for the work completed was agreed but payment of ¥16,500 in respect of retention was withheld. The contract documents are enclosed and it will

It can be observed that under Clause 8 the Superintending Officer is empowered to suspend the execution of the works. The contractors argue that the contract was not frustrated but was definitely terminated when evacuation was ordered and that the Department has no legal right to withhold payment of the retention money.

4. We are endeavouring to effect settlement of the contractor's claims in a sum representing moneys actually earned before frustration. With reference to minute 44, I think that in a case where the Contractor had at the time of evacuation under enemy pressure completed the work which he had to execute under the contract, the Court would not be disposed to hold on the ground of frustration that he should not be paid in full for that work, either by reason of the Department's right under the contract to retain part of the price during the maintenance period or because something remained to be done to determine the precise amount which the Contractor had earned; as regards the right of retention, the Court would, I think, take the view that it had ceased to be effective through failure of the purpose for which it was provided. Accordingly, in each of the first three cases specified in paragraph 3 of minute 44, the correct view appears to me to be that the Contractor is legally entitled to be paid the whole of the balance retained. In the fourth case, the order to evacuate one day prior to enemy occupation cannot, in my opinion, be regarded as termination of the contract and, as the contract work had not then been completed, I think that frustration prevented any legal claim on the part of the Contractor to the payment which, I understand, was made to him and that, while the amount of that payment would be irrecoverable as having been paid under a mistake of law, he is not, as a matter of law, entitled to be paid the sum which has been retained.

This is a general question, and I understand the case put to me to be as follows: that the contractor had completed the work under the contract and had handed over; that consequently the retention period of three months (see clause 25 of the conditions of contract at 4 of Cts/Abd/2062 - ? or 9 - ) had begun to run, but had not run out when the loss of the territory in question occurred. Subject to one exception, with which I deal below, I think that the retention money becomes payable at the expiration of the retention period. A contract is frustrated when its further performance is rendered impossible by a supervening event for which neither party is responsible. In the case under consideration the only thing remaining to be done is the payment of the retention money at the stipulated date, and since the loss of the territory in no way renders payment impossible there is no frustration. The exception to which I allude above arises in connection with clause 20 of the same conditions. If after handing over and before the expiration of the stipulated time defects are discovered and the Superintending Officer notifies the contractor requiring him to remedy them; and if this has not been done before the loss of the territory renders such work impossible; then I think that there is frustration and as the contractor is not able to do this remedial work, so also the Department is not bound to pay over the retention money. I must point out that the advice I am giving under this heading is all of it founded on these particular conditions of contract. It might have to be altered or modified in the case of conditions which differed substantially.

Speaking generally as regards the points with which I have dealt at 2 and 4 above I rather gather that the contractor would be willing to arrive at a settlement. If a reasonable settlement can be attained I think it would be at least as much in the interests of the Department as of the contractor. So far as I can forecast I doubt whether the Department's case will turn out to be a strong one.

It is, I think, clear that, subject to proof that the work had been completed and that the price had become due before the occupation of Malaya by the Japanese, a contractor is entitled to payment. The position in this respect is, I think, the same whether the payment has, by the contract, to be made in sterling in this country, which remains possible, or in Straits Settlements currency in Malaya, which has become impossible, since, if the payment had become due before the Japanese occupation, the contracting authority was strictly in default and accordingly could be sued here in damages for the sterling equivalent of the local currency converted at the rate current when the payment became due. Conversely, if, at the time of the Japanese occupation, payment had not become due, the contractor would legally have no claim.

A position of some doubt might arise if, for instance, the contract called for payment in local currency in Malaya, not against a specific event, e.g., the certificate of the superintending officer, but, say, "within one month of completion of the work." If in such a case the Japanese occupation occurred after the completion of the work and before the month had elapsed, then I think the strict legal position would be that the payment had not become due; that there could accordingly have been no default before the payment in local currency in Malaya became impossible; and that accordingly no action would lie in this country to recover the sterling equivalent as damages. I cannot, however, believe that any Court would hold that the payer was altogether relieved from paying for work that had been completed. The Court would, in my view, adopt one of two lines. One would be to hold that the obligation to pay was suspended until such time as payment to the contractor in local currency in Malaya became possible again; the other would be to hold that the price in local currency was merely a measure of the value of the work, that the stipulation for payment in Malaya was one for the sole benefit of the contractor which could be waived by him, and that he was accordingly entitled, if he wished, to be paid sterling in this country.

I find it impossible to say which of these two lines the Court would adopt. This would no doubt depend on the exact facts of each case; but I think that the Court would lean towards holding that the contractor was entitled to demand payment in sterling.

In stating my views above, I have made the following assumptions.

(1) That it is not intended to draw any distinction between contracts made by the War Department or other Department in this country and contracts made by the local Departments in Malaya.

===== Page 122 =====
(2) That the contractor, if a Malayan company or firm, has the necessary licence from the Trading with the Enemy Department to receive payment.

(3) That in all cases the works were completed before the Japanese occupation and the only payment in question is the "final" payment. Having regard to the recent House of Lords decision, a difficult question might arise as to whether, in any particular case, a payment on account which had become due could be recovered by the contractor. If such a payment was merely an advance payment on account of the contract price, it would, under the House of Lords decision, if paid be repayable by the contractor and therefore could not be recovered by him. On the other hand, if it was not purely an advance payment, but was supported, as would ordinarily be the case, by some consideration, e.g., the vesting of plant, materials or the works, it would, if due, be recoverable by the contractor and could be retained by him.
