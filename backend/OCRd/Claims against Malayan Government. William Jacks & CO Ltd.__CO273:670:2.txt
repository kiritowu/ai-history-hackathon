Dear Mr. Foster Hall,

I have received your letter of the 2nd of June regarding the Malayan Bulk Order for Steel, and as far as my memory goes I am able to send you the following notes:

2. The Bulk Order was prepared as the result of Secretary of State Circular No. 202, dated 11th of August, 1941. This is the number of the Circular received in Palestine, and I think it was sent to all Colonies. The sub-division of the order amongst Malayan importers of steel and iron was based on their proportionate past trade as determined from Malayan statistics.

3. I think the total order was in the region of 32,000 tons but this, of course, can be verified by reference to the Colonial Supply Mission in Washington. The order was actually transmitted to Washington but I have no information as to whether any part of the goods were ever shipped. In order to satisfy the Government as to the bona-fides of the importers who were taking part in the Bulk Order, the condition was made that they deposited a sum equal to $800,000 for every ton of iron or steel ordered by them. If my recollection is correct, both as to the amount of this deposit and the total quantity of the order, the Government would have received some $800,000 as the total of these deposits. Cheques for such deposits were received by me or by the Secretary-General of the Department of Supply, Singapore, those received in my office being forwarded to him for action. All were credited to a Supply Department Suspense account. I cannot recollect if Messrs. William Jacks and Company submitted a deposit in respect of the portion of the Order which was allocated to them, but it is most probable that they did so. My recollection is that the general majority of the participating importers did confirm their orders by making such deposits.

4. Participation in the Scheme was allowed only against an undertaking that the members would take delivery at Harbour Board's wharf, transport, store and distribute the iron

===== Page 8 =====
and steel at a margin not exceeding 5% on the estimated landed value. This low margin was dictated by Government to comply with the conditions of Lease/Lend supply.

5. I have, of course, no records whatsoever of these transactions as my departure from Singapore only an hour or two before its capitulation was done in circumstances which did not permit removal of any records. You may be able to get some confirmation from Mr. W. A. Fell, who was the Director-General of Supply, Malaya, at the time, and who went to Washington on behalf of the Malayan Governments to ensure that supply of the order was carried out, or to arrange for substitute materials to be sent to Malaya in accordance with the needs of the country of which Mr. Fell had a long and intimate knowledge. I do not know the whereabouts of Mr. Fell at the present time, but I have no doubt he can be located through the Colonial Office or the British Colonial Supply Mission in Washington. If there is any other information which you require on this subject, I shall be pleased to do the best I can in so far as my recollection of the scheme goes.

Yours sincerely,

J. Thawardt

BRITISH RAW MATERIALS MISSION

11th March, 1943

Dear Mr. Foster Hall,

I have to acknowledge your letter of the 16th February, 50759/2/42-43.

Some months ago, Melville of the Colonial Supply Mission here, told me that Guthrie Co. Ltd., London, were claiming a refund of a deposit stated to have been made by their Singapore Office against an order for steel, placed with the Straits Settlements Government for execution in U.S.A. I can do no better than repeat to you the information I then gave to Melville.

When the bulk order scheme was instituted, the Malayan Governments gave the merchants the option either to deposit cash or a letter of guarantee, endorsed by a Bank, for the equivalent of Straits $25 per ton of steel indented for. The Public Work Department controlled the bulk order scheme, and were responsible for the collection of merchant's cash deposits, or guarantees. The details of that work, therefore, did not come under my control, but I recollect that Mr. Edwards, the P.W.D. Officer in charge of the work, told me that merchants were taking advantage of the concession to lodge guarantees instead of paying cash. This is easily understood, for a cash deposit would cost merchants an interest charge over an indefinite period of time, while Banks would endorse a guarantee for a client for a nominal fee.

To express an opinion, I would say that very few, if any, cash deposits were made. I heard with great regret that a number of the P.W.D. personnel were lost in leaving Singapore but if it is possible for you to contact Mr. Edwards, or an engineer who was assisting him, perhaps you could gather from them more particular information than I can give you.

I left Singapore on November 12th, 1941.

If there is any further information you think I can offer, please let me know.

Yours sincerely,

B. Foster Hall Esq.,
Colonial Office,
Downing Street,
London, S.W.1.
