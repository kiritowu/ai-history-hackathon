This letter is one of three which Sir George Maxwell has recently written us, and which were originally registered on 51102/46 beneath. The three letters have been acknowledged in No.10 on that file, and no further acknowledgment is necessary.

The enclosure to No.1 is of some interest and importance. As has already been noted in the minutes on 51102/46, it shows Sir George Maxwell as a constructive critic rather than a mere thrower of ill-considered bricks. It pleads for a systematic extension of the policy of reservation of land for the Malays, and above all for firm central control in dealing with Malayan land questions. I do not know if we can altogether endorse the first of these points, but we can certainly endorse the second. In one of our latest telegrams to Sir Edward Gent on constitutional policy, we have mentioned land as one of the subjects calling for central direction.

I hardly think that any further action is required, but Mr. Lloyd, Sir George Gater and Mr. Creech Jones may care to see (it will be observed that Mr. Creech Jones is mentioned by name in the last sentence of the letter).

Sir,

I have the honour to acknowledge the receipt of your Telegram Saving No. 129, dated 18th June, 1946, enclosing a letter and memorandum from Sir George Maxwell, K.B.E., O.M.G., and of your Malayan Union despatch No. 142 dated 1st July, 1948, on the subject of Malay Reservations Policy in Malaya, and of the steps which it is proposed to take in the future application of the system of Malay Reservations.

2. With regard to Sir George Maxwell's memorandum, I have the following comments to offer:

(a) Land policy under the Malayan Union will still, as previously, be national in its enactment and local in its administration.

(b) The (Final) Long Term Policy Directive dated 28th December, 1944, paragraph 8, states that "the prewar policy of Malay Reservations in operation in the Federated Malay States should be adopted to the extent to which it may be found to be expedient". Existing Malay Reservations will individually be maintained, and if necessary fresh Reservations will be made, provided that in both categories they fulfil the necessary purposes of the policy.

(c) Prior to the war, Malay Reservations Enactments, based on the original Enactment in the Federated Malay States, had been passed in all the Malay States. Indeed, in Kelantan, the whole State was in 1940 declared a Malay Reservation, with the exception of certain specified areas already occupied by European and other non-Malay owners.

(d) Regarding the suggested declarations of policy,

   (1) There is no reason to fear that any but a Malay will be eligible to hold land in a Malay Reservation, since this is the existing law throughout the Malay States "for the purpose of securing to Malays their interest in land";

   (2) I am advised by the Commissioner of Lands that Malays experience no difficulty in obtaining land, and that greatly increased alien immigration in the future is not at present expected: the policy of vigorous extension of Malay Reservations does not therefore, on these grounds, appear to be called for;

   (3) ...

The Right Honourable
Arthur Creech Jones, P.C., M.P.,

(3) The suggestion of a statutory and permanent Malay Reservations Committee is noted; but the Consultative Committee to advise the Commissioner of Lands under Section II of the Directive will, it is expected, be competent to keep the special needs of the Malays under constant review.

(4) An individual Malay Reservation, it may be agreed, will only be diminished, or the special provisions (if any) relating to it varied, if there is an over-riding national or State (or Settlement) need. This power is vested in the State Council concerned.

(e) The question of qualifications for Malayan Union Citizenship is under consideration but this, whatever the outcome, does not affect the definition of the word Malay nor therefore the question of Malay Reservations.

3. In conclusion, an assurance can, in response to any individual enquiry, be given to the effect that the intention of the Government is that the policy of securing to the Malays their interest in land will be maintained, as indicated in the Directive; but I see no need for any public pronouncement at present.

4. With regard to the concluding paragraph of your despatch under reply, the administration and responsibility for Malay Reservations has always resided in the past with State Councils, who have assiduously safeguarded the interests of Malays, and I cannot understand the fears which have assailed Sir George Maxwell. The allocation of subjects for legislation as between the Central legislature and State Authorities is, as you are aware, at present under discussion and the Malay members have agreed that legislation should continue to be a matter for the Central legislature while pressing very hard for the administration of Malay Reservations to be the responsibility of State Authorities. They made it quite clear to the Government representatives that the State Authorities can be trusted to look after the interest of Malays.

5. There are no other steps which I am advised that it is desirable to take in this matter at present.

I have the honour to be,
Sir,
Your most obedient, humble servant,

Sir,

The Malay Reservations Policy in Malaya

I have the honour to submit for your information a copy of an article which I have written for publication in a monthly magazine. The subject is mentioned in paragraph 7 of the White Paper (Command 6724), and Mr. Creech Jones referred to it in the Debate in the House of Commons (Hansard 8th March page 647), but the expressions used there were "maintained" and "safeguarded". The article contains an earnest plea for the vigorous extension of the policy. Writing with long and intimate experience of the subject, I can say that the great fault of the past administration has been an essentially parochial outlook and performance, and the entire lack of a central executive authority. The "De-centralisation" policy had the direct effect of increasing its parochialism. I submit that it is only by making it a national policy, and by putting it in the forefront of the Government's programme of matters requiring immediate attention, that it will be possible to avert an imminent danger to the Malay agricultural population. Knowing that Mr. Creech Jones is personally interested in the subject, I venture to take the liberty of expressing the hope that this letter may be brought to his attention.

I have the honour to be,
Sir,
Your obedient servant,

THE "MALAY RESERVATIONS" POLICY IN MALAYA

(By Sir George Maxwell, K.B.E.; C.M.G.)

In 1913, the Federal Council of the Federated Malay States passed a law titled "An Enactment to provide for securing to Malays their interests in lands", short titled "The Malay Reservations Enactment, 1913". In the Enactment, a Malay was defined as "a person belonging to any Malayan race who habitually speaks the Malay language or any Malayan language and professes the Moslem religion". The law empowers the Government of any State to declare, by Gazette Notification, any area, however extensive, of State land to be a Malay Reservation; thereafter, the State Authorities are unable to dispose of any land within the Reservation to any person other than a Malay; and no proprietor of any land within the Reservation may dispose of his land to any person other than a Malay.

Admirable as the intentions were, and beneficent as the results have been, the legislation suffered from three major defects.

First. The outlook was parochial, whereas it should have been national. Instead of there being a national policy for creating all the Reservations that might be required for the needs of future generations, with full regard to the possibility of transmigrations of Malays from one area to another to satisfy their "land-hunger", and to the certainty of further immigrations of Malays from the Netherlands East Indies, everything was left to the discretion of the local authorities whether or not they would

===== Page 13 =====
Create any Reservations.

Secondly, not even in any State was there any Authority entrusted with the charge of initiating the local policy and of carrying that policy into execution. A Central Committee, fully representative of Malay interests and covering every State, with State Sub-committees, could have made the policy a national one, and almost certainly would have created more Reservations than now exist. The need for a national policy before there is further alienation of State Lands is readily apparent. In this connection, I may say that I do not know to what extent, if any, Malay Reservations have been established in the States of Kelantan and Trengganu. These two States, with their preponderating Malay population and their still wide areas of untouched State land, seem to require special attention.

Thirdly, under Section 4 of the Enactment, any State Authority had the power, at any time, and without reference to the parties concerned, to reduce the size of any Reservation, and even to abolish it completely. All that was required was a Gazette Notification.

Though there are still considerable areas of State land, much of it is either inaccessible or unsuitable for cultivation, and the demand by persons of other nationalities for such land as is accessible and suitable for cultivation is so great that the Malays highly prize a law which aims at preventing them from being land-less in their own country. A fear which is ever present in the Malay mind is that, when any foreign company or

===== Page 14 =====
Any person of another nationality may apply for a concession covering perhaps some thousands of acres. The State Authorities may welcome the introduction of foreign capital and foreign labour and be disposed to approve the application without full regard to the possibility of its being suitable for a Malay Reservation. When land requires development on a large scale, a small group of Malays cannot hope to be successful. Land may be suitable either for rice or for rubber. For rice cultivation, drainage always, and irrigation sometimes, are required. In the past, the generosity of the Government in draining and irrigating Malay Reservations has been unbounded, and there can be no doubt that this will be the policy of the future. All that is required is the certainty that the land will be held in reserve and ready for occupation when required. A simple manner of ensuring this would be a Government instruction that, whenever any Land Officer forwards for approval any application for the alienation of any large area of land, he should accompany it by his formal certificate that it was not likely to be required for any future Malay Reservation.

The Malays have not failed to observe the statement in paragraph 7 of the White Paper that the Malay Reservations policy will be "maintained." But they are far from being satisfied. In order that they may be fully re-assured, it is desirable to set forth some declarations of policy in the most explicit terms.

(1) That no one but a Malay shall be eligible to hold land in a Malay Reservation, and that the present definition of

===== Page 15 =====
(1) A Malay shall not be varied without the formal consent of the Council of Malay Rulers.

(2) That, in view of the increasing difficulty of obtaining land, and of the certainty of greatly increased alien immigration in the near future, the policy of creating Malay Reservations shall be, not only "maintained" (which is all that the White Paper contemplates) but vigorously extended.

(3) That a statutory and permanent "Malay Reservations Committee" should be established for the purpose of maintaining and extending Malay Reservations, and generally for the purpose of securing to Malays their interests in land. (Here, as will have been seen above, I am quoting the title of the present legislation.) This Committee should be under a statutory obligation to have every Malay Reservation inspected once a year by some of its members or by persons nominated by it. The Committee would prepare a formal report, with its observations and recommendations. The Committee should also have full powers and every facility to arrange with Government officers for visits of exploration, survey and inspection of any land that might appear to be suitable for the creation of a new Malay Reservation. Here, too, it would make a formal report with its recommendations. The Committee should also be required to make an Annual Report for publication in the Gazette. At all times, the Committee should have direct access to the Council of Malay Rulers.

(4) That, when once a Malay Reservation has been created, neither its area shall be diminished, nor the special provisions

===== Page 16 =====
If any relating to it shall be varied, without the formal approval of the Council of Malay Rulers.

Having regard to the provision in the policy of the "Malayan Union Citizenship," whereby any alien of any nationality may acquire full rights of "citizenship" after a residence of only five years, although he may have no intention of residing permanently in the country, the Malays are painfully afraid that these "citizens" may claim entry to these Reservations. In order to remove any doubts on this point, a complete assurance in respect of it should be given to the Malays at the earliest possible opportunity. The Malays should also be assured that the intention of the Government will be to prevent them from being "land-less" in their own country; that the policy of the immediate future will be to extend, and not merely to maintain, "Malay Reservations"; and that, in order to give practical effect to this policy all necessary legislation will be introduced and implemented by executive action.
