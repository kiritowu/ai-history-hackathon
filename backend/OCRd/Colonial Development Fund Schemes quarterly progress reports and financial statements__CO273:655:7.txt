QUARTERLY PROGRESS REPORT (April - June 1939).

Our work during the second quarter of this year was, on the whole, a continuation of the projects mentioned in the first quarterly report.

The work on the vitamin A content of Malayan bananas was concluded and a report prepared for publication. This report appeared in September (Jour. Malaya Branch B.M.A. vol. 3, page 156).

Considerable progress was made with our vitamin C assays. Biological tests on guinea pigs to compare the antiscorbutic potency of (1) reduced ascorbic acid, (2) reversibly oxidised ascorbic acid, and (3) irreversibly oxidised ascorbic acid were completed. Our results showed that the "reduced" and "reversibly oxidised" forms are equal in antiscorbutic potency. Irreversibly oxidised ascorbic acid, formed by the action of heat on the reversibly oxidised form, was found to possess no antiscorbutic activity. With this information, it was possible to start a survey of the vitamin C content of foods using the titration method with indophenol. A full report of this work has now been prepared and will appear in December.

Our dietetic survey of the 17 foods mentioned in the first quarter's report continued throughout the second quarter. As this experiment progressed, we took the opportunity of making some sections of the brain and spinal column with a view to detecting, if possible, any changes from normal in animals which had died on the poorer diets. This work did not pass beyond the stage of getting accustomed to the difficult and special technique involved during this quarter. In order to give ourselves some diagnostic experience, we attempted to treat a few animals with carbon monoxide with the intention of producing paralysis more rapidly, and due to a known cause. The surprise has been the large amount of carbon monoxide that is tolerated by our animals. It is infinitely quicker and more certain to induce paralysis by feeding polished rice and salt fish.

Some progress was made in respect to our analyses of the trace elements. Aluminium, cobalt, nickel, and zinc came under review. Work done during this quarter has enabled us to start a survey of the zinc content of local foods.

The experiments on the basal metabolism of Malay schoolboys came to an end and a report was prepared for publication. It appeared in September (Jour. Malaya Branch B.M.A. Vol. 3, p. 143). The conclusions reached were that the B.M. of local Malay boys does not differ significantly from the standards of temperate climates. From these results, together with similar results on adults, we feel that as far as basal energy requirements are concerned, standards found to apply to temperate climates should be applied here. This aspect of the subject will be discussed more fully in my annual report for 1939. We started "efficiency" experiments, using a Chinese subject, with a view to classifying "work" under the different categories.

Sd./- J. L. Rosedale.

MEMORANDUM.

From the Professor of Biochemistry.
To the Principal, College of Medicine, Singapore.

Date: 30th May, 1939.

Quarterly Report for First Quarter of 1939.

Our original list of the vitamin values of foods was prepared before the system of International Units for expressing these values had been adopted. During 1938, we decided to bring our values into line with present custom and before the end of the year had prepared the new list of vitamin A values which was published during the quarter now under review in the March issue of the Journal of the Malaya Branch of the British Medical Association.

During the present quarter, we have continued colorimetric estimations of the pro-vitamin A in bananas and have concluded biological tests on two common varieties - pisang rajah and pisang mas. It appears that the colorimetric method gives values 2.5 times higher than the biological tests.

Continuing the work of expressing vitamin C values in terms of International units, we are taking the opportunity of studying the activity of different forms of ascorbic acid. Ascorbic acid has been oxidised by (1) "ascorbic acid oxidase", and (2) oxidising agents such as iodine and bromine. Biological tests have been started to compare the activity of (1) reduced ascorbic acid, (2) reversibly oxidised ascorbic acid, and (3) irreversibly oxidised ascorbic acid.

Our work on the basal metabolism of Malay boys has continued. During this quarter some 36 subjects were dealt with, and this phase of metabolic study is nearing completion.
