TELEGRAM
FROM 3 members Federal Council MALAY STATES

D. 6th December, 1940.
R. 7th December, 1940, 12.00 hrs.

Today we have sent to your Lordship through the High Commissioner a Petition against the imposition of Income Tax in Federated Malay States bearing the signatures of a very great number of representatives of Agricultural, Mining, Commercial and other interests. The text of the Petition is as follows:

Petition begins.

1. Your Lordship's Petitioners represent Agricultural, Mining, Trade, Commercial, and Industrial interests in Federated Malay States.

2. Petitioners are loyal in their attachment to the British Empire and entertain sentiments of profound gratitude to H.M.G. as the protecting power of the country in which they carry on business.

3. Petitioners most strongly desire the victory of the British Empire in the present struggle for Freedom and Justice, and are prepared to render full assistance to the Empire in the conduct of the Empire's war effort.

4. Petitioners acknowledge the clear duty of Federated Malay States to contribute towards the cost of the war. In this respect, Petitioners are in complete agreement with the Government but differ from the Government as to the means whereby that desired end should be achieved and are absolutely opposed to the proposal of the Federated Malay States Government to impose Income Tax as a method of raising such contribution.

5. Petitioners do not desire to enter into academic discussion as to the relative merits or demerits in general of Income Tax, but Petitioners do desire respectfully and strongly to represent that the introduction of such a code of taxation as Income Tax into the Federated Malay States is undesirable in the extreme in view of conditions existing among the many and varied communities constituting the population of these States. They are in general agreement with the contents of paragraph 10 of the report of the Committee appointed in June 1940 by the High Commissioner Malay States to make recommendations on the question of raising loans as gifts to the Imperial Treasury for the purpose of the prosecution of the war. Extracts from this paragraph are as follows (quotation begins):

"10. We most of us agree that so far as the Federated Malay States are concerned an Income Tax, whatever its theoretical merits may be in practice, would be neither a very equitable nor an economical..."

===== Page 30 =====
6. Main objections raised by Petitioners to imposition of Income Tax are difficulties of assessing tax equitably and collecting it both from the aspect of persons taxed and of persons collecting the tax.

7. In amplification of paragraph 6, Petitioners refer to complicated systems, trading business organisation, and vernacular bookkeeping peculiar to a number of polyglot communities of these States which would undoubtedly give rise to many opportunities for evasion of payment of the tax and also to strong probability of corruption on part of persons employed to collect tax.

8. Petitioners point to the Commission of Enquiry sitting in connection with corrupt practices known to exist in Mines Department of Federated Malay States and to recent convictions of certain past and present officers of that Department. Petitioners believe that corruption in Federated Malay States is not confined to officers of Mines Department; indeed recent conviction of chief clerk of the Federated Malay States Estate Duty Office and convictions of certain officers of other Departments of Government afford full support for that belief. Although it occurred in adjacent colony of Straits Settlements, recent conviction of Captain R.G. Loveday Royal Engineers is mentioned. Captain Loveday was sentenced by General Court Martial to be cashiered and to receive four and a half years penal servitude for accepting illegal gratifications in connection with Military Contracts. Petitioners therefore urge extreme undesirability of creating a new Department of Government employing great number of tax collectors and persons connected with collection of tax having direct relations with public, thus affording new and exceptional opportunities for obtaining illegal gratification.

9. Your Lordship doubtless aware of monetary gifts already made by Federated Malay States to Imperial Treasury, of addition taxation already imposed for war purposes, of local war loan already floated, and of voluntary War Fund already started by a newspaper published in Federated Malay States. Efforts to help H.M.G. towards cost of prosecuting war will certainly continue in Federated Malay States but Petitioners are convinced no necessity whatsoever for such further assistance to take form of proceeds of Income Tax specifically imposed for purpose. Whatever may be conditions existing in the Colony of Straits Settlements which has different Fiscal system from Federated Malay States, in Federated Malay States there are many sources from which additional revenue can be obtained by taxation which would be more politically desirable, more efficient and more equitable, and which would afford fewer opportunities for corrupt practices than Income Taxation.

10. Petitioners strongly opposed to the introduction into British Protected Federated Malay States of Income Tax merely to facilitate the imposition of that tax in British Crown Colony of Straits Settlements. During a period of the 1914-18 war or year or two thereafter, Income Tax was levied in Straits Settlements but not in Federated Malay States.

STRAITS SETTLEMENTS TO DO MORE

WIDER TAXATION BASIS

FROM OUR CORRESPONDENT
SINGAPORE, Oct. 14

The Straits Settlements are budgeting for a deficit of $11,000,000 in 1941. The revenue is estimated at $46,000,000, and the expenditure $57,000,000.

Mr. Jones, the Officer Administering the Government, in a speech in the Legislative Council today, estimated that the total cost of the Colony's war effort to the end of the year would be $41,000,000, including $1,000,000 as a gift to the Imperial Government as well as the local military expenditure and that of the various war-time departments, but not including the war loan of $25,000,000, which has now been remitted to London. The 1940 deficit was at present estimated at $17,000,000. Every effort would be made not to curtail the social services. The Colony's vital contribution to the Empire's war effort, Mr. Jones emphasized, was the supply of essential raw materials and the provision of foreign exchange. "Though lacking the banners and trumpets of actual fighting," he said, "any failure of our efforts to supply these needs would have results not less disastrous than cowardice in battle."

Mr. L. G. Corney, Acting Financial Secretary, said the Colony had contributed $62,000,000 to the Imperial Government in the past three years, but the rate of contribution could not be continued without tapping fresh sources of revenue or using up reserves. The plain truth, said Mr. Corney, was that they had hardly yet begun to mobilize the financial resources of the Colony for war. The Council would agree that an estimated revenue of $4,500,000 from war duties could not be allowed to represent the Colony's effort for 1941. Mr. Corney added: "What we require is new money for war purposes, and this we can provide only by widening the basis of taxation. This problem, which vitally concerns not only the credit but the very security of this country, is the most pressing of our financial problems."

The Acting Financial Secretary referred to the investigations of the joint Federal Malay States and the Colony committee on income-tax, and expressed the opinion that income-tax was workable in Malaya.

The Legal Adviser: Sir, I beg to move the Resolution standing in my name, No. A (i) on this list of Resolutions. I beg leave of Council slightly to amend the wording so that it shall read as follows:

At this Council resolves that Part II of the First Schedule to the War Taxation Enactment 1940 be amended by adding at the end thereof the following paragraph:

(ix) by deleting the word 'four' in the phrase 'chargeable with a duty of four cents' wherever that phrase occurs in the Enactment, and by substituting therefor the word 'six' and that such amendment shall take effect from the 22nd day of May, 1940.

MINUTES.

The minutes of the meetings held on the 20th February 1940 and the 1st April 1940, which had been previously circulated amongst members, are confirmed.

AFFIRMATION.

Mr. George William Bryant, M.C.S., and Dato’ Hussain bin Mohamed Taib, M.C.S., Orang Kaya Indera Shahbandar make the affirmation as members of the Federal Council.

===== Page 38 =====
The amendment is one which is consequential on what this Council has already enacted—Items 15 and 25 of the Schedule: the stamp duties on bills of exchange and cheques were increased from 4 cents to 6 cents. Now, in the body of the Enactment itself, reference is sometimes made to "bills of exchange" and sometimes to "cheques", but the original draftsman has referred to "any instrument chargeable with a duty of 4 cents"; and this is therefore a consequential amendment required so that the body of the principal Enactment should, where necessary, read "chargeable with a duty of 6 cents", i.e., the new duty to which this Council has already agreed.

I therefore beg to move this Resolution.

The Acting Financial Secretary: Sir, I second the motion.

The Resolution is passed.

AMENDMENT TO THE SECOND PROVISO TO PART VI OF THE FIRST SCHEDULE TO THE WAR TAXATION ENACTMENT 1940.

The Legal Adviser: Sir, I beg to move the Resolution standing in my name, No. A (ii): "That this Council resolves that the Second Proviso to Part VI of the First Schedule to the War Taxation Enactment 1940 be amended (a) by deleting the words 'owner or'; and (b) by inserting therein immediately after the words 'is liable' the words 'or will become liable' and that such amendment shall take effect from the 22nd day of May, 1940."

The amendment is due to the fact that the language "the owner or lawful occupier" is somewhat ambiguous and open to misconstruction: if the owner is working his estate he is the lawful occupier even if he is working it by an agent or manager, so that the words "lawful occupier" suffice by themselves. On the other hand, if the owner who is liable to pay income tax is not working his estate, but merely lets it out to somebody who is not liable to pay income tax, the wording, as it stands, will be open to the argument that the occupier who is not paying income tax might be exempted from paying this duty merely because the actual owner of the land was liable to income tax, an exemption which this Council and the Government never intended.

The second part of the motion is necessitated by the fact that in England income tax is really paid on last year's income, speaking roughly, so the wording "is liable" might result in no one getting exemption at all. In other words, the profits on the rubber exported this year will not be liable to income tax until next year. The wording is therefore being altered to "is liable or will become liable".

The Comptroller of Customs: Sir, I second the motion.

The Resolution is passed.

If a Loan is floated and is financed and redeemed by means of indirect taxation and if the present policy of limiting taxation to those forms which affect mostly the rich is maintained, it is the rich who, in the long run, will pay for the Loan.

It has been remarked and it will be remarked again that the servicing of a Loan or Loans solely by means of indirect taxation would be too great a burden for the taxable public to bear. The taxable public in this country must expect to carry any burden in the form of indirect taxation placed upon it during these hazardous times up to maximum capacity. If this maximum does not come up to the requirements of the servicing of a Loan or Loans which this country may successfully float during this War, I see no reason why the Colony’s reserves should not be taken into account. Reserves are provided for specific purposes and one of these purposes is, without doubt, a period of emergency. What greater emergency can we imagine than that which is upon us now? The country should be run upon sound business principles. It is for the Directors to decide when the emergency warrants falling back upon reserves and I consequently urge that those responsible bear this point in mind when considering this whole question of a Loan and the servicing thereof.

In advocating the Loan measure I am not suggesting that it should be carried out on lines proposed elsewhere. To embark immediately upon a campaign to raise, by means of a Loan, a huge sum of money would be impolitic and probably a failure with its consequent unhappy psychological effect. Furthermore, it would be most inadvisable to start out with the idea of raising, for example, one hundred million dollars in the course of a year. We have recently experienced the disturbing effect of an uncertain state of affairs and I believe, consequently, that if this principle is adopted there should be set a definite sum for which subscriptions would be accepted. The successful flotation of a Loan of this nature would have a material and most beneficial psychological effect. Not only would there be a beneficial effect within the country but, also, there would be added something more to the weight of British propaganda throughout neutral countries. After a lapse of time, when it becomes clear to those in the know that more funds have become available, there should be introduced a further Loan of an amount which would be quickly taken up and which, consequently, could be promptly remitted Home.

With these ideas in mind and with these convictions and with the knowledge that such a matter cannot be dealt with in detail without most careful consideration, I now propose to Government to consider the setting up of such a Body as Government think advisable for enquiring into the practicability or otherwise of the flotation of a Loan for the purpose which we all have at heart and I trust that it will not be long before, as a result of an investigation into the possibilities of raising funds by this means, we shall all be in a position to place available funds at the Imperial Government’s disposal for the defence of our Empire.

Your Excellency, before resuming my seat, I should like to take this opportunity of informing you, Sir, how pleased the Unofficial Members are to see you in the Chair which you now occupy. We offer you our congratulations upon your attainment of such high office. We know that it is through your successes in the past that you now hold the important post of Officer Administering the Government of this Colony and we extend to you our best wishes for continued successes.

It is perhaps superfluous to give such assurances, but even so, I do declare that throughout your period of office, you, Sir, may count on the full co-operation and support of the Unofficial Members of this Council. (Applause).

His Excellency, the Officer Administering the Government:—Honourable Members, I thank the Senior European Member for Penang very sincerely for the kindness of his reference to me and all the Unofficials on whose behalf he has spoken. I have not been in the Colony this time very long, but I have been long enough to realise that a promise of full support from the Unofficial Members of this Council is certain to make the bearing of any responsibility and the discharge of any task as easy and as pleasant as the other circumstances would allow, and perhaps the only matter in which I feel any confidence is that I shall be able to count on the assistance of the Unofficial Members of this Council.

In regard to the Honourable the Elected Member for Penang’s proposal for a loan, I have this to say. The Honourable Member sounded as if he were suggesting two conditions for the loan: that the cost of it should be met by indirect taxation alone, and if that method proved inadequate, the deficit should be made up by raiding our reserves. There is a great difference between meeting an urgent occasion when time is of the essence, as we have done today by our gift, and failing to provide for a liability which has been purposely incurred by neglecting to increase our incomings. We cannot foresee the future, and it may happen that our circumstances will become so straitened that in desperation we may have to resort to the methods the Honourable Member has suggested. But any proposal that we should do so of set purpose as an integral part of this policy for repayment of the loan is not acceptable to the Government. Neither is the Government disposed to commit itself to an undertaking that the cost of the loan shall be met only from indirect taxation. But there are no objections to the general proposal for the appointment of a body which shall consider the practicability of a war loan, and it will be duly considered with a view to taking the proper action.

Meanwhile, the public I am sure is more than willing—it is anxious—to offer its sacrifices in the cause of freedom and democracy which Great Britain and her Allies are now struggling so grimly to defend. And so we have on the one hand a public pining to pay increased taxes and on the other, a Government apparently reluctant to impose them. In this paradoxical situation, people are naturally beginning to ask why, since we have all the necessary machinery for its collection at little or no extra cost, there should occur this delay in imposing increased taxes along the lines which appear to be most ready to hand—along the lines in fact to which the public is already accustomed and would willingly subscribe.

Honourable members will have observed from leading articles which have appeared in the Press during the past few days that this question of delay is giving rise to unfortunate rumours and suspicions that the Government may intend to introduce income-tax into the Federated Malay States regardless of strong public opposition to this form of taxation. That there is strong and widespread opposition to any such form of taxation here has already become almost too obvious to need emphasis. All the unofficial members of this Council are opposed to it, as is also a very large majority of those people who are conversant with local conditions. Even if it could be regarded as financially desirable, the gravest possible objections could be advanced on ethical grounds against the adoption of income-tax in this country. Perhaps the necessity to recount all the objections does not at present arise, but I consider I should be neglecting my duty to the public as well as to the Government if I failed to take this opportunity to sound a note of warning as to the deplorable results which would inevitably attend any attempt to introduce income-tax into the Federated Malay States. In their loyalty and devotion to the British Empire and their eagerness to contribute every possible sacrifice to the common cause—an eagerness which has not been lacking in practical demonstration as we have seen today—people of all races in this happy federation of protected States are united by a unanimity of purpose which should on no account be disturbed. To excite distrust, opposition and disruption by introducing income-tax at this critical stage would be like throwing a spanner into the works; and I know that I have all my unofficial colleagues solidly with me when I express the earnest hope that nothing of the kind will be done. (Applause.)

Mr. Yong Shook Lin:

War Taxation.

Sir, at the last Budget Meeting of this Council held on the 21st November last, in your annual address to this Council, when dealing with the subject of taxation, you said that the question of imposing additional taxation as a war measure had been in the minds of the Government since the commencement of the war and gave reasons for not having then arrived at a decision.

The first announcement which was made some days after the Budget Meeting related to increases in import duties on intoxicating liquors and tobacco and such announcement was received with unanimous approval. Such increases entail no extra cost to the Government in the collection thereof, as the existing machinery of collection is employed in such collection.

The delay in making subsequent announcements as to the imposition of additional taxation as a war measure has caused wild speculations and rumours as to the nature of such additional taxation, and the impression is that there is a growing feeling of uneasiness amongst the public as a result of such rumours. Whilst I agree with the remarks of Your Excellency in your annual address that it is better to do these things properly than in a hurry, the position is that there is this feeling of uneasiness which I think ought to call forth from the Government at an early date some definite announcement which will stop these wild speculations and rumours. The principle of any further contribution to the Imperial Government for the prosecution of the war out of additional taxation for the duration of the war will be accepted with unanimous approval without any exception; and the only question will be that of the form and nature of such additional taxation. As I said earlier in the morning, the need of the Imperial Government for funds for the prosecution of the war is very urgent, and will continue to be very urgent. If additional taxation as a war measure is required very urgently, it appears to me that it can only be collected quickly by means of existing machinery of collection, entailing no extra cost of expenditure whatsoever to the Government. There does not appear to be any necessity to experiment with any new measure of taxation which will require the setting up

===== Page 80 =====
Of new machinery of collection, and the training of new staff, and involve delay in collection. It appears to be quite unnecessary to impose any new measure of taxation if other simple measures of additional taxation will bring in the required amount of revenue.

Sir, subject to the remarks which I have made, I trust that the Government will follow the path of least public resistance by imposing such additional taxation, the burden or the incidence of which will not be realised or opposed by the public, and that the Government will, to use the words of a Cambridge lecturer on economics, when lecturing on the principles underlying taxation, "pluck the goose with as little squealing as possible."

Sir, in conclusion, I wish to associate myself with the remarks generally of my honourable friend Mr. Blunn on the subject of war taxation. (Applause).

Dr. S. R. Krishnan:

War Taxation.

Sir, I entirely agree with the views expressed by the two previous speakers. As regards my community, we are prepared to undergo any sacrifice towards the successful prosecution of the war. If at all there is any contemplation of introduction of income-tax, I think it will not be acceptable to us. Perhaps, Sir, you are aware that Indians have to pay income-tax to the Government of India on their earnings from this country. (Applause.)

Retirement of Mr. G. L. Ham and Dr. R. D. Fitzgerald.

We hope that they will have many years of health and happiness in their retirement. (Applause.)

War Taxation.

The speakers on the subject of war taxation will not expect me to reply to them as fully as they have spoken to me. It is difficult, as I have said before, to rise up at a moment's notice and answer set speeches on vitally important subjects. I say again that if any honourable member has a subject of such importance to bring up, the right way to do it is by means of a formal motion which can be seconded and debated.

But in regard to the delay in announcing fresh proposals of taxation, the Honourable Mr. Blunn, to whom I desire to express my sincere thanks for all the work he did, was a member, with the Honourable Mr. Joynt, of the Joint Committee set up to consider the subject. The Joint Committee consisted of the Financial Secretaries of the Colony and the Federated Malay States, the Honourable Mr. Blunn representing the Federated Malay States, and on the Colony side there was an unofficial also. We thought it better at the time not to include the Unfederated States in the personnel of the Committee because conditions vary very considerably in those States and what might be suitable for one might not be suitable for another. That Committee has presented its report: and if this were an ordinary country it would have been easy to have decided whether to have accepted it or not; but Malaya is a country of many Governments and it is not possible to act upon the approval of one Government until you have got the approval of the others. That is one reason for the delay. The report was not in my hands until perhaps a fortnight or three weeks ago.

The other reason for the delay is that I myself naturally had to examine the report very carefully and to satisfy myself, so far as I could, that it has really included all possible sources of taxation in its scope. That, too, has taken time: and, of course, there is the fact that in these days, I suppose, war work alone takes up half of my time, and I cannot give concentrated and continuous attention to one subject alone for a sufficient time.

But I wish to assure honourable members and the public, if they are uneasy, that now that this Council has met there will be no delay in dealing with the matter. I think that at the moment it will be better for me not to say anything more.

THE CASE FOR INCOME TAX AS I SEE IT

Press by H.E. the Governor, Sir Shenton Thomas, to be sent from Singapore, Penang, and Kuala Lumpur, at 7.25 p.m. Day, March 3rd.

I am going to talk to you tonight about a tax on incomes or purposes. Quite a lot has been said about it during the new weeks, but the reasons why I have felt obliged to consider it have never yet been given. You will understand that on a matter of importance I had first to be personally convinced that it was justified, and this meant a great deal of preliminary discussion. I had then to prepare a case to put before the public. It is neither fair to them nor to the Imperial Government that they be asked to make up their minds on anything short of a full statement of reasons.

Soon after the war broke out, I appointed a committee to review proposals for war taxation in the Colony and the Federated States. The recommendations of their Report did not include an income tax, and they have in the main been accepted by both Governments and will shortly be considered at special meetings of the native and Federal Councils. I do not think it would be right to disclose tonight what the recommendations are, as I ought not to commit any member of Council in advance.

I can say, however, that the annual revenue which this war tax is estimated to produce in the Federated Malay States is just 8 million, and in the Colony about $5 million. These are, of course, appreciable sums but nothing like what the Colony and the Federated Malay States are capable of producing.

The Chancellor of the Exchequer has recently stated that our expenditure in Great Britain in 1940 will be half the national. In 1941 it will undoubtedly be even more. Persons of large estates are already being taxed to the extent of 16 shillings in the pound.

===== Page 131 =====
In every speech by responsible persons at home we are told this war means real and personal sacrifice. People in England have to do without many things which add to their comfort and indeed which they looked upon as necessities.

When, therefore, I received the committee's report I had all this in mind. I had also to bear in mind that the Colony and the Federated Malay States together constitute by far the wealthy unit in the Colonial Empire and that their taxation, as shared with that in most other Colonies, is exceedingly low. I was entitled to bear in mind the sentiments of loyalty unanimously pressed by all classes of all the communities of Malaya at the break of war, and to remember how that — to quote one speaker — it is "our duty and our desire and determination to make readily sacrifice may be required to ensure a speedy victory" for the sake of freedom.

Two of the recommendations of the committee do impose some office on certain industries, whose representatives have been utilized and have accepted it readily. I felt sure they would, and I am very grateful to them. In the other recommendations would see no sacrifice at all. I had therefore to ask myself the questions:

1) Is there more money in the Colony and the Federated Malay States than this taxation would appear to suggest? Undoubtedly.

2) Is it the duty of the Governments to secure as much of this money as possible for the prosecution of the war? Undoubtedly.

3) Is it the duty of each one of us to support proposals to this end, whatever they may cost us? Undoubtedly.

I examined the various tariffs but I could not find anything that would be of much help. It is of interest to note that in 1938 the Customs revenue of the Federated Malay States was just $24 million, of which about $21 million came from tin, rubber, nuts, beer, tobacco, and petrol. The only other item which yielded million dollars was textiles; all the rest are small.

I found therefore that the purpose which I had in mind could be achieved by indirect taxation, and there were also the following reasons why this form of taxation should not be given preference over others:

First, it increases the cost of living and so creates demands for higher wages.

Secondly, if raised too high — and it would have to be high to bring in an appreciable sum of money — it is likely to result in decreased purchases, which mean less revenue as well as diminishing trade.

Thirdly, it hits the poor more hardly than the rich. When I speak of the poor, I mean people on small incomes as well as the daily paid labourer. The man on $300 a month pays as much to the State for each stengah as the man who has $3,000 a month. The tax on the coolie's cigarettes means much more to him than it does to the well-to-do.

Fourthly, on most articles indirect taxation means no sacrifice: people pay only according as they feel inclined.

Another suggestion is for a house tax. That would hit only house owners in the towns and any number of people would escape.

There is no profit in that. And as it may be supposed that after the war it will be a slump, as there was after the last war, the governments guaranteeing the loan would have to find large sums for interest and sinking fund at a time when they were already short of money for essential social services. They do not deal with the real issue, that Malaya is in honour bound to contribute to the cost of the war right up to her capacity, and they involve no personal sacrifice. Even if every one of them was adopted, there would still be large sums of money which could be secured by a direct tax, and should be secured if Malaya is to play her proper part.

And so, the more I considered the matter, the more I am driven to the conclusion that the addition of a tax on income to our existing forms of taxation provides the only possible means of collecting our proper contribution to the cost of the war.

Opposition to the tax only arose in 1922, when the government utilised the proceeds to meet local expenditure instead of giving them to the Imperial Government.

Before anything in the shape of an income tax was introduced in 1917, a petition was presented to the government, signed by representatives of the non-European communities including Indians, Chinese, and Arabs—protesting against cases in indirect taxation on the ground that they would involve hardship to the poor, and advocating an income tax instead, on the said basis that it would not affect the poor. Fifthly, the proposed income tax will not affect the poor. The suggestion is that it should be a plain straightforward tax with as few complications as possible.

It is suggested that the man with an income of $5,000 a year should pay nothing at all. Very different from the burden imposed on his relations and is at Home, let me remind you. In this war we stand to lose everything we have, and the rich man stands to lose more by defeat than the poor man. Right that he should contribute more to victory. The standard of living of the poorer classes in Malaya is low; of the richer classes, high.

MALAYA'S WAR EFFORT

GOVERNOR ON INCOME-TAX PROPOSALS

FROM OUR CORRESPONDENT

SINGAPORE, MARCH 3

The Governor, Sir Shenton Thomas, broadcasting last night on war taxation, announced projected taxation bringing in annually 8,000,000 Straits dollars (nearly £1,000,000) in the Federated Malay States and $5,000,000 in the colony. This was nothing like what the colony and the Federated Malay States were capable of producing.

Sir Shenton Thomas said that Malaya was by far the wealthiest unit in the Colonial Empire, yet its taxation was exceedingly low compared with most of the other Colonies. He rejected the idea of increased indirect taxation and also a proposal for a war loan, in the case of the latter because there was no reason why the Government should pay interest for money which they ought to get for nothing. Alternative schemes being only palliatives, evading the real issue that Malaya was in honour bound to contribute to the cost of the war according to her capacity, Sir Shenton Thomas concluded that income-tax provided the only possible means of collecting the country's proper war contribution.

He answered current objections to income-tax, and said that the present proposal would not affect the poor man. It was a plain, straightforward tax, with few complications— the first $5,000 would be untaxed, 6 per cent. would be payable on the second $5,000, and 12 per cent. on anything above that. He did not think that evasion was likely on a large scale owing to the loyalty of all the communities; furthermore, the authorities now possessed information which made evasion difficult.

Following proposals by the Committee set up to consider war taxation have been accepted by the Executive Council of the Straits Settlements and by the unofficial members of the Federal Council of the Federated Malay States. I ask authority to bring them into force.

(1) Estate duty taxes to be those in the United Kingdom before September 1939.

(2) General increase in stamp duty.
(3) Entertainment Tax.
(4) Mineral Waters Tax (Colony only).
(5) Increase of 50% in Tax on private cars.
(6) Export Duty of 2½% ad valorem on rubber and sterling companies, and possibly companies subject to Empire income Tax being exempted. These have been accepted by the industries concerned.

(7) Increase in betting and sweepstakes tax.
(8) Surcharge of two cents on existing five cents internal postage stamps and postcards.
(9) Increase in minimum charge for internal telegrams.
(10) Imports and Excise Duties on imported or locally made playing cards.
(11) Import Duty on fireworks (Colony only) similar to that already in force in the Federated Malay States.

ALAYA’S CONTRIBUTION TO THE WAR

WILLING TO PAY MORE TAXES

FROM OUR CORRESPONDENT

SINGAPORE, FEB. 27

While the Governor, Sir Shenton Thomas, is still considering recommendations of the joint committee of the Straits Settlements and Federated Malay States on war-time taxation, the Malayan newspapers give prominence to discussions on the advantages and disadvantages of the various methods of raising increased revenue to be sent to London as Malaya’s contribution to the cost of the war.

From every quarter there is a willingness to pay extra taxation—Malaya is comparatively lightly taxed—and a general feeling that the country is well able to contribute in a more substantial way than hitherto in order to lighten the home taxpayer’s burden. The only difference of opinion concerns the ways and means.

According to the Straits Times, the authorities aim at raising £1,000,000 annually in the Colony, and a further £1,000,000 in the rest of Malaya for a contribution to the Imperial Treasury. The authorities have recently been examining the possibilities of income-tax, but there is much opposition to this from Europeans, who feel that the Chinese and Indian business systems will permit widespread evasion, leaving the burden unequally on the one community; in addition, the most complicated machinery would be required. Alternative schemes include a higher export duty on rubber and tin and additional indirect taxation, while some are in favour of raising a local war loan, perhaps of 100,000,000 Straits dollars, representing £12,000,000, or the country’s probable war contribution for six years, repayment to be made over 30 years from the country’s normal and special taxation.

The Government’s decision is expected soon. Meanwhile the current discussion is valuable in revealing the remarkable willingness of all communities to meet taxation as a part of the war effort.
