My minute of 25.7.45 above refers to a minute which I had written on the same day on the file about the draft Moratorium Proclamation. I have now detached that file. The suggestion made in my minute was that Mr. Caine might speak urgently to Mr. Harris of the War Office both about the Moratorium Proclamation and about the Fiscal Guide, and might possibly arrange for an early meeting under his own auspices. I subsequently discovered, however, that Mr. Caine was not in this country, and action has now been taken separately as regards the Moratorium Proclamation.

This question of the Fiscal Guide is not so immediately urgent, though it has been hanging about for a long time and it ought to be cleared out of the way. The draft Fiscal Guide is at No.1, and the subsequent correspondence on the file (Nos.3 and 4) contains the comments of the Bank of England and of M.P.U. It will be seen from the covering slip attached to No.1 that the draft actually originated with M.P.U., but it was first prepared some time ago, and they have evidently had some pretty hefty second thoughts.

The paragraphs in the draft which particularly concern us are paragraphs 12, 13 and 14 (see paragraphs 8, 9, 10 and 11 of No.4). The point which I should like to clear out of the way first relates to paragraph 12 of the draft, which runs as follows:

"The

===== Page 11 =====
The Fiscal and Tax Laws of the Straits Settlements, for Singapore and the similar Laws of the Federated Malay States for the Mainland territory, will be maintained and enforced as soon as possible. Alterations in the procedure and rates of taxation may be necessary but no such alterations or additions may be made without the approval of the War Office.

The comments on this in paragraph 8 of No.4 seem to me to be entirely correct. Surely, this passage in the draft Fiscal Guide is anticipated in our proposed Constitutional changes. Hitherto, these changes have only been provisionally approved by the Cabinet, and moreover we cannot tell exactly when they will come into force, since they depend on the signature of new treaties with the Sultans. On the other hand, the draft Fiscal Guide rightly says that the Fiscal and Tax Laws will be "maintained and enforced as soon as possible." The occasion for enforcing them may easily arrive before our Constitutional changes (if decided upon at all) come into force. I think the first sentence of paragraph 12 of the draft Guide should read as follows:

"The Fiscal and Tax Laws of the Straits Settlements will be maintained and enforced in the Straits Settlements as soon as possible, as will the similar laws of the Federated and Unfederated Malay States for the Mainland territory."

This point is referred to in the second paragraph of Mr. Emanuel's minute.

Of 18/7 above. He is right, I think, in saying that the creation of a Malayan Union will involve the unification of the laws on the Mainland and that F.M.S. For the time being, I think we can only go on the basis of the past. I have had this file by me for some time. First, Colonel McFadzean wished to discuss certain points before he left for India; second, because it seemed that it might be desirable to discuss further with the War Office and the Treasury during various meetings which we have had on Hong Kong points. That opportunity has not arisen, and on balance I do not think there are any major points upon which we need express any views.

One of the main points of doubt raised in Colonel McFadzean's letter, enclosed in No. 4, related to the treatment of the Siamese baht. After discussion with him and Mr. J. B. Williams, I still feel that the line it is proposed to take is correct, and that it is justifiable to give some recognition to the baht in Northern Malaya in spite of the fact that we are giving no recognition at all to the Japanese yen in the rest of Malaya. Apart from the fact that Siam is being treated generally as more like a convert than an enemy, it is a very important consideration that the baht circulating in Malaya are indistinguishable from the baht.

===== Page 15 =====
Baht circulating in Siam has a definite backing in currency reserves and is intended to have a definite sterling value. There is therefore no risk in giving them the corresponding value in Malay dollars, and it would be inconsistent to treat them as having a value in Siam but no value in Malaya. The important point is to ensure that the value fixed in Malaya corresponds properly with the value fixed in Siam. This has been ensured through consultation on other files.

I have attached a copy of a letter just received from Mr. Fisher. On the first point he raises, I think we should stick to the terms of the revised draft directive. It is true that the whole question of bank deposits will need considerable examination, but it may be for public convenience to permit restricted operations against old deposits before that examination is completed and comprehensive policy decided upon. I do not think there is anything seriously wrong with the directive, which allows such operations to the extent justified by the banks' position. PROVISIONAL MONETARY AND FISCAL GUIDE - MALAYA

(Applicable to the Colony of the Straits Settlements, the Federated and Unfederated Malay States)

1. From the date of entry into the territory the Allied Forces in Malaya will use British Malaya notes, denominated in dollars and issued by the Currency Commissioners of Malaya. Adequate supplies of these notes will be made available from the commencement of operations in denominations ranging from 1 cent to 10 dollars, but these will be supplemented at an early date by:

(i) coinage, which will gradually replace fractional notes of less than 50 cents face value;

(ii) Notes of higher denominations, up to $10,000, which should not be put into circulation sooner than circumstances demand, more particularly if an appreciable black market, to which their availability would present a convenience, has developed.

A proclamation should be published making all such currency legal tender, together with Straits Settlements and Malayan notes and coin of pre-occupation issues found in the country, but excepting pre-occupation issues of $10,000 and $1,000 notes, which denominations should not be legal tender and which the holders should be instructed to deposit with your administration for investigation as to their source. Where investigation reveals that such notes were lawfully obtained the holders should be given legal tender currency in exchange.

2. Subject to the above exception in respect of old notes of $10,000 and $1,000, Straits Settlements and Malayan notes of pre-occupation issues should be accepted by the Military Government on a basis of parity with the new issues. Unless, however, a temporary shortage of currency develops in any area, they should not be re-issued. In this way it is intended to secure a gradual withdrawal of the old notes in favour of the new without the difficulties of a special exchange. Straits Settlements and Malayan coin found in circulation should be permitted so to continue.

3. No notes issued by the Japanese, whether denominated in dollars or yen, should be declared by you to be legal tender. It must be assumed that such notes have, owing to the lapse of time, become general currency, at any rate in towns, but it is anticipated that by the time the territory is liberated they will have so depreciated in value, that it will be practicable completely to ignore them. Nevertheless, if, as is possible, this policy occasions undue hardship, you should accept such notes, in denominations of $5/- and less, from individuals in payment both for relief supplies and Government dues of any kind. For this purpose an exchange rate of 20 Japanese yen (or dollars) to one Malayan dollar should be adopted initially, subject to alteration downwards without notice. This limited acceptance should be discontinued as soon as Malayan notes are adjudged to be in adequate circulation.

4. In the States of Kedah, Perlis, Kolantan and Trenggamu (transferred by the Japanese to the Sianese authorities) it is likely that Sianese baht (ticals) will be found in circulation. You should not declare these to be legal tender, but should treat them as foreign currency. In these four States they may be accepted by your Administration in payment for services and Government dues, or in exchange for Malayan dollars at the rate of $1 buht = 1 dollar. No special withdrawal need be organised, and they should not be accepted by your Administration elsewhere in Malaya, unless at the request of the Army for use in Siam. Sianese notes once accepted should not, however be re-issued, but should be placed at the disposal of the Army, for use in Siam, against payment at the above rate.

5. You should prohibit all exports and imports of currency except for official purposes.

6. Upon re-occupation any part of Malaya automatically resumes its position within the sterling area. Property held in the United Kingdom on behalf of persons resident in or carrying on business in Malaya remains subject to trading with the Enemy regulations, but as substantial parts of Malaya are liberated steps will be taken in London to lift the restrictions.

===== Page 27 =====
7. Upon taking over an area you will immediately:

(a) (i) Close all Banks, Treasuries, Financial Institutions including P.O. Savings Banks, vaults and safe deposit boxes and take all steps possible to safeguard valuables, records, and premises.

(ii) Declare a general moratorium on pre-occupation debts.

(b) As soon as possible you will scrutinise the accounts and open the vaults and boxes covered by (a) with a view to:

(i) Impounding the assets of Enemy Agencies.

(ii) Blocking for scrutiny and, where appropriate, e.g., in the case of those resident in enemy territory, handing to the Custodian of Enemy Property the assets of enemy nationals. Reasonable maintenance withdrawals should, however, be allowed to those who are present in the territory, where the circumstances permit.

(iii) Blocking for future consideration the assets of persons, other than enemy nationals, suspected to have co-operated with the enemy.

(iv) Blocking for future consideration the assets of any other individuals which you may suspect to have arisen from co-operation with the enemy.

(v) Blocking for future consideration any assets which are suspected to have been acquired by looting.

(vi) Determining to what extent the Banks and Financial Institutions are solvent.

8. (a) Japanese banks and other Japanese Financial Institutions will not be re-opened. As soon as circumstances permit the records of such undertakings will be examined and a scheme for their liquidation prepared.

(b) You will, at the earliest opportunity, examine the position of the non-Japanese banks and as soon as you are satisfied that they are under adequate control you should permit them to re-open, subject to 7(b), for:

(i) The conduct of such official business as you may require.

(ii) The receipt and withdrawal of new deposits and the making of new advances.

(iii) The receipt of monies due to the banks in respect of outstanding loans.

(iv) The operation of old deposits as may be decided by each bank individually, subject to your being satisfied that their proposals are sound.

It is likely that during the initial phase it will be possible to open the banks only for the purpose enumerated in sub-paragraphs (i) and (ii) above.

(c) As soon as possible the representatives of the British and other Allied banks which operated in Malaya prior to the Japanese occupation should be permitted to re-enter and every facility should be afforded them to re-construct their position from the records of the Japanese banks which acted as liquidators. You should consult with these representatives on all banking problems with a view to restoring normal banking service in the territory at the earliest possible moment and resolving outstanding problems with regard to pre and post-occupation deposits and advances.

9. (a) You will, as soon as possible, introduce an exchange control on the basis of the Straits Settlements Defence (Finance) Regulations, modified if necessary to meet current local conditions.

(b) As from a date to be determined by you, no transfer of stocks and shares, other than bearer securities (the control of which is regarded as impracticable) or of immovable property, will be valid without your authority. 10. (i) The Post Office Savings Banks should be re-opened as soon as possible. New deposits will not be accepted against old accounts but will be credited to new accounts. 11. The moratorium should be lifted, wholly or partially, as soon as is practically possible, provided that you are satisfied that the requisite means of payment exist to permit discharge of debt, and courts exist competent to adjudicate claims.

I have just been reading the Provisional Monetary and Fiscal Guide for Malaya and there are certain points of policy that I should like to raise on it. I am assuming that the decision to squeeze out the Japanese currency as indicated in para. 3 of the Guide is an irrevocable one. We all appreciate that there is no simple and ideal way out of the Malayan currency difficulties and that every solution has its elements of arbitrariness and expediency and is largely conditioned by local circumstances and what is administratively feasible, but on balance I am of the opinion that the line we propose taking is a wrong one. Unaware that the policy had been settled, I had discussions in India with Hone and Newboult, who agreed broadly with an alternative scheme involving the valorisation of the Japanese dollar. If, however, the policy has been settled at a high level - as Turnbull assures me it has - we shall need to base our actions on it, but one or two of the practical difficulties involved are examined later in this letter.

2. The proposed treatment of the baht, laid down in para. 2 of the Guide, involves for all practical purposes the fixing of a rate with sterling of 1/2d and stands out in strange contrast to our attitude to the Japanese dollar. I have no doubt that the main reason for this difference in treatment is to be found in the fact that Thailand has sterling assets in London and we shall be able to obtain these or some other form of valuable consideration for the Thai notes which we accept. It is, however, suggested that while the consideration which we may receive for any or all of the enemy currencies should come into the picture when considering the total note circulation for the colony, it is grossly inequitable to distribute the consideration directly to the holders of the enemy currency in respect of which it is received.

C.E. Key, Esq.,
F.5,
The War Office.

Perhaps the point can best be made if we look purely at the internal aspects of the currency problem. The country has had two invaders ruling it - Thailand in the four Northern States and Japan in the South. Both the invaders have issued intrinsically valuable pieces of paper, bearing a certain relationship with each other and a certain purchasing power, which the local population has been forced to accept and for which the vast mass of the holders have given value either in goods or service. When we arrive, we make the purchasing power of one of the pieces of paper - the Japanese dollar - just about equal to its intrinsic value and we valorise the other piece of paper - the baht - at 1/2d. The holders of the Japanese dollars are penalised not because they are in any way less deserving than the holders of Thai currency but merely due to what, for the Malayan, is the purely fortuitous circumstance that Thailand holds substantial sterling assets against its currency and Japan does not. This seems inadequate justification for the policy which the Guide directs us to follow. The internal purchasing power of Malaysia should be distributed on the basis of equity as between individuals or classes or individuals within the country and not on the basis of what we hope to obtain eventually from one or other of the enemy States.

4. Another point on pars: 4 of the Guide which will cause some difficulty without achieving anything very solid is the provision that the administration may not declare the baht legal tender, but may accept it "in payment for services and government dues or in exchange for Malayan dollars." This provision must result in the administration taking in most of the baht circulation in the four Northern States and, if the administration is prepared to accept this currency, it is difficult to see why the people should not be made to accept it inter se until such time as we have the staff, etc., to withdraw it from circulation. The limited status which we propose to give the baht must lead to:

(a) A black market rate of exchange between the baht and the Malayan dollar according to speculators' anticipation of what our eventual intentions are with regard to the baht and the purchasing power of the baht in Thailand.

(b) Considerable difficulty in the administration of our price control proclamations which will declare prices in Malayan dollars of which the community will possess none in the initial stage.

(c) A number of legal difficulties concerning the currency in which debts can be discharged.

If we are going to recognise the baht at all, let us do it in a clear cut manner by granting it temporary legal tender status until such time as we can withdraw it.

MONETARY AND FISCAL GUIDE - MALAYA

(Applicable to the Colony of the Straits Settlements, the Federated and Unfederated Malay States)

1. From the date of entry into the territory, the Allied Forces in Malaya will use British Malayan notes, dated 1942, denominated in dollars and issued by the Currency Commissioners of Malaya. Adequate supplies of these notes will be made available from the commencement of operations in denominations ranging from 1 cent to 10 dollars, but these will be supplemented at an early date by:

(i) coinage, which should wherever possible be used instead of fractional notes of less than 50 cents face value.

(ii) Notes of higher denominations, up to $10,000, which should not be put into circulation sooner than circumstances demand, more particularly if an appreciable black market, to which their availability would present a convenience, has developed.

A proclamation should be published making all such currency legal currency, together with Straits Settlements and Malayan notes and coin of pre-occupation issues found in the country, but excepting pre-occupation issues of $10,000 and $1,000 notes, which denominations should not be legal tender and which the holders should be instructed to deposit with your Administration for investigation as to their source. Where investigation reveals that such notes were lawfully obtained the holders should be given legal currency in exchange.

2. Subject to the above exception in respect of old notes of $10,000 and $1,000, Straits Settlements and Malayan notes of pre-occupation issues should be accepted by the Military Government on a basis of parity with the new issues. Unless, however, a temporary shortage of currency develops in any area, they should not be re-issued. In this way it is intended to secure a gradual withdrawal of the old notes in favour of the new without the difficulties of a special exchange. Straits Settlements and Malayan coin found in circulation should be permitted so to continue.

3. No notes issued by the Japanese, whether denominated in dollars or yen, should be declared by you to be legal tender. It must be assumed that such notes have, owing to the lapse of time, become general currency, at any rate in towns, but it is anticipated that by the time the territory is liberated they will have so depreciated in value, that it will be practicable completely to ignore them. Nevertheless, if, as is possible, this policy occasions undue hardship, you should accept such notes, in denominations of $5/- and less, from individuals in payment both for relief supplies and Government dues of any kind. For this purpose an exchange rate of 20 Japanese yen (or dollars) to one Malayan dollar should be adopted initially, subject to alteration downwards without notice. This limited acceptance should be discontinued as soon as Malayan notes are adjudged to be in adequate circulation.

4. In the States of Kedah, Perlis, Kelantan and Trengganu (transferred by the Japanese to the Siamese authorities) it is likely that Siamese baht (ticals) will be found in circulation. You should not declare these to be legal tender, but should treat them as foreign currency. In these four States they may be accepted by your Administration in payment for services and Government dues, or in exchange for Malayan dollars at the rate of 2 baht = 1 dollar. No special withdrawal need be organised, and they should not be accepted by your Administration elsewhere in Malaya, unless at the request of the Army for use in Siam. Siamese notes once accepted should not, however, be re-issued, but should be placed at the disposal of the Army, for use in Siam, against payment at the above rate.

===== Page 60 =====
5. You should prohibit all exports and imports of currency except for official purposes.

6. Upon re-occupation, any part of Malaya automatically resumes its position within the sterling area. Property held in the United Kingdom on behalf of persons resident in or carrying on business in Malaya remains subject to Trading with the Enemy regulations, but as substantial parts of Malaya are liberated, steps will be taken in London to lift the restrictions.

7. Upon taking over an area you will immediately:

(a) Close all Banks, Treasuries, Financial Institutions including P.O. Savings Banks, vaults and safe deposit boxes, and place them in Military custody.

(b) Declare a general moratorium.

(c) As soon as possible you will scrutinise the accounts and open the vaults and boxes covered by (a) with a view to:

(i) Impounding the assets of Enemy Agencies.

(ii) Blocking for scrutiny and where appropriate, e.g., in the case of those resident in enemy territory, handing to the Custodian of Enemy Property the assets of enemy nationals. Reasonable maintenance withdrawals should, however, be allowed to those who are present in the territory.

(iii) Blocking for future consideration the assets of persons, other than enemy nationals, suspected to have co-operated with the enemy.

(iv) Blocking for future consideration the assets of any other individuals which you may suspect to have arisen from co-operation with the enemy.

(v) Determining to what extent the Banks and Financial Institutions are solvent.

(vi) Blocking for future consideration any assets which are suspected to have been acquired by looting.

8. (a) Subject to 7(c) and under your overriding control you should:

(i) As soon as possible re-open such local Banking Institutions as you consider to be required for the internal needs of the territory and are in a position to meet their liabilities.

(ii) As soon as possible permit the re-entry into Malaya of the representatives of the British and Allied Exchange Banks which previously operated in Malaya, and the re-establishment and re-opening of such banks.

(b) Old accounts may be operated if not blocked under paragraph 7(c).

(c) Unblocked assets may be withdrawn from vaults and safe deposit boxes subject to D.P.F. and T.W.E. regulations.

9. (a) You will as soon as possible introduce an exchange control on the basis of the Straits Settlements Defence (Finance) Regulations, modified, if necessary, to meet current local conditions.

(b) As from a date to be determined by you no transfer of stocks and shares, other than bearer securities (the control of which is regarded as impracticable) will be valid without your authority. The validity of transfers before that date is a matter to be determined by the Courts in due course.

===== Page 61 =====
10. (i) The Post Office Savings Banks should be re-opened as soon as possible. New deposits will not be accepted against old accounts, but will be credited to new accounts. Balances in old accounts outstanding from before the Japanese occupation of Malaya should be dealt with in accordance with the instructions already communicated to your G.F.A.

10. (ii) Straits Settlements War Savings Certificates should be accepted for encashment as soon as the Civil Affairs postal organisation is established.

11. The general moratorium should be lifted, wholly or partially, as soon as is practically possible provided that you are satisfied that the requisite means of payment exist to permit discharge of debt, and courts exist competent to adjudicate claims.

12. The fiscal and tax laws of the Straits Settlements, for Singapore, and the similar laws of the Federated Malay States for the Mainland territory, will be maintained and enforced as soon as possible. Alterations in the procedure and rates of taxation may be necessary but no such alterations or additions should be made without the approval of the War Office.

13. You should pay, or recognise the claims to emoluments or pensions of former Government Servants under the basic rules agreed by H.M.G.

14. The payment or recognition of other claims against the Governments of Malaya in respect of the period before the Japanese occupation of the territory will be made subject of specific instructions from H.M.G.

15. You will, on behalf of the War Office, control and direct all receipts and disbursements by the Military Administration, and will render to the War Office quarterly accounts in a form to be agreed with your G.F.A.

The account should show the net cash cost of the Civil Affairs Service.

16. (a) You will also maintain approximate records of stores, supplies and services furnished to by the Civil Affairs Services by other Services of the British Army, the Royal Navy, the Royal Air Force and Allied Forces. Where exact costing is considered to involve an unjustifiable expenditure of man-power, approximate estimates of value or estimates in kind will suffice.

(b) Expenditure upon the rehabilitation of private property should be separately recorded. Where no exact record is possible, an approximation should be made. But the War Office (after consultation with the Colonial Office) will in due course issue specific directions as to what recovery should be made in respect of rehabilitated private property.

"Services" furnished to the Civil Affairs Service by other Services of the British Army will, of course, include payments to Officers of the Civil Affairs Service by Pay Offices with which no cash adjustment is made by the G.F.A.

17. A separate directive will be issued to you on the subject of Custodianship of Property.

18. Your general policy should be framed to guard against inflation.

Note on draft Monetary and Fiscal Guide - Malaya

The draft Guide is in a form similar to that of the Burma Guide. Reference is made below to the several changes.

Paragraph 1. It will not be possible to introduce new coin in the very early stages, but it is very desirable that the coin provided should replace fractional notes at the earliest opportunity.

It will certainly be necessary to put higher denomination notes into circulation sooner or later, but this should be delayed as long as possible.

Paragraph 2. The reasons for the minor changes herein are self-evident.

Paragraph 3. This is put forward as an alternative to paragraph 3 of the Burma Guide for consideration in the light of the latest information from Burma. If, as now seems likely, we can make a practical success of a policy of non-recognition of Japanese currency, arrangements for the deposit of Japanese notes would seem to be unnecessary.

The exchange rates suggested to meet cases of hardship is, of course, purely tentative and may well be too high.

Paragraph 4. Is introduced to provide for the Malay States ceded by the Japanese to Siam.

The succeeding paragraphs of the Malayan Guide should be compared, in each case, with the preceding paragraph of the Burma Guide.

Paragraph 7. The changes are only minor ones of wording.

Paragraph 8. Provides for the different banking arrangements made for Malaya.

Paragraph 9. Sub-paragraph (b) excludes bearer securities, to which I referred in previous correspondence.

Paragraph 10. The policy to be pursued in connexion with Post Office Savings Banks has already been approved by the War Office and the Colonial Office. I regret I cannot quote the references, as my own files have already been despatched to India.

Paragraph 14. The treatment of pre-occupation claims has already been the subject of much correspondence, and, in accordance with instructions from the D.G.A., a proposal for the creation of a separate Claims Commission is now under consideration by the Colonial Office.

Paragraph 16. I have thought it unnecessary to include paragraph 15 (b) of the Burma Guide in view of the financial arrangements made between the War Office and the Colonial Office.

I had considered the desirability of directing a prohibition in the early stages of dollar-sterling exchange transactions, but since (a) no such transactions will be possible until we provide the facilities, (b) the Currency Commissioners hold full sterling cover for the dollar currency and (c) it will be to the benefit of our currency policy that such transactions should be permitted as soon as possible, I had not thought fit to include any such reference.
