Nov. 152 and 154.

This is the first recorded case of the death of a municipal employee while a prisoner-of-war.

This widow had been receiving allowances of £7.10.0 a week from the Malayan Govt. Agent at Sydney until Aug. 1942 when an increase to £40 a month was authorised (at 81 and 86). Total paid to her to end of March 1943 = £460.17.2.

Questions now raised by Mr. Agents are:

a) In absence of any ruling that salary of Mr. employee who are prisoner-of-war accrued during captivity, what should be the treatment of the sums already advanced?

Mr. Agents P. Oldmo suggest that they should remain for the time being a charge against Municipal funds up to 30th June 1943.

b) What allowance should continue to be made to the widow and from what source should it be paid?

Mr. Agents P. Oldmo suggest a reduction from £40 to £25, to be debited to the deceased's Provident Fund account, the interest on which would be sufficient to meet the cost of the allowance.

c) Refund of Provident Fund balance (£10,326.16.11 on 31 Dec. 1941).

Mr. Agents P. Oldmo state that this would have to await proof of title by the widow.

I suggest that:

a) should be approved that the amount already paid should be charged against Municipal funds in accordance with the original instructions received from Sponge (Nov. 4/50/44/8/42) that allowances to wives were to be paid from sums remitted to Mr. "in cases of extreme urgency".

b) should be approved and a risk ignored that the widow might not be the legal person representative, in which case recovery might be difficult.

c) should be approved on production of title. If (a) is approved, there would be no need to withhold a portion of the Provident Fund balance to cover a possible claim at some future date for refund of allowances already paid.

Sague, but ready to consider the general art of execution.

The principle of "accruing pay" for Penang and Singapore municipal employees who are missing or prisoners of war has never been applied and it is thought that the time has now come to consider the matter. These employees may be divided into two groups:

A. Contributors to Provident Fund. These were the permanent staff, engaged both locally and non-locally, for whom contribution was compulsory.

B. Non-contributors to Provident Fund. These constituted the labour staff and were all locally engaged.

Alignment of Municipal to Civil Service Employees.

Contributors to Provident Fund would appear to rank pari passu as Pensionable Civil Servants, but the Municipal Agents have all along made no distinction between Non-locally recruited and Locally recruited, two groups which are differently regarded in the Colonial Civil Service, and have been paying allowances up to 50% of salary to dependants of officers of both the above categories who may be missing or prisoners of war.

Non-Contributors to Provident Fund would appear to rank with Locally recruited non-pensionable civil servants in whose case the principle of accruing pay has not been conceded by the Treasury. The dependants of a few of these Municipal employees are outside Malaya but are not being given allowances from Municipal funds. They are drawing civil relief.

If the principle of accruing pay were to be generally conceded, it would conflict with the Treasury ruling in the case of A (locally recruited pensionable employees) and B (locally recruited non-pensionable employees).

If Colonial Civil Service practice is to be followed, and the principle of accruing pay is confined to non-locally engaged pensionable or Provident Fund staff, I fancy that the Municipal Agents would find themselves faced with some difficulty and, later on, with a flood of criticism.

I consider, therefore, that this question of accruing pay, with special reference to locally engaged staff, must await a settlement of the problem by C.S. Department who referred a similar question to Colonial Governors on 1st September 1942.

As regards allowances to dependants, the Municipal Agents are paying to all except those of Non-Contributors to Provident Fund (equivalent to Locally recruited non-pensionable Civil Servants).

I consider that it would not be out of place to treat these dependants - who are few in number at the moment - on the same basis as C.S. Department treat the equivalent category, i.e., to pay ex gratia allowances according to need from Municipal funds if

1) the officers concerned were engaged prior to September 1939 and have since been continuously employed (a slight latitude has been conceded by the Treasury in regard to this date),

or

2) the officers concerned were transferred to or retained [POSSIBLY MISSING]

Sir,

I received a letter from the Malayan Government Agent in Sydney a few weeks ago informing me that you have given instructions that my services with the Singapore Municipal Water Department will terminate at the end of three month's notice. I had at least hoped that I might be offered some suitable employment in another part of the Empire, and would willingly have accepted and at the end of hostilities would have been prepared to be told that my services are not required further.

I was Engineer in Charge of the Singapore Water Supply from Johore, comprising Reservoirs, Catchment area, Pipe Lines, Pumping Stations and Filters, and evacuated to Singapore Island on 29th January. The water was shut off on that date and the Johore Causeway which carried the two 39" dia Pipes was blown up on 31st January. These two 39" Pipes carried two day's supply of water to Singapore.

During the last few days in Johore I often met Colonel Steadman, who was Chief Engineer in the construction of a perimeter camp at our Pulai 1 Reservoir, and Colonel Dalley who was in command of the "Dalforce", a civilian group of Volunteers who did some good guerilla warfare in the Catchment area. On our last day in Johore I assisted Colonel Dalley to put a party of 12 Dalforce officers in a concealed part of the Catchment area, with three month's stores and our motor boat, and these men were to await the approaching Japs, and if possible do some damage behind the lines. With local knowledge of this part of Johore I was asked for a "Yes" or "No" answer, if this plan could succeed in holding the Japs for at least a few days.

In Singapore I worked with the Water Department on a new raw water pumping Station at Seletar which was to supply a good fire fighting service to Tengah and Seletar Aerodromes. This station was functioning for a few days when the delivery main was destroyed by a bomb, and on the following day this part of the Island was captured by the Japs.

I was working until the fall of Singapore and living in the office of the Municipal Stores and Workshop, with Mr. Norman Coulson, Superintendent in charge, and when hostilities ceased on the afternoon of Sunday February 15th I was approached by Lieutenant Wyatt Smith of the Dalforce about manning a launch and make an effort to get away.

These Dalforce Officers were given permission by General Newbiggen to get out of Malaya, and I agreed to go as Engineer on the "Kembong" a Government launch, and make for Benkalis, Sumatra. We arrived there on Wednesday February 18th. At Benkalis we received instructions to proceed to Pakan Bahru, about 280 miles away and take 30 Australian soldiers who had reached Benkalis by another route. At Pakan Bahru we found a motor coach waiting to take us to Paja Goemok and from there we got a train to Padang on the West Coast of Sumatra. We stayed in a school for two days and then boarded H.M.S. "Danae" for Djilatjap in Java. At this place I parted from the military as they were going to Ceylon on a Shanghai river steamer and we came to Australia on a Pontianak river steamer and had for a crew some of the crew of the "Repulse" and "Prince of Wales".

===== Page 83 =====
I have a note signed by Major Marshall of the Dalforce and Mr. McKerron, M.C.S., who was Chief of the Man-Power Bureau in Singapore, stating that I left Singapore after hostilities ceased, and was doing a service to my country. The complete trip lasted 24 days - Singapore to Australia.

If you decide that my services are not acceptable and you have nothing to offer me for the duration, I would be grateful to accept my Provident Fund in full, also the leave pay due and passage to the United Kingdom when required. Dear Mr. Monson,

Thank you for your note of the 4th instant reference 50744/8/42, regarding Messrs W. Rose and W.L. Kerr, both of the Singapore Municipality.

We were not aware, prior to your letter, that Mr. Rose was in Australia, and we do not know the period for which he has been granted leave. We think he should be informed that his service with the Singapore Commissioners will terminate at the end of his leave, assuming that leave to be for an unexpired period of three months or more. If his leave should be for an unexpired period of less than three months, he should be given notice of termination of his services at the end of three months.

With regard to Mr. W.L. Kerr, we received a letter from the Agent General for Tasmania dated the 17th February quoting the following cable received from the Premier's Office at Hobart:

"Please ascertain from Peirce & Williams No.1 Victoria Street London whether they have knowledge of transfer of fund of Singapore Municipality (STOP) Enquiry is made on behalf of William Lockhart Kerr Deputy Superintendent of Machinery Stores Workshop Singapore now resident in Tasmania (STOP) He has approximately £5,000 in Municipal Provident Fund if available please arrange transfer to him through Treasury of full amount due.

We had no instructions from Singapore regarding Mr. Kerr, but from the above cable we assumed that he had already retired and was concerned only with his Provident Fund. If Mr. Kerr can satisfy the Premier's Office that he is not on leave prior to retirement he should receive three months notice and pay.
