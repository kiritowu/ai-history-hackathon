Mr Parkin,

I mentioned to you that Col. Spencer Wilkinson had asked to be informed, as soon as we were in a position to inform him, what legislation required on the resumption of civil government in Malaya would be done here, so that he could plan accordingly. It is not yet possible to give a clear idea, but I suggest a reply on the lines of the attached draft.

I discussed the position with Mr Robert-Wray, who said that no definite commitment, beyond the Constitutional instruments, should be given at present.

I think that, of the subjects mentioned in this draft, if Mr Hartings could tackle some of the Defence Regulations, debtor-creditor relations & Companies, I could probably manage the rest. Land is going to be a difficult one; can't yet foresee what will be ensured, but I have been on this from the start and had better do it.

Would you please pass this to Mr Robert-Wray?

K. K. O'Connor

Defence regulations.

One. To bring up-to-date and continue finance regulations in Malay Peninsular Division where both defence and emergency regulations are involved, proclamation is in preparation giving DCCAO necessary detailed regulation-making powers. Such regulations will be independent of any emergency legislation or existence of state of emergency and will, if inconsistent with existing regulations, prevail under section 7 of interpretation proclamation. Similar proclamation for Singapore. This is being done as matter of urgency.

Two. Suggest similar action might be taken regarding Straits Settlements defence regulations generally for following reasons.

A. Probable gap between expiration of Act and assumption legislative powers by Government.

B. Complications in Malay Peninsular due to incorporation of Penang and Malacca.

C. Desirability of uniformity throughout territory. Civil Government could repeat or vary as necessary.

Three. Your letter 30 Nov refers. Copy brought by Paskin; original not yet received.

Four. Request your views on paragraph two.

Dear Spenser Wilkinson,

On the subject of legislation required for the changeover to civil government I am not yet able to give you a clear picture, but the following may help you in your planning.

2. All Constitutional instruments are being drawn here. These are:

(i) Straits Settlements (Repeal) Act, 1945. (The Draft Bill has been passed by Legislation Committee. I enclose a print of the Bill and also the Memorandum which I compiled to accompany Instructions to Parliamentary Counsel. This Memorandum is, I think, a complete Constitutional record of the Straits Settlements and may be useful to you.)

(ii) The Straits Settlements Order in Council, 1946 bringing the Straits Settlements (Repeal) Act into operation and fixing the "appointed day".

(iii) The Singapore Colony Order in Council, 1946. (This constitutes Singapore a separate Colony and sets up a Governor, Executive Council, Legislative Council, Courts, etc., etc., and contains Transitional Provisions providing for the continuing validity of existing laws, for continuance of Court proceedings pending before 15th February, 1942, for interim powers for the Governor to legislate with an Advisory Council until the Executive Council and Legislative

COLONEL T.C. SPENSER WILKINSON,

===== Page 13 =====
Council are convened, for transfer of assets and liabilities including Government loans and War Savings Certificates, for amendment of all Imperial Acts and Orders in Council applying to the Straits Settlements, and other provisions.

(iv) Royal Instructions for Singapore.

(v) The Malayan Union Order in Council, 1946, containing similar provisions to those mentioned under (iii) plus the provisions necessary for the different constitutional arrangements proposed for that territory.

(vi) Royal Instructions for the Malayan Union.

(vii) Letters Patent revoking all existing Letters Patent which apply to the Straits Settlements.

I am proceeding with these as quickly as possible.

3. The position with regard to Straits Settlements Defence Regulations has been under consideration. The point is that the Defence Regulations will lapse when the Emergency Powers Acts expire in February, unless some of them are saved by action under the Supplies and Services (Transitional Powers) Bill now before Parliament. In view of the limited class of Regulations which could be saved under that Act and the fact that the Regulations are not up to date and to bring them up to date would require action by the Governor of Ceylon (Emergency Powers (Straits Settlements / Defence) [POSSIBLY MISSING].

===== Page 14 =====
Defence Order in Council, 1942), it would seem better that this matter be dealt with on resumption of civil government in Singapore by local (Governor's) Ordinance. As mentioned above, the Governor will have legislative powers on the commencement of civil government. In any event, it will be necessary to consider, select for retention, and amend the existing Straits Settlements Defence regulations. I think that this task might be undertaken, and the Ordinance might be drafted, here; but the ability to do this here depends on time and the staff position. I am not allowed to give a definite undertaking.

4. I completed, some time ago, the draft of an Indemnity Ordinance. This is under consideration here. I enclose a copy.

5. The Interim Lands Directive will require legislation to implement its recommendations. This Directive has been approved here, except for Part IV - Mortgages and Charges. This is tied up with the question of debtor-creditor relations, now under Treasury consideration. We await your comments on the remainder of the Directive. I think that the necessary legislation should be drawn here; but, again, I can give no undertaking at present.

6. The subject of debtor-creditor relations, when the policy has been settled, will require legislation.

7. Legislation will also be required with regard to companies and with regard to compensation for war losses, a subject which is under consideration here. If time and staff permit, these may also be tackled here.

8. The present proposal is that the Rural Board of Singapore should be re-constituted for a period.

After resumption of civil government, if this is done, the Municipal Ordinance will not require immediate amendment.

9. The Constitutions will provide for the continuing validity, mutatis mutandis, of existing laws in force in the various territories, until altered or repealed by the local legislatures (or by the Governor under his interim powers) or by other lawful authority. I will send you drafts as soon as possible. The drafting of these Constitutions requires close attention and considerable research. To take one instance, every Imperial Act and Order in Council relating to the Straits Settlements has to be traced, turned up, and considered for amendment or reference, and the process takes much time.

10. I fear that paragraphs 3, 6, and 7 are indefinite, but I cannot be more definite just at present. Can you, please, let me have your views?

Yours sincerely,

J. K. O'Connor

P.S. There is another Bill now before Parliament - the Emergency Laws (Transitional Provisions) Bill - which extends some Defence Regulations until 31st December, 1947, notwithstanding the earlier expiry of the Emergency Powers Defence Acts. But this is too limited in scope to be of much assistance. I will send you, as soon as possible, a copy of a Minute which sets out the whole position.
