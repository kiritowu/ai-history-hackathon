My dear Ahearne,

A few days ago C.H. Koh came in to thank me for giving him the opportunity of meeting you at my house and to let me know that he is now with the Malayan Representative. He is full of gratitude for what you have done for him, and I am sure you will find a most loyal officer in him. I am very happy indeed that you have been able to secure the employment of one of our men so quickly. You may remember we spoke about this question. This matter is very near the hearts of all true Malayans because it touches the welfare of our descendants and would be a real incentive to the perpetuation of the permanent type of Malayan. Since the early twenties I have taken keen interest in this question. I have taken a hand in many struggles and have watched the creation and development of, first, the S.S. Civil Service and, later, the S.S. Legal Service. Finally, I have seen the admission of Lim Koon Teck into the COLONIAL Legal Service a few months before the fall of Singapore. You may have noticed that these three stages mark the gradual acceptance of the principle of better treatment in the Civil Services for the local non-European. They are the result of heavy pressure of local public opinion. You may remember that for many years the "Straits Times" and the "Malaya Tribune" carried many leaders and long articles on the subject. Our men in the S.S.C.S. and S.S.L.S. have also proved their merits. E.B. Williams, Official Assignee, S.S., had a few in his department and was well satisfied. Forerer, the District Judge, liked his Asiatic Magistrates, and I have heard him myself. These two are hard men to convince as you may know.

We have been very frank with each other, and I like your sincerity especially when it results in quick action. Would you therefore permit me to say that I am rather unhappy about the financial side of Koh's appointment? I fully appreciate that there may be many factors unknown to me, and as Koh has accepted the salary offered to him I leave him in your hands. But his salary in Malayan currency appears paltry for a person of his qualifications, and I really hope that his action will not be taken as a precedent for future officers. I know that he needed a job badly and is very thankful to get it. But others may say that the Government took advantage of his helplessness to impose harsh terms. Anything that may give occasion for sayings like this should be carefully avoided. They do much harm. After all both Europeans and Asiatics need jobs badly at this time, and these wartime posts cannot be compared with peace-time employment in that a certain amount of sympathetic treatment has led to the creation of many posts.

===== Page 13 =====
Within my knowledge, yet Europeans invariably receive very good salaries, whereas the Asiatic gets much less. It may be true that one principle is used in both cases: the principle of basing present pay on salaries actually received in Malaya. The obvious answer is that if the unfairness and lowness of the scale of pay for Asiatics in Malaya has been sympathetically acknowledged by the Colonial Office (vide the different but increasingly better scales for the S.S.C.S., S.S.L.S., and C.L.S.), are we now to have a retrogressive policy? Though Lim Koon Teck alone went into the C.L.S., there were many, equally qualified, left in the S.S.L.S., and the S.S.C.S. had many men who could be in the M.C.S. Arguing against this policy of discrimination, Sir George Maxwell, in his article in the magazine of the Association of British Malaya (February 1943) entitled "The Mixed Communities of Malaya," quotes the case of Mr. Gunn Lay Teck of the Agricultural Dept.

If any fair but just changes are to be put into effect on return to Malaya, I sincerely hope that the subordination of merit to colour will be completely eradicated. In the West Indies and in Ceylon all are equal. May Malaya be the same and British rule be loved by all. Let that same sympathetic heart which beats for the European beat equally sympathetically for the non-European.

May I make an especial appeal to you to see that full justice be done in this matter of the employment and official titles of non-Europeans and that there be no excuses or 'quibbling' or mental reservation whatsoever in dealing with them? In particular, I would ask you, if possible, to let me know any reasons put forward in minute papers in favour of discrimination, so that I may effectively answer them instead of an answer being supplied 'confidentially' by the other side only. I speak to you earnestly now and for the welfare of our beloved Malaya.

I am sending a copy of this letter to Mr. Gent, as you may be late in receiving this. I understand you went by sea.

With kindest regards to you and the whole family, whom I trust you will find very well.

Yours very sincerely,

Jacob Chen Glock

LONG-TERM POLICY DIRECTIVES

5/7/44

PUBLIC SERVICES

1. There should be a single Public Services Commission for the Malayan Union and Singapore to be appointed by the Secretary of State. Its functions will be to advise the respective Governors in the exercise of their powers relating to appointments, promotions, transfer, dismissal and disciplinary control of Public Officers.

2. Save with the consent of the Governor no person who is not a British subject or a Malayan Union citizen should be appointed to the Public Service in the Malayan Union or in Singapore.

3. All officers of the Public Services should be liable for service in the Malayan Union, in Singapore, or in Borneo.

4. Appointments (with the exception of judicial appointments) to the Public Services in the Malayan Union and in Singapore may be made locally by the respective Governors on the recommendation of the Public Services Commission after selection on lines similar to the selection system of the Colonial Service Appointments Board, subject to the right of the Secretary of State to select on the advice of the C.S.A.B. such candidates as he may at any time consider it advisable to appoint from outside Malaya. Such selection by the Secretary of State will be open to non-European candidates who are citizens of the Malayan Union provided that they are not resident in Malaya at the time of selection. Officers so selected by the Secretary of State will be liable for transfer anywhere in the Colonies at the Secretary of State's discretion.

Judicial appointments may be made by the respective Governors on the recommendation of a Standing Judicial Appointments Board to be convened by the Chief Justice.

===== Page 23 =====
5. Qualifications of candidates for appointment will be prescribed and published as approved by the Secretary of State.

6. All appointments will be subject to a three years' period of probation.

7. It should be a condition for confirmation in the higher grades of appointment that an officer shall be proficient in the English and Malay languages.

8. Courses of instruction or study, on selection or at any time during an officer's service, may be prescribed by the Governor on the advice of the Public Services Commission with the approval of the Secretary of State.

9. The posting of officers in Malaya will be a matter for local direction, and, in the case of senior appointments, will be subject to confirmation by the Secretary of State.

10. Leave, allowances and other conditions of service in the Malayan Union and in Singapore should be prescribed in Public Service Regulations made on the recommendation of the Public Services Commission with the approval of the Secretary of State.

11. For all officers wherever selected there should be common basic scales of salary, but officers whose permanent homes are not in Malaya will receive in addition to the basic Malayan scale, a pensionable expatriation allowance at rates approved by the Secretary of State.

12. Pending review by a Salaries Commission the basic scales of salary for new entrants should be equivalent to the pre-war scales, with the exception that in categories of appointments for which candidates were selected by the Secretary of State, the basic scales should be equivalent to the pre-war scales less 30 per cent.

I. Constitution of the Public Services Commission

Article 89 of the Ceylon (State Council) Order in Council (1931) states: "There shall be a Public Services Commission to advise the Governor in exercise of the powers conferred upon him by Article 86 (Article 86 refers to the appointment, promotion, transfer, dismissal and disciplinary control of public officers). The Chief Secretary shall be the Chairman and such other persons in the Public Service as the Governor shall appoint shall be members. In the absence of the Chief Secretary such other person as the Governor shall appoint shall be the Chairman of the Commission. (Sub Section 2) The Governor may at any time revoke any appointment made by him under this Article. (Sub Section 3) The Governor may by regulation, subject to the approval of the Secretary of State, prescribe the duties of and the procedure to be followed by the Commission in the exercise of their duties and the number which shall form a quorum."

Article 90. "The Public Services Commission may require any Public Officer to attend and to give evidence before it touching any matter which it shall have under consideration and may require the production of any official documents relating to any such matter."

The regulations referred to in Article 89 are called Public Service Regulations and those relating to the Public Services Commission are summarised hereunder:

II. Public Service Regulations

1. Procedure.

Every question at a meeting of the Public Services Commission is determined by a majority of votes of the members present and in case of equal division of votes the Chairman has a second or casting vote. If the number of the members of the Commission is two or three, the quorum for a meeting is two; if the number of members is more than three, the number is three.

2. The Commission has to discharge that part of the duties assigned to them by the Governor in connection with the administration of the Public Services in accordance with the procedure specified in regulations.

3. Appointments.

Appointments in the Public Services to posts with an initial salary exceeding 300, or rupees 9,000 per annum, are subject to approval of the Secretary of State. Any vacancies are reported to the Public Services Commission by the Head of the Department who makes his recommendation for filling the vacancy.

4. In the case of a post usually held by Civil Servants, where there is no proposal to fill the same otherwise than from the Civil Service, the Head of the Department reports the vacancy to the Chief Secretary who makes the required recommendation for filling the same to the P.S.C.

5. In the case of any judicial posts on the staff of the Supreme Court, the Chief Justice makes recommendations to the Governor direct.

6. In the case of posts usually held by officers under the scheme for Accountants, the Head of the Department reports the vacancy to the Financial Secretary who makes the required recommendation.

===== Page 26 =====
Required recommendation for filling the same to the P.S.C.

7. Where vacancies are filled according to the results of the examinations in conformity with an approved scheme of recruitment, Heads of Departments are not required to make any recommendations. On receipt of notification of vacancies in such cases, the Public Services Commission makes their recommendations to the Governor for filling the same in accordance with the examination scheme applicable in each case.

8. The Public Services Commission, through its Chairman, advises the Governor regarding the filling of the vacancy, and the Governor makes his recommendations to the Secretary of State.

9. Procedure for making salary acting appointments and seconded appointments is the same as that prescribed for appointments.

10. Judicial Appointments
There is a separate Judicial Appointments Board to advise the Governor in regard to Judicial Appointments. There is the Legal Secretary and two Judges of the Supreme Court nominated by the Chief Justice. The Legal Secretary is Chairman. The Legal Secretary and one Judge form a quorum.

11. Appointments in Public Service to posts in which initial pensionable and non-pensionable salary of officer is Rupees 3,200 but will not exceed Rs. 600 or Rupees 9,000, are not subject to approval of the Secretary of State but require the sanction of Government. Procedure for making such appointments is the same as that prescribed in the above Regulations except that the Governor will make the appointment without forwarding the recommendation to the Secretary of State.

12. Heads of Departments are authorised to make appointments whether permanent or temporary, pensionable or non-pensionable posts, initial salaries of which do not exceed Rs. 3,200, or in the case of salary scales authorised for June 1934, Rs. 2,600.

13. Retirements
Age for retirement of pensionable officers is 55 years. Every Public officer may be required to retire after attaining the age requirement if Head of Department considers it in the public interest. If an officer should be so required to retire, he makes a recommendation to the Public Service Commission accordingly.

14. To judge if he should now retire of a Head of Department who has reached the age of retirement devolves upon the Chief Secretary who recommends to the P.S.C.

III. Discipline
The Public Service Commission has disciplinary powers in the case of officers whose pensionable emoluments exceed Rs. 300 or Rs. 4,500. Charges are investigated in accordance with Colonial Regulations by a Committee of Enquiry appointed by the Governor, which reports to the Public Services Commission. The Public Services Commission may refer the matter back to the Committee for further enquiry or report, but does not itself hear witnesses, save in exceptional circumstances and at the request of the Governor. The Commission forwards the proceedings of the enquiry to the Governor with their recommendation as to punishment, if any.

2. In cases where the Chief Secretary considers it necessary to proceed against an officer for general inefficiency which cannot be dealt with by specific charges, he reports the case to the Governor who, if he decides that procedure for framing the

===== Page 27 =====
Charges is not applicable, refers the matter to the P.S.C. which, after procuring from the Heads of Departments in which the officer has served, statements as to the general standard of efficiency, makes its report to the Governor.

2. Public Officers whose emoluments exceed 1,500 rupees but do not exceed £450, or rupees 6,750 per annum. In these cases, the authority of the Secretary of State is not required for dismissal or punishment, but the Head of the Department can proceed by way of reprimand, fine, suspension, stoppage, reduction, or deferment of increment for a period not exceeding one year. After considering the officer's reply, if he considers it necessary to punish him with any punishment except those referred to above, he forwards the case to the P.S.C. with the statement of the evidence, charges, and defence. The P.S.C. forwards the documents received from the Heads of the Departments to the Governor with their recommendation as to punishment. Similarly, in cases of general inefficiency, if the Head of Department considers any order is called for exceeding deferment of increment for one year, he reports the case to P.S.C. which, after procuring from the Heads of Departments in which the officer has served, statements as to his general standard of efficiency, forwards such statements and documents to the Governor with their advice.

Examinations

The Public Services Commission in Ceylon had been, up to March 1942, conducting or making regulations for the conducting of any examinations, but in view of the impossibility of recruiting officers through examinations held in England, the P.S.C. in India had been asked to set papers for examination for recruitment to the Ceylon Civil Service. This they had done somewhat unwillingly as there were papers to be set in Sinhalese and Tamil, and in Ceylon history. They had suggested that, in future, such examinations should be held in Ceylon, the papers being set if necessary by the Ceylon University staff or Education Department. I understand that since that date the P.S.C. in India has refused to have anything to do with Ceylon examinations, and the position now probably is that the Ceylon P.S.C. is making arrangements for recruitment by examination to the Civil Service.

As regards the general clerical service examination in Ceylon, the P.S.C. had no duties. The regulations and conditions of the examination were framed by the Chief Secretary and laid down from time to time in the Gazette and the examination was conducted by the Education Department.

1. There should be a single Public Service Commission for the Malayan Union and Singapore (with functions similar to the present Public Service Commission in Ceylon).

2. All officers of the Public Services should be liable for service in Borneo.

3. Appointments to the Public Services in Malaya may be made locally by the Governor of the Malayan Union (hereinafter referred to as the Governor) on the recommendation of the Public Services Commission after selection on lines similar to the selection system of the Colonial Service Appointments Board subject to the right of the Secretary of State to select on the advice of the C.S.A.B. such candidates as he may at any time consider it advisable to appoint in England.

The selection in England will not necessarily be restricted to European candidates and non-European candidates who are citizens of the Malayan Union will be eligible for selection by the Secretary of State if they are in England at the time of selection. Any officer selected by the Secretary of State in England will be liable for transfer anywhere in the Colonial Service at the Secretary of State's discretion.

4. Qualifications of candidates for appointment will be prescribed and published as approved by the Secretary of State.

5. All appointments will be subject to a three years period of probation.

6. It should be a condition for confirmation in service that an officer shall be proficient in the English and Malay languages.

Dear Wodeman,

I am sorry that I have been so long in replying to your note of 20th April, 1944 in M 101/9 on the subject of long-term policy directives. We wished, however, to review all the drafts as a whole and to discuss the matter in our Advisory Council. I enclose an extract from the minutes of the meeting held on 19th May. It suggested, you will see, that the Colonial Office might convene a special meeting to review the position in the light of the second paragraph of the minute, as soon as Cabinet approval of the main constitutional directive has been received.

2. As regards the proposed long-term directive on the Public Services in Malaya with particular reference to the employment of Asiatics in the senior Services, we suggest that as a first step the Colonial Service Department might be informed of our request for some guidance in this matter, because fundamental questions of general Colonial policy are involved and it is understood that certain decisions as to the future method of recruiting for the Senior Colonial Services have been or are about to be taken. If the Colonial Service Department could let us have a note as to how the general policy will probably affect Malaya, we should be very glad to record our views on its local application and then to meet to discuss further with them and endeavour to produce a draft directive.
