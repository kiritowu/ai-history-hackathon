Mr. Gent.

The War Office asked for information about evacuation of useless mouths from Singapore - I believe the Prime Minister is enquiring. After discussion with Mr. Calder and Sir Cosmo Parkinson, the following was telephoned over at 1.15 p.m. on January 10th.

"Figures are not yet available but evacuation of useless mouths is being pushed on, without racial discrimination, as fast as shipping can be made available.

Four hundred and fifty European women and children were evacuated from Penang to Singapore, where two thousand five hundred Europeans now await shipping facilities; most of them want to go to Australia. They will be got away as soon as possible.

Indian women are generally unwilling to sail without their menfolk. Facilities are being given for Chinese to get away to China via Burma, but few wish to go."

This afternoon Mr. Hall had a visit from the President of the Association of British Malaya (Mr. G.E. Cator, C.M.G.) and the Honorary Secretary (Captain Gammons, M.P.). The Association recently issued a broadcast announcement stating that they were open to receive enquiries from those with friends in Malaya and would distribute any information they received about such people in the current situation there. The Association has had many enquiries from the public, including from people here claiming to be dependents on remittances from husbands or parents in Malaya, which they presume are now stopped, either because Malaya is enemy-occupied or because their source of income has disappeared due to the Japanese forces overrunning the tin and rubber districts of the Malay States.

We have had a similar situation in Hong Kong and have made emergency arrangements for the immediate future with the Treasury's consent. We are consulting the Treasury about adopting a similar system for Malayan dependents. However, it is not expected that the Treasury will provide continuous public funds to maintain the income levels to which dependents have become accustomed from family remittances.

It was suggested that a broadcast statement might be issued regarding the evacuation of women and children from Singapore. It was understood that this is a shipping and defense issue, and it would be wrong to encourage a flood of refugees into Singapore, as the goal is to clear out "useless lives." The extent to which evacuation can be done depends on available shipping. Nevertheless, we could give the press some assurance in the U.K. that facilities are being arranged for women and children wishing to leave Malaya, and that financial assistance for the voyage would be given if necessary. Treasury concurrence will be needed, as costs may fall on the UK's public funds, although initially Malayan funds may cover the expenses. War Office concurrence might also be obtained by telephone from M.O.10.

Date: 13.1.42

When I have reviewed enquiries received through the Casualty Section, I will assess the usefulness of more statements. Below is some relevant correspondence, including a reply regarding certain inquiries about Malayan movements.

In the "Times" of today there is a report of a statement in the "Malaya Tribune" that it was stated in authoritative quarters that the Government are not providing special facilities of any kind for the evacuation of European women and children, or of any community; every facility, however, is being given to women and children who wish to leave and who can make their own arrangements; there is no racial discrimination in the allocation of shipping passages, and the only rule imposed by the Government is that priority must be given to women and children, irrespective of race; there is nothing to prevent Chinese, Indian, Malay, or Eurasian women from leaving Singapore, the newspaper adds, if they have the means to go and to support themselves overseas.

The effect of this announcement seems to me that while there is no discrimination on a racial basis, there is practical discrimination - of an even more objectionable type - on the basis of personal wealth. To people in this country this may appear that rich Chinese can get away, whereas the subordinate classes of Europeans will not. That would obviously appear to be a fantastic position here.

We have just sent out a telegram to the Governor about the provision of care and funds for evacuees (No. 16), and we are taking up with the Treasury the question of H.M.G. funds being available if necessary to ensure that funds are available both immediately and in the future. The M.O.10 objection has possibly disappeared in view of the local statement, and I should like to take it up with them and with the Straits Settlements on the basis of a quick authoritative statement here on the lines suggested in my minute of 13th January.

With reference to your letter of the 16th March, No. P.127-6/43-0.8., and in continuation of India Office letter No. Pol. 305/42 dated 10th April, I am directed by the Secretary of State to state that the Secretary of State for the Colonies has taken notice of the allegations regarding discriminatory treatment of Indians in the evacuation of civilians from Penang and Singapore. He has expressed much sympathy with the Government of India in their concern on account of the allegations which have been made, but regrets that an immediate inquiry is impracticable. Viscount Granborne is, however, fully alive to the importance of avoiding discriminatory treatment on racial grounds in any evacuation of the civilian population in other territories that may become necessary, and has issued strong instructions to this effect.

As regards the evacuation in Malaysia, I am to say that the official reports that have been received do not confirm the allegation that Indians were seriously prevented from leaving Penang. Arrangements were made for the evacuation from Penang of relations along with Europeans of military age on December 16th, but owing to lack of transport, families could not be safely accommodated. No information is available with regard to the number of Indians who actually left, but there is evidence that Indians, both men and women, refused to leave without their families although they were given the opportunity to do so.

The Government of India's allegations relate to the local evacuation of Penang and to the general evacuation from Singapore. Their letter can be most conveniently considered under these separate heads.

(a) Penang.

The Government of India refers to reports that while the entire European population was removed from the Island, practically no Asiatics were evacuated.

A full report on the circumstances of the evacuation was received from the Governor of the Straits Settlements on the evacuation of Penang in his telegram No. 52 Government House of the 16th January (a copy of which was sent to the India Office after receipt). It will be appreciated from that report that the evacuation was carried out under circumstances of great difficulty, particularly owing to the rapid advance of the Japanese forces on the peninsula, the destruction of a large part of the town by the enemy's air action, and by the disappearance of the bulk of the Asiatic population and the restricted means of transport available for carrying out evacuation. In effect, movement had to be restricted to the ferry service to Province Wellesley and evacuation by the longer sea voyage by the Malacca Straits was on an extremely limited scale.

Instructions were given that evacuation should be carried out in the following stages:

(a) night of December 13th: European women and children and European males who wished to leave;

(b) night of December 15th: remaining European women (except hospital sisters) and all unwanted European males;

(c) December 16th: remaining European females, European males of military age and Asiatics who had not deserted their posts if they wished to leave. Their families, however, could not be safely accommodated in the

The transport available.

Some Asiatics, mainly with connections outside Penang, were taken off as well as Asiatic female telephone operators. There is no information as to the number of Indians included in these parties.

The following categories of Asiatics were offered the opportunity of leaving according to the Governor's report:

(i) Members of Volunteer and Passive Defence Services:

(ii) Those "natural" leaders of the Asiatic community whom the Resident Counsellor could contact.

(iii) Asiatic police inspectors.

The majority in each category elected to remain. The Indian police contingents, whose conduct, except that of the Sikhs, seems to have been unsatisfactory, were paid off and further money left for them with one of the inspectors. The Chief Police Officer considered that the Sikh police would not leave without their families and did not convey an offer of evacuation to them.

There is no confirmation in the Governor's report that Indians were forcibly prevented from leaving Penang but it appears unlikely as far as those who had stayed at their posts are concerned.

Evacuation of useless mouths from Singapore.

On 15th January His Majesty's Government approved (subject to reservation by Secretary of State for War that he should be consulted before any decision were made not to enforce compulsion) Mr. Duff Cooper's policy in respect of European British women and children at Singapore as follows:

(1) Evacuation of women with children, who wish to leave, should be expedited.

("This will take time and we desire to avoid anything in nature of panic rush.")

(2) Evacuation of remaining wives with children should be compulsory.

(3) Remaining women (with no children) should then be considered for purpose of deciding whether pressure should be used to make them leave.

("Compulsory evacuation of wives of officers without children would create serious situation since many hundreds of them are engaged on essential war service." - N.B. But they surely would not be 'useless mouths'?)

Even a rough estimate of total women and children population of Singapore at present is not practicable. In 1939 the population of Singapore island was European 11,412, Malays 73,940, Indians 59,815, Chinese 565,182.

On the 21st January, the Governor reported that the numbers of women and children evacuated to that date were European 5,296, Indian 665, and Chinese 36. The G.O.C. reported the next day that 4,200 women and children had applied for passages, and the remaining 600 (presumably European) were employed in war work. Up to that date every available berth on all ships leaving Malaya had been filled.

It is the policy of His Majesty's Government that there shall be no racial discrimination in facilities for evacuation. Experience in Penang and the Malay States has shown that the Asiatic population prefers to stay where it is. At Singapore Indian women and children have priority on ships to India, but generally refuse to sail without their menfolk. As regards Chinese, there have not been many applications; few Chinese women wish to go and the Chinese Consul General has stated that it is the policy of his Government to discourage them.

If this attitude is maintained and compulsion has to be used for Asiatics, the local effects on essential Asiatic male labour may well be very demoralising. Secondly, there is the shipping problem; thirdly the problem of destination and accommodation, particularly in the case of Chinese. As regards the last it may be expected that Indians will be accommodated in India, and it is hoped to move 4,000 to 5,000 shortly from Singapore if shipping is available as anticipated (possibly not more than 50% of these will be women and children but "useless" men will be given accommodation as available). SINGAPORE.

Press reports today quote a statement by Malaya Tribune based on information obtained in authoritative quarters at Singapore as to Government policy regarding the evacuation of women and children. The quotation emphasizes the point that there is to be no racial discrimination but adds that there is nothing to prevent women leaving Singapore if they have the means to go and support themselves overseas.

There is, of course, widespread concern here as to European women and children in Malaya, and you will now have received my telegram No. 30 as to the provision of care and funds for evacuees. I am arranging for a similar correction to be published here.

Troopers telegram 60,694 December 28th.

Orders have already been given that women with children, whether service wives or not, should leave and steps are being taken to facilitate their doing so. But this will take time and we desire to avoid anything in the nature of a panic rush.

If wives of officers who have no children here are compelled to leave, a very serious situation will arise as several hundreds of such women are engaged in essential war services and cannot be replaced.

I would propose therefore first to expedite evacuation of wives with children who wish to leave, secondly to compel evacuation of remaining wives with children, thirdly to reconsider in the light of the then existing circumstances whether pressure should be brought to bear on the remaining women and what substitute can be found for their contribution to the war effort.

I should be grateful if you would consult Service Ministers as it is important that the three services should adopt the same policy and inform me of the decision.

D. 9th January, 1942. 17.08 hrs. (local time)
R. 10th January 01.00 hrs. (London time)

No.26 Government House. 8th January.

There were various alerts up country yesterday and Tanjong Malim was raided on 6th January, but I can get no information regarding civilian damage and am asking the military authorities to supply.

Singapore: two nights of bad weather and peace. General attitude of the population satisfactory and the Indian and Chinese leaders fully aware of the importance of maintaining this and are doing all they can.

Evacuation of women and children from here proceeding as shipping becomes available, but there are at least 2,500 applications from Europeans mostly for Australia, apart from considerable numbers still up country.

Indian women and children get priority on the ships to India but generally refuse to sail without their menfolk and offer of one man to five women not generally acceptable.

I have informed the Government of India, and also [POSSIBLY MISSING: group omitted] that there is a ship here now which can take 550 if they wish.

Our first duty is clearly to get away as many women and children as possible.

As regards Chinese, applications to Burma and India for permission to enter on transit visa or as temporary residents have not been answered, and I have therefore taken the responsibility of approving transit visas to Rangoon for one hundred of good character and independent means by ship leaving immediately and have informed the Governor I have done so.

There are not many applications but China is our ally and it is essential we should give some facilities to the women and children if only as a gesture.

Initial enemy successes came as a severe shock to the population as a whole, but I think it can be said there is now more sign of determination, though not of optimism, which needs some success or arrival of reinforcements.

Campaign is difficult for everyone as enemy tactics are to get into the country, live in it, and harass communications.

He has the advantage of being rice-eater and much less dependent on communications than our forces.

He enters village and lives there, fed by the villagers who under threats can hardly be expected to do anything else.

Thence he undertakes guerrilla warfare.

We are trying to play him at his own game and are doing all we can to foster determination by food distribution, propaganda, etc.

The Sultan of Johore has agreed to broadcast tomorrow night, which should have much value.

In amplification of the above, I should explain that instructions are that on receipt of information from the military authorities that an area is threatened, advice is given by the Resident to women and children of all races that they should leave if they wish. No European women have remained behind in such areas, and Asiatics for the most part stay put or go off into rural areas. There has been no stream of Asiatic evacuees to the south. European men can accompany the women if they have no special duty, but most are in some form of civil defence and stay until the military retire. These instructions were not carried out in the Kedah evacuation because the military intimation allowed only a few hours in which to get away. In Penang this applied only to European men, as European women and children left earlier, but as to this see my telegram No. 5 Government House. Both in Penang and Kedah, ample supplies of food were left behind, and in case further withdrawals are ordered, we are now dispersing food stocks into villages and small hamlets as well as in towns so that they may be available for the civil population, and as little as possible shall be left to be destroyed at the last moment. As regards estate labour, the usual procedure has been to give one month's wages and supply of food. All information shows that the Asiatic population is not being molested by the enemy whose propaganda is directed entirely against Europeans. Furthermore, enemy movements, though embracing a large area, do not touch more than a small portion, and as the rice crop will shortly be harvested, Asiatic population should have sufficient food for some time.
