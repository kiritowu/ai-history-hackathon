days during which a merchant must hold gold etc. articles before melting down. They complain that this will mean tying up large capital sums (with loss of interest on the capital so immobilised) and they represent that they do not keep large free sums of working capital. They point out, though not admitting that there is an exact parallel between the positions in the two countries, that the law of England prescribes a period of 48 hours. In conclusion they ask that either this requirement be cancelled or that the period be reduced to one day or not more than 2 days.

The Governor states in paragraph 2 of his covering despatch that it is clear that the petitioners "do not object to a period of 2 days", and seems to confine his argument to the question whether the additional 24 hours are required for police purposes and constitute any real hardship.

I do not feel satisfied that the real objection of the goldsmiths has been sufficiently brought out. Perhaps an indication of the point which weighs most in their minds can be found in paragraph 28 of the earlier petition, where they say that as a result of the immobilisation of the merchant's stock for 3 days "he cannot take advantage of any rise in the market nor can he escape loss if the market falls during that period". Dealing in gold is a speculative business and the Chinese are no doubt as much addicted to gambling in this as in other branches of commerce. In a rapidly fluctuating market a goldsmith (particularly one with small capital resources - and the number of firms suggests that many of them must in fact be small concerns) is at an obvious disadvantage if he must pay on demand for gold articles returned to him under the arrangement described in paragraph 11 of the first petition, but cannot himself melt down the gold and pass it on to one of the larger bullion dealers or banks until 3 days have passed. It would be interesting to know how the local gold price is fixed and to what extent it fluctuates from day to day.

The Government are under no obligation to assist speculation and the purpose of the requirement of the law, viz. to assist the police in recovering stolen goods, is laudable; but the difficulty of the goldsmiths seems to deserve further consideration than has been given to it so far as this despatch indicates. It might possibly be desirable to give the Governor power to suspend the requirement in question if he considered it desirable in view of the abnormal market fluctuations.

Perhaps comments on this aspect of the matter might be asked for, saying that it would be interesting to know how the daily price of gold is fixed and the extent of daily fluctuations.

I have the honour to acknowledge the receipt of Sir Shenton Thomas's despatch No. 86 of the 26th March, 1940, regarding the memorial from Weng Cheong and Company and others praying for the repeal or amendment of Sections 36 and 36 A of the Minor Offences Ordinance, (Chapter 24).

I have the honour to refer to my Straits Settlements despatch No. 44 of the 13th February, 1940, in which I forwarded a Memorial from Weng Cheong and Company and others praying for the repeal or other amendment of sections 36 and 36A of the Minor Offences Ordinance (Chapter 24). The Bill effecting the amendment which formed the subject of the petition was unanimously passed by the Legislative Council at its meeting on the 12th February, 1940, and is now Ordinance No. 7 of 1940. A copy of the Ordinance is enclosed for ease of reference.

2. The principal reason given by the petitioners for the alteration of new section 36 is that their capital will be immobilised for a period of 3 days from the day of purchase of the articles referred to in that section. It is clear, however, from paragraph 14 of their petition that they do not object to a period of 2 days. The point at issue, therefore, is a period of 24 hours.

3. In Malaya, complainants are notoriously lax in reporting losses of property to the Police and in many cases as much as 24 hours elapse before a report is made. As soon as the report is received action is taken by the Police to circularise to goldsmiths, silversmiths and jewellers a description of the articles which might be disposed of to such businesses. This cannot, however, normally be done in less than 24 hours with the result that after a theft it may happen that 48 hours have elapsed before

4. While little sympathy can be felt for an owner who is dilatory in reporting his loss, it is undesirable to facilitate theft by making it easier for the thieves to dispose of stolen property. It is the opinion of my advisers that, if all possible measures are to be taken to effect the recovery of stolen property, 48 hours is the minimum period which can be allowed for the dissemination of information and the identification of property and that even a period of 3 days is barely sufficient for this purpose. I do not consider that the additional period of 24 hours constitutes any real hardship to the petitioners or that their arguments are of sufficient substance to outweigh the considerations I have stated above. I am unable therefore to recommend that the law should be amended or that you should intervene in the matter on their behalf.

(c) by the insertion in subsection (5) after the words “public road” in the second line thereof of the words “or in any public place or place of public resort”; and

“(6) For the purposes of this section—

“(2) Any officer or non-commissioned officer of His Majesty’s Army, or of His Majesty’s Indian Army, and any officer or warrant or petty officer of His Majesty’s Navy, or non-commissioned officer of Marines or any officer or non-commissioned officer of His Majesty’s Air Force, may with or without warrant arrest any person offending in his view against the provisions of this section, and such person may be brought before a Police Court to be dealt with according to law.”

The Humble Petition of WENG CHEONG and others of Singapore, Penang and Malacca, Goldsmiths and Bullion Merchants.

SHEWETH as follows:

1. Your Petitioners presented a Memorial to you on the subject of Sections 36 and 36A of the Minor Offences Ordinance (Chapter 24) which Memorial bore date the 20th October 1938. Subsequently discussions took place and in the result the Government of the Straits Settlements are now placing before the Legislative Council a Bill to amend inter alia these sections of the Minor Offences Ordinance. It is proposed to repeal Sections 36 and 36A of the Minor Offences Ordinance and to substitute three new sections Nod. 36, 36A and 36B.

2. Your Petitioners gratefully recognise that the Government of the Straits Settlements has considered their objections to the old sections in a very fair and open minded manner and although they still have objections to the new Section 36 they are very appreciative of the manner in which the Government has received the arguments advanced by and on behalf of your Petitioners.

3. With regard to the new proposed sections 36A and 36B your Petitioners are now satisfied with these draft sections. They have accepted the principle of registration on the understanding that registration is to be ministerial and that none of them is liable to be removed from the register merely at the discretion of any licensing officer. The Government also now proposes that Sections 36 and 36A are not to apply to mere employees and thus has done away with the objection to registration of every employee.

===== Page 21 =====
4. The Government has also modified Section 36 in such a manner as to exclude bullion merchants from the operation of the Sections.

5. But the goldsmiths, silversmiths and jewellers still object to the proposed new Section 36.

6. That proposed new Section reads as follows:

“36. If any pawnbroker or any dealer in second-hand goods, or any worker in platinum, gold, or silver or any dealer in platinum, gold, or silver articles, within three days of receipt of any goods or any such metals, or articles, or after receiving information from a police officer that any specified goods, metals or articles, have been stolen or fraudulently obtained, melts, alters, defaces, or put away, without the previous permission of the Chief Police Officer, and it is found that such goods, metals, or articles, were stolen or fraudulently obtained, such pawnbroker, second-hand dealer, worker, or dealer, shall be liable to a fine not exceeding five hundred dollars or to imprisonment of either description for a term not exceeding six months or to both.”

7. Your Petitioners ask leave to repeat paragraphs 11 and 12 of their Memorial dated 20th October 1938. These paragraphs read as follows:

“11. One of the customs of the goldsmiths trade in Singapore is as follows. Any purchaser of any article of jewellery from a goldsmith is at liberty to return it to the goldsmith at any time and get a refund of part of the purchase price calculated as follows. The value of the gold returned is calculated as on the day of return of the object of jewellery; 3% is deducted for the labour and loss involved in remelting down the gold and the balance will be refunded to the purchaser. (Precious stones are not taken back but only gold). These terms are the regular terms of trade used by practically all goldsmiths and they are often clearly set out on the firm’s bill-heads. This option to return the article purchased and the cancelling of the original contract of sale upon terms fixed to protect the goldsmiths against loss in one of the terms of the original contract of sale. The goldsmith has no option in the matter and he is bound to take back the jewellery upon being compensated in the manner above referred to. A goldsmith of course will not buy or take back in that way any article of jewellery except articles manufactured or sold by himself.

“12. This right (or term of the sale contract) which the purchaser enjoys of returning jewellery to the goldsmith who sells it (by suffering a trifling loss) is regarded as one of the most important elements of the contract by local purchasers many of whom invest large portion of their savings in purchases of jewellery as it ensures the ready convertibility of the article into money at a price which is almost a fixed price.”

===== Page 22 =====
8. Your Petitioners point out that the customs of their trade differ in material particulars from the customs of the goldsmiths' and silversmiths' trade in Europe, the important point being that they are under contractual obligation to repurchase all articles sold by them whenever the owner of such articles calls upon them to do so. (In Europe it is believed that no goldsmith or silversmith would agree to accept any such obligation to repurchase). The result is that they can be compelled to repurchase quantities of jewellery sold by them without having the option of refusing it and this may necessarily tie up their capital in a very serious way.

9. If they are further to have large capital sums tied up for a minimum period of three days they anticipate that this will have a very serious effect upon their business. Of course they will lose the interest on their money for the three day period during which the articles cannot be dealt with. This is itself of some importance, but it is perhaps not so serious as the obligation to tie up so much capital for that purpose. This is sure to leave many of them short of working capital for the purpose of carrying on their business. Goldsmiths and silversmiths do not usually keep large free sums of working capital; the greater part of their capital they naturally and reasonably employ in their businesses. This is probably more true of the goldsmiths' and jewellers' business than of any other business because of the fluid and easily convertible nature of the goldsmiths' and jewellers' stock-trade.

10. Your Petitioners are not unaware of the English Act "24 and 25 Victoria (Cap. 110)" (the Old Metal Dealers' Act 1861). By that Act dealers in old metals are under the obligation to keep all old metals purchased or received by them without changing the form in which such articles were when so purchased, or disposing of the same in any way for a period of forty-eight hours after such article has been purchased or received. Your Petitioners say there are considerable differences between the state of affairs in England and the state of affairs in the Colony and that this Act does not afford a true or fair parallel or basis for comparison with business in the Colony.

11. But in any case in the first place under the English Act the property is only to be held for 48 hours instead of 3 days. It stands to reason that the shorter the time the less are the objections of the goldsmiths and the jewellers to property being immobilised and rendered incapable of being dealt with.

12. The English Act does not deal with precious metals. There is a clear distinction between the precious metals and "old junk" which is one of the expressions used in the Old Metal Dealers' Act 1861. Attention may also be drawn to Section 7 of the new proposed Prevention of Crime Ordinance 1939 which is directed at old metal dealers and sets out the metals in which they deal. A dealer who buys scrap iron or old junk usually expects his capital to be locked up in such goods for a fairly considerable time, whereas a dealer who puts his money into the precious metals expects to turn it over quickly and he naturally receives a much smaller profit on each transaction.

===== Page 23 =====
13. The number of goldsmiths and jewellers in the Straits Settlements of course is considerable but it is very small compared with the number of such traders in England and therefore it is much easier to control and supervise these traders in the Straits Settlements. It is also easier to investigate transactions by them in the Straits Settlements than it is in England as the actual number of shops to be investigated is so very much smaller. It is therefore suggested that there is no adequate reason why any longer time should be required in the Straits Settlements than in England for holding up any property for enquiry. The necessary time should indeed be less.

14. Your Petitioners therefore humbly and respectfully ask (1) either that the said Section 36 be altered by omitting the following words, that is to say:—

“within three days of receipt of any goods or any such metals, or articles.”

or alternatively (2) that the period of three days mentioned in the said Section be reduced to one day or to not more than two days.

And your Petitioners as in duty bound will ever pray etc., etc.

Singapore, 35 January, 1940.
