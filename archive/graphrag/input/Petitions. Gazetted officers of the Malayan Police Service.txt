Downing Street,
12 August, 1940.

I am etc. to acknowledge receipt of your confidential despatch of the 28th May, forwarding a memorial from certain gazetted officers of the Malayan Police Service recruited as Probationers during the years 1927 to 1934 regarding the application of the revised salary scale for officers in the Police Department.

2. The decision to improve the initial salaries of Probationers, with effect from the 1st of January, 1937, was based upon the recommendation in the MacGregor Commission Report, and, in considering the application of that recommendation, it clearly became necessary, in practice, to effect/

===== Page 6 =====
effect an adjustment of the salaries of certain Police Probationers, who had been appointed in the recent years preceding 1937, in order that their position might not be prejudiced in relation to new entrants.

I am, however, satisfied that it was never intended to involve a general enhancement of the remuneration of officers on the time scale, and I have no reason to believe such a course to be justified.

3. I shall be obliged if you will cause the petitioners to be informed accordingly, adding that I regret that I am not prepared to intervene on their behalf in the manner suggested in their memorial.

I have, etc.

My Lord,

I have the honour to transmit, in duplicate, a memorial addressed to Your Lordship by certain gazetted officers of the Malayan Police Service recruited as Probationers during the years 1927 to 1934 regarding the application of the revised salary scale for officers in the Police Department. Copies of a similar petition addressed to Sir Shenton Thomas together with the letter sent by his direction rejecting it are enclosed with the memorial.

2. The amendment of the salary scale of Police Cadets formed the subject of Sir Shenton Thomas's Malayan Establishment Confidential despatch dated the 21st of April, 1938, in which he supported the recommendations of a Committee appointed to consider the question of salaries paid in this and other Departments. So far as officers in the Police Department are concerned approval of the Committee's proposals meant that with effect from the 1st of January, 1937, all Unpassed Probationers drew $300 a month instead of $250 a month, all Passed Probationers $350 a month instead of $300 a month, while confirmed officers in their first year drew $400 a month instead of $350 a month. A printed copy of the report, extracts from which are quoted in paragraph 6 of Enclosure 'A' to the memorial, is attached to facilitate reference.

3. I regret the delay which has occurred in submitting this petition but it has been thought desirable to give full consideration first to a proposal made by Your Lordship's advisers in Colonial Office letter No. 50007/172/39 dated the 15th of December, 1939, that the application of the revised salary scale should be modified to the advantage of the nineteen

senior officers of the Malayan Customs Service whose petition formed the subject of Sir Shenton Thomas's Malayan Establishment Confidential despatch dated the 29th of July, 1939. Copies of that letter and of my reply to it are enclosed for perusal in this connection as the arguments held to justify refusal of any concession in the Customs Department apply with equal, if not greater, force in the present case.

4. It does not appear to be necessary that I should recapitulate in full here the views set out by Sir Shenton Thomas in his Confidential despatch dated the 29th of July, 1939. As in the case of the Customs officers, petitioners base their claim to share in the benefit of the higher salary approved for Cadets and Passed Cadets with effect from the 1st of January, 1937, on the ground that the Committee was wrong in stating that

(a) "the age of recruitment has altered and is now some two years greater than it was formerly", and

(b) "educational standards have risen too and Probationers with University degrees are now quite common".

5. With regard to paragraph 4(a) above the petitioners, in seeking to prove that there is little difference in age between Probationers appointed prior to 1931 and those appointed subsequently, emphasise the average age to a degree which seems to indicate that the statement in the report is erroneous. The dates of recruitment have been entered on Enclosures 'A' and 'AA' to the petition addressed to Sir Shenton Thomas and from these figures the following comparative table has been compiled:

| 1930 | 3 | 4 | 4 | 2 |

===== Page 9 =====
It will be observed that out of 45 officers recruited during 1927-1931, 28 were under 21 years of age, while during the period 1934-1937 only 7 such officers were appointed; and that 17 out of 45 officers recruited in the former years were aged 21 or 22 years as against 14 out of 21 in the latter. I consider that these figures justify the statement of the Committee which was an approximation only. It should further be pointed out that the age for qualification for entry to the Service was raised in 1938 and candidates must now be over 21 and under 22 years of age on the 1st of January in the year in which selection takes place.

6. As to paragraph 4(b) above I would invite Your Lordship's attention to paragraphs 11 to 16 of the letter dated the 9th of March, 1939, written by the Inspector-General of Police, Federated Malay States, in which he comments on the comparative educational qualifications of Probationers appointed before and after 1931, a copy of which is attached to Enclosure 'A' to this memorial. The fact that only one out of 45 officers in schedule B obtained a University degree as compared with 6 out of 21 in schedule BB attached to the petition addressed to Sir Shenton Thomas taken with the greater age of the majority of Probationers recruited from 1934 onwards supports the contention that there was in 1934 a change in the system of recruitment and an improvement in the educational qualifications of the officers selected.

7. The statement in paragraph 3 of the memorial that officers on the timescale of the Malayan Police Service are serving on three different salary scales requires explanation although it hardly affects the case which petitioners endeavour to establish. The circumstances in which officers appointed prior to 1927 were, on promotion to the grade of Assistant Commissioner, paid a salary of $375 a month instead of $350 as provided in the scheme are explained in Sir Laurence Guillemard's

===== Page 10 =====
Federated Malay States despatch No. 658 dated the 27th of November, 1926, and Mr. L.S. Amery's Federated Malay States despatch No. 213 dated the 20th of April, 1927. The special treatment it was found necessary to accord to these officers arose out of a mistake which cannot, I consider, be used to the advantage of petitioners now.

8. It must be emphasised that the Committee with whose recommendations petitioners are dissatisfied was appointed to investigate a suggestion put forward in paragraph 29 of the Report of the Committee on the Temporary Allowances in Malaya, 1937, for raising minimum salaries which were considered in certain cases to be too low. Having decided that better terms for Police Probationers were justified on the ground that the age of recruitment and the educational qualifications had been raised, the Committee recommended that the revised salary scale should be brought into force on the 1st of January, 1937, to coincide with the effective date for the payment of Children's Allowances. It was never intended, and the Committee did not suggest, that officers in receipt of adequate salaries should derive any benefit from the improved terms granted to officers at the bottom of the scale. The most senior of the Assistant Superintendents of Police appointed in 1934 were not confirmed until after the 1st of January, 1937, and they therefore on confirmation drew $400 instead of $350 a month under the old scheme. Owing to the fact that there was no recruitment during the years 1932 and 1933 the need to adjust salaries to provide that no officer should be in receipt of a lesser salary than his junior did not arise in the Police Department.

9. I have given this matter very careful consideration and as a result I am convinced that the recommendations of the Committee are sound and that petitioners have no just cause

===== Page 11 =====
for complaint. I would therefore ask that Your Lordship will authorise me to inform the officers who have signed this memorial that Your Lordship has received and examined their petition but that Your Lordship is not prepared to intervene on their behalf.

I have the honour to be,

My Lord,

Your Lordship's most obedient, humble servant,

SINGAPORE, Straits Settlements,
3rd January, 1940.

Sir,

We have the honour to submit the following documents for your consideration:

A. The petition of certain Gazetted Officers of the Malayan Police Service addressed to His Excellency the Governor and Commander-in-Chief of the Straits Settlements and High Commissioner of the Malay States together with comments thereon by the Inspector-General of Police, F.M.S. and replies thereto.

B. A copy of the covering letter by the Honourable The Inspector-General of Police, S.S.

C. A copy of the letter by His Excellency the Governor and Commander-in-chief of the Straits Settlements and High Commissioner of the Malay States rejecting the petition.

- 2 -

His Excellency the Governor has stated in the last paragraph of his letter that as the recommendations of the Committee appointed by His Excellency to consider paragraphs 29 and 30 of the MacGregor Committee's report were accepted by you he is not prepared to recommend any modification. We now therefore submit our petition direct to you. We regret at this time of war to burden you but we would appreciate a decision. The petition was originally submitted before the outbreak of hostilities.

- 3 -

The aim of our petition is not to secure a salary scale higher than that of officers of equal rank; it is to obtain a uniform salary scale. At present officers of equal rank with equal responsibilities,

===== Page 13 =====
with equal duties, with the same standard of living and with the same qualifying examinations to pass before confirmation in appointment, have three different salary scales, all operative within a short period of six years (end of 1927 to beginning of 1934).

- 4 -

With reference to paragraph 2(i) of His Excellency's letter we would remark that this very fact is fully covered in paragraphs 6, 7, 8, 9 and 10 and in the schedules of the petition.

- 5 -

We are fully aware of the facts stated in paragraph 2(ii) of His Excellency's letter. They are quoted in paragraph 2 of the petition.

- 6 -

With reference to paragraph 2(iii) of His Excellency's letter we are well aware of the facts as stated and indeed they are the very facts against which we petition.

- 7 -

His Excellency states in paragraph 3 of his letter that:

"...out of forty-five officers recruited during the period 1927 to 1931, twenty-eight were under and seventeen over the age of 21; and that out of twenty-one officers recruited during the period 1934 to 1937 only seven officers were under 21 while fourteen were over that age"

In our opinion this is not in accord with a reasonable interpretation of the analysis of the very complete facts given in paragraph 8(ii) and the schedules of the petition. His Excellency has taken an incomplete view, whereas we have based our deduction on averages which can be the only true distinction. We would stress the word average where it occurs in the petition.

===== Page 14 =====
In paragraph 4 of his letter His Excellency has stated that the benefits received by those officers under the revised scheme have been in no way at the expense of those under the old scheme and cannot be held as giving rise to any claim for equal consideration. But we point out that however this may be, officers recruited before us are on a higher salary scale and officers recruited after us are on a still higher salary scale. In this connection we draw attention to paragraphs 5 and 13 of the petition.

We would ask in conclusion that special attention be paid to the letter of Mr. A. H. Dickinson (now Inspector-General of Police, S.S.) attached hereto, in which he states that Mr. R. H. de S. Omraet (late Inspector-General of Police, S.S.) studied the petition and gave it his full support.

We have the honour to be,

Sir,

Your most obedient servants,

The Rt. Honourable the Secretary of State for the Colonies,

HIS EXCELLENCY SIR THOMAS SHENTON WHITELEGGE THOMAS,
G.C.M.G., O.B.E., GOVERNOR AND COMMANDER-IN-CHIEF
OF THE STRAITS SETTLEMENTS AND HIGH COMMISSIONER
OF THE FEDERATED MALAY STATES.

THE PETITION OF THE UNDERMENTIONED
GAZETTED OFFICERS OF THE MALAYAN
POLICE SERVICE,

HUMBLY SHEWETH that

1. Your Petitioners were recruited as Police Probationers since 1927 and prior to 1934.

2. On the 27th July 1938, Federated Malay States Head Quarters Order No. 30 was issued and contains the following revised scale of salaries (hereinafter referred to as "the revised scale") approved for Police Cadets and Assistant Superintendents appointed after 1st January 1934 (hereinafter referred to as "the new recruits") namely:-
$300 per mensem for Cadets, $350 per mensem for Passed Cadets and $400 per mensem on confirmation in the rank of Assistant Superintendent and thereafter by annual increments of $25 per mensem to $800, with a stipulation that an efficiency bar must be passed before $600 per mensem may be drawn - the revised scale taking effect from the 1st January 1937 with arrears of salary payable from that date. (No corresponding Straits Settlements Head Quarters Order has been issued as there is as yet no Officer serving in the Straits Settlements who benefits under the revised scale).

3. The salary scale of your Petitioners was and is $250 per mensem for Probationers, $300 per mensem for Passed Probationers, $350 per mensem on confirmation in the rank of Assistant Superintendent and thereafter by annual increments of $25 per mensem to $800 per mensem with the same stipulation for the passing of an efficiency bar before $600 per mensem may be drawn. It will be observed therefore that there is a difference of $50 per month between the salary scale of the new recruits and that of your Petitioners of equal rank.

4. The revised scale purports to give effect to a recommendation made in the report of a Committee (hereinafter referred to as "the said Committee") appointed by Your Excellency to consider certain suggestions put forward in paragraphs 29 and 30 of a confidential Report of the Macgregor Committee on Temporary Allowances, 1937.

5. The aim of this Petition is to secure a uniform scale of salary for Gazetted Officers of equal rank as there now obtains in the service three different salary schemes. Officers recruited prior to 1927 are on a scale of one $25 increment higher than your Petitioners. Officers recruited after 1934 are on a scale of two $25 increments higher than your Petitioners.

===== Page 18 =====
6. An examination of the recommendation in so far as it is material to the purpose of this petition reveals that it is founded upon two propositions, namely that

(a) "... the age of recruitment has altered and is now some two years greater than it was formerly ..."
(i.e. prior to 1934).

(b) "Educational standards have risen too and probationers with University degrees are now quite common."

In support of (a), the said Committee alluded to the fact that

"the four Police Probationers recruited in 1931 were all born in 1912, while of the four Police Probationers recruited in 1934, three were born in 1912 and one in 1911."

No illustration was given by the said Committee in support of (b), and although there is no direct reference made to your Petitioners, it is stated that they

"were recruited at a time when it was the normal practice to recruit candidates direct from school."

7. Your Petitioners respectfully submit that

(a) the two abovementioned propositions are founded upon a misapprehension on the part of the said Committee, which is attributable to incorrect, incomplete and inadequate data.

(b) it is misleading to select from a class of persons certain individual cases for the purpose of establishing a guiding rule or principle unless it can be said with certainty that the examples chosen are in every material respect typical of the class to which such rule or principle is sought to be applied.

(c) the cases cited by the said Committee were extreme rather than typical.

8. As regards the first of the said Committee's propositions quoted in paragraph 6 (a) (supra), your Petitioners submit that

(i) there were five and not four Police Probationers recruited in 1931. It is not understood why the fifth, namely Mr. C.N. Godwin (B.A.Law Cantab) at present Officer-in-charge of the Police District Port Dickson, was omitted from consideration. He was born in 1909 and his age at the date of recruitment in 1931 was therefore 22.

(ii) a perusal of the attached Schedules "A" and "AA" shews that the average age of recruitment of your Petitioners was 20 years and 6½ months and that the average age of the new recruits at the date of recruitment was 21 years and 3 months - a difference of 8½ months, and not "some two years" as stated by the said Committee. The fact that some of your Petitioners were recruited between the ages of 19 and 20 does not per se warrant the sweeping statement that "the age of recruitment has altered and is now some two years greater" inasmuch as Probationers of 22 years of age and even more than that were, in fact, recruited prior to 1934. The true and proper test is the average age, and not the age of only the younger recruits.

===== Page 19 =====
9. As regards the second of the said Committee's propositions quoted in paragraph 6 (b) (supra) coupled with the statement that "it was the normal practice to recruit candidates direct from school", your Petitioners submit that

(i) University degrees and training are among the qualifications of some of your Petitioners as appears from the attached Schedule "B" wherein it will also be seen that others were engaged in activities and training no less useful. (For convenience a similar Schedule "BB" has been compiled relating to the new recruits).

(ii) the proportion of your Petitioners who were recruited direct from school is only 10 out of 45 i.e. 22.2% as appears from Schedule "B" and such proportion, in the humble opinion of your Petitioners, cannot afford adequate evidence to establish what has been alluded to as "the normal practice".

10. Your Petitioners venture to suggest that the particulars given in paragraphs 8 and 9 hereof and in the Schedules annexed to this Petition would have exerted without doubt a considerable influence on the findings of the said Committee and in the humble opinion of your Petitioners the said Committee's recommendation would in all probability have been wider in scope and included your Petitioners within its purview.

11. Any scheme evolved by the said Committee which aimed at reducing, if not eliminating, the existing difference in the salary scales of recruits prior to 1927 and of your Petitioners, would have been welcomed by your Petitioners. The revised scale, while making no attempt whatsoever to adjust such difference, creates a third salary scheme which serves to emphasize the inequality prevailing to the further detriment of your Petitioners.

12. The said Committee in paragraph 2 of its Report expressed the opinion that it was not "satisfactory merely to increase the minimum salary payable under a scheme without taking into consideration the consequent effect that such an increase may have upon the scheme as a whole". The recommendation subsequently made by the said Committee completely ignores the salary scale of your Petitioners and is not only at variance but cannot logically or even reasonable be reconciled with such opinion.

13. Your Petitioners respectfully submit that the revised scale operates on them inequitably and creates a differentiation which could not have been in the contemplation of those responsible for it. A few examples are given below to illustrate the discrimination which now exists in the salary scales of Officers of equal rank.

(a) While your Petitioners are required to serve a period of 13 years before reaching the efficiency bar at $600 per mensem, Officers recruited prior to 1927 reach the bar after 12 years service and Officers benefiting under the revised scale reach the bar after only 11 years' service.

(b) Those of the new recruits who joined in 1934 draw the same salary as those of your Petitioners who have two years longer service to their credit and in addition, arrears of salary calculated from the 1st January 1937 have been paid to all the new recruits entitled thereto.

(c) In the event of retirement through ill health or for other good causes, the pension payable is calculated upon the salary earned by the Officer on the date of retirement.

===== Page 20 =====
retirement. A new recruit would therefore be entitled to receive the same pension as that which would be paid to one of your Petitioners who had served two years more than the new recruit.

(d) There is a tendency to regard Officers drawing equal salary as having the same or similar experience and it is apprehended by your Petitioners that seniority in the service may be overlooked when selecting Officers for promotion.

(e) Your Petitioners, while still on probation, were required to discharge the duties attached to full executive posts or to proceed to China for the purpose of furthering study of a dialect - a system which has since been abandoned in favour of one which provides facilities enabling the new recruits to devote their whole time to the passing of the examinations before being called upon to do either.

Under the former system it was possible for few of your Petitioners to become Passed Probationers at all, whereas under present conditions it is not only possible but a normal expectation to attain this within 18 months.

(f) Many of your Petitioners while still Police Probationers with a salary of $250 per mensem were assigned to what are now termed "Duty Posts" entailing the performance of the work of Assistant Superintendents of Police with all its concomitant responsibilities, but few received any extra remuneration for it. The new recruits as Passed Probationers when assigned to similar "Duty Posts" received the remuneration of acting Assistant Superintendents of Police which, in their case, amounts to $375 per mensem.

(g) The cost of living was no less when your Petitioners were recruits. It was often necessary for most of your Petitioners to seek financial assistance from their parents and other sources, during their first tour of service.

14. As hereinbefore stated the aim of this Petition is to secure a uniform scale of salary for Officers of equal rank, and your Petitioners consider that this can only be achieved by drawing Your Excellency's attention to the existing discrimination in the service which is entirely without justification. Your Petitioners feel that an appeal to Your Excellency's high sense of impartiality and sincere desire to do justice will meet with the usual sympathetic consideration and they confidently hope to be advised of an early termination of a state of affairs the continuance of which is both invidious and eminently undesirable.

And your Petitioners as in duty bound will ever pray.

Dated this 22nd day of February, 1939.

Report of the Committee appointed by His Excellency the Governor and High Commissioner to consider certain suggestions put forward in paragraphs 29 and 30 of the report of the MacGregor Committee on Temporary Allowances, 1937.

===== Page 39 =====
We were appointed as a Committee to consider certain suggestions in paragraphs 29 and 30 of the report by the Committee presided over by Sir A. D. A. MacGregor and to report how these suggestions could best be implemented.

While it is true that the MacGregor Committee confined their remarks to the minimum salaries paid to certain classes of the more lowly-paid European officers, it is not in our opinion satisfactory merely to increase the minimum salary payable under a scheme without taking into consideration the consequent effect that such an increase may have upon the scheme as a whole. We have therefore not hesitated to suggest the recasting of certain schemes, even at the risk of being held to have gone outside our terms of reference.

The question of the inadequacy of the basic salaries drawn by certain classes of European officers, such as Prison Warders and Police Inspectors, has been raised from time to time throughout the last ten years and in 1933, although the financial situation did not render the time opportune for a revision of basic salaries, a so-called "Palliative Allowance" was granted to married officers drawing salaries below certain levels. This was admittedly a purely temporary measure and there was at least an implied promise that basic salaries would be revised when the financial situation improved. We venture to suggest that that time has now come. If the various revisions which we recommend are accepted the "Palliative Allowance" will of course disappear.

Our task in drawing up revised salary scales has been immensely facilitated by the acceptance of the MacGregor report, as it is no longer necessary for us to face the dilemma that a salary scale adequate but no more than adequate for a married man without children may be inadequate for one with several children, whereas a scale based on the latter possibility will in many cases be unduly generous and therefore unwarrantably expensive. This problem, which was perhaps the most difficult of all the problems that faced the "Palliative Allowance" Committee, is no longer a serious one and we have based our scales on the salary we consider necessary to enable a married man without children to live in a way consonant with his position in life and have allowed for a moderate increase as his service lengthens. We accept as approximately correct the findings of the "Palliative Allowance" Committee as to the minimum salary necessary for the various classes of officers with which we are concerned.

In view of the fact that the recommendations of the MacGregor Committee have had effect from the 1st January 1937 and for precisely similar reasons to those adduced by that Committee, we recommend that the salary scales proposed by us, if agreed to, should be brought into force from the same date, namely the 1st January, 1937. This would mean that the Palliative Allowance would disappear as from that date and be replaced by the "Children's Allowance" recommended by the MacGregor Committee.

In the unlikely but just conceivable case of this involving any officer in a refund, we recommend that such refund should be waived. If such a case does in fact exist, which is doubtful, the amount involved cannot at most exceed a few dollars.

(a) Police and Customs Probationers—

This amounts to giving these officers an increase of $20 and $30 respectively over their present maxima.

My dear Gent,

I regret that your letter No. 50007/172/39 dated the 15th of December, 1939, in which you proposed that the application of the revised salary scale should be modified to the advantage of the nineteen senior officers of the Malayan Customs Service whose petition was forwarded with Sir Shenton Thomas's Malayan Establishment Confidential despatch dated the 29th of July, 1939, has remained for so long unanswered. Both Governments have now expressed themselves opposed to the grant of any concession to petitioners and I agree with this view.
